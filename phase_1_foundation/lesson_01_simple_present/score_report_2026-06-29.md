### SECTION C — Sentence Transformation

**Q26 — Correct** ✅ Tom → "does not go" (he/she/it → does not + base verb). Correct.

**Q27 — Correct** ✅ "She drinks" → "Does she drink…?" — auxiliary "does" moves to front; main verb reverts to base form.

**Q28 — Incorrect**
- Correct answer: `When do they play football?`  |  Your answer: `When do they play football on weekends?`
- ✅ Why it's correct: The Wh-word "When" stands in for the time expression "on weekends". Once "When" is at the front, "on weekends" must be removed — otherwise you have two time expressions ("When" + "on weekends"), which is contradictory.
- ❌ Why yours is wrong: Keeping "on weekends" makes the question redundant and slightly odd ("When do they play football on weekends?" implies you already know it's on weekends and are asking for a more precise time — that is not the intent of the transformation).
- 📘 Rule: Wh-word replaces the information being questioned — see `lecture.md – PHẦN 1 §1.4 Câu hỏi Wh-`. Remove the original phrase entirely.
- ⚠️ Tip: A quick check: after adding the Wh-word, scan the sentence for the phrase it replaced. If it's still there, delete it.

**Q29 — Incorrect**
- Correct answer: `My brothers study medicine at university.`  |  Your answer: `My brothers studies medicine at university.`
- ✅ Why it's correct: "My brothers" is a plural subject (= they). Plural subjects use the base form of the verb: "study", not "studies".
- ❌ Why yours is wrong: "studies" is the third-person singular form (he/she/it). After changing the subject from "my sister" (singular → she) to "my brothers" (plural → they), the verb must change too: studies → study.
- 📘 Rule: Subject-verb agreement — singular subjects take V-s/es; plural subjects take the base form V — see `lecture.md – PHẦN 1 §1.1 Câu khẳng định`.
- ⚠️ Tip: Whenever you change the subject, always re-check the verb. Ask: "Is the new subject he/she/it? → -s. Is it we/they/you? → base form."

**Q30 — Correct** ✅ "He doesn't like" → affirmative: "He likes" (restore the -s and remove "doesn't"). Correct.

**Q31 — Correct** ✅ "Do you eat breakfast…?" with new subject "your father" (= he) → "Does your father eat breakfast every day?" Correct.

**Q32 — Incorrect**
- Correct answer: `The train leaves at 7 am, doesn't it?`  |  Your answer: `The train leaves at 7 am, is it?`
- ✅ Why it's correct: Question tag rules — for an affirmative main clause using a main verb ("leaves"), the tag is formed with the auxiliary "does" in its negative form + subject pronoun. "The train leaves" → auxiliary = does → negative tag = "doesn't it?"
- ❌ Why yours is wrong: "is it?" uses "be", but "leaves" is not a be-verb — it's a regular main verb. You cannot use "is" as the tag auxiliary here. Also, tags for affirmative clauses are always negative.
- 📘 Rule: Question tag formula — Affirmative clause + negative auxiliary tag: use the same auxiliary (or "do/does" for main verbs) in its negative form + pronoun — this topic extends beyond `lecture.md` content for this lesson; note it for self-study.
- ⚠️ Tip: For Simple Present with main verbs, the auxiliary is always do/does. Tag pattern: "S + V-s/es …, doesn't + pronoun?" → "She works hard, doesn't she?"

**Q33 — Correct** ✅ "I don't always do my homework." — inserting "don't" before "always" correctly negates the sentence while keeping "always" in its proper position (between the auxiliary and main verb).

### SECTION D — Error Correction

**Q34 — Correct** ✅ Two errors were present in the original (the instruction says one, but there are actually two): "don't" → "doesn't" (she = third-person singular) AND "prefer" → "prefers" (same subject). Your correction addresses both and matches the answer key.

**Q35 — Correct** ✅ In "Does … go?", the main verb must stay in its base form: "go", not "goes". The auxiliary "Does" already carries the third-person singular marker.

**Q36 — Incorrect**
- Correct answer: `My mother is always tired after work.`  |  Your answer: `My mother always tires after work.`
- ✅ Why it's correct: The error is the **word order** of the frequency adverb "always" with the be-verb "is". The rule: frequency adverbs come **after** "be": "is always", not "always is". The corrected sentence is "My mother **is always** tired after work."
- ❌ Why yours is wrong: You removed "is" and replaced it with "tires", which changes the sentence's meaning completely. "Always tires" = she repeatedly becomes tired (a process), while "is always tired" = she is consistently in a state of tiredness after work. The original sentence describes a state (she feels tired), not an action.
- 📘 Rule: Adverb of frequency placement with be-verb: S + **am/is/are + adverb** — see `lecture.md – PHẦN 3: VỊ TRÍ CỦA TRẠNG TỪ TẦN SUẤT` and `lecture.md – LỖI THƯỜNG GẶP §Lỗi 5`.
- ⚠️ Tip: When you see "always is / never is / often is", the fix is never to remove "is" — simply swap the order: → "is always / is never / is often". The be-verb stays; only the position changes.

**Q37 — Correct** ✅ "He" is third-person singular → "work" → "works". Correctly identified and fixed.

### SECTION E — Translation

**Q42 — Partial credit (1/2 pts)**
- Model answer: `My sister wakes up at 5 o'clock every morning because she works far from home.`
- Your answer: `My sister wakes up at 5 o'clock every days because she works very away from home.`
- ✅ What you got right: Both verbs are perfectly conjugated — "wakes up" (third-person singular) and "works" (third-person singular). Sentence structure and word order are correct.
- ❌ What to fix: Two vocabulary/grammar errors: (1) "every days" → "every day" — the determiner "every" always takes a singular noun; (2) "very away" → "far" — "very away" is not idiomatic English. "Far" (or "far from home") is the correct adverb for distance.
- 📘 Rule: "every" + singular noun (every day, every morning, every week). Distance adverb: "far" — not a phrase with "very".
- ⚠️ Tip: Memorise the fixed phrase: "works far from home" (làm việc xa nhà). "Far" does not pair with "very" — use "very far" only: "she lives very far from home" is fine, but "works very away" is never correct.

**Q43 — Partial credit (1/2 pts)**
- Model answer: `Our school starts at 7:30 am and finishes / ends at 5 pm.`
- Your answer: `Our school starts at 7:30 AM on the morning and end at 5 PM.`
- ✅ What you got right: "Our school starts at 7:30" is correct — "starts" has the right -s suffix. The overall sentence meaning is conveyed.
- ❌ What to fix: Two errors: (1) "end" → "ends" — the subject "Our school" (= it) requires third-person singular -s on both verbs: "starts … and ends"; (2) "on the morning" → either omit it (the key doesn't include this phrase) or use "in the morning" — the preposition for parts of the day is "in" (in the morning / in the evening), never "on".
- 📘 Rule: Third-person singular -s applies to ALL verbs in the clause, including the second verb in a compound predicate. Time-of-day preposition: **in** the morning/afternoon/evening; **at** night; **on** + specific days (on Monday).
- ⚠️ Tip: "Starts … and end" is a classic coordination error. When two verbs share the same subject, both must agree with it: "Our school starts … and **ends**."

**Q44 — Partial credit (1/2 pts)**
- Model answer: `He never eats fast food because he cares about his health.`
- Your answer: `He never eats fast food because he takes care strength.`
- ✅ What you got right: The first clause is perfect — "He never eats fast food" correctly places "never" before the main verb and conjugates "eats" for third-person singular.
- ❌ What to fix: The second clause fails on both vocabulary and grammar: (a) "takes care strength" is not idiomatic — the correct phrase is "cares about his health" or "takes care of his health" (requires "of"); (b) "strength" (sức mạnh) ≠ "health" (sức khỏe) — wrong word choice; the Vietnamese "sức khỏe" means health, not strength.
- 📘 Rule: "care about something" OR "take care of something" (not "take care + noun" directly). Vocabulary: sức khỏe = health; sức mạnh = strength.
- ⚠️ Tip: "He cares about his health" is the most natural phrasing here. When translating, flag Vietnamese words that look similar in meaning (sức khỏe vs. sức mạnh) — they translate to very different English words.

**Q45 — Correct** ✅ (2/2 pts)
Both the question and the answer are perfectly formed: "Does she speak Japanese?" uses the correct auxiliary for third-person singular; "she speaks Japanese very well" correctly conjugates "speak" → "speaks". Word order and punctuation are right.

**Q46 — Partial credit (1/2 pts)**
- Model answer: `What do you usually do in the evening after work?`
- Your answer: `What do you do on the evening after go home?`
- ✅ What you got right: The Wh-question frame "What do you do …?" is correctly formed — auxiliary "do" for "you", main verb in base form.
- ❌ What to fix: Two errors: (1) "on the evening" → "in the evening" — the preposition for parts of the day is "in", not "on" (save "on" for specific days: on Monday); (2) "after go home" → "after going home" or "after work" — after a preposition, a verb must be in the -ing form (gerund): "after go" → "after going". Alternatively use "after work" (noun phrase).
- 📘 Rule: Preposition for time-of-day parts → **in** the morning/afternoon/evening. Gerund after prepositions: prep + V-ing (after going, before leaving, without saying).
- ⚠️ Tip: "After go" is a very common error for Vietnamese learners. Replace the mental habit "sau khi + verb" with "after + V-ing": sau khi đi → after going, sau khi ăn → after eating.

**Q47 — Correct** ✅ (2/2 pts)
"Water freezes at 0°C and boils at 100°C." — both verbs correctly formed for the subject "water" (= it, third-person singular), scientific facts in the Simple Present. Precise and idiomatic.

**Q48 — Partial credit (1/2 pts)**
- Model answer: `My parents often take a walk together in the morning. They rarely miss a day.`
- Your answer: `My parent usually go together on the morning. They rarely forget.`
- ✅ What you got right: Sentence structure and the frequency adverbs ("usually", "rarely") are used correctly. "Go together" conveys the general idea of walking together.
- ❌ What to fix: Three errors: (1) "parent" → "parents" — "bố mẹ" = two people, so the English noun must be plural; (2) "on the morning" → "in the morning" — wrong preposition (same error as Q46); (3) "They rarely forget" → "They rarely miss a day" — "forget" is vague (forget what?); the Vietnamese "bỏ qua ngày nào" = skip/miss a day, not "forget".
- 📘 Rule: Plural nouns for plural people. Preposition **in** the morning. Vocabulary: bỏ qua = miss/skip; quên = forget.
- ⚠️ Tip: "My parent" is one of the most common Vietnamese-English errors because "bố mẹ" already implies two people but looks singular. Always use "parents" (plural) when referring to both father and mother together.

## KHUYẾN NGHỊ

- **Frequency adverb word order:** Review `lecture.md – PHẦN 3: VỊ TRÍ CỦA TRẠNG TỪ TẦN SUẤT` and `lecture.md – LỖI THƯỜNG GẶP §Lỗi 5`. Write 5 sentences about your own daily routine using "is always / is never / am usually" to practise placing the adverb correctly after be-verbs.

- **Prepositions of time (in/on/at):** This is a persistent gap across three translation questions. Memorise the pattern: **in** the morning/afternoon/evening; **at** night/noon/midnight; **on** + specific day names (on Monday, on weekends). Make a flashcard set with these three categories.

- **Sentence transformations — Wh- questions:** When transforming to a Wh-question, always delete the phrase that the Wh-word replaces. Practise by underlining the target information before writing the question.

- **Question tags:** This topic is not covered in depth in `lecture.md` for this lesson. Look up "English question tags" for a focused review. Key rule for Simple Present: affirmative clause → "doesn't / don't + pronoun?" tag.

- **Free-writing subject-verb agreement:** You are excellent at fill-in-the-blank and multiple choice (29/30 combined), but the -s rule occasionally slips in free translation. After writing any sentence in Section E, scan back and ask: "Is the subject he/she/it or a singular noun? → verify -s is on the verb." Do this for every verb in the sentence, including verbs in compound predicates.

- **Gerund after prepositions:** "after / before / without / by + V-ing" is a rule to memorise. Practice: convert 5 "sau khi + verb" phrases into "after + V-ing" English equivalents.
