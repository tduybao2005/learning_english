# IELTS ACADEMIC WRITING — TEST 10

**Time allowed:** 60 minutes
**Total:** Task 1 + Task 2
**Note:** Spend approximately 20 minutes on Task 1 and 40 minutes on Task 2.

---

## Task 1 (20 minutes — minimum 150 words)

**You should spend about 20 minutes on this task.**

**The table below shows the percentage of households using renewable energy in five countries in 2015 and 2022.**

**Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.**

---

### Data: Household Renewable Energy Use (%)

| Country | 2015 | 2022 | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Germany | 22% | 41% | +19 pp |
| Denmark | 39% | 62% | +23 pp |
| Vietnam | 8% | 19% | +11 pp |
| South Africa | 5% | 11% | +6 pp |
| Brazil | 41% | 53% | +12 pp |

*(pp = percentage points)*

---

### Model Answer — Band 7.5 (~175 words)

The table shows the proportion of households using renewable energy in five countries in 2015 and 2022, revealing substantial growth across all countries over this seven-year period.

Overall, every country recorded an increase in renewable household energy use, with Denmark and Brazil maintaining the highest levels throughout. Germany showed the most dramatic percentage point increase, nearly doubling its share from 22% to 41%, while Denmark — which already had the highest figure in 2015 at 39% — grew further to lead all five nations with 62% by 2022.

Brazil maintained a consistently high share, rising from 41% to 53%, reflecting its established tradition of hydroelectric and bioenergy use. Vietnam and South Africa started from much lower bases — 8% and 5% respectively in 2015 — but both more than doubled their figures, reaching 19% and 11% by 2022, suggesting growing momentum in renewable adoption despite remaining far below the European and Brazilian figures.

In summary, while absolute levels varied greatly between nations, the trend towards increased household renewable energy use was universal across all five countries during this period.

---

### Examiner Notes

- **Task Achievement (Band 7-8):** Clear overview (all countries increased); key features selected and compared (Germany's doubling, Denmark's leadership, Vietnam/South Africa starting from low base); accurate data cited; appropriate grouping
- **Coherence and Cohesion (Band 7):** Logical paragraph progression — overall trend, then high performers, then low base countries; effective discourse markers ("while", "reflecting", "despite", "In summary")
- **Lexical Resource (Band 7):** "percentage point increase", "nearly doubling", "established tradition", "growing momentum", "far below"
- **Grammatical Range and Accuracy (Band 7):** Varied structures; correct comparative forms; accurate past-tense narration

---

### Writing Tips — Task 1 Tables

1. **Group and compare.** Do not describe each country one by one in a list — identify patterns and group countries with similar characteristics.
2. **Include the clearest numbers.** You do not need to mention every data point; select the most significant.
3. **Show change over time.** For tables comparing two years, the change between years is often more interesting than the raw figures.
4. **Write an overview first** (not last). The overview should identify 2-3 dominant trends without specific numbers.
5. **Use varied expressions for increase:** rose, grew, increased, climbed, doubled, jumped, expanded.
6. **Do not speculate about reasons** unless the question asks you to. ("This may be because..." = incorrect for Task 1)

## Task 2 (40 minutes — minimum 250 words)

**You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.**

**Write about the following topic:**

> *"Wealthy nations have a moral obligation to provide financial and technological assistance to developing countries to help them transition to renewable energy. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"*

**Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.**

**Write at least 250 words.**

---

### Model Answer — Band 7.5 (~280 words)

The responsibility that wealthy nations bear for the current global climate crisis is rooted in historical fact: since the Industrial Revolution, high-income countries have collectively been responsible for the vast majority of cumulative greenhouse gas emissions now accumulated in the atmosphere. Given this context, I strongly agree that wealthy nations have a genuine moral obligation to support developing countries in accessing clean energy technologies and financing their renewable transition.

The most compelling argument rests on historical accountability. The economic prosperity that wealthy nations enjoy today was built in significant part on carbon-intensive industrialisation — the very emissions now driving climate disruption. Asking developing nations to forgo the same development pathway, without adequate compensation or viable alternatives, would be to ask them to sacrifice their economic aspirations to address a problem they did not primarily create. This is difficult to justify on any coherent ethical framework. The UN Framework Convention on Climate Change reflects this reasoning in its principle of "common but differentiated responsibilities" — acknowledging that while climate change requires a global response, the obligations of different nations should reflect their historical contributions and economic capacities.

Furthermore, the practical reality is that developing nations often lack the capital required to independently finance a rapid renewable transition, regardless of their political will to do so. Mechanisms such as the Green Climate Fund, established under the UNFCCC, and the Paris Agreement's commitment of $100 billion annually in climate finance represent formal acknowledgements of this obligation, even if their implementation has fallen consistently short of stated targets.

Critics of this position raise legitimate concerns about accountability and efficiency in aid and technology transfer. There are genuine risks that funds may not reach their intended beneficiaries, and that technology transfers without adequate training and infrastructure support may be ineffective. These concerns, however, point towards the need for better-designed and more transparent mechanisms, not towards abandoning the underlying obligation altogether.

In conclusion, the combination of historical responsibility for emissions and the practical financing constraints facing developing nations creates a clear and genuine moral obligation for wealthy countries to share both financial resources and clean energy technologies. Imperfect implementation of this obligation is an argument for reform, not for inaction.

---

### Examiner Notes

- **Task Achievement (Band 8):** Clear strong-agree position; three coherent arguments (historical accountability, UNFCCC principle, financing gap); counterargument addressed and rebutted; conclusion consistent with stated position
- **Coherence and Cohesion (Band 7):** PEEL structure in each body paragraph; effective signposting ("The most compelling argument", "Furthermore", "Critics of this position", "In conclusion")
- **Lexical Resource (Band 7-8):** "historical accountability", "carbon-intensive industrialisation", "cumulative greenhouse gas emissions", "common but differentiated responsibilities", "viable alternatives", "financing constraints"
- **Grammatical Range and Accuracy (Band 7-8):** Complex noun phrases; accurate conditional and passive structures; varied clause types; no significant errors

### Common Errors for Vietnamese Learners — Test 10

| Error type | Incorrect | Correct |
|---|---|---|
| Preposition with "invest" | "invest on renewable energy" | "invest **in** renewable energy" |
| "Technology" uncountable | "technologies are important" ✓ | "technology is important" ✓ (both OK depending on meaning) |
| Overgeneralisation | "All developing countries are poor" | "Many / some developing nations face..." |
| Missing article | "help them achieve goal" | "help them achieve **a/their** goal" |
| Awkward passive | "should be obligated" | "should be **obliged**" / "have an obligation to" |
| Run-on sentences | Long Vietnamese-style compound sentences | Break into two sentences with clear logical connector |
