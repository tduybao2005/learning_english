# IELTS SPEAKING — TEST 10

**Format:** Three parts | Total time: 11-14 minutes
**Topics this test:** Energy & Environment / Money & Finances / Living Arrangements / Wealth Inequality / Economic Policy / Climate Finance

---

## Part 1 (4-5 minutes)

*The examiner will ask questions on familiar everyday topics. Give extended answers — 2-4 sentences. Avoid one-word replies.*

---

### Topic 1: Energy & Environment

**Question 1:** Do you try to reduce your energy consumption at home? How?

**Model Answer:**
"I do make a conscious effort, yes. I'm fairly disciplined about turning off lights in empty rooms and unplugging devices rather than leaving them on standby, which apparently makes a more meaningful difference than people realise. I've also started thinking more carefully about my diet — reducing meat consumption is something I've been doing gradually, partly for health reasons and partly because of the environmental footprint. I'm not perfect at it, but I feel it matters to try."

**Vocabulary notes:** *conscious effort, disciplined, environmental footprint, standby*

---

**Question 2:** Has electricity ever been cut off in your home? How did you manage?

**Model Answer:**
"Yes, a couple of times during storms that damaged power lines in the area. It's surprisingly disruptive — far more than you'd expect until it actually happens. The first few hours were almost enjoyable in a novel way, candlelight and no screens, but by the second day the practical difficulties — food in the refrigerator, no hot water, not being able to work — became genuinely inconvenient. It gave me a much stronger appreciation for how dependent modern life is on reliable electricity."

**Vocabulary notes:** *disruptive, novel, practical difficulties, dependent on, reliable electricity*

---

**Question 3:** Do you think people in your country are aware enough of environmental issues?

**Model Answer:**
"There's growing awareness, certainly more than a decade ago, but I think the gap between awareness and action remains significant. Most people are aware that climate change is happening and that it's serious, but behavioural changes — particularly around consumption, travel, and diet — lag behind that awareness considerably. Part of the challenge is that individual actions feel small relative to the scale of the problem, which can produce a kind of paralysis or fatalism rather than motivation. I think stronger policy frameworks matter more than awareness campaigns at this stage."

**Vocabulary notes:** *growing awareness, lag behind, fatalism, paralysis, policy frameworks*

---

**Question 4:** Would you consider buying an electric vehicle in the future?

**Model Answer:**
"Very likely, yes — the economics are becoming increasingly compelling as battery costs fall and charging infrastructure expands. My main reservation currently is the charging situation: I live in an apartment building, so home charging isn't straightforward, and public charging is still inconsistent in coverage and reliability where I live. But I think within five to ten years the convenience gap between electric and conventional vehicles will have largely closed, and I'd genuinely prefer to make the switch on both environmental and running-cost grounds."

**Vocabulary notes:** *economics are compelling, charging infrastructure, reservation, convenience gap, running costs*

---

### Topic 2: Money & Finances

**Question 5:** Do you think you are good at managing money?

**Model Answer:**
"Reasonably so, though I've had to learn the hard way in some respects. I'm good at tracking my spending and distinguishing between things I genuinely need and things I simply want in the moment — that discipline took time to develop. Where I'm less disciplined is in planning for longer-term goals like saving for large purchases. I'm working on building more consistent saving habits, but I'd say money management is an area of active improvement for me rather than a strength I've fully mastered."

**Vocabulary notes:** *managing money, tracking spending, discipline, consistent saving habits, active improvement*

---

**Question 6:** Did your parents teach you about money when you were young?

**Model Answer:**
"In practical ways, yes — there were clear expectations about spending carefully and not wasting money, which I think is fairly typical in Vietnamese families. What wasn't explicitly discussed was investing or building wealth over the long term; the focus was much more on spending less than you earn and saving for security. I've had to develop more sophisticated financial thinking largely through my own reading and experience as an adult. I think financial literacy is something schools should take more seriously than they typically do."

**Vocabulary notes:** *financial literacy, building wealth, investing, sophisticated financial thinking, security*

---

**Question 7:** Do you prefer to pay with cash or card? Why?

**Model Answer:**
"Card and mobile payment almost exclusively at this point — cash feels increasingly anachronistic in everyday transactions, at least in the urban environment I live in. There's a real convenience advantage: no change to deal with, instant record-keeping, and contactless payments are simply faster. That said, I'm aware that going entirely cashless has equity implications — elderly people and those without bank accounts can find themselves excluded from the payment ecosystem as cash becomes rarer. So I think cash should remain available as an option, even if I personally don't use it often."

**Vocabulary notes:** *anachronistic, contactless payments, record-keeping, equity implications, payment ecosystem*

---

**Question 8:** Have you ever made a purchase you later regretted? What was it?

**Model Answer:**
"Yes — a few years ago I bought a fairly expensive piece of fitness equipment on a fairly impulsive basis after a period of genuine motivation about exercising more regularly. The motivation didn't outlast the enthusiasm that led to the purchase, and the equipment ended up serving mainly as an expensive clothes rack for about two years. I eventually sold it at a significant loss. It was a useful lesson in the gap between what I imagine I'll do and what I actually do — I try to impose a 48-hour waiting period on significant purchases now before committing."

**Vocabulary notes:** *impulsive, motivation, outlast, 48-hour waiting period, significant purchase*

---

### Topic 3: Living Arrangements

**Question 9:** Do you currently live alone or with other people?

**Model Answer:**
"I live with a flatmate, which works out well in practical terms — sharing costs makes living in a central location much more financially feasible than it would be alone, and I'm fortunate in that we have compatible habits and respect each other's space and schedules. I do sometimes think about having my own place — mostly for the freedom to use shared spaces without consideration for someone else — but the financial reality makes that a fairly distant aspiration at the moment. Shared living has grown on me more than I expected."

**Vocabulary notes:** *financially feasible, compatible habits, shared spaces, distant aspiration, grown on me*

---

**Question 10:** How important is location when choosing a place to live?

**Model Answer:**
"Extremely important — probably the single factor I weight most heavily, above size or even price to a significant degree. Being within convenient distance of work, public transport, green space, and the places I regularly visit saves an enormous amount of time and reduces the low-level stress of daily logistics. I've lived in a larger, cheaper place further from everything I needed, and the time cost of commuting quickly eroded whatever financial saving it provided. For me, a smaller but well-located home is almost always preferable to a larger but inconveniently situated one."

**Vocabulary notes:** *weight heavily, green space, logistics, commuting, well-located*

---

**Question 11:** Have you moved home many times in your life?

**Model Answer:**
"Yes, more than most people my age, I think — five or six times counting moves for study and work. Each move has been significant in reshaping my sense of what I consider home and what I actually need in a living space. The early moves were driven by necessity and felt disruptive; more recent ones have felt more like deliberate choices about what kind of environment I want to inhabit. I've come to feel that home is less about a specific place and more about the habits and routines you establish wherever you are."

**Vocabulary notes:** *reshaping, deliberate choices, inhabit, habits and routines, sense of home*

---

**Question 12:** What would your ideal home look like?

**Model Answer:**
"It would be in a walkable neighbourhood, close to parks and amenities, in a building with some kind of outdoor space — a balcony at minimum. Internally, I'd prioritise natural light above almost anything else; I find dark or artificially lit spaces genuinely draining. A dedicated space to work quietly, separate from sleeping and relaxing areas, has become increasingly important to me since working from home became more common. I'm not particularly drawn to large spaces — I'd take a well-designed, well-lit smaller apartment over a large but poorly arranged one without hesitation."

**Vocabulary notes:** *walkable neighbourhood, amenities, natural light, dedicated workspace, well-designed*

## Part 2 — Cue Card (3-4 minutes)

*Read the card carefully. You have 1 minute to prepare notes. Then speak for 1-2 minutes.*

---

**Describe a time when you or someone you know made a significant financial decision.**

You should say:
- what the decision was
- what the circumstances were at the time
- how the decision was made
- and explain what the outcome was and what you or they learned from it

---

### Preparation notes (1 minute)

```
WHAT:    Decision to invest savings in further professional qualification
WHO:     Myself, about two years ago
CIRCUM:  Had saved some money, uncertain career prospects, considered spending vs investing
HOW:     Research, spoke to people who had taken the course, compared cost vs earning potential
OUTCOME: Completed qualification, pay increase justified the cost, also opened new opportunities
LEARNED: Investment in skills differs from consumption — long-term thinking required
```

---

### Model Response (~200 words, Band 7-8)

"I'd like to talk about a financial decision I made about two years ago — whether to invest a significant portion of my savings in a professional qualification course that was quite expensive, but which I believed would meaningfully improve my career prospects.

At the time, I was feeling somewhat stuck professionally. I had enough savings to cover the course fees but doing so would have depleted nearly everything I had set aside, which felt genuinely alarming. I was also aware that this money represented considerable security, and spending it on something whose returns were uncertain felt difficult to justify to myself.

The decision process took several weeks. I researched the outcomes for people who had completed the same qualification, spoke to three or four people I knew who had done it, and drew up a fairly rough cost-benefit calculation comparing the upfront cost against the likely salary improvement. The calculation was encouraging, though not certain.

I went ahead with it. The qualification took seven months to complete, and within a year of finishing I had received a salary increase that, over three years, will more than cover what I spent. Beyond the financial return, it also opened doors to projects and opportunities that wouldn't otherwise have been available to me.

What I learned is that there's a meaningful distinction between spending money on consumption and investing it in something that compounds over time — and that the fear of depleting savings can sometimes lead to paralysis when a considered risk would be the wiser choice."

---

### Follow-up Question

**"Do you think people today are better or worse at financial planning than previous generations?"**

**Model Answer:**
"Mixed, I think. People today have access to far more information about financial planning — investment apps, financial education content, online resources — than previous generations did. However, there are also more temptations and frictions: one-click purchasing, subscription services that accumulate unnoticed, and social media-driven consumption pressure. Previous generations often saved by default because spending was more effortful. For my generation, discipline has to be more deliberate because the system's defaults push towards spending. Whether people are better or worse overall probably varies enormously by education, income level, and individual habits."

## Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 minutes)

*More abstract questions linked to the themes of money, economic inequality, energy, and governance. Extend your answers with reasoning and examples.*

---

**Question 1:** Do you think the gap between rich and poor people is growing or narrowing, and does it matter?

**Model Answer (~85 words):**
"Most evidence suggests the gap is widening within many wealthy nations, even as extreme poverty has declined globally. It matters enormously — not just for moral reasons, but because extreme inequality undermines social mobility, corrodes democratic institutions, and is associated with worse outcomes across a wide range of social indicators from health to educational attainment. Societies with extreme inequality tend to be less socially trusting, more politically polarised, and less economically efficient than more equal ones. The evidence for this is, I think, quite robust."

---

**Question 2:** Should very wealthy individuals be taxed more heavily? Why or why not?

**Model Answer (~80 words):**
"I think there's a reasonable case for it, particularly on wealth accumulated through inheritance or asset appreciation rather than earned income from work. The diminishing marginal utility of money — the idea that an additional dollar matters much less to a billionaire than to someone struggling to cover necessities — provides a philosophical basis for progressive taxation. The practical question is whether higher taxes on wealth reduce investment and economic activity sufficiently to offset the revenue and equity benefits. The evidence on this is more contested than the theory."

---

**Question 3:** Is economic growth always desirable, or are there circumstances where it should be limited?

**Model Answer (~80 words):**
"Increasingly I think the relationship is more nuanced than traditional economics suggests. GDP growth as conventionally measured doesn't distinguish between activities that genuinely improve wellbeing and those that are merely economically large but destructive — cleaning up pollution counts as growth, for instance. There's a growing body of thought around 'degrowth' in wealthy nations — deliberately reducing throughput of materials and energy while maintaining or improving wellbeing — that I find intellectually serious, even if politically challenging. Whether it's practically achievable at the national level remains genuinely unclear."

---

**Question 4:** What role should governments play in directing economies towards renewable energy?

**Model Answer (~85 words):**
"A significant one, I think — the energy transition is unlikely to happen fast enough through market forces alone, given the scale of incumbent fossil fuel interests, the underpriced external costs of carbon, and the public goods nature of grid infrastructure. Carbon pricing, renewable energy standards, investment in grid infrastructure, and support for research and development all represent legitimate policy tools. The debate shouldn't be about whether governments should be involved, but about which policy instruments are most efficient and equitable in a given national context."

---

**Question 5:** Should developing nations be allowed to use fossil fuels to develop their economies, even if this increases global emissions?

**Model Answer (~85 words):**
"This is one of the most genuinely difficult questions in climate politics. Historically, wealthy nations industrialised using fossil fuels and then acquired the economic resources and political leverage to advocate for restrictions on others doing the same. However, the physical reality is that the atmosphere doesn't distinguish between historical and current emissions — additional fossil fuel use worsens climate outcomes regardless of the emitter's development status. The morally coherent response is for wealthy nations to finance renewable alternatives aggressively enough that developing nations genuinely don't need fossil fuels to develop."

---

**Question 6:** How could international climate finance mechanisms be made more effective?

**Model Answer (~85 words):**
"Several improvements seem clear. First, the pledges should actually be fulfilled — wealthy nations have repeatedly failed to deliver on $100 billion annual commitments, which erodes trust badly. Second, a greater proportion should be grants rather than loans, since loading climate finance onto already-indebted developing nations as further debt is counterproductive. Third, recipient nations should have far more agency in how funds are directed — top-down conditionality has a poor track record in development finance generally. Transparency, accountability mechanisms, and genuine partnership rather than donor-recipient dynamics would all help substantially."

---

**Question 7:** Do you think future generations will live in a more or less economically equal world?

**Model Answer (~80 words):**
"I genuinely don't know, which is an honest answer even if it's unsatisfying. There are forces pushing in both directions. Automation and artificial intelligence may concentrate wealth further if the gains accrue primarily to capital owners. On the other hand, renewable energy could democratise energy access and reduce the resource rents that concentrate power in fossil fuel-rich nations. Policy choices will be decisive — the outcome is not technologically determined. Whether political systems prove capable of making the necessary redistributive choices is, I think, the central uncertainty."

---

**Question 8:** Is it more important to solve poverty or to address climate change? Can you do both simultaneously?

**Model Answer (~85 words):**
"I'd challenge the framing of a choice between them — the populations most vulnerable to climate change are overwhelmingly the world's poorest, so addressing climate change is substantially a poverty issue. The more interesting question is whether the transition to renewable energy can be designed in ways that simultaneously expand energy access, create economic opportunity in developing regions, and reduce emissions. I think it can — off-grid solar in sub-Saharan Africa being the clearest existing example. The design of the transition matters at least as much as its pace."

---

**Question 9:** How has the digital economy changed people's relationship with money?

**Model Answer (~80 words):**
"Quite profoundly in several ways. Contactless payments and digital wallets have made spending psychologically easier — the friction of physical cash has been removed, which most research suggests leads to greater spending. Financial services are becoming more accessible to previously underserved populations through mobile banking. However, digital platforms also enable new forms of financial exploitation: predatory lending apps, gambling-adjacent products disguised as investment tools, and the almost frictionless accumulation of subscription costs. The democratisation and the exploitation are both real; the balance depends largely on regulation."

---

**Question 10:** What economic system would best serve humanity in the twenty-first century?

**Model Answer (~85 words):**
"That's a large question I'd approach with considerable humility. I don't think any pure economic ideology — unfettered free markets, central planning, or anything else — has a convincing track record across the full range of human needs. What the evidence seems to support is a mixed economy with robust public institutions: markets where they efficiently allocate resources, public provision where markets systematically fail — healthcare, education, infrastructure, carbon pricing — and redistributive mechanisms to prevent the concentration of economic power from translating into self-reinforcing political power. The details matter enormously."

## Speaking Assessment — Part 3 Target Language

### Hedging expressions to signal uncertainty or balance:
- "I think on balance the evidence suggests..."
- "That's a genuinely difficult question — I'd approach it with some caution..."
- "I'm not entirely certain, but my impression is..."
- "There are strong arguments on both sides, though I lean towards..."
- "The honest answer is probably that it depends significantly on..."

### Concession structures:
- "That said, it's worth acknowledging that..."
- "However, this needs to be balanced against..."
- "I'd challenge the framing of a choice between..."
- "The evidence is more contested than the theory on this..."

### Extending and elaborating:
- "What I find interesting about this is..."
- "The more interesting question, I think, is..."
- "This connects to a broader issue about..."
- "Whether this is practically achievable at scale remains genuinely unclear..."
