## TASK 1 (Recommended time: 20 minutes | Minimum 150 words)

**"The table below shows the percentage of plastic waste that is recycled, incinerated, or sent to landfill in five countries in 2020."**

**Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.**

---

### DATA (Table)

| Country     | Recycled | Incinerated | Landfill |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| Germany     | 38%      | 44%         | 18%      |
| South Korea | 49%      | 38%         | 13%      |
| UK          | 19%      | 42%         | 39%      |
| USA         | 9%       | 12%         | 79%      |
| Vietnam     | 5%       | 4%          | 91%      |

*(Figures represent percentages of total plastic waste, 2020)*

---

### TASK 1 — BAND 7.5 MODEL ANSWER (~185 words)

The table compares how five countries disposed of their plastic waste in 2020, examining three methods: recycling, incineration, and landfill.

Overall, there are considerable differences in plastic waste management between the countries, with Vietnam and the USA relying heavily on landfill while Germany and South Korea employed more diversified approaches. The most striking contrast is between these two groups.

Vietnam sent the highest proportion of plastic waste to landfill at 91%, with both recycling (5%) and incineration (4%) remaining negligible. The USA showed a similar dependence on landfill at 79%, with recycling at just 9% and incineration at only 12%. The UK occupied a middle position, sending 39% to landfill and incinerating 42%, though its recycling rate of 19% was comparatively low.

Germany and South Korea stood out as the most sophisticated waste managers. South Korea recycled the highest proportion of any country at 49%, while Germany combined strong recycling (38%) with the highest incineration rate across all five nations at 44% — a method that can recover energy from waste. Both countries sent less than 20% to landfill, in stark contrast to the remaining three.

## TASK 2 (Recommended time: 40 minutes | Minimum 250 words)

**"Individuals should take personal responsibility for reducing plastic pollution rather than expecting governments to impose regulations. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"**

---

### TASK 2 — PLANNING GUIDE (Before writing)

**Position:** Disagree — systemic problems require systemic, government-led solutions.

**Paragraph plan:**
- Introduction: Define the debate, state position (disagree — both matter, but government regulation is primary)
- Body 1: Individual choices are constrained by available options — infrastructure must exist first
- Body 2: Corporations produce the plastic; regulation targeting producers is more effective than consumer behaviour change
- Concession: Individual behaviour does matter and should complement regulation
- Conclusion: Restate position — government action is the necessary foundation

**Key examples to use:** EU Single-Use Plastics Directive (2021), Extended Producer Responsibility, deposit-return schemes, recycling infrastructure gaps in USA and Vietnam (from Task 1 data)

---

### TASK 2 — BAND 7.5 MODEL ANSWER (~285 words)

Plastic pollution has reached a scale that demands urgent and comprehensive action. While some argue that individuals must take the lead in reducing plastic waste through changes to their personal behaviour, I largely disagree with this view. Addressing what is fundamentally a systemic problem requires systemic solutions, with government regulation playing the primary role.

The most significant limitation of relying on individual responsibility is that individuals can only make choices from options that exist. A consumer who wishes to avoid single-use plastic packaging cannot do so if all available products are packaged in plastic; a household that wants to recycle cannot do so effectively if their city lacks adequate collection and processing infrastructure. As the data from countries like Vietnam and the USA illustrates, even when individuals may wish to recycle, rates remain very low where supporting infrastructure is absent. Governments must create the conditions — through investment in infrastructure, standards for product design, and accessible collection systems — within which individual responsible choices become possible.

Furthermore, the scale of plastic production means that upstream intervention targeting manufacturers is more efficient than attempting to change the behaviour of billions of consumers individually. The European Union's 2021 Single-Use Plastics Directive, which banned a range of plastic products including straws and disposable cutlery, eliminated billions of plastic items without requiring any individual decision-making at all. Extended Producer Responsibility schemes, which require companies to finance the collection and recycling of their products, create powerful economic incentives for manufacturers to reduce plastic use from the outset.

This is not to say that individual behaviour is irrelevant. Consumer demand for sustainable products sends important market signals, and cultural shifts away from disposability complement regulatory frameworks. However, individual action alone, without the structural reforms that only governments can implement, is wholly insufficient to address the scale of the plastic pollution crisis.

In conclusion, while individual responsibility has a supporting role to play, government regulation addressing production, design, and waste infrastructure is the necessary foundation for any meaningful reduction in plastic pollution.

---

### TASK 2 — EXAMINER NOTES (Band 7.5 Justification)

- **Task Response:** Position clearly stated and maintained throughout. Specific, named examples used (EU Directive, Extended Producer Responsibility). Concession integrated naturally. Conclusion restates without new arguments.
- **Cohesion & Coherence:** PEEL structure in each body paragraph. Clear discourse markers ("Furthermore", "This is not to say", "However"). Logical progression across paragraphs.
- **Lexical Resource:** "systemic problem", "upstream intervention", "Extended Producer Responsibility", "disposability", "structural reforms" — wide, precise vocabulary. No lexical errors.
- **Grammatical Range:** Complex sentence structures with subordinate clauses. Accurate conditional and modal use. Passive voice where appropriate ("were banned", "must be created").

### TASK 2 — COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| "Government must to regulate..." | "Government must regulate..." (modal + infinitive, no "to") |
| "In my personal opinion, I think that..." | "I believe..." — avoid redundant phrases |
| Writing body paragraphs without examples | Always include at least one specific named example per main paragraph |
| "Individual should be responsible" | "Individuals should be responsible" (plural noun) |
| Repeating the same vocabulary | Use synonyms: "plastic pollution / plastic waste / plastic debris" |
| One-sided essay with no concession | Always acknowledge the opposite view before dismissing or qualifying it |
