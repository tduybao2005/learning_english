### Topic 1: Cities and Where You Live

**Q1. Do you currently live in a city or a smaller town or village?**

Model answer: "I live in a fairly large city — it has a population of several million and all the features you would associate with urban life: a busy metro system, lots of restaurants and shopping centres, heavy traffic, and a real mix of different types of people. I grew up in a smaller town further from the centre, so I have experienced both. City life suits me well now, but I sometimes miss the quieter pace of where I grew up."

**Q2. What do you like most about where you live?**

Model answer: "What I appreciate most is the convenience — almost everything I need is accessible within a short distance, and public transport connections mean I can reach most parts of the city relatively quickly. I also enjoy the variety of the city: different neighbourhoods have different characters, and there is always something new to discover or try. The cultural facilities — museums, cinemas, live music — are also things I wouldn't want to give up."

**Q3. What would you change about your neighbourhood if you could?**

Model answer: "The thing I would most like to change is the amount of traffic on the main road near my building. The noise and air pollution are genuinely unpleasant, and I think it makes the area less safe for pedestrians and cyclists. I would love to see it redesigned as a slower, quieter street with more trees and wider pavements. There is a growing body of evidence that reducing car traffic in urban streets improves both public health and local economic activity, so it seems like an obvious improvement."

**Q4. Do you think your city has changed significantly in the past decade?**

Model answer: "Yes, quite noticeably. There has been a great deal of new construction — new apartment buildings, commercial developments, and infrastructure. The city has also become considerably more expensive, particularly for housing, which has pushed many people further from the centre. On the positive side, public transport has improved substantially, and there is more awareness of sustainability issues in urban planning than there used to be. It feels like a city in the middle of a significant transition."

### Topic 2: Shopping Habits

**Q5. How often do you go shopping, and what do you typically buy?**

Model answer: "My shopping habits are fairly divided. For groceries and everyday items, I go to local markets or supermarkets two or three times a week. For clothing or larger purchases, I tend to think carefully before buying anything and go out deliberately for specific items rather than browsing. I have become more conscious in recent years about avoiding impulse purchases and trying to buy things that are well-made and will last rather than cheap items that need replacing quickly."

**Q6. Do you prefer shopping online or in physical shops?**

Model answer: "It depends entirely on what I'm buying. For books, electronics, and household items where I already know what I want, online shopping is faster and often cheaper. But for clothing, I strongly prefer to try things on in person before buying — I find the experience of returning online orders frustrating and wasteful. I also think there is something valuable about supporting local shops and markets in my neighbourhood, so I try to do a proportion of my shopping there when the option exists."

**Q7. Do you think people buy too many things they don't actually need?**

Model answer: "I think in affluent societies, there is definitely a tendency towards overconsumption driven by constant advertising, social media comparison, and the way that shopping is promoted as a leisure activity. Many people accumulate possessions faster than they discard them and have homes full of things that are barely used. I include myself in this to some extent. Whether this constitutes 'too many' is a value judgement, but from an environmental perspective, the volume of goods produced, packaged, transported, and eventually discarded is clearly unsustainable."

**Q8. Have your shopping habits changed in recent years? Why?**

Model answer: "They have changed somewhat. I have become much more aware of plastic packaging as an issue, partly because of things I have read about ocean pollution. I now try to choose products with less packaging when there is a realistic alternative, bring my own bags and containers when possible, and buy from markets where produce is sold loose rather than pre-wrapped. I don't think individual consumer choices alone can solve plastic pollution — systemic change is needed — but I prefer to behave consistently with what I believe even when the impact of my personal choice is small."

### Topic 3: Environmental Awareness

**Q9. How environmentally aware do you think you are as a person?**

Model answer: "Moderately, I would say — more than average but certainly not as much as I could be. I pay attention to issues like plastic waste, energy use, and food choices, and I try to make reasonably informed decisions. But I also travel by air occasionally and consume more than people in less developed countries, so my overall environmental footprint is not as low as it could be. I try not to be self-righteous about environmental issues, while still taking them seriously and working to improve my own habits."

**Q10. Did you learn about environmental issues at school?**

Model answer: "Some, though looking back it feels quite limited compared with the scale and urgency of the issues that are now apparent. I remember learning about deforestation and the depletion of the ozone layer, and there were some lessons on recycling. But topics like plastic pollution, the full extent of climate change, and biodiversity loss were not covered in any depth. I think the current generation of school students is much better informed about environmental challenges than I was at the same age, partly because these topics are now unavoidable."

**Q11. Do you think individuals or governments are more responsible for environmental problems?**

Model answer: "Both carry responsibility, but at different scales. Individuals make daily choices that collectively matter — what we buy, eat, how we travel. But individual choices are constrained by the options and infrastructure that governments and corporations provide. If a city has no effective public transport, people have little practical choice but to drive. If all products are packaged in plastic, avoiding plastic packaging is nearly impossible for a typical consumer. So while individual responsibility is real and meaningful, systemic change through government policy and corporate regulation is ultimately more powerful and more efficient."

**Q12. Are people in your country becoming more environmentally conscious?**

Model answer: "There is a noticeable trend in that direction, especially among younger people, who seem to take environmental issues much more seriously than older generations. Social media has clearly played a role in spreading environmental awareness and enabling collective action. However, I think there is still a significant gap between people's stated environmental concerns and their actual behaviour — a gap that researchers call the 'attitude-behaviour gap' or 'green gap'. Genuinely changing behaviour at scale requires both shifting attitudes and making sustainable choices easier and more affordable."

You should say:
- what the place is and where it is located
- what makes it important or special
- what threats or problems it currently faces
- and explain what you think should be done to better protect or preserve it

### PART 2 — BAND 7.5 MODEL RESPONSE (~210 words)

"The place I'd like to talk about is a stretch of coastline near my hometown — a bay with a long sandy beach backed by sand dunes that have formed over thousands of years. It's about two hours from the city by road, and while it's well known locally, it doesn't attract mass tourism the way that more famous beaches do.

What makes it special is that it's one of the few stretches of undeveloped coastline left in the region. The sand dunes support a distinctive ecosystem — with specialised grasses, wildflowers, and nesting birds that have adapted specifically to that habitat — and the beach itself is one of the cleanest in the area. When you're there in the morning, before any visitors arrive, it genuinely feels like a natural landscape that hasn't been shaped by human intervention.

The threats it faces are real and growing. Plastic debris washes ashore from the ocean, and while local volunteers organise regular cleanups, the volume of incoming waste is relentless. There is also pressure from property developers who would like to build holiday accommodation on the adjacent land.

I think the area should be given formal protected status as a nature reserve, which would restrict development and ensure long-term environmental management. It would also benefit from a permanent infrastructure for waste collection rather than relying on volunteer efforts alone."

---

### FOLLOW-UP QUESTION

**Examiner:** "Do you think it's possible to promote tourism in natural areas without causing damage to them?"

**Model answer:** "It is possible, but it requires very deliberate management. The concept of ecotourism — tourism that is designed to minimise environmental impact, support conservation, and benefit local communities — shows that it can be done. The key seems to be limiting visitor numbers through booking systems, investing in good infrastructure to concentrate visitors in less sensitive areas, and using tourism revenue to fund conservation directly. Without active management, even well-intentioned visitors can collectively cause significant damage simply through volume and thoughtlessness."

### Theme A: Urban Planning

**Q1. How should cities plan for population growth in the coming decades?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "Cities that are expecting significant population growth need to think very carefully about infrastructure capacity — transport, water, energy, schools, healthcare — before that growth arrives rather than trying to retrofit solutions afterwards. Mixed-use, high-density development around public transport hubs is generally more efficient and sustainable than sprawling low-density suburbs. Green space must be protected and expanded even as population increases, because the health and wellbeing benefits of green space become more valuable, not less, as urban density increases. Long-term planning with genuine community participation seems essential."

**Q2. What do you think are the biggest challenges facing cities today?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "The challenges vary enormously depending on where a city is in the world, but some seem nearly universal. Housing affordability is a crisis in cities from London to Nairobi — the gap between housing costs and ordinary incomes has widened dramatically in recent decades. Climate change poses existential threats to many coastal cities and threatens the liveability of cities in already hot regions. And inequality — the sharp division between wealthy and impoverished urban populations often living in close physical proximity — generates persistent social and political instability."

**Q3. Do you think the concept of the 'smart city' — using technology to manage urban infrastructure — will improve urban life?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "It has real potential, particularly for improving efficiency in transport, energy, and waste management. Sensor networks and data analytics can identify patterns that allow systems to be optimised in ways that were previously impossible. However, smart city technology also raises serious concerns about surveillance, data privacy, and who controls the infrastructure and the data it generates. The risk is that these technologies benefit primarily the already privileged while creating new forms of social control for others. The governance question seems to me as important as the technical one."

---

### Theme B: Plastic Pollution

**Q4. Why do you think plastic pollution has become such a serious problem in recent decades?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "The problem is fundamentally a product of extraordinary success combined with poor planning for consequences. Plastic was developed because it is cheap, durable, lightweight, and versatile — genuinely useful properties that drove explosive growth in production from the mid-twentieth century onwards. The problem is that durability is a liability as well as an asset when it comes to disposal: plastic persists in the environment for centuries. Waste management infrastructure, particularly in rapidly industrialising countries, simply did not expand fast enough to handle the volumes produced."

**Q5. Do you think single-use plastic bans are an effective solution to plastic pollution?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "Bans on specific single-use plastic items — straws, bags, cutlery — are a useful and symbolically important part of the solution, but they address only a fraction of the plastic that enters the environment. The items banned tend to be highly visible and emotionally resonant rather than necessarily the largest contributors by volume to ocean plastic. Meaningful progress requires much more comprehensive action targeting plastic production volumes, product design for recyclability, and investment in waste management infrastructure — particularly in the regions where most ocean plastic originates."

**Q6. What responsibilities do large corporations have in relation to plastic pollution?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "Very significant responsibilities, in my view. A substantial proportion of the plastic in the ocean can be traced to a relatively small number of companies whose products and packaging consistently appear in beach and ocean cleanup surveys. These companies have profited from the convenience of plastic packaging while externalising the environmental costs onto society. Extended Producer Responsibility schemes, which require manufacturers to finance the collection and processing of their products at end of life, are a logical policy response that creates proper economic incentives for reducing plastic use."

---

### Theme C: Consumer Responsibility and Government Regulation

**Q7. To what extent can changes in consumer behaviour solve environmental problems?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "Consumer behaviour change is a necessary component of any serious environmental strategy, but it cannot do the work alone. The scale of change required exceeds what can realistically be achieved through voluntary individual choices, particularly when sustainable alternatives are not consistently available, affordable, or convenient. Consumer choices also operate within systems designed by corporations and governments, so changing consumer behaviour without changing those systems produces limited results. I think of individual behaviour change and systemic policy change as complements, not alternatives — both are needed, but policy must lead."

**Q8. Do you think governments should have the right to restrict individuals' consumption choices for environmental reasons?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "This is a question about the limits of individual liberty versus collective responsibility. I think reasonable restrictions on environmentally harmful products and practices are justified when the environmental costs are clearly demonstrated and the restrictions are applied fairly. We already accept many restrictions on individual choice — we cannot buy unsafe food, dump waste wherever we choose, or drive without a seatbelt — on the grounds that individual freedom ends where serious harm to others begins. Plastic pollution clearly harms others; regulation seems a proportionate response."

**Q9. How important is international cooperation in addressing environmental problems like plastic pollution?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "Absolutely essential, because plastic pollution does not respect national borders. Plastic produced in one country ends up in the oceans of all countries. Without international frameworks that set minimum standards for production, design, and waste management, the risk is a race to the bottom — countries competing economically by exempting their industries from environmental costs that others bear. The UN Global Plastics Treaty negotiations represent exactly the kind of international cooperative framework that is needed, though the challenge of getting all major producing and consuming nations to agree on binding commitments is formidable."

**Q10. Do you feel optimistic or pessimistic about the future of the environment?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "I try to hold a position of realistic hope rather than either naive optimism or paralysing pessimism. The scale of the environmental challenges we face — climate change, plastic pollution, biodiversity loss — is genuinely alarming, and current trajectories in many areas are deeply concerning. At the same time, the speed at which public awareness has grown, the emergence of increasingly viable clean technologies, and the real policy changes that have been achieved in some areas give me grounds for believing that a better outcome is possible. What it requires is sustained political will and ambition."
