# IELTS Practice Test 30 — Speaking
**Chủ đề: Urban Planning & Architecture**

## Part 1: Interview Questions

**Hướng dẫn:** Trả lời tự nhiên, mỗi câu 2–3 câu. Examiner sẽ hỏi các câu hỏi về cuộc sống hàng ngày.

### Topic 1: Where You Live

**1. Do you live in a city, a town, or a village?**

I live in a fairly large city — Hanoi, the capital of Vietnam. It's a busy, fast-paced place with a lot going on, which I generally enjoy, though it can be overwhelming at times.

**2. What do you like most about the area where you live?**

What I appreciate most is the convenience — shops, restaurants, and public transport are all within easy reach. I also love the mix of old French colonial architecture and modern buildings, which gives the city a unique character.

**3. Has the area where you live changed a lot in recent years?**

Enormously. Even in the past five years, new high-rise developments have appeared, old streets have been widened, and shopping malls have replaced what were once quiet residential neighbourhoods. It's a bit bittersweet to see the transformation.

**4. Would you prefer to live in a different type of place?**

Sometimes I think I'd enjoy living somewhere smaller and quieter, perhaps a coastal town. However, I'd probably miss the energy and job opportunities that come with city life, so I'm not sure I'd make the move permanently.

---

### Topic 2: Transport & Commuting

**5. How do you usually get around your city?**

I mostly use a combination of motorbike and the metro, depending on where I'm going. The metro is newer and more comfortable, but the motorbike is still faster for short trips through the back streets.

**6. How long does your daily commute take?**

On a good day, it's about twenty-five minutes each way. But during rush hour, especially in the morning, it can easily stretch to an hour due to heavy traffic. It's genuinely one of the most frustrating parts of city living.

**7. Do you think public transport in your city is good enough?**

It's improving, but not yet sufficient for a city of Hanoi's size. The metro network only has a couple of lines so far, so most people still rely on personal vehicles. I hope the government follows through on its plans to expand the system significantly.

**8. Have you ever used a bicycle or a scooter to get around? Do you enjoy it?**

Yes, I ride a scooter regularly. It gives you a real sense of freedom in the city and lets you navigate through traffic in ways a car never could. It can feel precarious at busy intersections, but you get used to it quickly.

### Topic 3: Architecture & Design

**9. Do you pay attention to the design of buildings when you walk around the city?**

Definitely. I find myself constantly looking up at buildings — noticing details like decorative facades, interesting window shapes, or unexpected combinations of materials. Architecture tells you a lot about a city's history and ambitions.

**10. Are there any old buildings in your city that you think are particularly beautiful?**

Yes, the old French quarter in Hanoi has some stunning colonial-era villas and public buildings with yellow-painted facades, shuttered windows, and shaded verandas. They feel both elegant and completely out of place in the modern city, which is part of their charm.

**11. Do you think modern buildings are more or less attractive than older ones?**

That really depends on the specific building. Some contemporary architecture is bold and inspiring, while other modern developments seem purely functional and rather soulless. Older buildings often have handcrafted details and proportions that are difficult to replicate today.

**12. Would you like to work in a building with an impressive or unusual design?**

I think I would, yes. Studies suggest that well-designed workplaces improve mood and productivity, and I certainly notice the difference between working in a cramped, poorly lit office and one with natural light and interesting spaces.

## Part 2: Cue Card

**Đề bài:**

**Describe a building or place in your city that you find particularly impressive.**

You should say:
- what the building or place is
- where it is located
- what it looks like
- and explain why you find it particularly impressive.

**Thời gian chuẩn bị:** 1 phút | **Thời gian nói:** 1–2 phút

---

**Bài mẫu (~200 từ):**

The building I'd like to talk about is the Temple of Literature in Hanoi, which is one of the most historically significant and visually striking places in the entire city.

It's located in the Ba Dinh district, not far from the city centre, and it's easily accessible by both motorbike and the newer metro line. The complex dates back to 1070 and was originally built as a temple dedicated to Confucius before becoming Vietnam's first university.

In terms of its appearance, it's genuinely breathtaking. The architecture is traditional Vietnamese with strong Chinese influences — you have a series of walled courtyards, ornate pavilions with sweeping tiled roofs, and stone stelae mounted on the backs of carved stone tortoises. These stelae list the names of doctoral graduates from centuries past, which gives the whole place an extraordinary sense of accumulated history and learning.

What I find most impressive is the contrast between the serenity of the complex and the roaring city surrounding it. The moment you step through the main gate, the noise of traffic fades and you feel transported to a completely different era. It's remarkable that this ancient place has survived centuries of war and rapid urban development almost entirely intact.

I visit it at least a couple of times a year and always come away feeling both humbled and inspired.

## Part 3: Discussion Questions

**Hướng dẫn:** Đây là phần thảo luận các chủ đề trừu tượng liên quan đến quy hoạch đô thị, nhà ở và giao thông công cộng. Mỗi câu trả lời 3–4 câu.

---

**1. How important is urban planning for the development of cities?**

Urban planning is absolutely fundamental. Without coordinated planning, cities tend to develop in chaotic and inefficient ways — with traffic gridlock, inadequate infrastructure, and unequal access to services. Good planning looks decades ahead, allocating land for housing, transport, parks, and commercial activity in a way that serves the whole population rather than just the most powerful developers. Cities like Singapore demonstrate how effective planning can transform a small city-state into a highly liveable and economically competitive place.

**2. What are the main challenges that urban planners face today?**

I think the most pressing challenge is balancing the need for growth with sustainability and quality of life. Cities need to accommodate growing populations, but doing so through unplanned sprawl or high-rise overdevelopment destroys the character and environment that made them attractive in the first place. Climate adaptation is another enormous challenge — cities need to redesign drainage systems, increase green cover, and reduce heat islands in response to extreme weather events that are becoming more frequent and intense.

**3. Do you think governments should prioritise affordable housing in cities?**

Absolutely, and most governments haven't done nearly enough. When housing becomes unaffordable, essential workers — nurses, teachers, bus drivers — can no longer afford to live near their workplaces, which undermines the functioning of the whole city. The private market alone has consistently failed to provide housing that lower-income residents can afford. Governments need to intervene directly, whether through public housing programmes, rent controls, or regulations that require developers to include affordable units in new projects.

**4. Should cities limit their geographical expansion, or should they continue to grow outwards?**

There are strong arguments for setting clear urban boundaries. Unconstrained outward expansion consumes agricultural land, increases car dependency, and extends the cost of providing services over ever-greater areas. On the other hand, restricting growth artificially can push housing prices to unaffordable levels within the boundary, as has happened in cities like London and San Francisco. The ideal solution is probably to allow moderate densification within existing boundaries while protecting key environmental areas beyond them.

**5. How can cities be made more environmentally sustainable?**

The most impactful changes involve reducing car dependency through investment in public transport, cycling infrastructure, and pedestrianised zones. Buildings need to be retrofitted with better insulation and renewable energy sources, and new construction should meet high energy efficiency standards. At the city scale, tree-planting programmes, green roofs, and urban farming initiatives can reduce the urban heat island effect and improve air quality. However, all of these changes require significant political will and public investment over long time horizons.

**6. Do you think tall buildings are a good solution to housing shortages?**

Tall buildings can certainly increase housing density, but they are not a straightforward solution. Construction costs are high, maintenance of lifts and shared systems is expensive, and very tall residential buildings can create social problems — a lack of community, difficulty for children and the elderly, and poor natural light for lower floors. I think mid-rise development, perhaps five to eight storeys, often strikes a better balance between density and liveability than either sprawling low-rise development or skyscraper-dominated skylines.

**7. What role should green spaces play in city design?**

Green spaces are far more than just aesthetic amenities. Parks, urban forests, and waterways provide vital ecosystem services: cooling the air, absorbing rainfall, filtering pollutants, and supporting biodiversity. Psychologically, access to nature is strongly linked to mental health and wellbeing, which is particularly important in dense, high-stress urban environments. I believe cities should treat green space as essential infrastructure, not a luxury to be sacrificed when land prices rise.

**8. How has technology changed the way cities are managed?**

Technology has transformed urban management in remarkable ways. Real-time data from traffic sensors, weather monitoring systems, and energy grids allows city managers to respond dynamically to changing conditions rather than reacting days or weeks later. Predictive analytics can help identify where infrastructure is likely to fail before it actually does. Digital platforms allow residents to report problems, access services, and participate in planning consultations more easily. However, technology also introduces new risks around privacy and cybersecurity that cities need to manage carefully.

**9. Do you think people will continue to move to cities in the future, or will some return to rural areas?**

I think large-scale urbanisation will continue, particularly in the developing world where cities still offer substantially better economic opportunities and services than rural areas. However, in wealthier countries, high-speed internet, remote working arrangements, and disillusionment with urban housing costs and congestion have led some people to move to smaller cities or rural areas. This trend accelerated notably during the COVID-19 pandemic and may persist to some degree, particularly for workers in knowledge industries who have more locational flexibility.

**10. What qualities do you think make a city truly liveable?**

A liveable city, in my view, combines several qualities: it is safe and clean, it offers efficient and affordable public transport, it has a variety of parks and public spaces where people can relax and socialise, it provides access to good schools and healthcare, and it maintains a strong cultural and economic offer. Perhaps most importantly, it must be affordable enough that people on ordinary incomes can live there with dignity. Cities that only work well for the wealthy have, in my opinion, failed at the most fundamental level.
