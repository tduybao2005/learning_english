# IELTS Practice Test 30 — Speaking

---

## PART 1 (4–5 minutes)

### Topic 1: Work and Career

**1. Do you currently work or study?**
I'm currently completing my university studies in business administration. I chose this field because I wanted to understand how organisations function and how to lead teams effectively. It keeps me intellectually engaged most of the time.

**2. What kind of work would you like to do in the future?**
I'd love to work in a role that combines strategy and technology — perhaps in management consulting or at a technology company. I'm particularly interested in how organisations can use data and AI responsibly to make better decisions without sacrificing fairness.

**3. What motivates you most when you are working or studying?**
I find I'm most motivated when I feel a sense of progress and autonomy — when I can see that what I'm doing is making a real difference and when I have some freedom over how I approach the work. Deadlines and grades help in the short term, but genuine curiosity keeps me going over the longer term.

**4. Do you think the way people are motivated at work is changing?**
Yes, quite significantly. Younger workers especially seem to value purpose, flexibility, and opportunities to learn over high salaries and job security. Employers are increasingly offering remote work, skills development programmes, and more participatory management styles to attract and retain talent.

### Topic 2: History and Culture

**1. Are you interested in history?**
Very much so. I find history fascinating because it reveals how present-day institutions — governments, economies, religions — evolved from earlier forms and why societies made the choices they did. Understanding the past helps me interpret the present more clearly.

**2. Which historical period do you find most interesting and why?**
I'm particularly drawn to the era of great empires — the Roman, Ottoman, and Mongol empires especially. What strikes me is how they managed to organise and govern vast and diverse territories before modern communication technology existed. The administrative and military innovations they developed are genuinely impressive.

**3. Do you think it is important for young people to learn about history?**
Absolutely. History teaches critical thinking, empathy, and humility. It shows us that the problems we face — inequality, war, environmental degradation — are not entirely new, and that past civilisations found both effective and catastrophic ways to respond to them. Ignoring that record seems both wasteful and dangerous.

**4. Has your country's history influenced your personal values?**
I think it has, yes. Growing up in a country with a long history of colonial experience has made me attentive to questions of power, self-determination, and justice. Those values shape how I think about international relations, economic development, and individual rights.

### Topic 3: Technology and Society

**1. How much time do you spend using technology every day?**
Realistically, probably eight to ten hours, if you count studying on a laptop, using my smartphone for communication and information, and occasional streaming in the evenings. It's become so integrated into daily life that it's difficult to separate it from everything else I do.

**2. Do you think technology has made life better or worse overall?**
On balance, better — but unevenly so. Technology has improved healthcare, education, and communication enormously. At the same time, social media has contributed to anxiety and polarisation, and automation is disrupting livelihoods faster than societies can adapt. The net benefit is real but the distribution of that benefit is deeply unequal.

**3. Are you concerned about artificial intelligence?**
Yes, in specific ways. I'm less worried about science-fiction scenarios of robot takeover and more concerned about immediate issues: AI systems that perpetuate discrimination, opaque algorithms making consequential decisions about people's lives, and the concentration of AI capabilities in a small number of powerful corporations with limited accountability.

**4. What technology do you think will have the biggest impact in the next decade?**
I believe AI, combined with advances in biotechnology, will be transformative. The ability to design new drugs, diagnose diseases earlier, and personalise education could address some of humanity's deepest challenges. But the governance frameworks we develop now will determine whether these benefits are broadly shared or concentrated among the already privileged.

### Model Answer

The person I'd like to talk about is a university professor I had in my second year — Dr Chen, who taught a course on technology ethics. I know she may not be a famous public figure, but she has been genuinely influential in shaping how I think.

What I admire most about her is her intellectual honesty. She never presented any topic as having a simple answer. When we were studying AI and bias, she showed us research from multiple sides and pushed us to identify the assumptions behind each argument rather than just accepting the conclusions. That kind of intellectual rigour is rare and genuinely valuable.

The way she influenced me is hard to overstate. Before her class, I thought of technology as largely neutral — a tool that could be used well or badly depending on who used it. She helped me understand that technology embeds values, that design choices have political and social consequences, and that engineers and policymakers share responsibility for the outcomes. That insight changed how I approach every subject I study.

The quality that makes her most effective as a leader — or as an educator, which amounts to the same thing — is her combination of high standards and genuine respect for students. She expected rigorous, evidence-based arguments, but she always engaged with our ideas seriously, even when she disagreed. That balance between demanding excellence and treating people as capable of achieving it is, I think, what the best leaders do.

## PART 3 (4–5 minutes)

### Discussion Questions

**1. How do you think immersive technologies like VR and AR will change education in the future?**
They have enormous potential — students could visit historical sites virtually, conduct laboratory experiments in safe simulated environments, or practice high-stakes skills like surgery without risk. The challenge is equitable access: if only well-funded schools can afford the hardware, these technologies may widen rather than narrow educational inequality.

**2. Do you think AI should be used to assess students' academic work?**
I think AI can play a useful supplementary role — for instance, providing automated feedback on writing drafts — but final assessment of complex work should involve human judgement. Assessment is not only about accuracy; it involves contextual understanding, fairness, and a relationship between teacher and student that algorithms cannot adequately replicate.

**3. What are the main risks of allowing AI to make decisions in the criminal justice system?**
The primary risks are bias and opacity. As research on tools like COMPAS has shown, algorithms trained on historical data can perpetuate systemic racial disparities. Additionally, defendants have a right to understand and challenge decisions that affect their liberty, which is difficult when those decisions emerge from a black-box system.

**4. Do you think individuals or governments bear more responsibility for managing AI risks?**
Governments must bear primary responsibility. Individual choices about technology use are constrained by what's available and what's affordable. Only governments have the authority to set binding standards, mandate transparency, require auditing, and ensure that the benefits and risks of AI are distributed fairly across society.

**5. How should history inform the way we govern powerful new technologies?**
History offers cautionary examples. The industrial revolution generated enormous wealth but also terrible working conditions and environmental damage because governance lagged far behind technological change. The lesson is that proactive regulation — anticipating harms before they become entrenched — is far more effective than trying to correct damage after the fact.

**6. What can ancient empires like Rome teach modern states about managing complex systems?**
Rome's success rested on standardised law, infrastructure that connected diverse regions, and a degree of local autonomy within a shared administrative framework. These principles remain relevant: effective governance of large, diverse systems requires both common rules and the flexibility for local adaptation. Rome's decline also warns against military overextension and fiscal irresponsibility.

**7. Do you think global financial regulation of AI companies is realistic?**
It's difficult but necessary. AI companies operate across borders, and regulation in one country can simply push activity to another with lighter oversight. International coordination — perhaps modelled on the global financial regulatory frameworks developed after the 2008 crisis — is essential, though geopolitical competition between major AI powers makes consensus harder to achieve.

**8. How might workplace motivation change as AI automates more tasks?**
If AI takes over routine cognitive tasks, the work left for humans may increasingly require creativity, empathy, and ethical judgement — capabilities that many people find intrinsically satisfying. However, if automation destroys more jobs than it creates, widespread unemployment and loss of purpose could cause a serious motivational and social crisis.

**9. Should there be an international treaty banning autonomous weapons?**
I strongly believe so. Delegating lethal force to machines removes the human moral accountability that is fundamental to the laws of war. The argument that autonomous weapons might be more precise doesn't outweigh the categorical concern about machines making life-and-death decisions. A legally binding treaty, like those banning chemical and biological weapons, is the appropriate response.

**10. What role do you think individual citizens can play in shaping how AI is developed and used?**
Citizens can exert influence through democratic participation — voting for politicians who prioritise responsible AI governance, supporting civil society organisations that advocate for algorithmic accountability, and making informed consumer choices. Public pressure has already prompted technology companies to withdraw or revise AI products that caused harm. Civic engagement is not sufficient alone, but it is a necessary part of the accountability ecosystem.
