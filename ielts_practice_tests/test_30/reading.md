## PASSAGE 1: The Growth of Megacities

**Hướng dẫn:** Đọc đoạn văn sau và trả lời câu hỏi 1–13.

---

A megacity is defined by the United Nations as a metropolitan area with a total population exceeding ten million people. In 1950, there were only two such cities in the world: Tokyo and New York. By 2025, that number had risen to more than forty, with projections suggesting it could reach sixty or more by 2050. This dramatic shift in global urbanisation represents one of the most significant demographic changes in human history.

The growth of megacities is driven by a combination of natural population increase and rural-to-urban migration. In developing regions such as sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia, young people move to cities in search of employment, education, and improved living standards. A factory job in a city, even if poorly paid by Western standards, may offer three or four times the income available in a rural village. Cities also concentrate hospitals, schools, and social services that are simply absent in remote areas.

Not all megacity growth, however, is planned or managed. Many cities have expanded through the rapid development of informal settlements, commonly known as slums or shanty towns. These areas often lack basic infrastructure such as clean water, sewage systems, and reliable electricity. Dharavi in Mumbai, once described as Asia's largest slum, houses approximately one million people in an area of roughly two square kilometres. Despite its reputation, Dharavi has a thriving informal economy, generating an estimated one billion US dollars annually through recycling, textile production, and food processing.

Urban planners and governments face enormous challenges in managing megacity growth. Traffic congestion costs major cities billions of dollars each year in lost productivity. Air pollution in cities such as Delhi and Beijing regularly exceeds safe limits by several times, contributing to widespread respiratory illness. Housing shortages drive up rents, pushing lower-income residents to the urban periphery, far from employment centres.

Some megacities have responded with innovative solutions. Curitiba in Brazil, though not a megacity itself, pioneered a bus rapid transit system that now serves as a model worldwide. Singapore has used strict land use planning and public housing programmes to house the vast majority of its population in government-built apartments. Seoul in South Korea demolished an elevated motorway and restored the Cheonggyecheon Stream beneath it, transforming a traffic corridor into a four-kilometre recreational park that now attracts millions of visitors annually.

The future trajectory of megacity development will depend heavily on policy decisions made in the coming decades. Cities that invest in public transport, green spaces, affordable housing, and digital infrastructure are likely to become more liveable and economically competitive. Those that fail to plan for growth risk becoming ungovernable, characterised by inequality, environmental degradation, and social unrest.

## PASSAGE 2: Green Architecture: Building for the Future

**Hướng dẫn:** Đọc đoạn văn sau và trả lời câu hỏi 14–26.

---

**A.** The built environment accounts for nearly forty percent of global energy consumption and a similar proportion of carbon dioxide emissions. As awareness of climate change has grown, architects, engineers, and urban planners have developed a range of approaches collectively known as green or sustainable architecture. These techniques aim to reduce the environmental impact of buildings throughout their entire lifecycle — from construction to demolition.

**B.** One of the foundational concepts in green architecture is passive design. Passive buildings are oriented and designed to exploit natural resources — sunlight, wind, and the thermal mass of walls and floors — to maintain comfortable temperatures without relying heavily on mechanical heating or cooling systems. In cool climates, south-facing windows (in the northern hemisphere) capture winter sunlight, while deep roof overhangs shade the same windows in summer. Thick walls of stone, concrete, or rammed earth absorb heat during the day and release it slowly at night, smoothing temperature swings. When implemented correctly, passive design can reduce energy consumption for heating and cooling by as much as seventy to ninety percent compared with conventional buildings.

**C.** Beyond passive design, many buildings now incorporate active technologies to generate renewable energy on-site. Solar photovoltaic panels, once prohibitively expensive, have fallen dramatically in cost, with the price per watt of installed capacity dropping by over ninety percent between 2010 and 2024. Building-integrated photovoltaics — where solar panels are embedded directly into roofing tiles, facades, or glazing — allow energy generation without compromising aesthetic appeal. Small-scale wind turbines, ground-source heat pumps, and rainwater harvesting systems are also increasingly common components of green buildings.

**D.** Green building certification schemes have played an important role in driving the adoption of sustainable design. The Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED) system, developed in the United States, awards points across several categories, including energy efficiency, water use, materials selection, and indoor air quality. Buildings can achieve certification at four levels: Certified, Silver, Gold, and Platinum. Similarly, the Building Research Establishment Environmental Assessment Method (BREEAM), developed in the United Kingdom, has been applied to over five hundred thousand buildings in eighty countries. These certification systems provide a common language for measuring and communicating sustainability performance.

**E.** Green architecture is not limited to new construction. Retrofitting existing buildings — upgrading their insulation, windows, ventilation systems, and lighting — can dramatically reduce their energy consumption without demolishing and rebuilding. In many European cities, where a significant proportion of the building stock dates from the nineteenth or early twentieth century, retrofit programmes have become a central element of urban sustainability strategies. The German government's KfW bank has provided subsidised loans for building retrofits since the 1990s, resulting in measurable reductions in national energy consumption.

## PASSAGE 3: Smart Cities: Technology and Urban Life

**Hướng dẫn:** Đọc đoạn văn sau và trả lời câu hỏi 27–40.

---

The concept of the smart city has gained considerable traction in urban planning circles over the past two decades. At its core, a smart city uses digital technology and data analytics to improve the efficiency, sustainability, and quality of life for its residents. Sensors embedded in roads, buildings, waste bins, and streetlights collect vast amounts of data, which are then processed and analysed to enable more responsive and efficient urban management.

One of the most mature applications of smart city technology is intelligent transport systems. In cities such as Amsterdam and Singapore, traffic lights are connected to a central management system that monitors vehicle flow in real time and adjusts signal timings to reduce congestion. Emergency vehicles can be given priority at intersections, reducing response times. Parking sensors guide drivers to available spaces, cutting the amount of time spent circling for parking — a significant source of urban traffic and emissions. In Stockholm, a congestion charge scheme that uses cameras to identify and charge vehicles entering the city centre reduced traffic by twenty percent and cut pollution levels measurably.

Energy management is another domain where smart technology is delivering benefits. Smart electricity grids, or smart grids, can balance supply and demand dynamically, integrating variable renewable energy sources such as solar and wind more effectively than conventional grids. Smart meters in homes and offices provide real-time feedback on energy consumption, enabling both residents and utility companies to identify inefficiencies and reduce waste. In some pilot projects, algorithms automatically adjust heating, cooling, and lighting in response to occupancy patterns and weather forecasts.

Water management has also been transformed by sensor technology. Leaks in urban water networks — a major source of water loss in older cities — can be detected quickly when pressure sensors monitor the network continuously. Singapore's PUB water utility has used smart metering and data analytics to reduce non-revenue water (water that enters the system but is lost before reaching a paying customer) to below five percent, one of the lowest rates in the world. By contrast, many cities in the developing world lose thirty to fifty percent of their treated water to leakage and theft.

Despite the promise of smart city technology, significant challenges remain. Privacy concerns are paramount: a city equipped with thousands of cameras and sensors generates enormous quantities of data about its residents' movements, habits, and behaviours. Without robust legal frameworks and transparent governance, this data could be misused by governments or corporations. The experience of Sidewalk Toronto — a high-profile smart city project on Toronto's waterfront that was cancelled in 2020 — illustrated how quickly public trust can erode when data governance arrangements are poorly designed.

The digital divide presents another challenge. Smart city services often depend on residents having smartphones, internet access, and sufficient digital literacy to engage with online platforms. In cities with high levels of poverty or inequality, the benefits of smart technology may accrue disproportionately to wealthier, more educated residents, while the most vulnerable are left behind. Urban planners and policymakers must therefore ensure that smart city programmes are designed with inclusion as a core principle, not an afterthought.

Finally, the infrastructure investment required to build a smart city is substantial. Laying fibre optic cables, installing sensor networks, and building data centres requires significant upfront capital expenditure. For cities in low-income countries, where basic infrastructure such as roads, schools, and hospitals remains inadequate, the prioritisation of smart city investment over more fundamental needs raises difficult ethical and practical questions.
