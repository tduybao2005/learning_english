### The Psychology of Motivation

**A** Motivation is the psychological force that initiates, guides, and sustains goal-directed behaviour. Psychologists distinguish between two fundamental types. Intrinsic motivation describes the drive to engage in an activity because it is inherently interesting, enjoyable, or personally satisfying — the desire to paint, to solve a puzzle, or to master a musical instrument for the sheer pleasure of doing so. Extrinsic motivation, by contrast, refers to behaviour driven by external outcomes such as money, grades, praise, or the avoidance of punishment. Research consistently demonstrates that intrinsically motivated individuals tend to display greater creativity, persistence, and overall well-being than those who are primarily extrinsically motivated. However, the relationship between the two types is more nuanced than a simple opposition; under certain conditions, external rewards can either complement or undermine intrinsic engagement.

**B** One of the most influential frameworks in motivational psychology is Abraham Maslow's hierarchy of needs, first articulated in his 1943 paper 'A Theory of Human Motivation'. Maslow proposed that human needs are arranged in a five-level pyramid. At the base are physiological needs — food, water, shelter — which must be satisfied before higher-order needs can be addressed. Above these are safety needs (security, stability), followed by social needs (belonging, affection), then esteem needs (achievement, recognition), and at the apex, self-actualisation: the realisation of one's fullest potential. Maslow argued that individuals generally progress up the hierarchy as lower needs are met, though he acknowledged that the order was not always rigid and that people might simultaneously pursue needs at multiple levels.

**C** A more empirically grounded account of motivation emerged in the 1980s with self-determination theory (SDT), developed by psychologists Edward Deci and Richard Ryan. SDT posits that human beings have three innate psychological needs: competence (the need to feel effective in one's interactions with the environment), autonomy (the need to experience one's behaviour as self-directed rather than externally controlled), and relatedness (the need to feel meaningfully connected to others). When these three needs are satisfied, individuals tend to experience greater intrinsic motivation, well-being, and psychological health. SDT research has shown that environments — whether in schools, workplaces, or clinical settings — that support autonomy rather than controlling behaviour through rewards and sanctions produce significantly better motivational outcomes.

**D** Another landmark contribution to the study of motivation is the concept of 'flow', introduced by Hungarian-American psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi in the 1970s. Flow describes an optimal psychological state that occurs when a person's skills are precisely matched to the challenge of a task, producing intense concentration, a distorted sense of time, and deep personal satisfaction. In a flow state, people become so absorbed in their activity that they lose awareness of external distractions and even of themselves as separate agents. Csikszentmihalyi's research, based on thousands of interviews across multiple cultures, found that flow experiences are most commonly reported by individuals engaged in activities with clear goals and immediate feedback — conditions that can be engineered in both educational and professional environments.

**E** The concept of the growth mindset, developed by Stanford psychologist Carol Dweck through decades of research with children and adults, has similarly transformed how educators and managers think about motivation. Dweck found that individuals who believe intelligence and talent are fixed traits — those with a 'fixed mindset' — tend to avoid challenges that might expose their limitations and respond poorly to failure. By contrast, people who believe that abilities can be developed through effort and learning — those with a 'growth mindset' — embrace challenges, persist through setbacks, and ultimately achieve at higher levels. Research in organisational contexts has found that autonomy at work is a stronger predictor of job satisfaction and sustained effort than salary level alone, a finding that has led many companies to restructure roles around employee choice and ownership rather than relying exclusively on financial incentives.

**F** One contemporary application of motivational psychology is gamification — the use of game design elements such as points, badges, leaderboards, and progress bars in non-game contexts including education, healthcare, fitness, and workplace training. Proponents argue that gamification harnesses the motivational power of achievement, competition, and reward to drive engagement in tasks that individuals might otherwise find tedious. Critics, however, point to a phenomenon known as the 'overjustification effect': when an external reward is introduced for an activity that people were already intrinsically motivated to perform, subsequent removal of the reward often leaves them less motivated than before the reward was introduced. This suggests that gamification, if poorly designed, can paradoxically diminish the very motivation it sets out to enhance.

### The Roman Empire

**A** According to Roman tradition, the city of Rome was founded in 753 BC by the twin brothers Romulus and Remus, though modern historians regard this account as largely legendary. What is clear from archaeological evidence is that a permanent settlement existed on the hills above the Tiber River from at least the eighth century BC. For nearly five centuries, Rome was governed as a republic, with power shared between elected magistrates and the Senate, an advisory body composed of Rome's leading citizens. The republican system produced remarkable political stability and military effectiveness, allowing Rome to extend its control progressively across the Italian peninsula, then the western Mediterranean, and ultimately into the eastern Hellenistic world.

**B** The republic was, however, deeply vulnerable to internal conflict. Growing inequality between wealthy landowners and the dispossessed rural poor, combined with the ambitions of successful military commanders who commanded the personal loyalty of their armies, produced a century of intermittent civil war. The most celebrated figure of this turbulent era was Gaius Julius Caesar, a military genius and shrewd politician who crossed the Rubicon River with his army in 49 BC, triggering the final crisis of the republic. Caesar's subsequent dictatorship ended with his assassination on the Ides of March, 44 BC, but the conflict he had unleashed continued until his adopted heir Octavian defeated his rivals at the Battle of Actium in 31 BC.

**C** Octavian, renamed Augustus by the Senate in 27 BC, became effectively Rome's first emperor while carefully maintaining the outward forms of republican government. The transition from republic to empire ushered in what historians call the Pax Romana — the Roman Peace — a period of approximately two centuries during which the empire enjoyed relative internal stability and prosperity. Augustus reformed the army, created a professional civil service, and embarked on an ambitious building programme that prompted his famous boast that he had found Rome a city of brick and left it a city of marble. The Pax Romana provided the conditions under which Roman culture, law, and language spread to the furthest corners of the empire.

**D** The Roman Empire reached its greatest territorial extent under Emperor Trajan, who ruled from 98 to 117 AD. At its peak in 117 AD, the empire encompassed approximately five million square kilometres, stretching from the Atlantic coast of Britain and Spain in the west to Mesopotamia in the east, and from the Rhine and Danube rivers in the north to the Sahara Desert in the south. Sustaining this vast territory required an extraordinary infrastructure. Rome's road network eventually extended to around 400,000 kilometres, with approximately 80,000 kilometres of paved highways connecting every corner of the empire. These roads served military, administrative, and commercial purposes simultaneously, enabling the rapid movement of legions, the efficient collection of taxes, and the transport of goods across enormous distances.

**E** Roman technological and legal innovations had a lasting influence on Western civilisation. Roman engineers perfected a form of concrete — opus caementicium — that was stronger and more durable than anything previously known, enabling the construction of structures such as the Pantheon and the Colosseum that have survived largely intact to the present day. Roman aqueducts, some of which transported water over distances of more than 100 kilometres through gravity-fed channels and underground tunnels, supplied cities with running water for drinking, bathing, and sanitation at a standard that would not be exceeded in Europe for nearly fifteen centuries. Roman law, codified under the emperor Justinian in the sixth century AD, provided the foundation on which most modern European legal systems are built.

**F** The economy of the Roman Empire rested on two principal foundations: agricultural production and long-distance trade, both of which depended heavily on enslaved labour. Estimates of the slave population during the empire's peak vary widely, but scholars generally agree that slaves constituted between 15 and 35 per cent of the total population of Roman Italy, performing tasks ranging from manual agricultural labour to skilled craftsmanship, teaching, and financial administration. Roman trade networks connected the empire to Sub-Saharan Africa, Arabia, India, and China through both overland and maritime routes, importing luxury goods such as silk, spices, ivory, and gemstones in exchange for Roman wine, olive oil, glass, and manufactured goods.

**G** The Western Roman Empire's decline was the product of multiple, mutually reinforcing pressures that accumulated over more than two centuries. Military overextension made the frontiers increasingly difficult and costly to defend, while a succession of capable emperors gave way to a period of chronic political instability in the third century AD — the so-called 'Crisis of the Third Century' — during which more than twenty emperors claimed the throne in less than fifty years. Repeated debasement of the silver coinage to finance military expenditure triggered severe inflation that disrupted trade and eroded public confidence. The division of the empire in 285 AD under Diocletian brought temporary relief, but the Western half never recovered its coherence. In 476 AD, the Germanic chieftain Odoacer deposed the last Western Roman emperor, Romulus Augustulus, in an event that historians conventionally mark as the fall of the Western Roman Empire.

### AI Ethics

**A** The rapid development of artificial intelligence has brought transformative benefits to medicine, science, commerce, and daily life, while simultaneously generating a range of profound ethical concerns. Among the most fundamental is the alignment problem: the challenge of ensuring that AI systems reliably pursue goals that are genuinely beneficial to human beings rather than pursuing proxy objectives that can diverge from human values in unexpected ways. The alignment problem arises because current AI systems learn their behaviours from data and reward signals specified by their designers, and it is technically difficult to guarantee that a powerful optimising system will not find unintended ways to achieve the goals it has been given. As AI systems become more capable and are deployed in higher-stakes environments, the importance of solving the alignment problem grows correspondingly.

**B** A more immediately pressing concern for many researchers is the presence of bias in AI systems. Machine learning models learn from historical data, and if that data reflects existing social inequalities — as most real-world datasets do — the models will tend to reproduce and often amplify those inequalities. Research by the MIT Media Lab found that facial recognition systems from major technology companies performed with error rates below one per cent for lighter-skinned men but with error rates of up to 34.7 per cent for darker-skinned women. Similar disparities have been documented in AI systems used for automated hiring, credit scoring, and medical diagnosis. The source of such bias is rarely a deliberate design choice; rather, it emerges from the historical patterns encoded in training data, making it difficult to detect and correct.

**C** The development of autonomous weapons — systems capable of selecting and engaging targets without human intervention — has provoked intense international debate. Proponents argue that lethal autonomous weapons systems could reduce civilian casualties by making targeting decisions more precise and by removing soldiers from dangerous combat environments. Critics, including a coalition of AI researchers and non-governmental organisations known as the Campaign to Stop Killer Robots, contend that delegating life-and-death decisions to machines raises fundamental questions about moral accountability: if an autonomous weapon kills a civilian unlawfully, who bears legal responsibility? No international treaty governing autonomous weapons currently exists, despite repeated calls from United Nations experts for a legally binding instrument.

**D** AI systems are increasingly being used to make or influence decisions in domains that profoundly affect people's lives, including hiring, lending, and criminal sentencing. The use of algorithmic tools in the criminal justice system has attracted particular scrutiny. The COMPAS recidivism prediction tool, used in several US states to estimate the likelihood that a defendant will reoffend, was examined in a landmark 2016 investigation by ProPublica, which found that the algorithm incorrectly flagged Black defendants as future criminals at nearly twice the rate of white defendants. Defenders of the system disputed the methodology of the analysis, but the controversy highlighted a broader tension between the statistical efficiency that algorithmic systems can achieve and the individual fairness that legal traditions demand.

**E** A persistent challenge in deploying AI ethically is the opacity of many state-of-the-art systems. Deep neural networks, which underpin most of the recent advances in AI, are often described as 'black boxes' because their internal processing is too complex for human observers to interpret in any meaningful way. This opacity creates serious problems in high-stakes contexts where decisions must be explainable and contestable. The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), introduced in 2018, established a right to explanation for individuals subject to automated decisions with significant effects on their lives. The emerging field of explainable AI (XAI) seeks to develop techniques that make AI decision-making more transparent, but achieving genuine interpretability without sacrificing performance remains an unsolved technical challenge.

**F** Governments and international bodies have begun developing regulatory frameworks to govern the use of artificial intelligence. The most comprehensive attempt to date is the European Union's Artificial Intelligence Act, which came into force in 2024. The EU AI Act adopts a risk-based approach, categorising AI applications according to the level of risk they pose. Systems deemed to present an 'unacceptable risk' — such as social credit scoring by public authorities and real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces — are prohibited outright. Systems classified as 'high risk', including AI used in education, employment, critical infrastructure, and law enforcement, must meet stringent requirements for transparency, human oversight, and accuracy before they can be deployed. Lower-risk applications face lighter regulatory burdens but are still subject to transparency obligations.

**G** Perhaps the most contested question in AI ethics concerns the possibility of transformative or even existential risk from advanced AI systems. The philosopher Nick Bostrom, in his influential 2014 book *Superintelligence*, argued that once AI systems surpass human intelligence in all cognitive domains, they could become virtually uncontrollable and might pursue goals catastrophically misaligned with human welfare, posing an existential threat to humanity. This position is contested by prominent AI researchers such as Yann LeCun, Chief AI Scientist at Meta, who argues that superintelligence is a speculative extrapolation from current capabilities and that the more immediate and tractable AI risks are those relating to bias, misuse, and inadequate governance rather than science-fiction scenarios of AI takeover. The disagreement reflects a deeper division about the likely trajectory of AI development and the appropriate allocation of limited research resources between near-term and long-term concerns.
