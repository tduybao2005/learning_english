## READING PASSAGE 1

**Coral Reef Ecosystems: Nature's Underwater Cities**

**Paragraph A**

Coral reefs cover less than 1% of the ocean floor yet support approximately 25% of all marine species. Despite their relatively small area, they provide habitat, feeding grounds, and nurseries for thousands of species of fish, invertebrates, and marine mammals. Coral reefs are sometimes called "the rainforests of the sea" due to the exceptional biodiversity they host. Beyond their ecological role, reefs provide vital services to human communities: they protect shorelines from wave erosion, support commercial and subsistence fisheries that feed hundreds of millions of people, and generate billions of dollars annually through tourism and recreation.

**Paragraph B**

The structural foundation of any coral reef is formed by tiny animals called coral polyps, which are closely related to jellyfish and sea anemones. Each polyp secretes a hard calcium carbonate skeleton, and it is the accumulation of millions of these skeletons over thousands of years that creates the physical structure of the reef. Living within the tissue of coral polyps are microscopic algae called zooxanthellae, which perform photosynthesis and provide the coral with up to 90% of its energy through the sugars they produce. In return, the zooxanthellae receive shelter within the coral tissue and access to sunlight, which they require to carry out photosynthesis.

**Paragraph C**

This relationship between coral polyps and zooxanthellae is a form of mutualistic symbiosis — both organisms benefit from the arrangement. When corals become stressed, however, typically through elevated water temperatures, they expel their zooxanthellae, causing the coral to turn white in a process known as coral bleaching. Without their primary energy source, bleached corals can survive for only a few weeks before beginning to die. While corals can recover from bleaching if temperatures return to normal quickly, repeated or prolonged bleaching events often result in permanent coral death. Even a temperature increase of just 1–2°C above the seasonal maximum, sustained for several weeks, is sufficient to trigger widespread bleaching.

**Paragraph D**

The rate of reef destruction has accelerated dramatically in recent decades. The Great Barrier Reef in Australia, the world's largest coral reef system, has experienced five mass bleaching events since 1998, with the most severe occurring in 2016 and 2017, when surveys estimated that approximately 50% of corals in the northern section died. Climate projections suggest that if global temperatures rise by 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels, 70–90% of all coral reefs will be severely degraded. A 2°C increase, which is considered the upper limit under the Paris Agreement, could eliminate more than 99% of the world's reef systems. The timeline for this damage is not centuries away — scientists warn that many reefs will be functionally extinct by the 2040s without dramatic reductions in greenhouse gas emissions.

**Paragraph E**

Human activities compound the threat posed by climate change. Agricultural and urban runoff carrying fertilisers and pollutants feeds algal blooms that can smother coral reefs, blocking the sunlight that zooxanthellae require for photosynthesis. Destructive fishing practices, including the use of dynamite or cyanide to stun fish for easy capture, directly damage reef structures and kill corals indiscriminately. Overfishing disrupts the ecological balance of reef ecosystems by removing species such as herbivorous fish that would otherwise graze on algae and keep reef surfaces clean. Tourism, while economically vital to many coastal communities, can cause physical damage through boat anchoring on reefs, coral trampling by swimmers and divers, and the introduction of sunscreen chemicals that are toxic to marine organisms.

**Paragraph F**

Conservation efforts take multiple forms. Marine protected areas (MPAs) restrict fishing and coastal development in sensitive reef zones, giving reef ecosystems space to recover from human disturbance. Coral gardening programmes, in which coral fragments are grown in underwater nurseries and then transplanted onto degraded reefs, have shown some localised success in restoring reef cover in places like Florida and the Caribbean. Researchers are also investigating the possibility of breeding heat-resistant coral strains through selective breeding or genetic engineering, aiming to create colonies that can survive warmer oceans. Critics argue, however, that such technological interventions do not address the underlying problem of climate change and could create unforeseen ecological consequences if artificially bred corals outcompete naturally evolved species.

## READING PASSAGE 2

**A History of Urban Planning: From Ancient Streets to Smart Cities**

**Paragraph A**

The idea that cities must be deliberately designed rather than allowed to grow spontaneously is far older than most people assume. Archaeological evidence from the Indus Valley civilisation, which flourished around 2500 BCE in what is now Pakistan and northwest India, reveals settlements such as Mohenjo-Daro and Harappa laid out in precise grids, complete with standardised brick sizes, covered drainage channels, and designated areas for different activities. Contemporary urban planning in ancient Mesopotamia, Egypt, and China similarly demonstrates that the conscious organisation of urban space was a feature of advanced civilisation long before the modern era. Greek cities were often arranged around the agora — a central public space for commerce and civic life — and the Romans systematically applied their gridiron street layout and infrastructure systems, including aqueducts and sewers, to settlements throughout their empire.

**Paragraph B**

In contrast, medieval European cities grew largely without central direction. Streets followed the contours of the land, the boundaries of individual property holdings, and the paths of pre-existing tracks and rivers. The result was a labyrinth of narrow lanes, irregular plots, and dense timber-framed buildings packed closely together. While such environments had their own organic vitality, they were also deeply hazardous: fire could leap rapidly between adjacent structures, sanitation was primitive and frequently relied on waste being thrown into streets, and epidemics spread easily through crowded, poorly ventilated districts. The Great Fire of London in 1666, which destroyed over 13,000 houses and 87 churches, served as a dramatic catalyst for rethinking the built environment. In the subsequent reconstruction, wider streets, brick construction, and building setbacks from street frontages were mandated — precursors of modern building regulation.

**Paragraph C**

The most ambitious and controversial planned urban intervention of the nineteenth century was the renovation of Paris undertaken between 1853 and 1870 under the direction of Baron Georges-Eugène Haussmann, appointed by Emperor Napoleon III. Haussmann demolished vast swathes of medieval Paris and replaced them with the wide, tree-lined boulevards, spacious parks, modern sewers, and gas lighting that define the city's visual identity today. The Haussmannisation of Paris was celebrated as a triumph of modern urban management, improving traffic flow, sanitation, and access to light and air. It was simultaneously condemned, however, for systematically displacing hundreds of thousands of working-class residents who could not afford the rents in rebuilt areas, effectively engineering a socially segregated city. The controversy over whether urban renewal serves the public good or primarily benefits wealthy residents and developers echoes persistently in planning debates to this day.

**Paragraph D**

The twentieth century produced its own radical vision of the ideal city in the form of modernist planning, most influentially articulated by the Swiss-French architect Le Corbusier. His "Radiant City" concept proposed replacing existing urban fabric with towers of glass and steel set in open parkland, with pedestrians and vehicles occupying entirely separate levels, and residential, commercial, and industrial functions strictly segregated by zone. This model was implemented on a large scale in the post-war housing projects built across Britain, France, and the United States — high-rise estates designed to replace bomb-damaged or overcrowded inner-city districts and offer residents modern amenities including indoor plumbing, central heating, and natural light. The results were initially praised but subsequently criticised heavily: the separation of functions eliminated the street-level activity that made traditional urban areas feel safe and liveable, high-rise living proved socially isolating especially for families with young children, and many estates became associated with poverty, crime, and neglect.

**Paragraph E**

The most influential critique of modernist planning came from the American journalist and activist Jane Jacobs, whose 1961 book *The Death and Life of Great American Cities* mounted a passionate defence of the messy, dense, mixed-use urban environments that planners sought to demolish. Jacobs argued that vibrant neighbourhoods required a diversity of uses — residential, commercial, and civic buildings mixed together — to ensure that streets were populated at different times of day, generating the natural surveillance she called "eyes on the street." She advocated for short blocks, aged buildings that accommodated lower-income businesses, and high population density, all of which she observed in her own Greenwich Village neighbourhood in New York. Her ideas profoundly influenced the New Urbanism movement, which emerged in the 1980s and advocated for walkable, mixed-use communities centred around public spaces. Portland, Oregon, where a planned freeway expansion was cancelled in the 1970s and replaced with light rail and pedestrian-friendly streets, became a celebrated example of planning principles aligned with Jacobs's vision.

**Paragraph F**

Contemporary sustainable urban design draws on a broadening toolkit. Biophilic design incorporates natural elements — living walls, rooftop gardens, urban forests — into the fabric of buildings and streets, with research suggesting that access to nature in urban environments improves mental health and reduces heat island effects. The "15-minute city" concept, pioneered by the urban planner Carlos Moreno and adopted by cities including Paris and Melbourne, holds that residents should be able to access work, shops, schools, healthcare, and leisure within a 15-minute walk or cycle ride from their homes. Transit-oriented development, exemplified by Singapore's planning model, concentrates dense housing and commercial development around mass transit stations to minimise car dependency. Green building certification standards such as LEED measure and incentivise energy efficiency, water conservation, and sustainable materials in new construction.

**Paragraph G**

The smart city paradigm applies information technology, sensors, and data analytics to urban management. Barcelona has embedded sensor networks into pavements, street lighting, and waste bins to monitor pedestrian flow, reduce lighting energy consumption, and optimise refuse collection routes. Songdo in South Korea was built from scratch as a fully integrated smart city, with pneumatic tube systems replacing rubbish trucks and sensors embedded in every aspect of its infrastructure. Singapore's Virtual Singapore project has created a detailed three-dimensional model of the entire city-state to support urban planning decisions. Advocates argue that such systems improve efficiency, reduce costs, and make cities more responsive to residents' needs. Critics, however, point to serious concerns: the mass collection of sensor data raises privacy issues, smart city systems tend to serve wealthier, more digitally literate residents while leaving others behind, and cities that depend heavily on proprietary technology infrastructure become vulnerable to system failures and vendor lock-in.

## READING PASSAGE 3

**The Economics of Happiness: Beyond GDP**

**Paragraph A**

For most of the twentieth century, Gross Domestic Product — the total monetary value of all goods and services produced within a country in a given year — served as the dominant measure of national success. Politicians celebrated GDP growth as evidence of progress, and international organisations used it as the primary lens through which to compare countries and evaluate development policies. Yet even its most committed advocates acknowledged that GDP has fundamental limitations as a measure of human welfare. It says nothing about how wealth is distributed among the population, excludes the value of unpaid work such as caring for children or elderly relatives, treats environmentally destructive activities as economic positives, and takes no account of whether people are actually satisfied with their lives. A country can have rapidly rising GDP while simultaneously experiencing growing inequality, environmental degradation, and declining life satisfaction — a paradox that has increasingly prompted economists and policymakers to search for alternative measures of national wellbeing.

**Paragraph B**

One of the most influential challenges to GDP-centric thinking came from economist Richard Easterlin in 1974. Studying data from multiple countries, Easterlin observed a puzzling pattern: within any given country, wealthier individuals tended to report higher levels of happiness and life satisfaction than their less wealthy counterparts. Yet when he compared happiness levels across countries over time, he found that rising average income did not consistently produce rising average happiness. Nations that became considerably richer over decades did not become correspondingly happier. This finding — known as the Easterlin Paradox — fundamentally challenges the assumption underlying much economic policy: that increasing national income is the principal means of improving human lives. Subsequent research has generated considerable debate, with some economists arguing that the relationship between income and happiness continues at all income levels, while others maintain that subjective wellbeing plateaus at around $75,000–$80,000 annual income, beyond which additional money makes little difference.

**Paragraph C**

One early alternative to GDP was developed not by a wealthy industrial nation but by one of the world's smallest countries. In the 1970s, the fourth King of Bhutan, Jigme Singye Wangchuck, introduced the concept of Gross National Happiness (GNH), arguing that the true goal of governance should be the wellbeing of citizens rather than the maximisation of economic output. Bhutan's GNH framework rests on four pillars: sustainable socioeconomic development, cultural preservation and promotion, environmental conservation, and good governance. The country conducts regular GNH surveys measuring citizen wellbeing across nine domains including living standards, health, education, cultural resilience, and psychological wellbeing. The model attracted substantial international attention in the 2000s and 2010s as a potential alternative to GDP, and several United Nations resolutions referenced it approvingly. Critics, however, point out that Bhutan is a small, relatively isolated, and culturally homogeneous country, and that its governance model, which has authoritarian features, would be extremely difficult to replicate in larger, more complex, and more diverse societies.

**Paragraph D**

International bodies have developed their own composite alternatives to GDP. The United Nations Human Development Index (HDI), introduced in 1990, combines GDP per capita with measures of life expectancy and educational attainment, providing a more nuanced picture of national development. It has successfully redirected some policy attention away from pure economic growth towards health and education outcomes. However, the HDI still fails to capture subjective dimensions of wellbeing — how people actually feel about their lives — and does not account for inequality within countries. The Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI) goes considerably further, adjusting GDP to subtract the costs of crime, pollution, and environmental degradation, and to add the value of unpaid labour such as childcare and voluntary work. In countries including the United States, GPI has been shown to have grown much more slowly than GDP since the 1970s, and in some studies has actually declined, suggesting that much headline economic growth has come at a significant human and environmental cost.

**Paragraph E**

Alongside these economic frameworks, the emerging discipline of positive psychology has contributed substantially to scientific understanding of what actually makes people flourish over the long term. Psychologists Martin Seligman and Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, who launched the formal study of positive psychology in the late 1990s, identified several key predictors of sustained wellbeing: strong and supportive social relationships, a sense of purpose and meaning in life, regular experience of "flow" states — deep engagement with activities that are challenging enough to stretch one's abilities — positive emotions, and a sense of personal achievement. Critically, research consistently finds that material possessions and social status, while providing a short-term happiness boost, appear to have sharply diminishing returns. People quickly adapt to improved material circumstances — a phenomenon psychologists call the "hedonic treadmill" — and return to their baseline level of happiness. These findings suggest that policies promoting community cohesion, accessible mental health services, and opportunities for meaningful work may be considerably more effective at improving societal wellbeing than policies focused narrowly on economic growth.

**Paragraph F**

The policy implications of happiness research remain contested. Some governments have moved towards what proponents call "wellbeing budgets" — frameworks that evaluate spending decisions at least partly on the basis of their projected impact on population wellbeing rather than purely on economic criteria. New Zealand became the first country to introduce a formal wellbeing budget in 2019, under Prime Minister Jacinda Ardern, directing government spending towards priorities identified through wellbeing data including child poverty, mental health, and indigenous community development. Scotland has developed similar frameworks. Proponents argue that such approaches lead to more sustainable and genuinely humane policy outcomes than GDP-focused budgeting. Sceptics question, however, whether happiness can be measured with sufficient reliability and consistency to serve as a policy foundation, and raise deeper philosophical concerns about whether governments have a legitimate role in seeking to manage citizens' emotional states — a question that touches on fundamental issues of individual autonomy and the appropriate limits of state power.

**Paragraph G**

The emerging consensus among researchers is that no single indicator can adequately capture the complexity of human wellbeing, and that policymakers must work with a dashboard of multiple measures simultaneously. The OECD's Better Life Index, introduced in 2011, attempts to address this challenge by measuring wellbeing across eleven dimensions including income, health, education, community, civic engagement, and life satisfaction. Uniquely, it allows individual users to assign different weights to each dimension according to their own values and priorities, acknowledging explicitly that what constitutes a good life varies across individuals and cultures. The challenge for governments is to move beyond the seductive simplicity of GDP without adopting a framework so complex that it becomes practically unworkable for policymakers or incomprehensible to the public. A nuanced approach that combines objective measures — health outcomes, employment rates, educational attainment — with regularly collected subjective wellbeing data appears to represent the direction in which international best practice is evolving.
