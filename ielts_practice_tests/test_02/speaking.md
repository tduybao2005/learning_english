# IELTS SPEAKING — TEST 02

**Topics:** Environment & Travel / Books & Reading / Leisure & Social Life
**Band Target:** 6.5–8.0
**Duration:** 11–14 minutes total

---

## Part 1 (4–5 minutes)

*The examiner asks short questions about familiar topics. Aim for 2–4 natural sentences per answer. Do NOT give one-word answers or over-prepared speeches.*

---

### Topic 1: Environment & Travel

**1. Have you ever visited a natural environment — such as a forest, mountain, or beach — that particularly impressed you?**

Model Answer:
"Yes, absolutely. I once visited Ha Long Bay in northern Vietnam, and I was genuinely awestruck by the scale of the limestone karsts rising out of the emerald water. What struck me most was how vast and untouched it felt — almost prehistoric. It gave me a genuine appreciation for how important it is to protect these natural landscapes from overtourism."

*Key vocabulary: awestruck / limestone karsts / prehistoric / overtourism*

---

**2. Do you think people in your country care enough about the environment?**

Model Answer:
"I'd say awareness has increased dramatically in recent years, particularly among younger generations who've grown up hearing about climate change and plastic pollution. However, there's still quite a gap between awareness and action. Many people express concern about the environment in surveys but continue habits that are environmentally harmful — single-use plastics, frequent flying, and so on. I think the key barrier is convenience rather than indifference."

*Key vocabulary: awareness / gap between awareness and action / single-use plastics / indifference*

---

**3. What do you usually do to reduce your environmental impact?**

Model Answer:
"I try to be fairly conscious about this, to be honest. I carry a reusable water bottle and coffee cup, I take public transport rather than taxis when possible, and I try to eat less meat, which I've read has quite a significant carbon footprint. I'm not perfect by any means, but I think small consistent actions do add up."

*Key vocabulary: reusable / carbon footprint / consistent actions / add up*

---

**4. Do you think tourism can be both a benefit and a threat to natural environments?**

Model Answer:
"Absolutely — it's a double-edged sword. Tourism brings revenue that can fund conservation and creates an economic incentive for local communities to protect natural landscapes. But unmanaged tourism can also cause physical damage to fragile ecosystems through pollution, foot traffic, and infrastructure development. The difference, I think, comes down to how well visitor numbers are regulated."

*Key vocabulary: double-edged sword / conservation / fragile ecosystems / regulated*

---

### Topic 2: Books & Reading

**5. Do you enjoy reading? What kinds of books do you read?**

Model Answer:
"I do enjoy reading, though I'd say I go through phases. At the moment I'm reading more non-fiction than fiction — I find myself drawn to books about science, psychology, and history. I think reading non-fiction gives me a feeling of learning something genuinely useful, whereas with fiction I sometimes feel I need to be in exactly the right mood to get absorbed in it."

*Key vocabulary: go through phases / drawn to / absorbed in*

---

**6. Do you prefer reading physical books or e-books?**

Model Answer:
"That's an interesting one, because I actually use both for different purposes. For leisure reading — novels or anything I want to savour slowly — I much prefer a physical book. There's something about the tactile experience and the absence of distractions that I find more immersive. But for studying or referencing something quickly, e-books are far more practical because I can search and highlight digitally."

*Key vocabulary: savour / tactile experience / immersive / practical*

---

**7. Do you think reading has become less popular with the rise of social media and video content?**

Model Answer:
"I think the nature of reading has shifted rather than declined entirely. People are reading more than ever in terms of volume — just in shorter, more fragmented bursts. What has suffered, I believe, is deep, sustained reading of long-form texts, which requires a kind of focused attention that short-form digital content actively discourages. Whether that's a problem depends on what you value reading for."

*Key vocabulary: fragmented / sustained reading / long-form / focused attention*

---

**8. Did your parents or teachers encourage you to read when you were a child?**

Model Answer:
"Very much so — my mother in particular was always putting books in front of me as a child, which I didn't always appreciate at the time but I'm grateful for now. My primary school also had a reading programme where you'd get rewards for finishing books, which actually worked remarkably well as a motivator. I think those early habits shaped my relationship with reading for the rest of my life."

*Key vocabulary: appreciate / motivator / early habits / relationship with reading*

---

### Topic 3: Leisure & Social Life

**9. How do you typically spend your free time?**

Model Answer:
"It varies a lot depending on my energy levels, honestly. When I have longer stretches of free time, I like doing something active — hiking, cycling, or trying a new restaurant with friends. But after a long day of work or study, I'm often happy just to watch something on a streaming platform or read. I've also recently got into cooking, which I find surprisingly relaxing."

*Key vocabulary: energy levels / stretches of free time / streaming platform / relaxing*

---

**10. How has socialising changed with the rise of technology and social media?**

Model Answer:
"I think it's genuinely complicated. On one hand, technology has made it much easier to stay in touch with people across distances — I have close friends in different countries who I probably wouldn't maintain meaningful contact with without messaging apps. On the other hand, I do notice that when people are together in person, phones often pull attention away. There's a quality difference between digital connection and genuine presence with someone."

*Key vocabulary: stay in touch / meaningful contact / genuine presence / quality difference*

---

**11. Do you prefer spending time in large groups or with just a few close friends?**

Model Answer:
"Definitely with a smaller group — I'd say I'm more of an introvert in that sense. Large gatherings can be fun in the right context, like a celebration or a festival, but they're also quite draining. I find conversations are much richer and more authentic when there are fewer people, because you can actually go deep on a topic rather than skipping across the surface of many things."

*Key vocabulary: introvert / draining / authentic / go deep on a topic*

---

**12. Do you think people today have enough leisure time?**

Model Answer:
"In my experience, not really — at least not in the cities I know. There seems to be a growing culture of busyness where people feel guilty about rest and are constantly connected to work through their phones even outside office hours. The boundaries between work and leisure have become genuinely blurred for many people, which I think is having real effects on wellbeing and creativity. Ironically, we have more time-saving technology than ever but feel more pressed for time."

*Key vocabulary: culture of busyness / blurred / wellbeing / pressed for time*

---

## Part 2 (3–4 minutes)

*You have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1–2 minutes. The examiner will ask 1–2 follow-up questions.*

---

**Cue Card:** "Describe a memorable journey or trip that you have taken."

You should say:
- where you went and when
- why you went there
- what happened during the trip
- and explain why this trip was particularly memorable

**Preparation time:** 1 minute

---

### Model Response (~200 words)

"I'd like to describe a trip I took about two years ago to Sapa, in the mountainous northwest of Vietnam. I went with two close friends over a long weekend — partly for adventure and partly because I'd been under a lot of stress from work and desperately needed a break from city life.

We spent three days trekking through terraced rice fields and remote H'mong villages. What made it particularly memorable was that we hired a local guide from the village who spoke both Vietnamese and some English, and she shared incredible stories about the community's traditions and the challenges they face from changing weather patterns affecting their farming cycles. It was completely unexpected — I'd gone there for the scenery, but I came back with a much richer understanding of how rural communities in Vietnam are directly experiencing the effects of climate change.

The physical landscape was stunning, of course — the views across the rice terraces at dawn were almost unreal — but it was that human connection and the new perspective I gained that made the trip truly unforgettable. I've thought about it many times since, and it's made me much more interested in sustainable tourism."

*Key vocabulary: terraced rice fields / remote H'mong villages / changing weather patterns / sustainable tourism*

---

**Follow-up question:** "Do you think travel changes the way people see the world?"

Model Answer:
"I genuinely believe it does, at least if approached with an open mind. Encountering different ways of living challenges assumptions we don't even know we have. The key, I think, is slowing down and engaging rather than simply ticking off tourist attractions. The most transformative travel experiences I've heard about all involve genuine human connection rather than just visiting famous landmarks."

## Part 3 (4–5 minutes)

*The examiner asks more abstract, discussion-based questions linked to Part 2 themes. Aim for 4–6 sentences per answer, showing ability to develop and justify ideas.*

---

**1. How has mass tourism changed local communities in popular destinations?**

Model Answer:
"Mass tourism has had profoundly mixed effects on local communities. On the positive side, it generates employment and can revitalise economies in areas that have few other industries. However, the negative consequences are often severe and long-lasting: property prices rise dramatically as accommodation is converted for tourists, local residents are displaced, and the authentic character of neighbourhoods is gradually eroded. In extreme cases — Venice, Dubrovnik, and parts of Barcelona are frequently cited examples — local populations have declined sharply as cities become effectively open-air museums for visitors rather than functioning communities. The challenge is that tourism revenue is addictive for local economies, making it very difficult to impose the regulations that would reduce these harms."

---

**2. Should governments limit the number of tourists allowed into sensitive natural areas?**

Model Answer:
"I think visitor limits in ecologically sensitive areas are not only justified but necessary, and in many cases long overdue. Without managed access, the cumulative impact of millions of individual visitors — foot traffic, waste, noise, and infrastructure pressure — can cause irreversible damage to fragile ecosystems. Places like the Galápagos Islands and Machu Picchu have already implemented visitor caps with measurable conservation benefits. The counterargument is that restrictions disadvantage less wealthy travellers who can only visit during peak seasons, but I think this is a secondary concern when weighed against the permanent destruction of irreplaceable natural heritage."

---

**3. What are the advantages and disadvantages of ecotourism?**

Model Answer:
"Ecotourism, at its best, creates a powerful economic incentive for conservation: when local communities earn income from wildlife and natural landscapes, they have a direct financial interest in protecting them rather than converting them to agriculture or industry. It also raises environmental awareness among visitors. However, the term 'ecotourism' is frequently used as a marketing label rather than a genuine commitment to sustainability, which is sometimes called 'greenwashing.' Even well-intentioned ecotourism can introduce invasive species, disturb wildlife during sensitive breeding seasons, or simply direct tourism pressure to previously untouched areas. The difference between genuine and performative ecotourism ultimately comes down to certification standards and rigorous third-party oversight."

---

**4. Do you think virtual reality will ever replace the experience of real travel?**

Model Answer:
"I don't think it will replace real travel, though it may significantly supplement it. Virtual reality can simulate visual and auditory environments with increasing realism, but travel involves far more than what you see and hear — it includes physical sensation, smell, taste, unexpected human encounters, and the experience of genuine displacement from your normal environment, all of which current and foreseeable VR technology cannot replicate. What VR might usefully do is make the experience of culturally or geographically remote places accessible to people who face financial or physical barriers to travel, which is genuinely valuable. But for those able to travel physically, I think the embodied reality of being somewhere will always feel categorically different."

---

**5. How has globalisation affected the way different cultures experience and enjoy leisure time?**

Model Answer:
"Globalisation has both homogenised and diversified leisure experiences simultaneously, which is a genuinely interesting paradox. On one hand, the global spread of streaming platforms, social media, and international fast food chains means that young people in Hanoi, Lagos, and Oslo are increasingly consuming the same entertainment content and spending leisure time in similar ways. On the other hand, globalisation has also spread interest in previously niche cultural forms — South Korean drama, Japanese anime, West African music — to global audiences who would never have encountered them before. The concern, I think, is not that all cultures are becoming identical but that dominant cultural industries, particularly those based in the US and increasingly China, exert a disproportionate influence on global tastes."

---

**6. Is the decline of reading a threat to society?**

Model Answer:
"I think it depends very much on what kind of reading we are talking about. If the decline is specifically in sustained engagement with complex, long-form texts — novels, serious non-fiction, journalism — then yes, I think there are genuine social consequences. The capacity for patient, deep reading is closely linked to the ability to evaluate complex arguments, tolerate ambiguity, and develop empathy through sustained immersion in another perspective. These are cognitive and social skills that have broad democratic value. Short-form digital reading — scanning headlines, social media feeds, brief articles — exercises quite different cognitive muscles and may actually undermine some of those capacities if it becomes the dominant mode of consuming information."

---

**7. How has the internet changed the publishing industry?**

Model Answer:
"The internet has been simultaneously the most disruptive and the most democratising force the publishing industry has ever encountered. On the disruptive side, it has devastated the economics of print journalism, destroyed the business models of physical bookshops, and made it extremely difficult for writers to earn sustainable incomes from their work when so much content is available for free. On the democratising side, self-publishing platforms, blogs, and digital distribution have made it possible for writers to reach global audiences without needing the approval of traditional gatekeepers — major publishers and literary agents. The result is a far larger volume of published material, though critics argue that the absence of editorial curation makes quality harder to find."

---

**8. Do you think reading fiction develops empathy? Why or why not?**

Model Answer:
"There is actually fairly robust psychological research supporting the idea that reading literary fiction — as opposed to genre fiction or non-fiction — enhances certain measures of empathic ability and what psychologists call 'theory of mind', which is the capacity to model other people's mental states. The proposed mechanism is that literary fiction requires readers to infer characters' emotions, motivations, and perspectives from indirect textual cues, which exercises the same cognitive processes involved in real-life social understanding. However, I would add the caveat that this effect is likely to depend on the quality of engagement: passively reading while distracted probably has far less effect than deep, attentive reading. And I think the relationship may also be bi-directional — more empathetic people may simply be drawn to literary fiction in the first place."

---

**9. Should governments fund public libraries in the age of digital media?**

Model Answer:
"Absolutely, and I'd argue the case for public libraries is actually stronger in the digital age than it was before. The most straightforward argument is one of equity: not everyone has reliable internet access, a device capable of reading digital content, or the financial means to purchase ebooks and streaming subscriptions. Public libraries provide free, equal access to information and cultural resources regardless of income — a fundamentally democratic function. Beyond access, libraries serve as community spaces, provide professional information literacy support, and increasingly offer digital skills training. The comparison with digital alternatives is misleading because libraries offer something different in kind, not just a slower version of what the internet provides."

---

**10. What responsibilities do tourists have towards the environments and communities they visit?**

Model Answer:
"I think tourists have meaningful responsibilities that most people underestimate or conveniently overlook. At the most basic level, this includes obvious things like following environmental regulations, not disturbing wildlife, managing waste responsibly, and respecting local customs and dress codes. But I think the deeper responsibility is economic: making deliberate choices about where money goes. Staying in locally owned accommodation, eating at local restaurants, and hiring local guides keeps tourist spending within the community rather than flowing to international hotel chains. There is also a responsibility around cultural humility — approaching the places you visit with genuine curiosity rather than treating them as a backdrop for social media content."

## Speaking Tips for Vietnamese Learners

**Common errors to avoid:**
- Saying "Yes, I very like it" → Should be "Yes, I really like it" or "Yes, I enjoy it a lot"
- Saying "depend on" without the subject → "It depends on..." (always needs the subject)
- Missing articles: "I went to market" → "I went to the market" / "I went to a market"
- Singular/plural confusion: "many informations" → "a lot of information" (uncountable)

**Pronunciation focus for Band 6.5+:**
- Final consonants: /t/, /d/, /k/, /p/ — do not drop these
- Vowel length: "ship" vs "sheep" / "fill" vs "feel"
- Word stress: pho-TO-graph / pho-TOG-ra-phy / PHO-to-graph-er

**Fluency strategies:**
- Use fillers naturally: "That's an interesting question..." / "Let me think about that for a moment..."
- Extend answers by adding "For example..." or "In my experience..."
- If you don't know a word, paraphrase: "the thing you use to..." / "a kind of..."
