## WRITING TASK 1

*You should spend about 20 minutes on this task.*

The bar chart below shows the number of students enrolled in four university subject areas — Science, Arts, Business, and Engineering — in the years 2000 and 2020.

**Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.**

*Write at least 150 words.*

---

### Data Table (Bar Chart Represented as Table)

| Subject Area | Enrolment in 2000 | Enrolment in 2020 |
|---|---|---|
| Science | 22,000 | 31,000 |
| Arts | 35,000 | 28,000 |
| Business | 29,000 | 48,000 |
| Engineering | 18,000 | 39,000 |

---

### Model Answer — Task 1 (Band 7–8)

The bar chart compares student enrolment figures across four academic disciplines — Science, Arts, Business, and Engineering — at universities in 2000 and 2020.

Overall, enrolment grew in three of the four subjects over the twenty-year period, with the most pronounced increases seen in Business and Engineering. Arts was the only subject to record a decline.

In 2000, Arts attracted the largest student body, with 35,000 enrolments, followed by Business at 29,000 and Science at 22,000. Engineering had the smallest intake at 18,000. By 2020, this picture had changed considerably. Business became the most popular discipline by a clear margin, rising from 29,000 to 48,000 — a gain of nearly 20,000 students. Engineering experienced similarly dramatic growth, more than doubling from 18,000 to 39,000.

Science enrolments also increased, though more modestly, climbing from 22,000 to 31,000. Arts, by contrast, saw a drop of 7,000, falling from its 2000 peak to 28,000 by 2020, making it the only subject area to decline over the period.

In summary, the data reflect a clear shift in student preferences towards vocational and technical disciplines and away from the traditional arts and humanities.

*(183 words)*

### Examiner Notes

- **Task Achievement:** All four subject areas addressed; key trend (growth in Business and Engineering, decline in Arts) clearly identified; specific figures cited with relevant comparisons.
- **Coherence & Cohesion:** Logical paragraph structure — overview, then detailed comparison; effective use of linking phrases ("by contrast", "similarly", "in summary").
- **Lexical Resource:** Good variety — "pronounced increases", "dramatic growth", "most popular discipline by a clear margin"; no repetitive phrasing.
- **Grammatical Range & Accuracy:** Mix of simple and complex structures; accurate use of passive and comparative forms; no significant errors.

## WRITING TASK 2

*You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.*

**Write about the following topic:**

> *Some people believe that advances in medical technology have done more harm than good. To what extent do you agree or disagree?*

*Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.*

*Write at least 250 words.*

---

### Model Answer — Task 2 (Band 7–8)

The claim that medical technology has caused more harm than good is one I find fundamentally unconvincing. While specific concerns about the misuse or unintended consequences of medical innovation are legitimate, the broader record of medical technology in reducing suffering and extending life is, by any objective measure, overwhelmingly positive.

The most compelling evidence in favour of medical technology's benefits lies in the dramatic decline of mortality rates from conditions that were once invariably fatal. Vaccines have eradicated smallpox and brought polio to the edge of elimination, saving tens of millions of lives annually. Diagnostic technologies such as magnetic resonance imaging and computed tomography allow clinicians to detect cancers, strokes, and arterial blockages at stages when effective intervention remains possible. Artificial intelligence systems trained on vast datasets of medical images are now matching — and in some tasks exceeding — the diagnostic accuracy of experienced specialists, with the potential to extend high-quality diagnostics to underserved populations worldwide.

Proponents of the opposing view typically raise concerns about over-medicalisation — the tendency to pathologise normal human experiences — and the risk that powerful technologies may replace the human empathy that lies at the heart of good medical care. These concerns carry genuine weight. There is also legitimate unease about equitable access: if the most advanced medical technologies remain available only to wealthy patients in wealthy nations, they may widen rather than narrow global health inequalities.

Nevertheless, these risks are reasons to regulate and distribute technology more equitably, not to reject its development. The challenge for healthcare systems is not to slow medical innovation but to ensure that its extraordinary benefits reach those who need them most.

*(278 words)*

---

### Examiner Notes

- **Task Response:** Clear position stated in the introduction and consistently defended; counterarguments acknowledged and addressed; well-reasoned conclusion that extends beyond simple summary.
- **Coherence & Cohesion:** Four well-organised paragraphs; smooth transitions between points; topic sentences guide the reader effectively.
- **Lexical Resource:** High-level vocabulary used accurately — "over-medicalisation", "pathologise", "arterial blockages", "equitable access"; avoids repetition.
- **Grammatical Range & Accuracy:** Wide range of complex sentence structures; relative clauses, nominalisations, and conditional forms all used correctly.
