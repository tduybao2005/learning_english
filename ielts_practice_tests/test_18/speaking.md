## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*The examiner will ask you general questions on familiar topics. Answer naturally and extend your responses.*

### Topic 1: Health and Fitness

**1. How often do you exercise?**

*Model answer:* I try to exercise at least three or four times a week, usually a combination of running and some basic strength training at home. I find it genuinely important not just for physical health but for managing stress, especially during busy study periods. When I skip a few days, I notice I feel more sluggish and less focused.

**2. Do you think people in your country live a healthy lifestyle overall?**

*Model answer:* It's a mixed picture, honestly. Younger people in cities tend to be more health-conscious — you see a lot of gyms, cycling culture, and interest in nutrition. But in older generations and in rural areas, sedentary habits and diets high in processed food remain common. I think awareness has improved significantly but actual behaviour is slower to change.

**3. What do you do personally to try to stay healthy?**

*Model answer:* Beyond regular exercise, I pay attention to sleep, which I think is underrated as a health factor. I try to get around seven to eight hours most nights and avoid screens for at least thirty minutes before bed. I also try to cook most of my own meals rather than relying on takeaways, though I'll admit I don't always succeed with that.

**4. Do you think young people today are healthier than previous generations?**

*Model answer:* In some respects yes and in others no. Young people today benefit from far better medical knowledge and access to healthcare than previous generations did. But the rise of sedentary digital lifestyles, ultra-processed food, and mental health challenges — particularly anxiety and depression — suggest that health in its broadest sense may not have improved uniformly. Physical activity levels among young people have actually declined in many countries.

### Topic 2: Science and Technology

**5. Are you personally interested in science?**

*Model answer:* Very much so. I find biology and medicine particularly fascinating — the fact that scientists can now read a person's entire genetic code and use that information to tailor medical treatments feels almost like science fiction, even though it is already becoming a clinical reality. I follow science news quite regularly and enjoy reading accessible science writing when I can.

**6. Do you think science and technology have improved people's lives overall?**

*Model answer:* I think the evidence is overwhelmingly positive, at least at the population level. Medical technology alone has extended average life expectancy by decades compared with a century ago. The ability to communicate instantly with people anywhere in the world, to access vast libraries of information from a handheld device — these things represent genuine improvements to the quality of daily life for enormous numbers of people.

**7. How do you keep up with new developments in science and technology?**

*Model answer:* I mostly follow science journalism online — I have a few websites and newsletters I trust for accessible, well-explained coverage of research. I also watch documentary series occasionally. I try to be cautious about news that seems sensationalist or that overstates a finding, because early-stage research often gets reported as though it were settled fact.

**8. Do you think there are any genuine risks associated with new medical technologies?**

*Model answer:* Certainly. One concern that comes up a lot is the risk of over-diagnosis — using highly sensitive tests that detect conditions which might never have caused symptoms, leading to unnecessary treatment with its own costs and side effects. There are also serious concerns about data privacy when health information is processed by artificial intelligence systems run by commercial companies. And the risk of widening inequality, if advanced technologies are only accessible to wealthy patients, is real and important.

### Topic 3: Education

**9. Did you enjoy learning science at school?**

*Model answer:* I did, though I think it depended a great deal on the teacher. When a teacher was genuinely enthusiastic and used real-world examples — showing how chemistry explains cooking, or how physics explains why aircraft fly — I found it compelling. When it became entirely about memorising formulas for exams, my engagement dropped significantly. The method of teaching makes an enormous difference.

**10. Do you think schools should teach more about health and medicine?**

*Model answer:* I think they absolutely should. Understanding basic biology, nutrition, how vaccines work, how to read health information critically — these are genuinely life-relevant skills that most people never formally learn. A lot of health misinformation spreads partly because people lack the scientific foundation to evaluate it. If schools taught basic health literacy as a core subject, the benefits to public health could be substantial.

**11. What was your favourite subject at school?**

*Model answer:* I was particularly drawn to biology, partly because the subject matter felt immediately personal — it was literally about how I and every living thing around me worked. I also liked the way biological knowledge kept evolving, with new discoveries regularly overturning what had been taught as established fact. That sense of a living, changing field of inquiry was exciting in a way that more static subjects were not.

**12. Do you think the way science is currently taught in schools is effective?**

*Model answer:* Not as effective as it could be, I think. In most systems, school science is assessed through memorisation and reproduction of known facts, which doesn't really reflect how science actually works — which involves uncertainty, revision of hypotheses, and drawing careful conclusions from imperfect data. I think science education would be improved if students spent more time conducting simple experiments and evaluating real evidence rather than copying definitions from textbooks.

### Cue Card

**Describe a time when you learned something new about science or medicine.**

*You should say:*
- what you learned and where the information came from
- what prompted you to explore this topic
- how the new knowledge affected your thinking or behaviour
- and explain why this experience was memorable or significant for you

---

### Model Answer — Part 2 (Band 7–8)

The experience that comes to mind most readily happened about two years ago, when I read a long article about how the immune system actually works — specifically the distinction between the innate immune system, which responds immediately to any foreign invader with a broad generalised reaction, and the adaptive immune system, which takes longer to deploy but produces a precisely targeted response to a specific pathogen.

What prompted me to explore this was quite mundane — I had a flu that lasted longer than I expected, and I started wondering why it took my body nearly a week to fully recover when the virus itself was so tiny. That curiosity led me down a rabbit hole of reading about immunology, and what I found was genuinely astonishing.

The part that affected my thinking most was learning about immunological memory — the fact that after you recover from an infection or receive a vaccine, your body retains specialised cells that can recognise that pathogen for years or even decades and mount a much faster response if it encounters it again. That is literally the biological mechanism underlying vaccination, which I had known about abstractly but had never truly understood at the cellular level.

The experience was significant because it changed the way I think about my own body. I stopped seeing my immune system as something vague and passive and began understanding it as a remarkably sophisticated defence network with its own memory and learning capacity. It also gave me a much more grounded appreciation of why vaccines work, which felt particularly relevant given how much public debate there has been about them in recent years.

## PART 3 — Discussion

### Theme: Technology, Healthcare, and Science

**1. How has modern technology changed the way doctors diagnose illnesses?**

*Model answer:* The change has been profound. Fifty years ago, diagnosis relied almost entirely on a doctor's observational skill, experience, and basic tests. Today, imaging technologies like MRI and CT scanners allow clinicians to visualise the body's internal structures with extraordinary clarity, and blood tests can detect hundreds of biomarkers simultaneously. Increasingly, artificial intelligence systems trained on millions of medical images are being used to detect cancers and other conditions at early stages, sometimes more accurately than experienced human specialists.

**2. Do you think artificial intelligence will eventually replace human doctors?**

*Model answer:* I doubt it will replace them entirely, though it will certainly transform the role. AI excels at pattern recognition in large datasets — identifying a tumour in a scan, flagging an unusual result in a blood test — but medicine involves far more than pattern recognition. It requires listening carefully to a patient's description of their experience, understanding their values and fears, making ethical judgements in uncertain situations, and offering genuine human empathy. Those capacities are not easily replicated algorithmically.

**3. What are the main advantages of using AI and technology in healthcare?**

*Model answer:* The most significant advantage is probably the potential to extend high-quality diagnostics to settings that currently lack access to specialist expertise. In many parts of the world, there simply are not enough radiologists or pathologists to analyse medical images. AI systems that can perform these analyses reliably could democratise access to accurate diagnosis in ways that would represent a genuinely transformational public health improvement. Speed and consistency — AI does not become fatigued — are additional advantages in high-volume settings.

**4. Are there any serious risks associated with relying on technology for medical decisions?**

*Model answer:* Several. One is the "black box" problem — some AI diagnostic systems produce accurate results but cannot explain how they reached their conclusions, which makes it difficult for clinicians to know when to trust them and when to question them. Another is the risk of bias embedded in training data: if an AI system was trained primarily on images from certain populations, it may perform less accurately when applied to others. And there is always the risk that over-reliance on technology erodes the clinical skills that remain essential when technology fails.

**5. Should governments invest more public funding in medical research?**

*Model answer:* I think there is a strong case for it, yes. Medical research produces public goods — knowledge, treatments, vaccines — whose benefits extend well beyond any individual patient or company. Private pharmaceutical investment tends to concentrate on conditions where large commercial markets exist, which means diseases primarily affecting poor populations are systematically underfunded. Public investment can target these gaps and can also fund basic scientific research — the kind with no immediate commercial application — that forms the foundation for future breakthroughs.

**6. How might continuing advances in medical technology affect life expectancy over the coming decades?**

*Model answer:* The trajectory is likely upward, though probably not uniformly. Advances in early cancer detection, gene therapy for inherited conditions, and treatments for cardiovascular disease have the potential to significantly extend healthy life expectancy for those with access to them. However, if the social determinants of health — poverty, poor nutrition, environmental pollution — are not addressed, the benefits of medical technology may accrue disproportionately to wealthier populations, potentially widening health inequalities even as average life expectancy rises.

**7. What role should the public play in supporting scientific research?**

*Model answer:* Beyond simply paying taxes that fund research, the public plays a crucial role in clinical trials — without large numbers of volunteers willing to participate in experimental treatments, medical research would advance far more slowly. Public support for scientific institutions, and for science-informed policy-making, is also important. In an era of significant misinformation about health and medicine, an engaged and scientifically literate public provides a foundation for the kind of evidence-based policy decisions that serve population health most effectively.

**8. Do wealthy countries have a moral responsibility to share medical technologies with poorer nations?**

*Model answer:* I believe they do, though the form that responsibility takes is debatable. The COVID-19 pandemic illustrated very starkly what happens when effective vaccines are developed but then hoarded by wealthy countries rather than distributed globally — the virus continued circulating and mutating in unvaccinated populations, generating variants that eventually returned to affect the vaccinated world. Narrow self-interest and genuine global altruism converge on the same conclusion: more equitable distribution of medical technology benefits everyone, not only its recipients.

**9. How important is strong science education for producing the next generation of medical researchers?**

*Model answer:* It is foundational. The researchers who will develop the treatments and diagnostics of the future are currently in schools, and the quality of scientific education they receive will directly influence their capacity to contribute to research as adults. Beyond producing researchers, science education shapes the public's capacity to engage with health information critically — to evaluate claims, understand risk, and make informed decisions. A society with strong science education is better equipped both to conduct medical research and to benefit from it intelligently.

**10. Some argue that too much money is invested in high-technology medicine while basic healthcare remains inaccessible to billions. Do you find this argument persuasive?**

*Model answer:* It raises a genuine tension, and I think it is partly correct. There is something troubling about spending enormous sums on treatments that benefit small numbers of wealthy patients in wealthy countries while millions die from preventable or easily treatable conditions for lack of basic sanitation, clean water, and simple medicines. However, I am reluctant to frame this as a binary choice. The more productive question is how to organise healthcare spending globally so that both the development of advanced treatments and the delivery of basic care to underserved populations are prioritised simultaneously, rather than treating them as competing alternatives.
