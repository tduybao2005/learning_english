## WRITING TASK 1

*You should spend about 20 minutes on this task.*

The table below shows the percentage of electricity generated from renewable sources (solar, wind, hydro, and other renewables) in five countries in 2023.

Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.

*Write at least 150 words.*

---

### Data Table

| Country | Solar (%) | Wind (%) | Hydro (%) | Other Renewables (%) | Total Renewables (%) |
|---------|-----------|----------|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Germany | 12 | 35 | 4 | 5 | 56 |
| China | 6 | 9 | 15 | 3 | 33 |
| USA | 5 | 10 | 7 | 4 | 26 |
| Brazil | 4 | 12 | 62 | 4 | 82 |
| India | 7 | 5 | 10 | 2 | 24 |

---

### Model Answer — Task 1

The table presents data on the proportion of electricity produced from four renewable sources — solar, wind, hydro, and other renewables — in Germany, China, the USA, Brazil, and India in 2023.

Overall, Brazil generated by far the highest share of its electricity from renewable sources at 82%, while India recorded the lowest proportion at 24%. The data also reveal that different countries rely on very different renewable technologies to achieve their totals.

Germany derived the majority of its renewable electricity from wind power, which contributed 35% — the highest wind share of any country in the table. Solar accounted for a further 12%, giving Germany a combined total of 56%, the second highest overall. Brazil, by contrast, was overwhelmingly dependent on hydropower, which alone supplied 62% of its electricity, far exceeding the hydro contributions of all other countries listed.

China and the USA had more moderate overall renewable shares of 33% and 26% respectively, with China relying most heavily on hydro at 15%, while wind was the USA's leading renewable source at 10%. India's total of 24% was similarly distributed across hydro (10%), solar (7%), and wind (5%).

In summary, while Brazil's dominance stems almost entirely from hydropower, Germany's strength lies in wind, and the remaining countries show more balanced distributions at lower overall levels.

## WRITING TASK 2

*You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.*

*Write about the following topic:*

> **Globalisation has made it easier for people to share ideas and cultures across the world. However, some argue it is leading to the loss of local traditions and languages. To what extent do you agree or disagree?**

*Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.*

*Write at least 250 words.*

---

### Model Answer — Task 2

Globalisation has undeniably transformed the way cultures interact, enabling the rapid exchange of ideas, artistic forms, and social practices on a scale unprecedented in human history. While I acknowledge that this process carries genuine risks for local traditions and minority languages, I broadly agree that its overall effect is more enriching than destructive, provided that societies adopt deliberate measures to protect cultural diversity.

Those who argue that globalisation erodes local cultures point to compelling evidence. The worldwide spread of English as the default language of business, technology, and popular culture has placed enormous pressure on smaller languages. UNESCO estimates that approximately half of the world's 7,000 languages may face extinction by 2100, and linguists widely regard this as an irreplaceable loss of human knowledge and diversity. Traditional crafts, ceremonies, and social practices have similarly been displaced as consumer goods and media from dominant economies flood local markets, reshaping tastes and behaviours in ways that often privilege global over local.

However, I would argue that globalisation also creates powerful tools for cultural preservation and innovation. Digital platforms allow indigenous communities to archive endangered languages, produce culturally rooted music and art, and reach audiences far beyond their geographic boundaries. The global popularity of K-pop, Nollywood films, and Latin American telenovelas demonstrates that non-Western cultures can participate actively in global cultural exchange rather than merely receiving it. Hybridity — the blending of global influences with local traditions — frequently produces creative and vital new cultural forms rather than simple replacement.

The critical factor, in my view, is agency. When communities retain the economic resources and institutional support to make choices about their own cultural futures — whether through government language policies, educational initiatives, or digital self-documentation — globalisation becomes a source of expanded possibility rather than homogenisation. Conversely, when communities lack such resources, external cultural pressures can indeed be genuinely threatening.

In conclusion, globalisation is neither inherently destructive nor unconditionally beneficial to local cultures. Its effects depend on the choices and resources of the societies involved. Rather than resisting cultural exchange, societies should invest in the conditions that enable communities to engage with the world on their own terms.
