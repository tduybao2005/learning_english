# IELTS Academic Speaking — Test 20

**Time allowed: 11–14 minutes**

***

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*The examiner will ask you general questions on familiar topics. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

### Topic 1: Traditions and Customs

**Question 1.** Are there any traditional customs in your country that you particularly enjoy?

**Model Answer:** Yes, definitely. One of my favourite traditions is the Lunar New Year celebrations, which involve families gathering for a big meal, giving red envelopes containing money, and decorating homes with flowers and lanterns. I enjoy it because it gives everyone a reason to slow down and spend time with relatives they might not see often throughout the year. There's also a strong sense of community atmosphere in the streets that feels quite special.

---

**Question 2.** Do you think young people in your country still follow traditional customs?

**Model Answer:** It depends on the custom, I think. Some traditions — like festivals and family meals — remain very popular among young people because they're enjoyable social events. Others, such as certain religious rituals or formal dress codes, seem to be fading as younger generations adopt more global lifestyles. I think most young people pick and choose which traditions feel meaningful to them personally rather than following everything automatically.

---

**Question 3.** Have any traditional customs in your country changed over time?

**Model Answer:** Quite a few have changed significantly. Wedding ceremonies are a good example — traditionally they could last several days and involve elaborate rituals, but nowadays many couples opt for shorter, more modern ceremonies that blend traditional and Western elements. The core meaning is still there, but the form has evolved to suit contemporary tastes and busier schedules.

---

**Question 4.** Do you think it is important to preserve cultural traditions? Why or why not?

**Model Answer:** I do think it's important, primarily because traditions carry collective memory and give communities a sense of shared identity. When a tradition disappears entirely, something of a community's history and values is lost with it. At the same time, I don't think preservation means freezing traditions in place — they can evolve and adapt while still maintaining their essential character and meaning.

---

### Topic 2: Language Learning

**Question 5.** How many languages do you speak, and how did you learn them?

**Model Answer:** I speak two languages fluently — my native language and English — and I'm currently learning a third. I acquired my native language naturally at home, and I learnt English through a combination of formal schooling, online resources, and watching films and television series without subtitles. Immersion, even digital immersion, has been by far the most effective method for me.

---

**Question 6.** Do you think learning a foreign language is difficult?

**Model Answer:** It certainly requires consistent effort, but I wouldn't say it's impossibly difficult. The level of difficulty depends enormously on how closely the new language is related to your own. Someone whose first language is Spanish will find Portuguese much easier to learn than, say, Mandarin Chinese, which has a completely different writing system and tonal structure. Motivation and daily practice seem to matter far more than any innate talent.

---

**Question 7.** What do you think is the best way to learn a new language?

**Model Answer:** I believe the most effective approach combines structured study with as much genuine communication as possible. Grammar and vocabulary give you the foundation, but you only truly internalise a language by using it — talking with native speakers, consuming authentic media, and making mistakes in real contexts. Language exchange partners and apps that simulate real conversations have become increasingly valuable tools for learners who can't easily travel abroad.

---

**Question 8.** Do you think it will be necessary to learn foreign languages in the future, given the development of translation technology?

**Model Answer:** That's an interesting question. Translation technology has become remarkably good in recent years, and it may reduce the practical necessity of learning languages for basic communication or business transactions. However, I still think language learning has value beyond pure utility — understanding a language deeply gives you access to a culture's nuances, humour, and worldview in a way that machine translation cannot fully replicate. I doubt technology will make language learning obsolete for those who want genuine cultural understanding.

---

### Topic 3: News and Media

**Question 9.** How do you usually get your news — through newspapers, television, or online sources?

**Model Answer:** Mostly online these days. I follow a few reputable news websites and have notifications set up for major stories, which means I can stay informed throughout the day without having to sit down and watch a scheduled broadcast. I do occasionally watch televised news programmes in the evening if there's a significant event, but print newspapers are something I rarely read now.

---

**Question 10.** Do you think social media is a reliable source of news?

**Model Answer:** Not always, unfortunately. Social media platforms can spread news very quickly, which is useful in breaking situations, but the lack of editorial oversight means that misinformation and sensationalised content travel just as fast as accurate reporting. I tend to use social media as a signal that something is happening, but I always look for confirmation from established news organisations before forming an opinion on the details.

---

**Question 11.** Do you think people today are better or worse informed than in the past?

**Model Answer:** It's genuinely complicated. People today have access to vastly more information than any previous generation, and on that level, they could be considered better informed. But the sheer volume of information — much of it inaccurate or deliberately misleading — makes it harder to distinguish reliable knowledge from noise. I think critical media literacy has become an essential skill that many people, particularly older generations, were never taught.

---

**Question 12.** Has the way you consume news changed in recent years?

**Model Answer:** Yes, quite a bit. A few years ago, I would browse news sites fairly indiscriminately, but I found that this left me feeling anxious and overwhelmed without being particularly well-informed. Now I'm more deliberate — I follow a smaller number of trusted sources, read longer-form analysis rather than just headlines, and try to set aside specific times for reading rather than checking news constantly throughout the day. That approach has made me feel more informed and less stressed.

### Model Answer — Part 2

I'd like to talk about a tradition from my culture called the Mid-Autumn Festival, which I find genuinely fascinating both as a cultural event and as an example of how ancient customs can stay relevant in a modern world.

The Mid-Autumn Festival takes place on the fifteenth day of the eighth month of the lunar calendar, which usually falls in September or October. It's one of the most widely celebrated festivals in East and Southeast Asia, and it centres on the full moon, which in many cultures in the region symbolises reunion, family togetherness, and abundance. The most iconic element of the festival is the mooncake — a dense, rich pastry filled with sweet lotus seed paste or red bean, often containing a salted egg yolk that represents the full moon. Families exchange mooncakes as gifts and gather in the evening to eat them together while admiring the moon.

Children play an especially important role in the celebrations. They carry lit lanterns — traditionally made from paper in the shapes of animals or mythological figures, though these days battery-powered LED lanterns are increasingly common — and parade through streets and parks after sunset. In some regions, dragon dances, lion dances, and fireworks accompany the evening festivities.

What I find most interesting about this tradition is the layering of meanings it contains. On one level, it's a harvest festival with roots in agrarian society's gratitude for abundance. On another, it's deeply bound up with mythology — particularly the story of Chang'e, the moon goddess who drank an immortality elixir and floated to the moon, where she lives to this day. And on yet another level, it's simply a warm, sensory, communal experience that people genuinely look forward to every year. The fact that it has survived urbanisation, modernisation, and the disruptions of the twentieth century says something important about the resilience of cultural traditions that are grounded in human emotions — the longing for family, beauty, and continuity — rather than in any particular material circumstance.

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion

*The examiner will ask discussion questions related to the Part 2 topic. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

**Question 1.** Why do you think cultural traditions are important for communities?

**Model Answer:** Traditions serve several functions simultaneously. They provide a sense of shared identity and historical continuity — a feeling that you belong to something larger than yourself and that connects you to previous generations. They also create regular opportunities for community members to gather and reinforce social bonds. From a practical standpoint, many traditions encode accumulated knowledge about ecology, medicine, or social organisation that can be genuinely valuable. A community that loses its traditions loses not only its past but often a great deal of practical wisdom as well.

---

**Question 2.** Do you think the younger generation values traditions as much as older generations do?

**Model Answer:** Generally speaking, I think younger people tend to engage with traditions more selectively than older generations. They're more likely to participate in traditions that are enjoyable social events and less likely to observe those with formal or religious dimensions that feel remote from their daily lives. This isn't necessarily a rejection of tradition so much as a reinterpretation of it — choosing which elements feel meaningful in a contemporary context. Whether this selective engagement is sufficient to keep traditions alive in any meaningful form is a question reasonable people disagree about.

---

**Question 3.** How has globalisation affected cultural traditions around the world?

**Model Answer:** The effects have been mixed. On the one hand, the spread of global consumer culture, Western entertainment, and the English language has exerted pressure on local traditions, particularly in communities that lack the economic or institutional resources to maintain them. On the other hand, globalisation has also given traditions a wider audience — folk music, traditional textiles, and culinary practices from small communities can now reach global markets and gain international appreciation. Digital technology in particular has enabled communities to document and share their traditions in ways that were simply impossible a generation ago.

---

**Question 4.** Should governments take responsibility for preserving cultural traditions? What can they do?

**Model Answer:** I think governments have an important role to play, though they should act as enablers rather than controllers. Practical measures include funding for cultural institutions such as museums, archives, and arts organisations; educational policies that incorporate local history, languages, and traditional arts into school curricula; and legal protections for intellectual property relating to traditional knowledge. France's system of cultural quotas in broadcasting is a well-known example of a more interventionist approach. The risk of heavy government involvement, however, is that it can turn living traditions into museum pieces — artificially preserved forms that communities no longer organically practise or feel connected to.

---

**Question 5.** Why do you think some languages are disappearing faster than others?

**Model Answer:** Several factors determine a language's vulnerability. Languages with small numbers of speakers, particularly those concentrated in a single geographic area, are especially at risk because there is limited redundancy — if that community is displaced or assimilated, the language may vanish with it. Economic incentives play a major role too: if speaking a dominant language provides significantly better employment and educational opportunities, parents may choose to raise their children in that language rather than their traditional one, and within a generation or two the heritage language can be lost. Political suppression historically accelerated language loss in many contexts — colonial policies that punished children for speaking indigenous languages had devastating long-term effects that some communities are still trying to recover from.

---

**Question 6.** Do you think it is possible to revive a language that is nearly extinct?

**Model Answer:** It's extremely difficult but not impossible. Hebrew is the most celebrated example of a language that was successfully revived — it went from being primarily a liturgical and scholarly language to the everyday spoken language of an entire nation over the course of the twentieth century. Welsh and Irish have also experienced significant revitalisation efforts, with mixed but encouraging results. The key factors seem to be strong community will, sustained government support, and the creation of contexts — schools, media, government services — where the language is genuinely used rather than merely studied. Without those conditions, revival efforts tend to remain symbolic rather than transformative.

---

**Question 7.** How does the loss of a language affect a community's culture?

**Model Answer:** The loss is profound and multidimensional. A language is not simply a communication system — it encodes a particular way of categorising and understanding the world. Many languages contain vocabulary for ecological, social, or psychological phenomena that have no direct equivalent in dominant languages, and when those words are lost, the concepts they express become harder to think and communicate. Stories, songs, ceremonies, and bodies of oral knowledge that exist only in a particular language are also lost or severely diminished when the language disappears. Many linguists describe language death as comparable to losing a library — an irreversible impoverishment of human knowledge and imagination.

---

**Question 8.** Do you think cultural exchange between nations is generally positive?

**Model Answer:** On balance, yes. Throughout history, contact between cultures has been one of the primary drivers of innovation — in art, science, cuisine, technology, and philosophy. The Renaissance was fertilised by the rediscovery of classical Greek and Roman thought; the scientific revolution drew on mathematical knowledge from the Islamic world; jazz emerged from the encounter of African musical traditions with European harmonic structures. The concern is not cultural exchange itself but the conditions under which it occurs. When exchange happens between roughly equal parties with genuine mutual interest, it tends to be generative. When it happens under conditions of economic dominance or political coercion, the results are more unequal and potentially more damaging to the less powerful party.

---

**Question 9.** Some people argue that a globalised world will eventually have one shared culture. Do you agree?

**Model Answer:** I find that scenario quite unlikely, and I think the evidence of the past few decades supports my scepticism. Despite intense globalising pressures, cultural diversity has proved remarkably persistent. People continue to speak thousands of languages, observe distinct religious and social traditions, and produce culturally specific literature, music, and visual art. In fact, in many parts of the world, globalisation has provoked a counter-reaction in the form of renewed interest in local identity and heritage. The desire to belong to a particular community with its own history and character seems to be a robust and enduring feature of human psychology that no amount of economic integration is likely to eliminate entirely.

---

**Question 10.** What advice would you give to a young person who wants to help preserve their community's cultural traditions?

**Model Answer:** I would suggest starting with genuine curiosity rather than obligation — seek out the traditions that personally resonate with you, ask older community members to share their knowledge and stories, and document what you learn through whatever medium you're most comfortable with, whether that's writing, photography, audio recording, or video. Beyond personal engagement, connecting with organisations working on cultural preservation can amplify individual effort significantly. And perhaps most importantly, find ways to make the tradition relevant and enjoyable for your own peers — traditions that feel like living, breathing expressions of identity will survive; those that feel like obligations imposed from above generally will not.
