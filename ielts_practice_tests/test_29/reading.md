## PASSAGE 1: The Global Rise of Obesity

Over the past four decades, obesity has transformed from a condition primarily associated with affluent nations into a worldwide health crisis affecting populations across every income level. The World Health Organization reports that global obesity rates have nearly tripled since 1975, with more than 650 million adults classified as obese in 2016. This dramatic escalation has prompted researchers and policymakers alike to investigate its underlying causes and potential solutions.

Diet is widely acknowledged as the primary driver of obesity. The widespread availability of ultra-processed foods — products that are high in calories, refined sugars, saturated fats, and sodium, yet low in nutritional value — has fundamentally altered eating patterns worldwide. Fast-food chains, once confined largely to North America, now operate on every inhabited continent, and sugary beverages have become a staple in households that previously relied on water or traditional drinks. Portion sizes have also grown substantially over the decades, with restaurant servings in many countries now two to three times larger than those typical in the 1970s.

Physical inactivity compounds the problem. Modern occupations increasingly demand prolonged sitting, while labour-saving technologies — from automobiles to escalators to remote controls — have reduced the energy people expend in daily life. Leisure time, too, has shifted away from physically demanding activities toward sedentary pursuits such as watching television and using smartphones. Studies indicate that adults in high-income countries walk on average 30% fewer steps per day than their counterparts did two generations ago.

Socioeconomic factors play an equally significant role. Healthy foods such as fresh fruits, vegetables, and lean proteins are often more expensive and less accessible than calorie-dense alternatives, particularly in low-income urban neighbourhoods known as "food deserts." Furthermore, stress — which is disproportionately experienced by those in poverty — triggers the release of the hormone cortisol, which stimulates appetite and promotes fat storage, particularly around the abdomen.

The health consequences of obesity are severe and far-reaching. Individuals with obesity face significantly elevated risks of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, certain cancers, sleep apnoea, and joint disorders. Mental health is also adversely affected; depression and anxiety are markedly more prevalent among people with obesity, partly due to societal stigma. Economically, obesity costs healthcare systems hundreds of billions of dollars annually and reduces workforce productivity through absenteeism and premature death.

Governments have begun responding with a mixture of public health campaigns, taxation on sugary products, restrictions on junk-food advertising directed at children, and urban planning initiatives designed to encourage walking and cycling. Some jurisdictions have reformulated school meal programmes to reduce fat and sugar content. While these interventions show promise, experts caution that no single policy is sufficient. Effective action requires coordinated efforts spanning education, healthcare, urban design, and food regulation — a recognition that obesity is a systemic issue demanding systemic solutions.

## PASSAGE 2: Antibiotic Resistance: A Looming Crisis

**A**
Since Alexander Fleming's discovery of penicillin in 1928, antibiotics have been the cornerstone of modern medicine. They have made routine surgeries safe, enabled cancer chemotherapy, and saved countless lives from previously fatal bacterial infections. However, decades of overuse and misuse have triggered an evolutionary response in bacteria that now threatens to render these life-saving drugs ineffective.

**B**
Antibiotic resistance occurs when bacteria evolve mechanisms to withstand the drugs designed to kill them. This happens through natural selection: when a population of bacteria is exposed to an antibiotic, most die, but a small number carrying resistance genes survive and reproduce, passing on their resistance to future generations. Horizontal gene transfer — a process by which bacteria share genetic material across species — accelerates this spread dramatically. Today, some strains of bacteria have become resistant to virtually all available antibiotics, earning them the label "superbugs."

**C**
The causes of the resistance crisis are multiple and interconnected. In human medicine, antibiotics are frequently prescribed for viral infections such as the common cold or influenza, against which they are entirely ineffective. Patients often fail to complete the full course of treatment when they begin to feel better, leaving behind partially resistant bacteria. In agriculture, antibiotics are administered to livestock not merely to treat disease but also to promote growth — a practice that has introduced antibiotic residues into food chains and waterways, selecting for resistant bacteria in environmental ecosystems far removed from clinical settings.

**D**
The pharmaceutical industry has done little to fill the pipeline with new antibiotics. Developing a new antibiotic is costly and time-consuming, and the return on investment is poor because resistance can emerge within years of a drug's introduction. Major pharmaceutical companies have largely abandoned antibiotic research in favour of more profitable chronic-disease medications. Governments and international organisations are now attempting to incentivise research through grants, market guarantees, and prizes, but the pipeline remains dangerously thin.

**E**
The human toll of antibiotic resistance is already substantial. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention estimates that antibiotic-resistant infections affect nearly 3 million people in the United States alone each year, resulting in approximately 35,000 deaths. Globally, a 2019 study attributed 1.27 million deaths directly to antibiotic-resistant infections, with millions more indirectly caused by the failure of standard treatments. Projections suggest that, without intervention, drug-resistant infections could kill 10 million people annually by 2050.

**F**
Addressing the crisis requires action on multiple fronts. Stewardship programmes in hospitals seek to ensure that antibiotics are prescribed only when genuinely needed and that the correct drug, dose, and duration are selected. Rapid diagnostic tests that distinguish bacterial from viral infections can reduce unnecessary prescribing. In agriculture, several countries have enacted legislation to restrict the non-therapeutic use of antibiotics in livestock. International surveillance networks monitor resistance patterns to guide treatment guidelines and alert health authorities to emerging threats. Perhaps most critically, public awareness campaigns aim to educate patients not to demand antibiotics for self-limiting illnesses and to always complete prescribed courses.

## PASSAGE 3: Mental Health in the Workplace: Breaking the Stigma

For much of the twentieth century, mental health was treated as a strictly private matter, discussed behind closed doors if at all. The workplace was considered an especially inappropriate setting for any disclosure of psychological difficulty. Employees who acknowledged struggling with depression, anxiety, or burnout risked being viewed as weak, unreliable, or professionally unsuitable. This culture of silence, far from protecting workers, served instead to amplify suffering — and, as mounting evidence now shows, to inflict significant harm on businesses as well.

The scale of the problem is considerable. The World Health Organization estimates that depression and anxiety disorders cost the global economy approximately US$1 trillion per year in lost productivity. In the United Kingdom, mental ill-health accounts for 51% of all long-term work absences, and a survey by the mental health charity Mind found that one in six workers experiences a mental health problem at any given time. In the United States, the American Institute of Stress reports that workplace stress causes around 120,000 deaths annually and accounts for US$190 billion in healthcare costs. These figures suggest that treating workplace mental health as a peripheral concern is not only ethically indefensible but also economically irrational.

Awareness has grown substantially in recent years, driven by a convergence of factors. High-profile public figures — athletes, celebrities, and executives — have spoken openly about their mental health struggles, normalising conversations that were previously taboo. Research linking psychological wellbeing to productivity, creativity, and staff retention has made the business case for investment increasingly compelling. Legislative changes in several countries now require employers to assess and mitigate workplace mental health risks alongside physical safety hazards, embedding mental health into mainstream occupational health practice.

Despite these positive developments, significant barriers remain. Stigma has not disappeared; it has simply become subtler. Many employees fear that disclosing a mental health condition will damage their career prospects, lead to being passed over for promotion, or result in being managed out of an organisation. These fears are not unfounded: studies in multiple countries have documented that people who disclose mental health conditions at work experience discrimination at rates comparable to those faced by people with physical disabilities. Managerial attitudes are critical; research consistently finds that the single most influential factor in whether employees seek help is the behaviour of their immediate supervisor.

Organisations that have successfully cultivated mentally healthy workplaces typically combine structural and cultural interventions. Structurally, they offer employee assistance programmes, access to counselling services, flexible working arrangements, and clearly defined workload management policies. Culturally, they train managers to recognise signs of distress, facilitate open conversations about wellbeing, and lead by example — with senior leaders openly acknowledging that mental health is a legitimate concern. Some organisations have appointed dedicated mental health champions or first-aiders who act as confidential points of contact for colleagues in difficulty.

The evidence suggests that investment in workplace mental health delivers measurable returns. Studies published in peer-reviewed journals consistently show that every dollar invested in treatment for common mental disorders yields a return of four dollars in improved health and productivity. Reduced absenteeism, lower staff turnover, and diminished presenteeism — the phenomenon of employees attending work while insufficiently well to perform effectively — all contribute to this return. The conclusion seems clear: addressing mental health in the workplace is not a charitable gesture but a strategic imperative, one that benefits employees and employers alike.
