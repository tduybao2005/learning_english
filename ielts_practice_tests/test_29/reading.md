## READING PASSAGE 1

**The Amazon Rainforest**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 1–13, which are based on Reading Passage 1.*

**A** The Amazon rainforest is the largest tropical rainforest on Earth, covering approximately 5.5 million square kilometres across nine countries in South America, with roughly 60 per cent lying within Brazil. Scientists estimate that the Amazon is home to around 10 per cent of all species on the planet, including approximately 40,000 plant species, 1,300 bird species, and three million animal species, many of which remain undiscovered. This extraordinary concentration of biodiversity has led researchers to describe the Amazon as the lungs of the Earth — though this label is contested, since the forest produces and consumes oxygen in roughly equal measure through respiration and photosynthesis.

**B** What is less disputed is the Amazon's critical role as a carbon sink. The forest stores an estimated 150 to 200 billion tons of carbon in its vegetation and soils, and actively absorbs approximately 2.2 billion tons of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere each year. This absorption function makes the Amazon one of the world's most important buffers against accelerating climate change. When trees are cut down or burned, the stored carbon is released back into the atmosphere as carbon dioxide, effectively transforming the forest from a carbon sink into a carbon source. Some recent studies suggest that heavily deforested parts of the eastern Amazon have already crossed this threshold, releasing more carbon than they absorb.

**C** Deforestation remains the most serious threat to the Amazon. Cattle ranching is by far the leading driver, accounting for approximately 80 per cent of all forest clearance in the Brazilian Amazon. Soy cultivation and commercial logging are also significant contributors. The expansion of both activities has been driven by growing global demand for beef and animal feed. Infrastructure development — particularly road building into previously inaccessible areas — has also accelerated forest loss by opening new frontier zones to settlers and agribusiness operations.

**D** Scientists have identified a tipping point, estimated to occur when between 17 and 20 per cent of the Amazon has been deforested, beyond which the forest may begin an irreversible process of degradation. Below a certain tree density, the rainforest cannot generate the moisture it needs to sustain its own rainfall cycle; without sufficient rainfall, vast areas of dense forest would gradually transform into a dry savanna-like ecosystem, a process researchers call 'dieback'. Brazil has already lost approximately 17 per cent of its Amazon forest, placing the ecosystem dangerously close to this threshold.

**E** Current deforestation rates remain alarming. According to Brazil's National Institute for Space Research, approximately 11,000 square kilometres of Amazon forest were cleared each year over the most recent five-year period, with significant variation depending on political and regulatory conditions. Enforcement of environmental protection laws weakened considerably during the late 2010s, contributing to a surge in illegal deforestation. Since 2023, however, the Brazilian government has implemented stronger monitoring systems and law enforcement measures that have significantly reduced deforestation rates in the short term.

**F** International efforts to protect the Amazon have taken several forms, including payments for ecosystem services, debt-for-nature swaps, and restrictions on the import of commodities linked to deforestation. The European Union's 2023 Deforestation Regulation, for example, requires companies to certify that products sold in the EU were not produced on recently deforested land. Whether such measures will prove sufficient to protect the Amazon's remaining forests depends, in large part, on the political will of the countries and corporations involved.

## READING PASSAGE 2

**The History of Banking**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 14–26, which are based on Reading Passage 2.*

**A** The concept of banking predates money itself. In ancient Mesopotamia, as far back as 3000 BCE, temples and palaces functioned as the earliest financial institutions, accepting deposits of grain and livestock and issuing loans to farmers and merchants. Clay tablets recovered from the ruins of Ur and Babylon document elaborate credit transactions, complete with interest rates and repayment schedules. These early institutions operated on principles remarkably similar to modern banking: they accepted deposits, extended credit, and served as trusted intermediaries between those with surplus resources and those in need of them.

**B** The transformation of banking from a local convenience into a sophisticated international system began in medieval and Renaissance Italy. The Medici family of Florence established one of the most powerful banking networks of the fifteenth century, with branches in Rome, Venice, London, and Bruges. Two innovations introduced during this period would prove foundational to modern finance. The first was the bill of exchange — a written order allowing merchants to settle debts across borders without the need to physically transport gold, dramatically reducing the risks of long-distance trade. The second was the development of double-entry bookkeeping, a system in which every financial transaction is recorded as both a debit and a credit, providing bankers and merchants with a clear, auditable picture of their financial position.

**C** The establishment of the Bank of England in 1694 marked a decisive shift in the role of banking institutions. Founded to raise funds for the government's war against France, the Bank quickly evolved into the nation's central bank — managing the money supply, serving as a lender of last resort to commercial banks in distress, and issuing standardised banknotes backed by gold. The gold standard, which formally tied the value of national currencies to fixed quantities of gold, provided monetary stability throughout much of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries and underpinned the expansion of global trade during the era of industrialisation.

**D** The fragility of unregulated financial systems was exposed with devastating clarity during the Great Depression of the 1930s. In the United States, thousands of banks collapsed between 1930 and 1933, wiping out the savings of millions of ordinary citizens and deepening the economic catastrophe. In response, the US Congress passed the Glass-Steagall Act of 1933, which erected a strict regulatory wall between commercial banking — deposit-taking and lending — and investment banking — the trading of securities and financial instruments. This separation was intended to prevent banks from gambling with their depositors' money in speculative financial markets.

**E** The latter decades of the twentieth century brought a wave of financial deregulation. The Glass-Steagall Act was repealed in the United States in 1999, allowing commercial and investment banking to merge once again. Banks around the world grew dramatically in size and complexity, developing elaborate financial instruments including mortgage-backed securities — bundles of home loans sold to investors as tradeable assets. When the United States housing market collapsed in 2007, the value of these instruments fell catastrophically, triggering the global financial crisis of 2008. Major financial institutions required government bailouts on an unprecedented scale, and the crisis plunged the world economy into its worst recession since the 1930s.

**F** The 2008 crisis prompted a comprehensive international reform effort. The Basel III regulatory framework, developed by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision, required banks to maintain higher levels of capital as a buffer against losses, limit their exposure to risky assets, and improve the transparency of their financial reporting. These reforms were designed to ensure that banks could withstand future financial shocks without requiring taxpayer-funded rescues. Critics argued, however, that the reforms did not go far enough to address the fundamental structural risks of the modern financial system.

**G** Today's banking landscape has been further transformed by digital technology. Online banking, contactless payments, and mobile financial applications have fundamentally altered how consumers interact with their money. Meanwhile, the emergence of cryptocurrencies — decentralised digital currencies operating without central bank oversight — has prompted governments and regulators to grapple with an entirely new set of challenges to financial stability and consumer protection.

## READING PASSAGE 3

**Augmented and Virtual Reality**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 27–40, which are based on Reading Passage 3.*

**A** Augmented reality (AR) and virtual reality (VR) are two distinct but related technologies that are reshaping the boundaries between the physical world and digital experience. Augmented reality overlays digital information — text, images, three-dimensional objects — onto the user's view of the real world, typically through a smartphone screen or specialised glasses. Virtual reality, by contrast, is fully immersive: the user wears a head-mounted display that blocks out the physical environment entirely and replaces it with a computer-generated three-dimensional world. Where AR enhances reality, VR replaces it.

**B** The hardware required to deliver these experiences has evolved rapidly. Head-mounted displays (HMDs) — the helmets or goggles worn by VR users — have become smaller, lighter, and more powerful, with modern devices capable of rendering high-resolution images at frame rates sufficient to produce convincing three-dimensional environments. Haptic gloves, which allow users to 'feel' virtual objects through vibrations and resistance mechanisms, add a further sensory dimension. Motion-tracking systems, using cameras or infrared sensors, translate the user's physical movements into corresponding actions within the virtual environment. Together, these technologies create a sense of 'presence' — the subjective experience of actually being inside the simulated world.

**C** The applications of AR and VR span an extraordinary range of fields. In the gaming industry, VR has enabled developers to create deeply immersive environments in which players experience action from a first-person perspective, moving through virtual spaces rather than viewing them on a flat screen. The global VR gaming market was valued at over four billion US dollars in 2023 and is projected to grow significantly over the coming decade. In medical training, both technologies have been adopted as teaching tools: AR systems allow trainee surgeons to practise procedures by overlaying anatomical diagrams on physical models or cadavers, while VR enables practitioners to rehearse complex operations in risk-free simulated environments.

**D** Architecture and engineering firms have embraced AR and VR as design and presentation tools. Architects can use VR headsets to walk clients through virtual representations of buildings that do not yet exist, allowing design modifications to be made before construction begins and reducing the cost of errors. Military forces in several countries use VR-based simulations for training soldiers in combat scenarios, tactical decision-making, and the operation of complex weapons systems. In education, VR has been deployed to take students on virtual field trips to historical sites, the surfaces of distant planets, or the interior of the human cell — experiences impossible to replicate in a conventional classroom.

**E** One of the most clinically significant applications of VR is in the field of therapeutic medicine. Exposure therapy for phobias — an established psychological treatment in which patients are gradually exposed to the source of their fear in a controlled environment — can be delivered through VR with a precision and repeatability that real-world exposure cannot match. Patients with a fear of heights, for example, can be exposed to escalating virtual scenarios at a pace tailored to their individual progress. VR has also shown promising results in the treatment of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), particularly among combat veterans, enabling clinicians to recreate and process traumatic memories in a safe and controlled setting.

**F** Despite its considerable promise, the widespread adoption of VR and AR faces significant obstacles. The cost of high-quality head-mounted displays remains prohibitive for many consumers and smaller educational institutions. Motion sickness — a condition triggered in susceptible users by a mismatch between the visual input from the VR system and the physical signals from the inner ear — affects a substantial proportion of users and limits the duration of comfortable VR sessions. Critics have also raised concerns about the social and psychological effects of extended immersion in virtual environments, including the risk of isolation from real-world relationships and the blurring of boundaries between virtual and physical experience.

**G** The concept of the metaverse — a persistent, interconnected network of virtual spaces where users can work, socialise, play, and conduct commerce — represents the most ambitious vision for the future of immersive technology. Proponents argue that the metaverse will revolutionise collaboration, entertainment, and commerce, creating economic opportunities on a scale comparable to the early internet. Sceptics, however, question whether demand for such deep digital immersion genuinely exists at mass scale, point to the enormous infrastructure investment required to sustain such environments, and warn of risks including the concentration of power in the hands of a small number of technology companies. Whether the metaverse will transform everyday life or remain a niche technology for enthusiasts remains to be seen.
