# IELTS Practice Test 29 — Speaking

*Time allowed: 11–14 minutes*

---

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*The examiner will ask you general questions on familiar topics. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

### Topic 1: The Environment and Nature

**Question 1.** Do you enjoy spending time in nature?

**Model Answer:** Yes, very much so. Whenever I can, I try to get out of the city and spend time somewhere green — a forest, a park, or near a body of water. I find it genuinely restorative. After a week of staring at screens, even a few hours outdoors seems to clear my head and improve my mood considerably.

---

**Question 2.** Are you concerned about environmental issues such as deforestation or climate change?

**Model Answer:** I am, quite deeply. I follow the news about major forests like the Amazon, and the scale of what's being lost is alarming. I think most people my age are more aware of these issues than previous generations were, partly because we can observe the effects — extreme weather events, biodiversity loss — within our own lifetimes. Whether that awareness translates into meaningful political action is another question entirely.

---

**Question 3.** What do you think individuals can do to help protect the environment?

**Model Answer:** The honest answer is that individual choices matter less than systemic change — reducing flights or eating less meat is worthwhile, but the most significant changes require policy decisions by governments and corporations. That said, individual action shapes social norms and signals to decision-makers that people care. Voting, supporting environmental organisations, and making sustainable choices where feasible all play a role in building the pressure for bigger change.

---

**Question 4.** Have you noticed environmental changes in your local area over the years?

**Model Answer:** Definitely. Where I grew up, patches of woodland nearby have since been cleared for housing. And the weather seems noticeably less predictable than it was when I was a child — summers feel hotter and drier, and intense rainfall events seem more frequent. It is difficult to attribute any single event to climate change, but the overall pattern is difficult to dismiss.

---

### Topic 2: Money and Banking

**Question 5.** Do you prefer to pay for things in cash or by card?

**Model Answer:** Mostly by card these days — it is simply more convenient, and many places no longer accept cash. I keep a small amount of cash for emergencies, but for everyday transactions a contactless payment is much faster. I have also started using mobile payment apps, which feel even more seamless. The shift has happened gradually but feels quite complete now.

---

**Question 6.** Do you think it is important to save money?

**Model Answer:** Absolutely, though the ability to save is often easier said than done, particularly for young people facing high living costs and modest starting salaries. In principle, savings provide security — a buffer against unexpected expenses or job loss — and open up opportunities that would otherwise be out of reach. In practice, building a savings habit takes both discipline and surplus income, which is not always available at the start of one's working life.

---

**Question 7.** Have you ever thought about how the banking system works?

**Model Answer:** I find it genuinely fascinating. The concept that banks do not simply hold your money but lend most of it out — creating new money in the process — is counterintuitive when you first encounter it. I read about the history of banking at university, from the Medici family's innovations in Renaissance Florence to the 2008 financial crisis, and found the ways in which financial systems have shaped and occasionally destabilised entire societies quite compelling reading.

---

**Question 8.** Do you think online banking is safe?

**Model Answer:** Reasonably so for most everyday purposes, provided users take basic precautions such as strong passwords and two-factor authentication. Banks invest heavily in cybersecurity, and regulations generally ensure customers are protected against fraud. The concern that interests me more is the systemic risk created by concentrating so much financial infrastructure in a small number of institutions and technology companies — that is a vulnerability individual caution cannot address.

---

### Topic 3: Gaming and Technology

**Question 9.** Do you play video games? Why or why not?

**Model Answer:** Occasionally, yes. I would not describe myself as a dedicated gamer, but I have played enough to appreciate the medium — especially games with strong narrative elements or complex strategy. What I try to avoid are the more deliberately addictive design patterns that many games use to keep players engaged indefinitely. I find those less enjoyable once you recognise what they are doing.

---

**Question 10.** Have you ever tried virtual reality technology?

**Model Answer:** Yes, once at a technology exhibition. I spent about twenty minutes in a VR headset exploring a virtual underwater environment. It was genuinely impressive — the sense of depth and spatial presence was quite convincing, and I instinctively ducked when a virtual fish swam toward my face. I did notice a slight feeling of disorientation when I removed the headset, which I understand is common for first-time users.

---

**Question 11.** Do you think technology has generally improved people's lives?

**Model Answer:** On balance, yes — though the benefits have been very unevenly distributed and the costs are real. Improvements in healthcare, communication, and access to information that modern technology has enabled are genuinely transformative. At the same time, the attention economy, automation-driven job displacement, and the environmental footprint of digital infrastructure are legitimate concerns. Technology tends to amplify existing social conditions rather than inherently improving or worsening them.

---

**Question 12.** Do you think people spend too much time looking at screens?

**Model Answer:** Many people do, myself included at times. Social media platforms and entertainment apps are deliberately engineered to maximise engagement, which in practice means maximising time spent. There is growing evidence that excessive passive screen consumption is associated with reduced well-being. The difficulty is that work, social life, and access to information are all increasingly screen-mediated, so simply cutting back is not straightforward for most people.

*The examiner will give you a task card. You have one minute to prepare, then speak for 1–2 minutes.*

---

### Cue Card

**Describe a memorable experience you had outdoors in nature.**

*You should say:*
- where you were
- what you were doing
- who you were with
- **and explain** why it was memorable.

---

### Model Answer — Part 2

I would like to describe a hiking trip I took about two years ago with a group of close friends over a long weekend.

We were in a mountainous national park several hours from the city — an area known for its old-growth forest and a network of trails running through valleys and along exposed ridge lines. We had set out early in the morning aiming for a viewpoint roughly twelve kilometres in. The walk took us through several distinct ecosystems: dense forest where the canopy blocked most of the light, open rocky stretches with long views across the valley, and finally a high point where the tree line ended and we stood in open grassland with a panoramic view of the surrounding peaks.

What made the experience particularly memorable was a moment in the late afternoon, about halfway back to the trailhead. We stopped to rest beside a small stream. The light was low and golden, filtering through the trees and reflecting off the water. There was almost no sound apart from the current and occasional birdsong. We had been talking continuously all day, but at that moment everyone fell quiet simultaneously — not awkwardly, but in a way that felt entirely natural.

I think what stays with me is the sense of scale and perspective that comes from being in genuinely wild places. The forest had existed for centuries and would outlast all of us. There is something grounding about that realisation that is very difficult to access in an urban environment, and it is the feeling I find myself returning to mentally whenever daily life feels disproportionately stressful.

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion

*The examiner will ask discussion questions related to the Part 2 topic and broader themes. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

**Question 1.** Do you think immersive technologies like VR could ever replace real outdoor experiences?

**Model Answer:** I do not think they could fully replace them, at least not in any psychologically meaningful sense. VR can replicate the visual and, to some extent, the auditory experience of a natural environment, and there is evidence that even virtual nature exposure has some restorative benefits. But the physical sensations — the temperature of the air, the feel of uneven ground, the smell of vegetation, the genuine unpredictability of the environment — are things current technology cannot replicate. The emotional weight of being truly out in nature comes partly from vulnerability and effort that a simulation removes.

---

**Question 2.** How might VR and AR technologies change education in your country over the next twenty years?

**Model Answer:** The impact will be significant but uneven. In well-resourced institutions, VR could transform science and history education — imagining studying the human circulatory system from the inside, or walking through ancient Rome as it appeared two thousand years ago. AR could make engineering and design far more interactive and practical. The concern is equity: expensive technology tends to widen the gap between well-funded and under-resourced schools. If these technologies are genuinely going to transform education, the policy challenge is ensuring access is broad rather than concentrated in elite institutions.

---

**Question 3.** Are there any risks in using VR with young children?

**Model Answer:** Yes, several. The most immediate is physical — the visual demands of current VR hardware are not well suited to developing eyes, and extended use may cause eye strain or discomfort. Motion sickness is also more prevalent in children than adults. Beyond the physical, there are developmental questions about whether extended time in virtual environments might impede children's ability to navigate real social situations and tolerate ambiguity. I think VR use with younger children should be approached cautiously and grounded in clear evidence rather than enthusiasm for the technology itself.

---

**Question 4.** What is the environmental cost of digital technologies, and do people think about this enough?

**Model Answer:** Probably not enough. The environmental footprint of digital infrastructure is substantial and growing — data centres consume enormous quantities of electricity, often from fossil fuels; manufacturing electronic devices requires mining rare-earth minerals with significant environmental and human costs; and the disposal of electronic waste is a poorly managed problem globally. The intangible nature of digital consumption — streaming a film feels nothing like burning fuel — tends to make its environmental costs invisible. Building better intuitions about those costs is an important part of environmental literacy that most digital education overlooks entirely.

---

**Question 5.** How has the internet changed the way people manage their finances?

**Model Answer:** Profoundly. The most visible change is convenience — paying bills, transferring money, and comparing financial products can all be done instantly on a phone. But the changes run deeper. The internet has democratised access to financial information and investment products previously available only to the wealthy or financially sophisticated. At the same time, it has created new risks: online fraud, predatory financial products marketed through digital channels, and the growth of volatile speculative assets accessible to retail investors with limited financial literacy.

---

**Question 6.** Should financial institutions be regulated more strictly to prevent crises like the one in 2008?

**Model Answer:** I think the answer is yes, though the form that regulation should take is genuinely complex. The 2008 crisis demonstrated that financial institutions, left to pursue short-term profits without adequate oversight, can generate systemic risks that harm millions of people with no direct involvement in financial markets. The Basel III reforms were a step in the right direction, but many economists argue they were insufficient. Stronger capital requirements, limits on leverage, and greater transparency in complex instruments seem like sensible priorities that most jurisdictions have not fully implemented.

---

**Question 7.** Do you think a global regulatory framework for banking is necessary?

**Model Answer:** Probably necessary in principle, though very difficult to achieve in practice. Financial markets are global but regulation is national, which creates opportunities for regulatory arbitrage — institutions moving risky activities to jurisdictions with the weakest rules. The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision represents an attempt at international coordination, but compliance is ultimately voluntary. A genuinely binding global framework would require a level of international cooperation that has proved elusive in most policy domains, particularly when major financial centres benefit economically from maintaining permissive regulatory environments.

---

**Question 8.** How do you think cryptocurrencies might change the financial system?

**Model Answer:** The honest answer is that long-term impact remains genuinely uncertain. Cryptocurrencies have so far functioned primarily as speculative assets rather than as the stable, decentralised currencies their proponents envisioned. The more significant development may be central bank digital currencies — digital versions of national currencies — which several major economies are currently piloting. These could improve payment efficiency and financial inclusion substantially, but also raise important questions about privacy and the extent of state visibility into individuals' financial activity.

---

**Question 9.** Should governments do more to protect natural environments like the Amazon?

**Model Answer:** Absolutely, and they should be supported by international frameworks and economic incentives. The Amazon is a global public good — its carbon sink function and biodiversity matter to the entire planet, not only to the countries where it lies. The challenge is that the economic pressures driving deforestation — cattle ranching, soy production, infrastructure development — generate real livelihoods for many people in the region. Effective protection requires not just regulation and enforcement but viable alternatives that allow communities to prosper without forest destruction. Payments for ecosystem services and sustainable land-use programmes are part of that answer.

---

**Question 10.** Do you think technology will ultimately help or harm efforts to protect the natural world?

**Model Answer:** I think it will do both, and the net outcome will depend on political and economic choices rather than technology itself. On the positive side, satellite monitoring has dramatically improved our ability to detect illegal deforestation in real time; renewable energy is providing cleaner alternatives to fossil fuels; precision agriculture may reduce the land required to produce food. On the negative side, the growth of data centres and consumer electronics generates its own environmental footprint, and the economic growth enabled by technology continues to drive resource consumption globally. Technology is a tool, and like most tools, its effects depend on who uses it and to what ends.
