# IELTS Practice Test 29 — Speaking
**Chủ đề: Health & Medicine**

## Part 1: Câu hỏi ngắn

*Giám khảo sẽ hỏi các câu hỏi về những chủ đề quen thuộc. Trả lời tự nhiên, không cần quá dài.*

---

### Chủ đề 1: Health & Fitness (Sức khoẻ & Thể dục)

**1. Do you try to keep fit? How do you do that?**

Yes, I make a conscious effort to stay active. I usually go for a 30-minute jog three or four times a week, and I try to take the stairs instead of the lift whenever possible. Staying physically active helps me manage stress as well.

**2. Have you ever had a period when you were not very healthy?**

Yes, a couple of years ago I went through a phase where I was working extremely long hours and neglecting sleep. I ended up catching every cold that came around and felt constantly drained. That experience taught me that rest is as important as exercise.

**3. Do you think most people in your country lead a healthy lifestyle?**

Honestly, I think it varies a lot. Younger generations in cities are increasingly health-conscious and follow trends like yoga and clean eating. However, many people, particularly those with demanding jobs or limited incomes, struggle to maintain consistently healthy habits.

**4. What do you think is more important — diet or exercise?**

That is a difficult choice because both are essential. If I had to prioritise, I would say diet, since what you eat determines your energy levels, weight, and long-term disease risk more fundamentally than exercise alone. But ideally the two work together.

---

### Chủ đề 2: Food & Diet (Ăn uống & Chế độ dinh dưỡng)

**5. What kinds of food do you usually eat?**

My daily diet is fairly balanced. I typically have rice or noodles as a staple, combined with vegetables, tofu, or fish. I try to minimise red meat and avoid deep-fried food during the week, though I am less strict at weekends.

**6. Have your eating habits changed since you were a child?**

Considerably. As a child, I ate whatever my parents cooked and never thought about nutrition at all. Now I actively read ingredient labels, limit added sugar, and try to include a wider variety of vegetables. I am also much more aware of portion sizes.

**7. Do you think it is important to eat together as a family?**

Absolutely. Shared meals are not just about nutrition; they are a form of social bonding. Family dinners provide an opportunity to talk, catch up, and reinforce a sense of togetherness. Research also suggests that children who eat with their families regularly tend to have healthier dietary patterns.

**8. How popular is eating out in your country?**

Very popular, especially in urban areas. With increasingly busy schedules, many people eat out or order food delivery several times a week. There is also a strong food culture in Vietnam, and dining out is seen as a social activity as much as a practical one.

---

### Chủ đề 3: Sleep & Relaxation (Ngủ & Thư giãn)

**9. How many hours of sleep do you usually get?**

I aim for seven to eight hours, though in reality I often get closer to six on busy days. I have noticed that when I consistently sleep less than seven hours, my concentration and mood suffer noticeably the following day.

**10. Do you have any particular ways to relax after a stressful day?**

I find reading before bed very effective for unwinding. I also enjoy making herbal tea and listening to quiet music. On weekends, spending time in nature — even just a short walk in the park — restores my energy far more effectively than scrolling through my phone.

**11. Do you think people today get enough sleep?**

Generally, no. Modern lifestyles, with their constant digital connectivity and demanding work schedules, are not conducive to adequate rest. Many people treat sleep as something to be sacrificed for productivity, which is counterproductive in the long run given how critical sleep is for cognitive function and immune health.

**12. Is relaxation more important for some jobs than others?**

Certainly. Jobs that involve high cognitive load, emotional labour, or physical danger — such as surgeons, air traffic controllers, or teachers — arguably require more deliberate rest and recovery. That said, chronic stress is harmful in any occupation, so the need to relax is universal, even if the urgency varies.

## Part 2: Cue Card

**Đề bài:**

*Describe a time when you or someone you know recovered from an illness.*

*You should say:*
- *what the illness was*
- *what treatment or care was received*
- *how long the recovery took*
- *and explain how you or that person felt after recovering*

*You have one minute to prepare. Then speak for one to two minutes.*

---

### Bài mẫu (~200 từ)

I would like to talk about my mother's recovery from pneumonia two years ago, which was quite a challenging experience for our whole family.

It started when she developed a persistent cough and high fever that did not improve after a week. After visiting the hospital and undergoing a chest X-ray, she was diagnosed with bacterial pneumonia and was admitted to hospital for five days. The doctors placed her on a course of intravenous antibiotics and monitored her oxygen levels closely. Once she was stable enough to return home, she continued a ten-day course of oral antibiotics and was advised to rest completely.

The full recovery took approximately three weeks. During that period, she was very fatigued and found even mild exertion tiring. Our family took turns looking after her — preparing nutritious meals, ensuring she took her medication on time, and keeping her company since she could not leave the house.

When she finally felt fully recovered, the change in her mood was remarkable. She described it as a profound sense of gratitude — both for modern medicine and for the support of the family. She has since become much more proactive about her own health, getting annual flu vaccinations and attending regular check-ups. The experience reminded all of us not to take good health for granted.

## Part 3: Câu hỏi thảo luận

*Giám khảo sẽ hỏi các câu hỏi trừu tượng hơn liên quan đến chủ đề Part 2.*

---

**1. How do you think healthcare systems should be funded — through taxes or private insurance?**

Both models have their merits, but I believe a predominantly tax-funded system is fairer. Private insurance tends to create a two-tier system where quality of care depends on financial means rather than medical need. Countries like the United Kingdom and Canada demonstrate that universal public health systems can deliver high-quality care while protecting the most vulnerable citizens. That said, a degree of private provision can complement the public system by reducing waiting times for elective procedures.

**2. Do you think people today are more health-conscious than previous generations?**

In many respects, yes. Access to health information through the internet has made people far more aware of nutrition, exercise, and preventive care than their parents or grandparents were. Fitness tracking apps, social media wellness communities, and widespread public health campaigns have all contributed to this shift. However, this awareness coexists with rising rates of obesity and stress-related illness, suggesting that knowledge alone does not automatically translate into healthy behaviour.

**3. What role should governments play in promoting public health?**

Governments have both the authority and the responsibility to create conditions in which healthy choices are the easier choices. This means regulating food labelling, limiting advertising of unhealthy products to children, subsidising preventive care, and designing urban environments that facilitate physical activity. Governments should not dictate personal behaviour, but they should address the structural factors — poverty, food deserts, lack of green spaces — that make healthy living more difficult for disadvantaged populations.

**4. Why do you think many people neglect their health until they become seriously ill?**

There are several reasons. First, many illnesses are largely invisible in their early stages, so people do not receive immediate negative feedback for unhealthy habits. Second, modern life is demanding, and health maintenance can feel like a low priority against work and family obligations. Third, there is a widespread psychological tendency to focus on immediate rewards over long-term wellbeing, which makes resisting unhealthy temptations particularly difficult.

**5. How can employers contribute to the health of their workforce?**

Employers have considerable influence over the daily environments and habits of their employees. Practically, they can offer subsidised gym memberships, healthy canteen options, ergonomic workstations, and access to mental health counselling. Culturally, they can foster a norm where overwork is not glorified and taking breaks, annual leave, and sick days is normalised. Some forward-thinking companies have also introduced mental health days and stress management workshops, recognising that employee wellbeing directly impacts productivity and retention.

**6. Do you think the focus on physical health overshadows mental health?**

Historically, this has certainly been true. Physical illness has always been taken more seriously because its symptoms are more visible and measurable. However, I believe the balance is gradually shifting. The COVID-19 pandemic, in particular, brought mental health into mainstream discourse in a way that was unprecedented. Nevertheless, in many parts of the world — including many Asian countries — mental health remains heavily stigmatised, and people are still reluctant to seek help for psychological difficulties.

**7. What impact has modern technology had on people's health?**

Technology has had both beneficial and detrimental effects. On the positive side, medical technologies — from diagnostic imaging to robotic surgery — have dramatically improved treatment outcomes. Health apps and wearables enable individuals to monitor their own wellbeing more proactively than ever before. On the negative side, excessive screen time, particularly social media use, has been linked to increased anxiety and depression, especially among adolescents. Sedentary technology-driven lifestyles also contribute to rising obesity rates.

**8. Should preventive medicine receive more investment than curative medicine?**

From a public health and economic perspective, the case for prioritising prevention is compelling. Treating a chronic disease such as type 2 diabetes over a lifetime is vastly more expensive than the lifestyle interventions and education that could prevent it. Studies consistently show that every dollar invested in prevention saves multiple dollars in treatment costs. However, curative medicine also needs robust investment because not all illnesses are preventable, and equitable access to treatment is a fundamental matter of human rights.

**9. How does socioeconomic status affect people's health?**

The relationship between poverty and poor health is well-documented and operates through multiple pathways. People with lower incomes have reduced access to nutritious food, safe environments for exercise, quality healthcare, and the information needed to make informed health choices. They also experience higher levels of chronic stress, which has direct physiological effects including elevated blood pressure and weakened immune function. Addressing health inequalities therefore requires addressing social and economic inequalities more broadly.

**10. How can communities raise awareness about mental health?**

Communities can do a great deal. Schools can integrate mental health education into their curricula, normalising conversations about emotions and coping strategies from an early age. Community centres and religious organisations can host open discussions and support groups that destigmatise seeking help. Local media campaigns featuring relatable stories from people who have experienced and recovered from mental health difficulties can be particularly powerful. Peer support networks, where trained volunteers provide initial contact and signposting for people in distress, have also proven effective in bridging the gap between need and professional care.
