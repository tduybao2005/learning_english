### Process Stages

| Stage | Process | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Evaporation | Solar energy heats surface water in oceans, lakes, and rivers; water turns to vapour and rises |
| 2 | Transpiration | Plants release water vapour through their leaves (combined with evaporation = evapotranspiration) |
| 3 | Condensation | Water vapour cools as it rises; condenses around dust particles to form clouds |
| 4 | Precipitation | Water falls as rain, snow, sleet, or hail depending on temperature |
| 5 | Surface runoff | Precipitation flows over land into rivers and streams, returning to oceans and lakes |
| 6 | Infiltration | Some precipitation seeps into the ground, replenishing groundwater aquifers |
| 7 | Collection | Water gathers in oceans, lakes, rivers, and aquifers; cycle begins again |

---

### Model Answer — Task 1

The diagram illustrates the seven key stages of the water cycle, a continuous natural process by which water moves through the Earth's atmosphere, land surface, and underground systems.

The cycle begins with evaporation, whereby solar energy heats surface water in oceans, lakes, and rivers, converting it into water vapour that rises into the atmosphere. Simultaneously, transpiration occurs as plants release water vapour through their leaves; together, these two processes are collectively known as evapotranspiration.

As water vapour rises and cools, it undergoes condensation, clustering around tiny dust particles to form clouds. Subsequently, precipitation takes place, and water returns to the Earth's surface in the form of rain, snow, sleet, or hail, depending on atmospheric temperature.

Once precipitation reaches the ground, it follows two distinct pathways. Some water flows across the land surface as runoff, draining into rivers and streams before returning to oceans and lakes. The remainder seeps downward through a process called infiltration, replenishing underground aquifers.

Finally, water collects in oceans, lakes, rivers, and aquifers, from which the cycle begins again. This process is continuous and self-sustaining, driven primarily by solar energy and gravity.

## WRITING TASK 2

*You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.*

*Write about the following topic:*

> **Virtual reality and augmented reality technologies will transform education and training in the coming decades. To what extent do you agree or disagree?**

*Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.*

*Write at least 250 words.*

---

### Model Answer — Task 2

The idea that virtual and augmented reality will fundamentally reshape education and professional training is one that I largely agree with, though I believe the pace and extent of this transformation will depend on whether significant technological and economic barriers can be overcome.

There is already compelling evidence that immersive technologies enhance learning in ways that conventional methods cannot. In medical education, virtual reality allows trainee surgeons to rehearse complex procedures in risk-free simulated environments, building confidence and technical precision before operating on real patients. In schools, VR-based virtual field trips can transport students to historical sites, the interior of a living cell, or the surface of a distant planet — experiences that no physical classroom can replicate. The opportunity to learn through direct, immersive engagement rather than passive reading represents a genuine pedagogical advance that educators are only beginning to explore.

AR technology similarly holds considerable promise. Architecture students can overlay building designs onto real physical spaces; chemistry students can interact with three-dimensional molecular models at desk level. These tools deepen conceptual understanding by making abstract ideas tangible and spatial in ways that textbooks alone cannot achieve.

However, I would temper this optimism with several practical concerns. High-quality VR equipment remains prohibitively expensive for many schools and training organisations, particularly in the developing world. Motion sickness limits usable session lengths for a significant minority of users. Furthermore, immersive technology should complement — not replace — human mentorship, collaborative dialogue, and the interpersonal skills that remain central to education.

In conclusion, I agree that AR and VR will transform education substantially, but this transformation will be gradual and uneven. Realising their full potential requires sustained investment, equitable access, and pedagogical frameworks that integrate these tools thoughtfully alongside existing approaches.
