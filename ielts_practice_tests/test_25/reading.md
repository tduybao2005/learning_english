## PASSAGE 1

**The Discovery of Tutankhamun's Tomb**

In the autumn of 1922, British archaeologist Howard Carter made what is widely considered the most spectacular archaeological discovery of the twentieth century. After years of painstaking excavation in Egypt's Valley of the Kings, Carter and his team uncovered the entrance to an almost entirely intact royal tomb — that of the young pharaoh Tutankhamun, who ruled Egypt around 1332–1323 BCE.

Carter had been searching the valley for years under the patronage of Lord Carnarvon, a wealthy English aristocrat who had funded the excavation project since 1914. By 1922, Carnarvon was losing patience and had nearly withdrawn his financial support, believing the valley had been exhausted. Carter, however, persuaded him to fund one final season of digging. That decision would change history.

On 4 November 1922, a young boy assisting the excavation team stumbled upon a stone step beneath the sand. Carter immediately ordered the clearing of the area and soon revealed a staircase leading down to a sealed doorway. Sensing the enormity of the find, Carter sent a telegram to Carnarvon in England: "At last have made wonderful discovery in valley; a magnificent tomb with seals intact." Carnarvon rushed to Egypt, and on 26 November the two men made a small hole in the blocked doorway and peered inside. When asked if he could see anything, Carter famously replied, "Yes, wonderful things."

The tomb contained four rooms packed with over 5,000 artefacts, including furniture, clothing, jewellery, weapons, and ritual objects — all in extraordinary condition. The innermost room, known as the burial chamber, contained three nested coffins. The innermost coffin, made of solid gold, housed the mummy of Tutankhamun, wearing the now-iconic golden death mask. The mask, inlaid with lapis lazuli, obsidian, and coloured glass, remains one of the most recognised objects in world history.

The excavation and documentation process took nearly a decade. Carter and his team carefully photographed, catalogued, and removed each item before proceeding further into the tomb. This meticulous approach set a new standard for archaeological practice and ensured that scientific data was preserved alongside the physical objects.

The discovery sparked a worldwide fascination with ancient Egypt, a phenomenon sometimes called "Egyptomania." Museums in Europe and North America mounted exhibitions, and Egyptian motifs influenced fashion, architecture, and design throughout the 1920s and 1930s. The alleged "Curse of the Pharaohs" — a story linking the deaths of several people connected with the excavation to supernatural forces — captured public imagination, though historians attribute the deaths to natural causes, coincidence, or exposure to ancient mould and bacteria.

Tutankhamun himself remains a figure of historical mystery. He became pharaoh at approximately nine years of age and died around eighteen or nineteen. Analysis of his mummy has revealed evidence of multiple disorders, including bone disease and malaria. The precise cause of his death continues to be debated by scholars, though the most widely accepted view is that a combination of genetic disorders and infectious disease proved fatal.

## PASSAGE 2

**The Fall of the Roman Empire: Causes and Consequences**

**A**
Few events in history have attracted as much scholarly debate as the collapse of the Western Roman Empire in 476 CE. This was the moment when the last emperor of the west, Romulus Augustulus, was deposed by the Germanic chieftain Odoacer — a date that historians have long used to mark the end of classical antiquity and the beginning of the Middle Ages. Yet the search for causes has proved enormously complex, with historians proposing dozens of explanations over the centuries.

**B**
One of the most influential theories, advanced by the eighteenth-century historian Edward Gibbon in his monumental work *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*, blamed the rise of Christianity and what Gibbon called the "barbarisation" of the army. According to Gibbon, the Christian emphasis on spiritual rather than earthly concerns sapped the civic virtue and martial spirit that had built the empire. Meanwhile, the increasingly heavy reliance on non-Roman, or "barbarian," troops to fill the legions gradually eroded the loyalty and cohesion that military power required.

**C**
Modern historians have largely rejected Gibbon's religious thesis but retain his attention to military factors. Many scholars emphasise the enormous financial strain of maintaining the empire's borders. Stretched across three continents, the Roman military required a vast and expensive infrastructure. As the cost of defending the frontiers grew, emperors resorted to debasing the currency — reducing the silver content of coins — leading to inflation and economic instability. Tax burdens on ordinary citizens increased dramatically, undermining agriculture and trade.

**D**
Political fragmentation played a central role as well. The third century CE, known as the "Crisis of the Third Century," saw over fifty claimants to the imperial throne in just fifty years, with many emperors ruling for only months before being assassinated or overthrown. This constant instability made coherent long-term governance almost impossible and drained resources that might otherwise have strengthened the empire's defences.

**E**
Climate and disease also contributed significantly. Recent research has highlighted the role of the "Roman Climate Optimum," a period of favourable weather that supported agricultural productivity for centuries, and its eventual deterioration. A series of devastating plagues, including the Antonine Plague of the second century and the Plague of Cyprian in the third, killed millions, reduced the labour supply, weakened the army, and disrupted trade. Some scholars now argue that environmental and epidemiological pressures were at least as important as political or military factors.

**F**
The consequences of Rome's fall reverberated across Europe for centuries. Urban populations collapsed, long-distance trade networks disintegrated, and literacy rates fell sharply as the institutional structures that had supported Roman learning and administration disappeared. Yet the legacy of Rome persisted: Latin evolved into the Romance languages, Roman law shaped the legal systems of much of Europe, and the Catholic Church preserved and transmitted classical knowledge through the monasteries of the early medieval period.

## PASSAGE 3

**Modern Archaeology: Technology Transforming the Past**

For most of its history, archaeology was a discipline defined by the spade. Excavation — the careful physical removal of soil and sediment to expose buried remains — was, and in many ways still is, the central activity of archaeological fieldwork. But over the past few decades, a revolution in technology has dramatically expanded what archaeologists can see, know, and do without ever lifting a trowel. Remote sensing, computational analysis, genetic sequencing, and isotope chemistry are among the tools now reshaping our understanding of ancient civilisations.

One of the most transformative technologies has been LiDAR — Light Detection and Ranging. This airborne scanning method fires millions of laser pulses at the ground from aircraft or drones, recording the time each pulse takes to return. The data can then be processed to strip away modern vegetation and produce extraordinarily detailed three-dimensional maps of underlying topography. In 2018, a LiDAR survey of the Guatemalan jungle revealed the full extent of the ancient Maya civilisation, uncovering more than 60,000 previously unknown structures — roads, palaces, fortifications, and agricultural terraces — beneath the forest canopy. The discovery fundamentally altered archaeologists' understanding of Maya population density and urban complexity.

Ground-penetrating radar (GPR) offers a complementary approach. Rather than surveying from the air, GPR devices are wheeled or dragged across the surface, sending radio waves into the ground and recording the patterns of reflection from buried features. At Stonehenge in England, GPR surveys have revealed dozens of previously unknown monuments, pits, and structures in the surrounding landscape, transforming the site from an isolated standing stone circle into the centre of a much larger ritual complex. Crucially, GPR allows archaeologists to map what lies beneath the surface without disturbing the ground — preserving the stratigraphic record for future investigators.

Ancient DNA analysis has opened an entirely new window onto human prehistory. Advances in sequencing technology now allow scientists to extract and read genetic material from bones and teeth thousands of years old. These studies have revolutionised understanding of prehistoric migration. The sequencing of genomes from ancient European skeletons, for instance, has demonstrated that the continent's population was replaced or substantially transformed at least three times in prehistory — by hunter-gatherers, by early farmers spreading from Anatolia, and by steppe pastoralists from the Pontic-Caspian region. Such findings could not have been inferred from artefacts or architectural remains alone.

Isotope analysis provides yet another avenue of investigation. By measuring the ratios of certain chemical isotopes — particularly strontium, oxygen, and carbon — preserved in teeth and bones, researchers can reconstruct where an individual lived during childhood, what they ate, and even what the climate was like during their lifetime. Analysis of skeletal remains from the Bronze Age site of Stonehenge has shown that some individuals buried there originated from as far away as the Mediterranean, challenging earlier assumptions that prehistoric Britain was an isolated society.

Despite these advances, important ethical questions surround the new archaeology. The analysis of ancient DNA from Indigenous communities has prompted debate about who has the right to study and interpret human remains. Many Indigenous groups argue that ancestral bones and objects should be repatriated rather than subjected to scientific analysis, and an increasing number of archaeologists recognise that community consent and collaboration are essential to responsible practice. Technology may expand the boundaries of knowledge, but it cannot by itself resolve questions of cultural ownership and respect.
