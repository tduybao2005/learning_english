# IELTS Academic Speaking — Test 25

**Time allowed: 11–14 minutes (approximately)**

***

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*The examiner will ask you general questions on familiar topics. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

### Topic 1: Health and Medicine

**Question 1.** Do you take care of your physical health? What do you do to stay healthy?

**Model Answer:** I try to, yes. I make an effort to exercise a few times a week — usually jogging or cycling — and I pay attention to what I eat, though I wouldn't say I follow a strict diet. I think the most important thing for me is getting enough sleep, because when I'm well-rested everything else seems easier to manage. I've also cut down on processed food in the past year, which has made a noticeable difference to my energy levels.

---

**Question 2.** How do you react when you feel ill — do you see a doctor immediately or try to recover at home first?

**Model Answer:** It depends on how serious it is. For minor things like a cold or mild headache, I tend to rest at home, drink plenty of water, and wait it out rather than rushing to a clinic. But if symptoms persist for more than a couple of days, or if something seems genuinely unusual, I wouldn't hesitate to see a doctor. I think a lot of people in my generation are fairly cautious about overusing medical services for things the body can often handle on its own.

---

**Question 3.** Do you think people today are more health-conscious than people were in the past?

**Model Answer:** In many ways, yes. There's far more information available now about nutrition, exercise, and preventive health, and people seem much more aware of the long-term consequences of lifestyle choices like smoking or a sedentary routine. On the other hand, chronic conditions related to stress, poor diet, and lack of physical activity are still extremely common, so I don't think being information-rich automatically translates into being healthier in practice. Awareness and behaviour change are two quite different things.

---

**Question 4.** How important do you think medicine and healthcare are in modern society?

**Model Answer:** Absolutely central, I would say. Modern medicine has transformed human life expectancy and quality of life in ways that are genuinely extraordinary — diseases that were death sentences a century ago are now manageable or even curable. Healthcare systems also have a broader social function in reducing inequality, since access to good medical care has historically been determined by wealth. The recent pandemic underscored just how dependent modern societies are on functioning healthcare infrastructure. Without it, social and economic life breaks down very quickly.

---

### Topic 2: Travel and Countries

**Question 5.** Have you ever travelled abroad? Where did you go?

**Model Answer:** Yes, I have. I've visited a few countries in Southeast Asia, which I found fascinating for their cultural diversity and the contrast between very traditional and very modern environments existing side by side. I also spent some time in Europe, which offered a completely different experience — older architecture, different food traditions, and a slower pace of life in many places. Travelling has genuinely changed the way I see my own country and culture, which I think is one of its most valuable aspects.

---

**Question 6.** What do you think is the most attractive thing about visiting a foreign country?

**Model Answer:** For me, it's the sense of stepping outside the familiar and encountering things you simply wouldn't have predicted. The differences in food, language, social customs, and attitudes can be disorienting at first, but that discomfort is often where the most interesting learning happens. I'm also drawn to landscapes and architecture that are completely unlike what I grew up with — there's something almost overwhelming about seeing a place you've only ever read about or seen in photographs and suddenly being physically present in it.

---

**Question 7.** Do you prefer travelling alone or with other people? Why?

**Model Answer:** Both have their appeal, but I think I marginally prefer travelling with one or two close friends rather than a large group. Solo travel gives you total freedom to set your own schedule and it pushes you to be more resourceful and self-reliant, which is genuinely valuable. But sharing experiences with someone — having a spontaneous conversation about something you've both just witnessed, or laughing together about something that went wrong — creates memories in a different and perhaps richer way. Large group travel, though, tends to slow everything down and turn decision-making into a committee exercise, which I find frustrating.

---

**Question 8.** Is there a country you would particularly like to visit in the future? Why?

**Model Answer:** Japan is at the top of my list, definitely. What draws me to it is the combination of things that seem almost contradictory from the outside — extraordinary technological modernity alongside deeply preserved traditional culture, extremely dense urban environments alongside some of the most serene and carefully tended natural landscapes in the world. I'm also fascinated by Japanese craft traditions, whether in ceramics, food, or architecture, where there's a philosophy of attending to detail and doing things with deliberate care that I find quite moving from a distance.

---

### Topic 3: Technology

**Question 9.** How much time do you spend using your smartphone each day?

**Model Answer:** Probably more than I would ideally like to admit. On an average day I'd estimate somewhere between three and five hours, which includes messaging, reading news, navigating, and various work-related tasks. I've tried to be more intentional about it — I keep my phone out of the bedroom at night and turn off most notifications — but it's genuinely difficult because so many aspects of daily life now funnel through a single device. I think the design of these platforms deliberately makes them hard to put down, which is worth being honest about.

---

**Question 10.** Do you think technology has made life more complicated or simpler?

**Model Answer:** Both, honestly. Technology has removed an enormous amount of friction from everyday tasks — navigating an unfamiliar city, translating a foreign language, accessing almost any piece of information almost instantly — and in that sense life is objectively simpler in many practical ways. But it has also introduced new kinds of complexity: the expectation of constant availability, the overwhelming volume of information and choices, the anxieties around privacy and online reputation. I think whether technology simplifies or complicates life depends enormously on how deliberately and critically you engage with it.

---

**Question 11.** Have you ever worried about the amount of personal data you share with technology companies?

**Model Answer:** Increasingly so, yes. A few years ago I didn't think much about it, but as I've read more about how platforms monetise user data and learned about the scale of information they collect — location histories, browsing patterns, emotional responses — I've become considerably more cautious. I've adjusted privacy settings on my devices, deleted a few apps I didn't really need, and started paying more attention to permissions when installing new ones. I wouldn't describe myself as paranoid about it, but I think a degree of wariness is rational given what we now know about how this data is used.

---

**Question 12.** Do you think children should be taught about digital privacy at school?

**Model Answer:** Absolutely, and ideally from a fairly young age. Children are becoming active users of digital platforms well before they have any understanding of what happens to their data or how to manage their online presence safely. Digital literacy — including an understanding of privacy settings, the permanence of online content, and the commercial motivations behind free platforms — should be as fundamental a part of modern education as reading and mathematics. The fact that this is still treated as optional or supplementary in many school systems is, I think, a significant policy gap.

### Cue Card

**Describe an important technological invention from the past that has changed the world.**

*You should say:*
- what the invention is
- when and by whom it was developed
- how it changed people's lives
- **And explain** why you consider it particularly important.

---

### Model Answer — Part 2

The invention I'd like to talk about is the printing press, developed by the German goldsmith Johannes Gutenberg in around 1440 in the city of Mainz. It's an invention that I find endlessly fascinating, not just because of its immediate practical impact, but because of the cascade of social and intellectual transformations it set in motion over the following centuries.

Before Gutenberg, books in Europe were produced by hand — copied laboriously by monks and scribes, which made them extraordinarily expensive and available only to the wealthy and the clergy. The printing press changed all of that by making it possible to produce hundreds or even thousands of identical copies of a text in a fraction of the time and at a fraction of the cost. Within fifty years of Gutenberg's invention, it's estimated that more than twenty million books were in circulation across Europe — more books than had existed in the entire previous millennium.

The social consequences were profound. The mass availability of printed texts directly enabled the Protestant Reformation of the sixteenth century, as Martin Luther's ideas spread across Europe in a way that would have been impossible without cheap print. It also accelerated the Scientific Revolution, allowing researchers in different countries to read and build on each other's findings in near real time. Perhaps most fundamentally, it began the long process of democratising knowledge — the idea that information does not belong exclusively to a privileged class but should be accessible to anyone who can read.

What I find most remarkable is that Gutenberg's press essentially created the information ecosystem that shaped the modern world. When we think today about debates over who controls information, who profits from its dissemination, and who gets silenced — these are questions with roots that go all the way back to that workshop in Mainz in the 1440s. In that sense, the printing press feels strikingly relevant to the conversations we're having right now about digital platforms and the power of information.

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion

*The examiner will ask discussion questions related to the Part 2 topic. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

**Question 1.** Do you think people are generally aware of how much of their personal data is collected by technology companies?

**Model Answer:** Mostly not, in my experience. Most people have a vague sense that their data is being collected in some way, but the full picture — the granularity of location tracking, the inference of psychological profiles from browsing behaviour, the resale of data to third parties — tends to come as a genuine shock when people encounter it directly. Part of the problem is deliberate design: the terms of service that govern data collection are written in language so dense and lengthy that almost nobody reads them, which creates a kind of structured ignorance. Awareness has improved since regulations like the GDPR began requiring more prominent consent notices, but real understanding remains limited.

---

**Question 2.** Should governments have the right to access citizens' personal data in the name of national security?

**Model Answer:** In principle, yes — targeted surveillance of individuals who are credibly suspected of serious crimes or terrorism seems like a reasonable power for governments to hold, subject to independent judicial oversight. The serious concern is the move from targeted to mass surveillance, which is a qualitatively different proposition. When a government collects the communications metadata of entire populations on the grounds that some individuals might be dangerous, it is treating every citizen as a potential suspect, which is fundamentally incompatible with the presumption of innocence that democratic legal systems are built on. The post-2001 expansion of surveillance powers in the US and UK went far beyond anything that democratic publics had explicitly authorised, which is why the Snowden revelations produced such a strong reaction.

---

**Question 3.** What protections do you think individuals should have over their personal data?

**Model Answer:** Several seem essential. First, meaningful informed consent — not the current model of 80-page terms of service, but genuine clarity about what is being collected and why. Second, purpose limitation: data collected for one purpose should not be repurposed for something completely different without fresh consent. Third, the right of access and deletion, so individuals can find out what is held about them and request its removal when appropriate. Fourth, strong accountability mechanisms — independent regulators with real investigative and sanctioning powers, rather than self-policing by the companies concerned. The GDPR is an attempt to codify several of these principles, and while imperfect, it represents a serious framework that others can build on.

---

**Question 4.** Do you think data privacy regulations like the GDPR are effective at protecting ordinary people?

**Model Answer:** Partially effective, I would say. The GDPR has genuinely raised standards — it has prompted organisations to take data protection more seriously, required more explicit consent notices, and resulted in significant fines for major violations, including a multi-billion-euro fine against Meta. But enforcement has been uneven: regulators in smaller EU countries, which are often the legal base for major tech companies, have been slow and under-resourced. And there are limits to what any regional regulation can achieve when the underlying business model of global platforms is built on data extraction. Regulation is a necessary but not sufficient response — changing the economic incentives that drive data collection in the first place is ultimately more important.

---

**Question 5.** How do you feel about facial recognition technology being deployed in public spaces?

**Model Answer:** I find it deeply troubling, for several reasons. The combination of ubiquitous CCTV and real-time facial recognition effectively ends anonymity in public spaces, which has historically been a crucial precondition for free expression and political assembly. If attending a protest or a meeting can instantly become part of your permanent record — accessible to employers, insurers, or foreign governments — the chilling effect on civil liberties is profound. The documented accuracy disparities are an additional serious concern: a technology that systematically produces higher error rates for Black women, for instance, will produce systematically unjust outcomes if used in law enforcement. I think the burden of proof for deploying such technology in public should be extremely high, and in most current contexts it simply has not been met.

---

**Question 6.** Do you think there is a fair trade-off between convenience and personal privacy in today's digital world?

**Model Answer:** Not currently, no. The trade-off is structurally unfair because it is not transparent or genuinely voluntary. Users are presented with a binary choice: accept the terms or don't use the service, often without being told what those terms actually mean in practice. When a service is so deeply embedded in social and professional life that opting out is effectively not an option — social media being an obvious example — calling it a 'trade-off' that people freely choose is misleading. A genuine trade-off would require real alternatives, clear and comparable information about privacy costs, and meaningful choice. None of those conditions currently exist at scale.

---

**Question 7.** Are younger generations more or less concerned about digital privacy than older people?

**Model Answer:** It's more complex than a simple more or less. Younger people tend to be more technically literate and more aware of how platforms work, which in some cases translates into better privacy practices — using privacy settings more actively, being more selective about what they share. But they have also grown up in an environment where sharing is completely normalised, and the psychological barrier against disclosing personal information online is considerably lower than it is for older generations who remember a world without these platforms. I think younger people are often more aware of the risks but also more fatalistic about them — the sense that surveillance is simply an unavoidable feature of modern life and there's not much point worrying about it.

---

**Question 8.** What responsibility should technology companies bear for protecting users' personal information?

**Model Answer:** A very substantial one, in my view — far greater than most have voluntarily accepted. If a company collects personal data as its primary economic input, it has a duty of care towards the people whose data it holds that is analogous to the duty a bank holds towards depositors. This means implementing genuine security measures, being transparent about breaches promptly, minimising the data collected to what is genuinely necessary, and refraining from selling or sharing data in ways that users would not reasonably expect or accept. The current model — collect everything, secure it inadequately, and wait to see what regulators will actually enforce — is not an ethical position. Companies that profit from data should bear proportionate liability for the harms when it is misused or exposed.

---

**Question 9.** Do you think the concept of the 'right to be forgotten' is a reasonable and workable idea?

**Model Answer:** Reasonable in principle, complex in practice. The underlying idea — that people should not be permanently defined by outdated or minor mistakes that resurface every time someone searches their name — reflects a genuinely important value. Human beings have always had the ability to move on from their past in ways that the internet, with its perfect memory, currently makes impossible. The practical difficulty is drawing the line between information that primarily affects the individual and information that the public has a legitimate interest in knowing — a convicted fraudster, for instance, might prefer their conviction not to appear in search results, but potential victims of future fraud have a reasonable claim to that information. The right has to be qualified and context-dependent, and adjudicating those judgements is complex. But the fact that it is complex doesn't mean it shouldn't exist.

---

**Question 10.** How do you think the issue of digital privacy will develop over the next twenty years?

**Model Answer:** I think the trajectory could go in very different directions depending on political choices made in the next few years. One plausible scenario is a gradual tightening of regulation globally, following the European model, as the harms of unregulated data collection become more visible and politically mobilising. Another is a deepening divergence between jurisdictions — stricter controls in Europe, looser in the US, and state-directed surveillance capitalism in China — creating a fragmented digital world with very different privacy norms depending on where you live. Technology itself will raise new challenges: advances in biometric monitoring, predictive behavioural modelling, and AI-driven inference will make current privacy debates look relatively straightforward. I think the critical variable is whether democratic publics decide they care enough about privacy to demand political responses, or whether the convenience of connected services gradually erodes any meaningful expectation of private life.
