# IELTS Practice Test 25 — Speaking

**Chủ đề: Lịch sử & Khảo cổ học**

---

## Part 1: Câu hỏi ngắn (4–5 phút)

**Chủ đề 1: History & Museums (Lịch sử & Bảo tàng)**

**1. Are you interested in history?**

Sample answer: Yes, I find history genuinely fascinating. I enjoy learning how past societies developed and how the decisions people made centuries ago still shape the world today. I particularly like reading about ancient civilisations such as Rome and Egypt.

**2. Did you study history at school? Did you enjoy it?**

Sample answer: Yes, history was a compulsory subject throughout secondary school. I enjoyed it more as I got older, especially once we moved beyond memorising dates and started analysing causes and consequences. The lessons about wars and empires were particularly engaging.

**3. Have you ever visited a museum?**

Sample answer: Several times, yes. The most memorable was a visit to the National History Museum in my city, where I saw an exhibition on ancient artefacts from Southeast Asia. The displays were beautifully curated and I spent far longer there than I expected.

**4. What type of exhibits do you find most interesting in museums?**

Sample answer: I am most drawn to exhibits about everyday life in ancient times — things like tools, clothing, and household objects. Grand artefacts like royal treasures are impressive, but I find it more moving to see objects that ordinary people actually used and touched.

---

**Chủ đề 2: Travel & Tourism (Du lịch)**

**5. Do you like visiting historical places when you travel?**

Sample answer: Absolutely. Whenever I travel to a new city, visiting historical sites is always at the top of my list. I think standing in a place where significant events happened gives you a sense of connection to history that books simply cannot replicate.

**6. Have you ever visited a famous historical landmark?**

Sample answer: Yes, I visited the Old Quarter of Hội An, which is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Walking through its narrow streets, with centuries-old merchant houses and temples on either side, felt like stepping back in time. It was a remarkable experience.

**7. Would you like to visit any historical sites abroad in the future?**

Sample answer: Very much so. I would love to visit the Colosseum in Rome and the Pyramids of Giza. Both are iconic symbols of their respective civilisations, and I think seeing them in person would be far more powerful than any photograph or documentary.

**8. Do you think tourism harms historical sites?**

Sample answer: It can, yes. Large numbers of visitors inevitably put physical strain on ancient structures and surrounding environments. However, tourism revenue is often essential to fund conservation efforts, so the relationship is complex and requires careful management rather than outright restriction.

---

**Chủ đề 3: Learning & Education (Học tập)**

**9. How do you prefer to learn about history — books, documentaries, or visits?**

Sample answer: Ideally, a combination of all three. Books provide depth and analytical rigour, documentaries bring events to life visually, and physical visits offer an emotional and sensory connection that nothing else matches. Each medium complements the others.

**10. Do you think young people today are interested in history?**

Sample answer: In my experience, it varies. Some young people are deeply passionate about history, particularly when it is presented through engaging media like podcasts, YouTube documentaries, or historical video games. Others find traditional classroom history dry and disconnected from their daily lives.

**11. Should history be a compulsory subject in schools?**

Sample answer: I believe so. Understanding history helps us make sense of current events, develop critical thinking, and appreciate cultural diversity. A society that ignores its past risks repeating the same mistakes, so equipping young people with historical knowledge seems essential.

**12. Is it important for children to visit historical places as part of their education?**

Sample answer: Very important, I think. Field trips to historical sites bring textbook learning to life and create lasting memories that reinforce classroom lessons. Children who visit ancient temples, museums, or heritage sites often develop a genuine curiosity about the past that stays with them into adulthood.

## Part 2: Cue Card (3–4 phút)

**Đề bài:**

> **Describe a historical place you have visited or would like to visit.**
>
> Bạn nên nói về:
> - What the place is and where it is located
> - Why it is historically significant
> - What you did or what you would do there
> - Why you found it (or think you would find it) memorable

---

**Bài mẫu (~200 từ):**

The historical place I would most like to visit is the ancient city of Pompeii, located near Naples in southern Italy. Pompeii was a thriving Roman city that was buried under volcanic ash and pumice following the catastrophic eruption of Mount Vesuvius in 79 CE. The city remained largely entombed beneath the earth for nearly seventeen centuries before systematic excavations began in the mid-eighteenth century.

What makes Pompeii extraordinary is the extraordinary level of preservation the eruption accidentally provided. Entire streets, houses, temples, and businesses survived intact beneath the ash. Even traces of food, graffiti, and the plaster casts of people and animals caught by the eruption can still be seen today. Walking through Pompeii, visitors are essentially walking through a Roman city frozen at a single moment in time.

If I visited, I would want to spend a whole day exploring the site — walking the cobblestone streets, looking into Roman homes with their intact frescoes and mosaic floors, and visiting the forum where citizens once gathered. I would also want to see the famous plaster casts of the victims, which are haunting but deeply humanising.

I believe the experience would be unforgettable because Pompeii does not feel like a ruin — it feels like a living city that simply fell silent one August afternoon nearly two thousand years ago.

## Part 3: Câu hỏi thảo luận (4–5 phút)

**Chủ đề: Bảo tồn lịch sử, di sản văn hoá, vai trò của bảo tàng**

**1. Why is it important for societies to preserve their historical heritage?**

Sample answer: Historical heritage provides communities with a sense of identity and continuity. It connects living generations to those who came before, fostering pride and cultural understanding. Without preservation, societies risk losing the evidence of their own origins, which can undermine social cohesion and a shared sense of purpose. Heritage sites also serve as powerful educational resources that make abstract history tangible and accessible.

**2. Who should be responsible for funding the preservation of historical sites — governments, private organisations, or international bodies?**

Sample answer: Responsibility should ideally be shared. National governments have an obvious duty to protect sites within their borders, as these form part of the public good. However, given the financial constraints many countries face, international organisations like UNESCO and private sponsors play a vital supplementary role. A collaborative funding model tends to be more sustainable and more likely to succeed than relying on any single source of money.

**3. Do you think modern development should ever be prioritised over the preservation of historical sites?**

Sample answer: In some circumstances, yes. If a historical site stands in the way of critical infrastructure — a hospital, a school, or a flood defence — difficult trade-offs are unavoidable. However, such decisions should never be taken lightly. Authorities should conduct thorough assessments, explore alternative development routes, and ensure that at minimum a detailed archaeological record is created before any destruction proceeds.

**4. How have museums changed in recent years to attract younger visitors?**

Sample answer: Museums have evolved significantly. Many now incorporate interactive digital displays, virtual reality experiences, and hands-on activities that engage visitors actively rather than passively. Social media campaigns and collaborations with popular culture — films, games, influencers — have also helped museums reach younger audiences. Some institutions have also diversified their collections to reflect a wider range of cultures and perspectives, making their displays more inclusive and relevant to diverse communities.

**5. Should artefacts that were taken from one country be returned to their country of origin?**

Sample answer: This is a genuinely complex question that provokes strong feelings on both sides. Many people argue that artefacts belong to the cultures that produced them, and that displaying them in foreign museums is a form of cultural imperialism rooted in colonial history. Others contend that major international museums provide global access to heritage that might otherwise be less visible. I personally lean toward repatriation, particularly when the acquiring country has clear evidence that objects were taken without consent, but I recognise that each case deserves individual evaluation.

**6. In what ways can technology help preserve historical knowledge for future generations?**

Sample answer: Technology offers remarkable possibilities. Three-dimensional scanning and printing can create exact replicas of fragile artefacts, allowing the originals to be stored safely while copies are displayed or shared. Digital archiving preserves manuscripts, photographs, and oral histories that might otherwise decay. Virtual and augmented reality can reconstruct destroyed sites — such as the ancient city of Palmyra in Syria — allowing people to experience places that no longer physically exist. These tools are expanding the boundaries of what preservation means.

**7. Do you think historical dramas and films accurately represent the past?**

Sample answer: Rarely with complete accuracy, though they can capture the spirit of an era. Filmmakers inevitably prioritise dramatic narrative over historical fidelity, which means details of dress, language, social norms, and events are often simplified or distorted. That said, well-researched productions can spark genuine public interest in historical periods and encourage audiences to read further. The key is for viewers to treat such media as an introduction rather than a definitive account.

**8. How important is it to learn about the history of other countries, not just your own?**

Sample answer: Extremely important. Understanding foreign histories fosters empathy and cross-cultural respect, which are increasingly vital in an interconnected world. It also helps us understand why different countries behave as they do in international affairs, trade, and diplomacy. People who know only their own national history risk viewing global events through an overly narrow lens and may be more susceptible to nationalistic or prejudiced thinking.

**9. Do you think the way history is taught in schools is effective?**

Sample answer: Often not as effective as it could be. In many systems, history education focuses heavily on memorisation of dates, names, and events rather than on developing students' ability to analyse sources, evaluate evidence, and understand causation. A shift toward enquiry-based learning — where students investigate historical questions rather than simply absorb prescribed facts — would likely produce more engaged and critically literate learners.

**10. What can individuals do to help preserve their local cultural heritage?**

Sample answer: There is quite a lot individuals can do. Supporting local museums through membership or donations is one concrete step. Participating in community heritage projects — oral history recording, local archaeological society activities, or heritage walks — is another. Individuals can also advocate for the protection of threatened local buildings or landscapes by engaging with local government. Perhaps most importantly, passing on knowledge of local traditions, stories, and history to younger family members helps ensure that informal cultural heritage survives even when physical structures do not.
