The line graph below shows the number of antibiotic-resistant infections recorded annually (in millions) in four regions between 2000 and 2020.

### Model Answer — Task 1

The line graph illustrates the number of antibiotic-resistant infections recorded annually, measured in millions, across four world regions — South-East Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, Europe, and North America — over a twenty-year period from 2000 to 2020.

Overall, all four regions experienced a consistent upward trend throughout the period. South-East Asia recorded by far the highest figures and the sharpest rate of increase, while North America had both the lowest absolute numbers and the slowest rate of growth.

South-East Asia began at 3.2 million cases in 2000 and rose dramatically to 13.4 million by 2020, more than quadrupling over two decades. Sub-Saharan Africa followed a similar steep trajectory, climbing from 2.1 million to 8.7 million over the same period.

By contrast, Europe and North America saw considerably more modest growth. Europe increased from 1.4 million to 2.4 million, while North America grew from just 0.9 million to 1.6 million — the slowest rate of increase of any region shown. Notably, the gap between South-East Asia and North America widened markedly across the period, from a difference of 2.3 million in 2000 to 11.8 million by 2020.

In summary, while antibiotic-resistant infections rose consistently across all four regions, the disparity between the highest and lowest regions grew substantially over the two decades shown.

## WRITING TASK 2

*You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.*

*Write about the following topic:*

> **Governments and large corporations collect increasing amounts of personal data from individuals. Do the benefits of this data collection outweigh the disadvantages?**

*Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.*

*Write at least 250 words.*

---

### Model Answer — Task 2

Governments and technology corporations now amass personal data on an unprecedented scale, from location histories and browsing behaviour to biometric identifiers. While proponents point to meaningful benefits in security and convenience, I believe that the disadvantages currently outweigh the benefits in the absence of robust and universally applied regulatory safeguards.

The principal arguments in favour of data collection centre on safety and efficiency. Governments use surveillance data to detect and disrupt terrorist plots and serious criminal activity, and security agencies argue that bulk data collection provides capabilities impossible to replicate through targeted methods alone. For corporations, data enables personalised services — recommendation algorithms, tailored advertising, and predictive healthcare tools — that many users genuinely value and that drive significant economic innovation.

Nevertheless, the harms are substantial and systemic. Mass data collection creates detailed profiles of individuals without their meaningful consent, exposing people to privacy violations, discrimination, and manipulation. The 2013 Snowden revelations demonstrated that government surveillance had extended far beyond what most citizens or legislators would consider proportionate. Corporate surveillance capitalism, as described by theorist Shoshana Zuboff, treats human experience as a raw material to be extracted and sold, undermining individual autonomy in ways that few users fully comprehend. Furthermore, data breaches — which have become alarmingly routine — transfer this intimate information to criminal actors, with lasting consequences for millions of people.

The critical issue is governance rather than collection itself. Data gathered under strict legal constraints, with meaningful consent, clear purpose limitation, and independent oversight, can deliver public benefits while protecting individual rights. The EU's GDPR represents a serious attempt at such a framework, but until comparable standards are adopted globally, the current reality — in which data collection vastly outpaces accountability — represents a net disadvantage to ordinary citizens.

In conclusion, the question is not whether data should ever be collected, but under what conditions. Until governance matches the scale of collection, the balance tips against individuals.
