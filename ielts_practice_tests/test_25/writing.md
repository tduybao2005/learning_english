## Task 1 (20 phút — khoảng 150 từ)

**Đề bài:**

The table below shows the number of archaeological sites discovered per decade in four world regions between the 1970s and the 2010s.

Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.

---

**Bảng dữ liệu:**

| Thập kỷ | Middle East | Europe | East Asia | Americas |
|---------|------------|--------|-----------|----------|
| 1970s   | 120        | 85     | 40        | 55       |
| 1980s   | 135        | 110    | 65        | 80       |
| 1990s   | 150        | 140    | 105       | 120      |
| 2000s   | 160        | 175    | 155       | 150      |
| 2010s   | 145        | 210    | 200       | 185      |

---

**Bài mẫu (Band 7–8, ~175 từ):**

The table illustrates the number of archaeological sites discovered across four regions — the Middle East, Europe, East Asia, and the Americas — over five decades from the 1970s to the 2010s.

Overall, discoveries increased significantly in all four regions across the period, with Europe and East Asia recording the most dramatic growth. The Middle East was the leading region in the earlier decades but was overtaken by others by the 2010s.

In the 1970s, the Middle East had the highest number of discoveries at 120 sites, while East Asia had the fewest at just 40. Europe and the Americas fell between these figures at 85 and 55 respectively. By the 1990s, all regions had seen considerable rises, with the Middle East continuing to lead at 150 sites.

The most notable shift occurred in the 2000s and 2010s. Europe overtook the Middle East, rising sharply from 140 in the 1990s to 210 sites in the 2010s — the highest of any region in the final decade. East Asia experienced equally impressive growth, climbing from 40 to 200. The Americas also grew steadily, finishing at 185. The Middle East, by contrast, declined slightly from its 2000s peak of 160 to 145 in the 2010s.

## Task 2 (40 phút — khoảng 250 từ)

**Đề bài:**

*"Governments should invest more money in preserving historical sites than in building new infrastructure. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"*

Viết một bài luận hoàn chỉnh. Trình bày ý kiến của bạn và hỗ trợ bằng lý lẽ và ví dụ cụ thể.

---

**Bài mẫu (Band 7–8, ~280 từ):**

The question of how governments should allocate limited financial resources is one of the most persistent debates in public policy. While historical preservation is undeniably valuable, I disagree that it should consistently take priority over infrastructure development, as the needs of living citizens must also be met.

There are compelling reasons to invest in the protection of historical sites. These places represent irreplaceable cultural heritage — once lost, they cannot be rebuilt or recovered. Sites such as Angkor Wat, Machu Picchu, and the ancient ruins of Rome attract millions of tourists annually, generating substantial economic revenue and creating employment in local communities. Beyond economics, preserved sites serve an educational function, allowing future generations to connect with the past and develop a sense of shared identity and continuity.

However, prioritising historical preservation over infrastructure carries significant risks. In many developing nations, populations lack access to reliable roads, clean water systems, hospitals, and schools. Diverting funds away from these essential services to protect ruins or monuments could cause real hardship for ordinary people. A government that allows its bridges to crumble or its hospitals to deteriorate in the name of protecting ancient stones is arguably failing its primary duty of care to its citizens.

A more balanced approach is therefore preferable. Governments can establish dedicated heritage budgets — potentially supplemented by international organisations such as UNESCO or private sponsorship — while simultaneously investing in modern infrastructure. The two goals are not mutually exclusive. Indeed, improved infrastructure often benefits heritage tourism, as better roads and facilities make historical sites more accessible.

In conclusion, while historical preservation deserves serious funding and attention, it should not be treated as categorically more important than the infrastructure that sustains everyday life. Smart allocation, rather than absolute priority, is the most responsible path.
