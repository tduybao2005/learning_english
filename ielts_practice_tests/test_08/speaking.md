## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*Duration: 4–5 minutes*

### Topic A: Sleep & Health Habits

**Question 1:** How many hours of sleep do you usually get each night?

**Model Answer:**
On weeknights, I usually get around six to seven hours, which I know is at the lower end of what health experts recommend. I tend to stay up too late either studying or looking at my phone, which pushes my sleep time back. At weekends I try to catch up a bit and sleep closer to eight hours, though I have read that this kind of "social jet lag" is not as effective as maintaining a consistent schedule every night.

---

**Question 2:** Do you think you get enough sleep?

**Model Answer:**
Honestly, probably not consistently enough. I notice a real difference in my concentration and mood on days after I have slept well compared to days when I have not. When I am well rested, I can focus more easily, process information faster, and feel more emotionally even. When I am sleep-deprived, everything feels harder — tasks take longer, I make more mistakes, and I am more likely to feel stressed or irritable over small things. So I know it matters, but changing the habit is easier said than done.

---

**Question 3:** Do you have a regular daily routine to maintain your health?

**Model Answer:**
I try to, with varying degrees of success. I make an effort to exercise at least three or four times a week — usually a combination of running and some strength work — and I try to eat fairly balanced meals without too much processed food. The area I am least consistent in is sleep and screen time in the evenings, which I know undermine the benefits of the other things I do. I think building good health habits is genuinely difficult when you have competing demands on your time and energy.

---

**Question 4:** Have you ever changed a habit because of health advice?

**Model Answer:**
Yes, actually. A couple of years ago I read about the effects of caffeine on sleep quality and realised I was drinking coffee in the afternoon and evenings without thinking about it. I shifted to cutting off caffeine after about 2pm, and I did notice an improvement in how easily I fell asleep at night — whether it was purely the caffeine change or other factors I could not tell you for certain, but the correlation was clear enough to maintain the habit. It made me more thoughtful about how everyday choices have compounding effects on wellbeing.

---

### Topic B: Science & The Ocean

**Question 5:** Are you interested in the ocean or marine life?

**Model Answer:**
Very much so. There is something genuinely awe-inspiring about the ocean to me — the fact that it covers most of the planet's surface and yet is so poorly explored. I find deep-sea creatures particularly fascinating because they look so alien compared to anything in the visible, sunlit world — anglerfish, giant squid, translucent jellyfish. Vietnam has a very long coastline and a rich fishing tradition, so the ocean feels culturally and practically significant here, not just scientifically interesting.

---

**Question 6:** Have you ever visited the ocean or gone diving?

**Model Answer:**
Yes, I grew up visiting beaches along the Vietnamese coast, and I have had the chance to snorkel in the coral reefs near Phu Quoc Island in the south. The experience of seeing marine life underwater for the first time was extraordinary — the colours and the density of life are so much greater than you can imagine from above the surface. I have not done proper scuba diving yet, but it is something I would like to do. I think experiencing nature directly, rather than through screens, changes your relationship to it in important ways.

---

**Question 7:** Do you think humans have done enough to protect the ocean?

**Model Answer:**
No, I do not think so, not remotely. The scale of damage to marine ecosystems from overfishing, plastic pollution, warming and acidification caused by climate change, and physical destruction of coral reefs and mangroves has been enormous and in many areas irreversible. The fact that less than 20% of the ocean has been mapped, let alone protected, suggests how far behind we are. There have been some positive steps — the establishment of marine protected areas in some regions, international efforts to reduce plastic — but they are slow and inadequate compared to the pace of destruction.

### Topic C: Emotions & Wellbeing

**Question 8:** What kinds of activities make you feel happy or content?

**Model Answer:**
Spending quality time with people I care about is probably the most reliable source of genuine happiness for me — good conversation over a meal, laughing with friends, that kind of thing. I also find creative activities satisfying — writing, or sometimes drawing — because there is a particular pleasure in making something and seeing it come together. Physical exercise also reliably improves my mood even when I do not feel motivated beforehand. Research on wellbeing consistently points to social connection, physical activity, and a sense of purpose or accomplishment as the most reliable contributors to happiness, which maps pretty well onto my own experience.

---

**Question 9:** Do you think people today are generally happier than people were in the past?

**Model Answer:**
This is a genuinely difficult question to answer because wellbeing is subjective and historical comparisons are inherently speculative. In material terms, people in most parts of the world are significantly better off than they were a century ago — less poverty, less early death, less exposure to catastrophic disease. But surveys of subjective wellbeing suggest that beyond a certain income threshold, additional material wealth does not reliably produce greater happiness. And there is some evidence — particularly for younger generations — that certain dimensions of wellbeing, including a sense of purpose, social connectedness, and mental health, have deteriorated in recent decades despite material improvements.

---

**Question 10:** How do you manage stress or difficult emotions?

**Model Answer:**
I have found a few things consistently helpful. Physical exercise is probably the most reliable short-term stress reliever for me — a run or a workout almost always makes whatever was bothering me feel more manageable afterwards. Talking to someone I trust is also very helpful when I am dealing with something that weighs on me. I am also trying to develop a more regular mindfulness or meditation practice, though I find it harder to maintain consistently than exercise. I think the key insight is that managing difficult emotions requires active strategies rather than just waiting for them to pass.

---

**Question 11:** Is mental health discussed openly in your culture?

**Model Answer:**
I think it varies a great deal by generation and context in Vietnam. Among younger, urban, educated Vietnamese people, there is increasingly more openness about mental health — the topic is discussed on social media, and awareness campaigns have made some progress in reducing stigma. But among older generations and in more traditional or rural contexts, mental illness still carries considerable stigma and is often either not recognised or attributed to moral weakness or spiritual problems rather than medical conditions. This means many people do not seek help when they need it, which has real human costs.

---

**Question 12:** Do you think physical health and mental health are connected?

**Model Answer:**
Absolutely, and the science on this connection has become increasingly clear. Physical health affects mental health through multiple mechanisms — for example, exercise increases levels of neurotransmitters like serotonin and endorphins that regulate mood; sleep deprivation impairs emotional regulation; chronic physical illness significantly increases risk of depression and anxiety. And the connection runs in the other direction too: mental health conditions like depression are associated with higher rates of cardiovascular disease, weakened immune function, and a range of other physical health problems. The mind-body distinction that dominated medical thinking for a long time has been increasingly challenged by evidence of these bidirectional connections.

### Task Card

**Describe a time when you found it difficult to sleep or felt very tired.**

You should say:
- when this happened
- what the reason was
- how it affected you at the time
- and explain how you dealt with it or how you felt afterwards

---

### Model Response (~200 words)

The time that comes to mind most clearly was during the period just before my university entrance exams — the Vietnamese national university admissions examinations. These exams are extremely high-stakes, and the social and family pressure surrounding them is intense. For several weeks beforehand, I was studying until midnight or later every night, running on five or six hours of sleep at most.

The reason was obvious: anxiety and the sheer volume of material I felt I needed to cover. But the irony was that the sleep deprivation made my studying less effective — I was reviewing material but retaining very little of it because my tired brain could not consolidate new information properly. I would read the same page multiple times and still struggle to recall what I had read.

During this period I felt constantly exhausted, irritable with people around me, and unable to focus for extended periods. Small things that would normally not bother me felt overwhelming. My appetite also decreased, and I had persistent headaches.

What helped most was when a family member persuaded me — somewhat forcefully — to stop studying at 10pm for the last few days before the exam and prioritise sleep. Counterintuitive as it felt, I performed better than I had in my most exhausted practice sessions. It taught me something lasting about the relationship between rest and performance.

---

### Follow-up Question

**Do you think sleep is taken seriously enough as a public health issue?**

**Model Answer:**
I do not think it is, particularly in cultures where long working hours and minimal sleep are seen as markers of productivity and dedication rather than as health risks. There is considerable public awareness about nutrition and physical exercise as health factors, but sleep tends to be treated as optional — something to be sacrificed when there are competing demands. The economic evidence is compelling: research suggests that sleep deprivation costs economies hundreds of billions of dollars annually in lost productivity. I think greater investment in public education about sleep, and cultural change in workplace norms around working hours, would have significant returns in both individual health and economic productivity.

### Discussion Theme: Mental Health, Modern Life, and Healthcare Systems

---

**Question 1:** Do you think modern lifestyles are contributing to mental health problems?

**Model Answer:**
I think there is substantial evidence that they are, though the relationship is complex. Several features of contemporary life that were largely absent in earlier periods have been associated with poorer mental health outcomes: chronic sleep deprivation driven by technology use and extended working hours; the replacement of face-to-face social interaction with screen-mediated communication, which appears to be less psychologically satisfying; economic insecurity and precariousness among young adults; and the specific psychological dynamics of social media, which expose users to a constant stream of social comparison signals. These are not trivial environmental changes, and it would be surprising if they had no psychological consequences. That said, better diagnosis and reduced stigma also contribute to higher reported rates, so the picture is not simple.

---

**Question 2:** Why do you think mental health remains stigmatised in many societies, even as awareness increases?

**Model Answer:**
Stigma around mental illness has multiple roots that are resistant to simple awareness campaigns. One is the persistent tendency to conceptualise mental health conditions as reflecting personal weakness or a failure of character rather than as medical conditions with biological substrates — a perception that does not apply to the same extent to physical health conditions. Another is fear and unfamiliarity — many people have limited personal experience with mental illness and rely on inaccurate media representations, which often sensationalise rare and extreme cases. Cultural and religious frameworks in some societies also provide explanations for psychological suffering — in terms of spiritual failings or moral lapses — that compete with medical interpretations. Dismantling stigma requires sustained, long-term cultural work rather than simply publishing information.

---

**Question 3:** Should employers be responsible for the mental health of their employees?

**Model Answer:**
I think employers have a meaningful and increasingly recognised responsibility, though I would not phrase it as the primary or sole responsibility. Employers control significant aspects of the environments that shape employee wellbeing — workload, autonomy, social support, fairness of treatment, the presence of psychological safety — and there is good evidence that working conditions have substantial effects on mental health outcomes. From a business perspective, supporting employee mental health also makes commercial sense: mental health conditions are a leading cause of absenteeism and reduced productivity in most developed economies. However, I would be cautious about over-medicalising what are fundamentally structural problems: overwork and precariousness are better addressed through labour market regulation than through individual therapy programmes.

---

**Question 4:** Is there enough investment in mental healthcare compared to physical healthcare in most countries?

**Model Answer:**
No, substantially not, by almost any measure. Mental health conditions account for an enormous proportion of the global disease burden — depression and anxiety are among the leading causes of disability-adjusted life years lost worldwide — yet investment in mental health services as a proportion of total healthcare spending is drastically lower than this burden would justify. In most low- and middle-income countries, the treatment gap — the proportion of people with diagnosable mental health conditions who receive no treatment — exceeds 75%. Even in wealthy countries with well-developed healthcare systems, waiting times for mental health services are often measured in months or years, and the evidence base for many widely used treatments has been slow to translate into clinical practice. The disparity reflects historical marginalisation of mental health within medical institutions and persistent stigma at the institutional level.

---

**Question 5:** How might technology be used positively to support mental health?

**Model Answer:**
Technology offers some genuinely promising possibilities that I think are worth taking seriously alongside the legitimate concerns about its negative effects. Digital mental health tools — including smartphone applications for mindfulness, mood tracking, and cognitive behavioural therapy techniques — have the potential to dramatically increase access to evidence-based psychological support for populations who cannot access traditional in-person services due to cost, geography, or stigma. Teletherapy platforms have expanded access to qualified therapists. Artificial intelligence may eventually be able to identify early warning signs of mental health deterioration through analysis of patterns in speech, text, or smartphone usage, enabling earlier intervention. The challenge is ensuring that these tools are rigorously evaluated for effectiveness and safety, and that they complement rather than replace human therapeutic relationships.

---

**Question 6:** Do you think the COVID-19 pandemic had a lasting impact on how society thinks about mental health?

**Model Answer:**
I think it genuinely did, in some important ways. The pandemic imposed conditions — social isolation, loss of routine, grief, economic anxiety, and existential uncertainty — that affected virtually everyone simultaneously, which may have helped destigmatise mental health struggles by making them widely shared and publicly acknowledged. The pandemic also demonstrated the acute mental health consequences of lockdown and social distancing in ways that were difficult to dismiss, generating greater policy attention and public discussion. At the same time, the pandemic also stretched mental health services at precisely the moment when demand increased dramatically, exposing the inadequacy of existing infrastructure. Whether the heightened awareness translates into sustained investment in mental health systems remains to be seen.

---

**Question 7:** How do work-life balance issues affect mental health?

**Model Answer:**
Research is quite consistent that poor work-life balance — characterised by long working hours, inability to disconnect from work during personal time, inadequate annual leave, and high work demands relative to available resources — is associated with elevated risk of burnout, anxiety, and depression. The mechanisms are multiple: insufficient time for recovery and restoration; disrupted sleep from late working; reduced time for social relationships and physical activity; and a chronic stress response from feeling unable to manage competing demands. The problem has intensified with smartphone technology, which has eroded the temporal and spatial boundaries that previously separated work from personal life. Countries with strong regulatory protections for working hours — such as France's "right to disconnect" legislation — appear to have somewhat better outcomes on these dimensions, suggesting policy levers are available.

---

**Question 8:** Is individual lifestyle change or systemic policy change more important for improving public mental health?

**Model Answer:**
I am fairly strongly of the view that systemic policy change is more important, though individual choices clearly matter. The framing of mental health as primarily an individual responsibility to be managed through personal habits, therapy, and self-care can obscure the structural drivers of mental ill-health: poverty, housing insecurity, social isolation, precarious employment, discrimination, and unmanageable work demands. These are not problems that individual mindfulness or better sleep hygiene can solve at scale. Evidence from public health more broadly — on tobacco, alcohol, physical activity, and diet — consistently shows that changing environments and incentive structures through policy produces larger population-level effects than interventions targeting individual behaviour change. That said, individual skills and resources matter enormously for how well people navigate difficult structural circumstances, so the dichotomy is false — both matter.

---

**Question 9:** Should mental health education be included in school curricula?

**Model Answer:**
Yes, I believe strongly that it should, and for several reasons. First, many mental health conditions emerge in adolescence or early adulthood, meaning schools are well-positioned for early identification and intervention. Second, providing young people with evidence-based knowledge about mental health — how to recognise symptoms, where to seek help, how to support a friend in difficulty — can reduce stigma and increase help-seeking behaviour. Third, teaching skills like emotional regulation, stress management, and healthy sleep and lifestyle habits during the formative years of school has a reasonable evidence base for long-term benefit. Countries including the United Kingdom have begun to incorporate mental health content into school curricula, and early evaluations have been cautiously positive.

---

**Question 10:** What do you think is the most important factor in maintaining good mental health throughout life?

**Model Answer:**
If I had to identify a single most important factor, I would say social connection — the quality of relationships with family, friends, and community. The research on this is remarkably consistent across cultures, methodologies, and time periods: social isolation and loneliness are among the most powerful predictors of poor mental health and early mortality, while strong social bonds are the most reliable protective factor. The Harvard Study of Adult Development, one of the longest longitudinal studies of human life ever conducted, followed the same group of people for over eighty years and found that the quality of relationships at midlife was a better predictor of health and happiness in old age than almost any other measured variable. This finding is both scientifically robust and humanly intuitive — most people, on reflection, would agree that their most meaningful experiences involve connection with others.
