## READING PASSAGE 1

**"The Final Frontier: Deep Sea Exploration"**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 1–13, which are based on Reading Passage 1.*

---

**A**

The ocean remains the least explored environment on Earth. Despite covering approximately 71% of the planet's surface, less than 20% of the world's ocean floor has been mapped with high-resolution detail — meaning that our topographic maps of the Moon and Mars are, in many respects, more precise than our charts of Earth's own seafloor. The ocean holds a staggering total volume of approximately 1.335 billion cubic kilometres of water, with an average depth of 3.7 kilometres. Its deepest point, the Challenger Deep in the Mariana Trench of the western Pacific Ocean, plunges to a depth of approximately 11 kilometres — a depth at which the pressure exceeds 1,000 times the atmospheric pressure experienced at sea level. The practical, logistical, and financial challenges of exploring these extreme environments have limited human investigation of deep sea environments far more severely than the technical challenges of exploring nearby bodies in the solar system.

**B**

One of the most remarkable biological phenomena of the deep ocean is bioluminescence — the production and emission of light by living organisms through biochemical reactions. Researchers estimate that approximately 76% of ocean organisms, at least in certain depth ranges, are capable of producing light, making bioluminescence by far the most common form of communication and signalling in the ocean's middle and lower depths, where sunlight does not penetrate. Bioluminescence serves a range of biological functions: predators such as the deep-sea anglerfish use a bioluminescent lure dangling from a modified dorsal spine to attract prey in otherwise total darkness; some species use light signals for intraspecies communication and mate recognition; others employ bioluminescence as a form of counter-illumination camouflage, matching the faint downwelling light to conceal their silhouette from predators below. The firefly squid (Watasenia scintillans), found in the waters of the western Pacific, produces dazzling bioluminescent displays across its entire body, while the vampire squid — despite its fearsome name — is a harmless organism that ejects a cloud of bioluminescent mucus to confuse potential predators.

**C**

Perhaps the most transformative discovery in the history of marine biology occurred in 1977, when researchers using the submersible Alvin discovered the first known hydrothermal vent systems along the Galápagos Rift on the floor of the Pacific Ocean at a depth of approximately 2,500 metres. These vents, where superheated water rich in dissolved minerals erupts from fissures in the ocean crust, were found to support extraordinarily dense and diverse ecosystems — thriving communities of tubeworms, giant clams, shrimp, crabs, and fish — in complete darkness and without any access to sunlight. The energy source underpinning these communities was not photosynthesis but chemosynthesis: the biochemical process by which certain bacteria and archaea convert inorganic compounds, particularly hydrogen sulphide, into organic matter using chemical energy rather than light. The discovery fundamentally challenged the previously universal scientific assumption that sunlight was an absolute prerequisite for the existence of complex life, and has profound implications for the search for life on other bodies in the solar system, particularly the ice-covered moons Europa (orbiting Jupiter) and Enceladus (orbiting Saturn), which are thought to have liquid water oceans beneath their surfaces.

**D**

The deep seabed has attracted increasing commercial interest because of the vast mineral wealth it contains. Manganese nodules — potato-sized concretions that form over millions of years on the abyssal plains of the deep ocean — contain significant concentrations of cobalt, nickel, copper, and manganese, as well as rare earth elements critical for the manufacture of electric vehicle batteries and other clean energy technologies. Cobalt-rich crusts on seamounts and polymetallic sulphide deposits around inactive hydrothermal vents contain similarly valuable mineral assemblages. Industry analysts have estimated the total commercial value of accessible deep-sea mineral deposits at figures exceeding $150 billion, attracting the interest of mining companies and sponsoring nations alike. The regulatory framework for mineral extraction in international waters — beyond any nation's national jurisdiction — falls under the authority of the International Seabed Authority, an intergovernmental body established under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea. Environmentalists have raised serious concerns about the potential impacts of seabed mining on ecosystems that are extremely fragile, poorly understood, and characterised by extraordinarily slow recovery rates following disturbance.

**E**

The practical challenges of exploring the deep ocean are formidable. Water pressure increases by approximately one atmosphere for every ten metres of depth, meaning that at the bottom of the Mariana Trench, pressures exceed 1,000 times standard atmospheric pressure — sufficient to crush unprotected equipment within seconds. Temperatures in the deep ocean hover near 0°C except in the immediate vicinity of hydrothermal vents, and the complete absence of sunlight below approximately 200 metres makes visual navigation extraordinarily difficult. These conditions place extreme demands on engineering. Crewed submersibles capable of reaching the deepest trenches are extraordinarily expensive to build and operate and remain rare. Increasingly, deep-sea researchers rely on remotely operated vehicles (ROVs) — cable-tethered, camera-equipped platforms that can be controlled from a surface ship — and autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs), which operate independently using pre-programmed instructions and artificial intelligence. These unmanned platforms have dramatically expanded the geographical range and temporal duration of deep-sea investigation, though questions persist about whether the cost of deep-sea exploration is adequately justified by scientific returns relative to other research priorities.

**F**

The deep sea covers approximately 65% of the total surface area of the Earth, making it the planet's largest biome by area — and its most poorly understood. Scientists estimate that more than 90% of deep-sea species remain unknown to science, and new species are discovered in virtually every research expedition. Yet the deep ocean faces threats from human activity even without direct human presence. Plastic pollution has been found in sediment samples and on the bodies of organisms collected from even the deepest ocean trenches, including the Mariana Trench, demonstrating that no part of the ocean is insulated from surface pollution. Some conservationists and marine scientists argue that protected area status should be established for key deep-sea ecosystems before large-scale resource extraction begins, to avoid the pattern seen in terrestrial and shallow-water environments where conservation efforts have arrived too late to prevent significant ecological damage.

## READING PASSAGE 2

**"A Brief History of Public Health"**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 14–26, which are based on Reading Passage 2.*

---

**A**

The history of public health is, in many respects, a history of humanity's gradual and incomplete understanding of the relationship between the environment, human behaviour, and disease. Civilisations of antiquity demonstrated sophisticated awareness of sanitation's practical importance long before the germ theory of disease was articulated. The ancient Indus Valley civilisation, flourishing around 2500 BCE, constructed elaborate brick drainage systems in cities such as Mohenjo-daro. Roman engineers built vast aqueducts and sewage systems — including the Cloaca Maxima, still partially functional today — that supplied clean water to urban populations and removed waste from city streets. Following the decline of Roman administrative infrastructure in medieval Europe, many of these sanitation achievements were abandoned, with devastating consequences for public health. The dominant medical theory explaining disease for over two millennia — the miasma theory, which held that disease was caused by "bad air" arising from rotting organic matter and filth — shaped public health practice and policy well into the nineteenth century, simultaneously obscuring and, paradoxically, sometimes encouraging sanitary improvements that were beneficial even if the underlying reasoning was incorrect.

**B**

The foundational event in the history of modern epidemiology — the scientific discipline concerned with the patterns, causes, and control of disease in populations — took place on the streets of London's Soho district in the summer of 1854. A severe outbreak of cholera killed more than 500 people within ten days. John Snow, a physician who had already begun questioning the miasma theory of disease, conducted a systematic door-to-door investigation of the outbreak, mapping the cases geographically and interviewing residents about their water sources. His analysis pointed conclusively to a single public water pump on Broad Street as the source of the outbreak. Snow persuaded local authorities to remove the handle of the pump, preventing further use and effectively ending the epidemic. Snow's investigation demonstrated that cholera was transmitted through contaminated water rather than through bad air — anticipating the formal discovery of the germ theory of disease by Louis Pasteur and Robert Koch in the following decade — and established the methods of epidemiological investigation that remain foundational to public health practice today.

**C**

The nineteenth century also saw the emergence of a powerful sanitary reform movement in Britain and other industrialising nations, driven by a recognition — motivated by miasma theory but practically effective regardless of its theoretical foundations — that poverty, filth, and disease were inextricably connected. Edwin Chadwick's landmark 1842 Report on the Sanitary Condition of the Labouring Population of Great Britain documented the appalling living conditions of industrial urban workers and provided compelling statistical evidence linking insanitary environments to high mortality rates. Chadwick's report was instrumental in driving legislative reform: a series of public health acts throughout the second half of the nineteenth century established systems of sewage disposal, clean water supply, housing inspection, and the appointment of medical officers of health in British towns and cities. Historians of medicine have observed that much of the dramatic decline in mortality rates that characterised the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries in industrialising nations occurred before the widespread availability of effective vaccines or antibiotics, suggesting that improvements in nutrition, sanitation, and living conditions were the primary drivers of this historic transformation in human health.

**D**

The history of vaccination represents one of the most successful chapters in the story of public health. Edward Jenner's development of a vaccine against smallpox in 1796 — using the observation that milkmaids who had contracted cowpox appeared to be protected from the far more deadly smallpox — was a landmark achievement that preceded any understanding of the immune system or the microbial nature of infectious disease. Louis Pasteur's germ theory of disease in the 1860s provided the theoretical framework that enabled the systematic development of vaccines against rabies, cholera, and typhoid. The twentieth century saw the development of highly effective vaccines against polio — Jonas Salk's inactivated vaccine in 1955 followed by Albert Sabin's oral vaccine — and a global vaccination campaign that achieved the complete eradication of smallpox by 1980, the first time in history that a human disease had been deliberately eliminated from the planet. Smallpox eradication is widely regarded as the greatest achievement in the history of public health.

**E**

The twentieth century brought a profound transformation in the disease burden of wealthy industrialised nations. As infectious diseases were progressively controlled through improvements in sanitation, nutrition, vaccination, and eventually antibiotics, the so-called "epidemiological transition" occurred: populations in high-income countries shifted from dying primarily of infectious diseases in youth and middle age to dying primarily of chronic non-communicable diseases — heart disease, stroke, cancer, and type 2 diabetes — in old age. This transition necessitated entirely new public health strategies, moving from the control of acute infections to the modification of long-term behavioural risk factors. The campaign against tobacco smoking represents perhaps the most significant public health achievement of the late twentieth century: systematic evidence linking smoking to lung cancer and heart disease, combined with public health campaigns, legislative restrictions on advertising and smoking in public spaces, and taxation, has reduced smoking rates dramatically in many high-income countries, preventing millions of premature deaths.

**F**

One of the central moral and political challenges of global public health is the stark and persistent inequity in health outcomes between wealthy and poor nations. A child born in a high-income country can expect to live 18 to 20 years longer than one born in a low-income country. Preventable and treatable diseases — including malaria, tuberculosis, and diseases preventable by widely available vaccines — continue to kill millions of people annually in low-income nations that lack access to the medicines, healthcare workers, and infrastructure that wealthy nations take for granted. The 1978 Alma-Ata Declaration, adopted by 134 nations, proclaimed the aspiration of "Health for All" and the centrality of primary healthcare in achieving it, but progress has been slow and uneven. International health initiatives established since the turn of the millennium — including the Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria, GAVI (the vaccine alliance), and the United States government's PEPFAR programme for HIV/AIDS — have made significant inroads, but the fundamental structural inequities that drive health disparities remain largely unaddressed.

**G**

Contemporary public health faces a complex and interconnected set of challenges. Antimicrobial resistance — the process by which disease-causing bacteria, viruses, and fungi evolve resistance to the drugs used to treat them — has been identified by the World Health Organisation as one of the greatest threats to global health, food security, and development, with some projections suggesting that drug-resistant infections could kill ten million people per year by 2050 if current trends continue. The COVID-19 pandemic of 2020–2023 exposed significant gaps in global pandemic preparedness infrastructure and coordination mechanisms, while also demonstrating the extraordinary potential of modern vaccine development technologies, particularly mRNA vaccines. Non-communicable diseases now account for approximately 74% of all deaths globally, representing an enormous and growing burden in both high- and middle-income countries. Mental health, long treated as a neglected stepchild of global health policy, is increasingly recognised as a critical dimension of overall health, with depression and anxiety among the leading causes of disability-adjusted life years lost worldwide. The intersection of human health with climate change — through its effects on infectious disease geography, food security, extreme heat events, and air quality — represents a further frontier of growing concern.

## READING PASSAGE 3

**"The Neuroscience of Sleep"**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 27–40, which are based on Reading Passage 3.*

---

**A**

Sleep occupies approximately one-third of a typical human life, yet it remains one of the least understood of all biological processes. All known vertebrate animals — including fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals — sleep in some form, a fact that strongly implies that sleep serves fundamental biological functions that were established early in the evolutionary history of complex animal life. A healthy adult human requires between 7 and 9 hours of sleep per night to maintain optimal cognitive and physical function. Chronic sleep restriction — consistently sleeping fewer than 6 hours per night — is associated with significantly elevated risk of a wide range of serious health conditions, including cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, obesity, impaired immune function, and mental health disorders including depression and anxiety. Despite the severity of these documented health consequences, and despite spending roughly a third of our lives in this state, science has only relatively recently begun to understand why we sleep at all.

**B**

Human sleep is characterised by a structured, cyclical architecture involving two fundamentally different types of sleep: NREM (Non-Rapid Eye Movement) sleep and REM (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep. NREM sleep is itself divided into three progressive stages. Stage N1 is the transitional drowsiness between wakefulness and sleep, lasting typically only a few minutes. Stage N2 represents consolidated sleep onset and is characterised by the appearance of distinctive brain wave patterns — sleep spindles (brief bursts of oscillatory brain activity) and K-complexes (large, sharp wave deflections) — that play important roles in memory processing. Stage N3, also known as deep sleep or slow-wave sleep, is the most physically restorative stage, during which the body repairs tissue, builds bone and muscle, and strengthens the immune system. A complete adult sleep cycle — progressing through N1, N2, N3, and then REM — lasts approximately 90 minutes, and a healthy adult typically completes 4 to 6 such cycles per night, with the proportion of REM sleep increasing and the proportion of deep N3 sleep decreasing in later cycles.

**C**

Decades of research have produced multiple competing and complementary theories about the specific functions that sleep serves in the brain. The memory consolidation theory holds that sleep is critical for the processing, integration, and long-term storage of new memories: during sleep, particularly during N3 slow-wave sleep and REM sleep, the hippocampus — the brain structure primarily responsible for the initial encoding of new memories — "replays" experiences from the preceding waking period, gradually transferring memory traces from fragile short-term storage to more stable long-term cortical networks. A separate and more recently discovered function concerns a remarkable biological system called the glymphatic system — a network of channels surrounding blood vessels in the brain that acts as a waste clearance system, flushing cerebrospinal fluid through brain tissue to remove metabolic byproducts that accumulate during waking activity. This glymphatic clearance system is far more active during sleep than during wakefulness, raising the possibility that one critical function of sleep is to "clean" the brain of toxic waste products. Among the substances cleared by this system is amyloid-beta, a protein whose abnormal accumulation in brain tissue is a hallmark feature of Alzheimer's disease, suggesting a possible mechanistic link between chronic sleep deprivation and elevated dementia risk.

**D**

The timing of sleep is regulated by an internal biological timing system known as the circadian rhythm — from the Latin circa dies, meaning "about a day" — which coordinates biological processes across an approximately 24-hour cycle. The master clock of the circadian system is located in a small structure called the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), a paired cluster of approximately 20,000 neurons in the hypothalamus that receives direct input from light-sensitive cells in the retina of the eye. The SCN is synchronised primarily by light, and light exposure — particularly in the short-wavelength blue portion of the visible spectrum — is the most powerful environmental signal for resetting the biological clock and suppressing the onset of sleep. Melatonin, a hormone produced by the pineal gland in response to darkness, serves as the biological signal that communicates to the body that it is night-time and that physiological preparation for sleep should begin. The widespread use of screens — smartphones, tablets, computers, and televisions — in the hours before sleep exposes individuals to blue-wavelength light that suppresses melatonin production and delays the natural sleep signal, a recognised contributor to delayed and shortened sleep in modern populations.

**E**

Sleep disorders represent a major and underappreciated public health challenge. Insomnia — persistent difficulty in initiating or maintaining sleep, or experiencing non-restorative sleep, combined with daytime impairment — is the most common sleep disorder, affecting an estimated 10 to 30% of adults globally. Obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA), a condition in which the airway repeatedly collapses during sleep, causing brief but repeated interruptions to breathing, affects an estimated 936 million adults worldwide and is strongly and independently associated with elevated risk of hypertension, heart disease, stroke, and type 2 diabetes. Narcolepsy, a neurological disorder characterised by uncontrollable episodes of daytime sleepiness and sudden muscle weakness (cataplexy), restless legs syndrome, and various circadian rhythm disorders each represent clinically significant conditions that impair quality of life and carry health risks. Despite the prevalence of sleep disorders and the severity of their health consequences, they remain substantially underdiagnosed and undertreated in most healthcare systems.

**F**

Adolescents present a particularly important case in the relationship between sleep and health. Research in chronobiology has established that puberty causes a genuine and physiologically mediated shift in the circadian rhythm, making teenagers naturally inclined to fall asleep later in the evening and to wake later in the morning — a pattern known as delayed sleep phase. This biological shift means that when school start times require adolescents to wake at 6:00 or 6:30 am, they are being asked to function during hours when their biology is oriented towards sleep. Research on the consequences of earlier school start times has documented associations with poorer academic performance, increased rates of depression and anxiety, and elevated rates of road traffic accidents in student drivers. Several school districts and educational authorities in the United States, United Kingdom, and other countries have begun to adjust school start times to better align with adolescent biology, with positive results reported in early evaluations.

**G**

Cross-cultural and historical perspectives on sleep reveal that current Western assumptions about how, when, and how long one should sleep are culturally specific rather than biologically universal. The historian A. Roger Ekirch documented, through extensive archival research into pre-industrial European texts, that the dominant sleep pattern before the widespread adoption of artificial lighting appears to have been "segmented sleep" — two distinct sleep periods of several hours each, separated by a period of quiet wakefulness of approximately one to two hours in the middle of the night. This two-sleep pattern, which has now virtually disappeared from industrialised societies, may represent the natural human sleep pattern in the absence of artificial light. Cross-cultural sleep research also reveals significant variation in sleep duration: studies of hunter-gatherer societies in Africa, South America, and Madagascar — populations living without artificial light or fixed work schedules — show that members typically sleep 6.5 to 7.5 hours per night and do not maintain strict sleep schedules. In Japan, a cultural practice known as inemuri — falling asleep in public settings such as trains, offices, and meetings — is widely accepted and is sometimes interpreted as a sign of dedication and hard work rather than laziness.

**H**

The economic consequences of insufficient sleep are substantial and are increasingly attracting the attention of economists, employers, and policymakers. The RAND Corporation, an American research institute, estimated in a 2016 report that the United States alone loses approximately $411 billion in economic output per year due to the reduced productivity and absenteeism associated with insufficient sleep among the working population; Japan was estimated to lose approximately $138 billion annually. Sleep deprivation impairs cognitive performance, reaction time, working memory, emotional regulation, and complex decision-making in ways that laboratory studies have shown to be functionally comparable to moderate alcohol intoxication at certain threshold levels of deprivation — yet unlike alcohol intoxication, sleep deprivation carries no social stigma and in many workplace cultures is actively normalised or even romanticised as evidence of hard work and commitment. This cultural valorisation of sleeplessness represents a significant barrier to the policy and workplace culture changes that the evidence suggests are necessary.
