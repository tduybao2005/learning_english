# IELTS ACADEMIC WRITING — TEST 09

**Time allowed:** 60 minutes
**Total:** Task 1 + Task 2
**Note:** Spend approximately 20 minutes on Task 1 and 40 minutes on Task 2.

---

## Task 1 (20 minutes — minimum 150 words)

**You should spend about 20 minutes on this task.**

**The two pie charts below show where people got their news in 2005 and 2020.**

**Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.**

---

### Data: News Sources 2005 and 2020

**2005:**
- Television: 52%
- Newspaper: 28%
- Radio: 10%
- Internet: 8%
- Other: 2%

**2020:**
- Social Media: 35%
- Online news sites: 30%
- Television: 20%
- Radio: 8%
- Newspaper: 5%
- Other: 2%

---

### Model Answer — Band 7.5 (~175 words)

The two pie charts compare the sources from which people obtained their news in 2005 and 2020, revealing a dramatic transformation in media consumption over this fifteen-year period.

In 2005, traditional media dominated the news landscape. Television was by far the most widely used source, accounting for over half of all news consumption at 52%, followed by newspapers at 28%. Internet-based news was in its infancy, reaching only 8% of consumers, while radio contributed a modest 10%.

By 2020, the picture had fundamentally changed. Digital platforms had overtaken all traditional sources: social media led with 35%, closely followed by online news sites at 30%, giving digital channels a combined share of 65% — compared to a mere 8% for internet-based news in 2005. Television's dominance collapsed to just 20%, while newspapers experienced the sharpest decline, falling from 28% to only 5%.

Overall, the charts illustrate a seismic shift from traditional to digital news sources over fifteen years, with social media emerging as the single largest category.

---

### Examiner Notes

- **Task Achievement (Band 8):** Clear overview present in final paragraph; data accurately reported; effective comparison drawn between years
- **Coherence and Cohesion (Band 7):** Well-organised — 2005 discussed first, then 2020 changes clearly flagged; good use of discourse markers ("By 2020", "while", "followed by")
- **Lexical Resource (Band 7-8):** Sophisticated vocabulary — "seismic shift", "in its infancy", "landscape", "collapsed", "dominance"
- **Grammatical Range and Accuracy (Band 7):** Varied sentence structures; accurate passive constructions; no significant grammatical errors

---

### Writing Tips — Task 1 Pie Charts

1. **Always write an overview.** Identify the single most striking overall trend (here: the shift from traditional to digital).
2. **Select, don't list.** Do not describe every figure — group related items and highlight the most significant changes.
3. **Use language of change:** "fell sharply", "rose dramatically", "remained stable", "declined from X to Y".
4. **Compare between charts** (2005 vs 2020) rather than just describing each chart separately.
5. **Do not give reasons or opinions** in Task 1 — only describe what the data shows.

## Task 2 (40 minutes — minimum 250 words)

**You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.**

**Write about the following topic:**

> *"The genetic engineering of human embryos to prevent inherited diseases is ethically acceptable and should be encouraged by governments. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"*

**Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.**

**Write at least 250 words.**

---

### Model Answer — Band 7.5 (~275 words)

The prospect of genetically modifying human embryos to eliminate inherited diseases occupies a complex position at the intersection of medicine, ethics, and governance. I partially agree with the statement, supporting the use of genetic engineering to prevent serious inherited conditions, while opposing its extension to non-medical or enhancement purposes.

The strongest argument in favour of permitting genetic embryo editing is the potential to eliminate devastating inherited diseases — conditions such as Huntington's disease, cystic fibrosis, and Tay-Sachs — that cause immense suffering and severely reduce quality of life. Unlike therapeutic treatments that manage symptoms, genetic modification could prevent these conditions from arising at all. The development of CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing technology has made such interventions increasingly precise and feasible, and the prospect of eliminating preventable hereditary suffering carries significant moral weight.

However, I do not believe that governments should actively encourage genetic engineering of embryos without robust ethical and legal frameworks. The primary concern is the slippery slope from disease prevention to enhancement. Once genetic modification of embryos becomes normalised, the boundary between eliminating diseases and selecting for non-medical traits — intelligence, height, athletic ability — becomes difficult to maintain. This could entrench genetic inequality, as wealthier families access genetic advantages unavailable to others.

In conclusion, I support the narrowly defined use of genetic engineering to prevent serious inherited diseases in clinical settings governed by strict ethical oversight, but oppose broader governmental encouragement of the technology without clear limits. The potential to reduce suffering is real, but the risks of misuse are equally significant and must be rigorously addressed.

---

### Examiner Notes

- **Task Achievement (Band 7):** Clear partially-agree position stated in introduction; both sides addressed with specific disease examples (Huntington's, cystic fibrosis, Tay-Sachs); logical distinction drawn between disease prevention and enhancement; conclusion consistent with position
- **Coherence and Cohesion (Band 8):** Smooth paragraph structure; strong discourse markers ("However", "The strongest argument", "In conclusion"); coherent argument throughout
- **Lexical Resource (Band 7-8):** Academic register maintained — "intersection", "normalised", "entrench", "robust ethical frameworks", "slippery slope", "germline editing"
- **Grammatical Range and Accuracy (Band 7):** Complex sentence structures used accurately; varied clause types; no major errors

---

### Writing Tips — Task 2 Opinion Essays (To what extent agree/disagree)

1. **State your position clearly in the introduction.** "I partially agree", "I strongly agree", or "I disagree" — examiners need to know your stance from paragraph 1.
2. **For "to what extent" questions,** a partial agreement (as modelled above) is often the strongest approach — it allows you to present nuanced arguments rather than defending an absolute position.
3. **Use PEEL structure for body paragraphs:**
   - **P**oint (topic sentence stating the argument)
   - **E**xplanation (elaborate on the point)
   - **E**vidence (example or data)
   - **L**ink (connect back to the question)
4. **Avoid generalisation without support.** Claims like "everyone knows" or "obviously" weaken academic writing.
5. **Maintain formal register throughout.** Avoid contractions (don't, can't), slang, or first-person narrative anecdotes (unless directly relevant).
6. **The conclusion must match your stated position.** Never introduce new arguments in the conclusion.

### Common Errors for Vietnamese Learners — Task 2

| Error type | Incorrect | Correct |
|---|---|---|
| Article omission | "Genetic engineering is solution" | "Genetic engineering is **a** solution" |
| Countable/uncountable | "many researches" | "much research" / "many studies" |
| Subject-verb agreement | "the benefits of this technology is" | "the benefits of this technology **are**" |
| Overly direct opinion | "I think everyone must agree" | "It can be argued that..." / "There is a strong case for..." |
| Conjunction overuse | "And also furthermore moreover" | Use one connector per transition |
