# IELTS SPEAKING — TEST 09

**Format:** Three parts | Total time: 11-14 minutes
**Topics this test:** Health & Wellbeing / Science & Innovation / Problems & Challenges / Stress and Mental Health

---

## Part 1 (4-5 minutes)

*The examiner will ask you questions on familiar topics. Give extended answers — 2-4 sentences. Do not give one-word or one-sentence replies.*

---

### Topic 1: Health & Wellbeing

**Question 1:** Do you think you lead a healthy lifestyle overall?

**Model Answer:**
"I try to, though I'd be the first to admit I'm not always consistent. I make an effort to exercise a few times a week — usually cycling or swimming — and I'm fairly mindful about what I eat. However, I know I could do better with sleep; I often stay up late, which I'm aware isn't ideal for overall health."

**Vocabulary notes:** *consistent, mindful, ideal for overall health*

---

**Question 2:** What do you do to manage stress in your daily life?

**Model Answer:**
"I've found that exercise is the most effective stress reliever for me — even a thirty-minute walk can completely change my mood. I also try to maintain clear boundaries between work and personal time, which isn't always easy in a culture where being constantly reachable is expected. Meditation is something I've experimented with and found genuinely helpful during particularly pressured periods."

**Vocabulary notes:** *stress reliever, clear boundaries, constantly reachable, genuinely helpful*

---

**Question 3:** Do you think modern life is becoming more or less stressful?

**Model Answer:**
"More stressful, I think, though in different ways than previous generations. The connectivity that technology enables means it's genuinely difficult to switch off — work follows you home, social media creates constant comparison, and the pace of change is disorienting. That said, previous generations faced different pressures — harder physical labour, greater economic insecurity for many — so it's not straightforward to compare."

**Vocabulary notes:** *connectivity, switch off, disorienting, economic insecurity, not straightforward to compare*

---

**Question 4:** How has your attitude to health changed as you've got older?

**Model Answer:**
"Quite significantly, actually. When I was younger, health felt like something I could take for granted — I could eat and sleep erratically without obvious consequences. As I've reached my mid-twenties, I've become much more aware of how daily habits compound over time. I'm more intentional now about sleep, movement, and nutrition in a way my younger self definitely wasn't."

**Vocabulary notes:** *take for granted, erratically, compound over time, intentional*

---

### Topic 2: Science & Innovation

**Question 5:** Are you interested in science? What area of science interests you most?

**Model Answer:**
"I'm genuinely fascinated by science, particularly the life sciences and their intersection with technology. Genetics has captivated me ever since learning about the human genome project — the idea that our entire biological blueprint fits inside every single cell is extraordinary. More recently, I've been following developments in gene editing with great interest, which connects directly to some of the most pressing ethical debates of our time."

**Vocabulary notes:** *fascinated, intersection, captivated, biological blueprint, pressing ethical debates*

---

**Question 6:** Did you enjoy science subjects at school?

**Model Answer:**
"Quite a bit, though I found it depended enormously on the teacher. When science was taught as a set of facts to memorise, I found it tedious. However, when I had teachers who framed it as a process of investigation — asking why and how rather than just what — I found it completely absorbing. Physics was probably my weakest subject, but biology and chemistry I genuinely looked forward to."

**Vocabulary notes:** *depended enormously on, tedious, framed as, absorbing, looked forward to*

---

**Question 7:** Have you heard about any recent scientific discoveries that impressed you?

**Model Answer:**
"Yes — the advances in mRNA vaccine technology during the COVID-19 pandemic genuinely impressed me, not just because of the speed of development, but because it demonstrated that a technology which had been in development for decades could be rapidly deployed when the need became sufficiently urgent. It made me reflect on how many other transformative technologies might be waiting in laboratories for the right conditions to be applied at scale."

**Vocabulary notes:** *advances in mRNA technology, rapidly deployed, transformative technologies, applied at scale*

---

**Question 8:** Do you think science and technology will solve climate change?

**Model Answer:**
"I think they're necessary but not sufficient conditions for solving it. The technologies — solar, wind, battery storage, carbon capture — are developing rapidly enough to make deep decarbonisation technically feasible. The harder problem is political and economic: ensuring those technologies are deployed quickly enough, equitably enough, and against the resistance of incumbent industries. Science can provide the tools; whether humanity uses them wisely is a separate question entirely."

**Vocabulary notes:** *necessary but not sufficient, decarbonisation, technically feasible, incumbent industries, equitably*

---

### Topic 3: Problems & Challenges

**Question 9:** What is the biggest challenge you face in your daily life?

**Model Answer:**
"Honestly, time management is my greatest daily challenge — specifically, the gap between how much I want to accomplish and what's realistically achievable in a day. I have a tendency to underestimate how long tasks take, which creates a persistent low-level stress when I'm always running slightly behind my own expectations. I've been working on setting more realistic daily goals, which has helped somewhat, though I haven't entirely resolved it."

**Vocabulary notes:** *time management, tendency to underestimate, persistent low-level stress, realistic goals*

---

**Question 10:** When you face a difficult problem, what do you typically do first?

**Model Answer:**
"My first instinct is to write the problem down, which sounds simple but makes an enormous difference. When a challenge stays abstract in my head, it tends to feel overwhelming. The moment I articulate it on paper — describe exactly what the problem is, what I know, and what I don't yet know — it becomes immediately more manageable. From there I try to identify whether it's something I can address alone or whether I need outside advice."

**Vocabulary notes:** *first instinct, articulate, abstract, overwhelming, manageable*

---

**Question 11:** Have you ever had to make a very difficult decision? What was it?

**Model Answer:**
"Yes — a few years ago I faced a significant choice about whether to accept a career opportunity that would require relocating away from my family. The opportunity was professionally exciting, but the personal costs were real — I was very close to my family and hadn't lived away from them before. I spent several weeks genuinely torn, consulting people I trusted, before ultimately deciding to go. Looking back, it was the right decision, though there were difficult periods of adjustment."

**Vocabulary notes:** *significant choice, relocating, genuinely torn, consulting, periods of adjustment*

---

**Question 12:** Do you think you have improved your ability to deal with challenges as you've grown older?

**Model Answer:**
"Significantly, yes. When I was younger, setbacks felt much more permanent and defining than they actually were — a failure or disappointment would feel catastrophic in a way I can now recognise was disproportionate. What's changed is perspective: I've accumulated enough experience of recovering from difficult situations to trust that most challenges are temporary. I'm also considerably more willing to ask for help than my younger self was, which makes a practical difference."

**Vocabulary notes:** *setbacks, catastrophic, disproportionate, accumulated experience, perspective*

---

## Part 2 — Cue Card (3-4 minutes)

*Read the card carefully. You have 1 minute to prepare notes. Then speak for 1-2 minutes.*

---

**Describe a time when you had to deal with a particularly difficult or stressful situation.**

You should say:
- what the situation was
- why it was so difficult or stressful
- how you dealt with it
- and explain what you learned from this experience

---

### Preparation notes (1 minute)

```
WHAT:    Exam + family health crisis at the same time (~18 months ago)
WHY:     Emotional weight + practical pressure simultaneously
HOW:     Small daily units / accepted help / radical prioritisation
LEARNED: Value of asking for help / prioritisation under compound stress
```

---

### Model Response (~200 words, Band 7-8)

"I'd like to describe a situation from about eighteen months ago when I was simultaneously preparing for an important academic exam, managing a family health crisis, and trying to keep up with work responsibilities. The timing was genuinely awful — my mother was unwell, which meant frequent hospital visits, while my exam was only three weeks away and required intensive preparation.

What made it so difficult was the emotional weight of the family situation layered on top of the practical pressure of the exam. I found it almost impossible to concentrate, and there were days when I questioned whether pushing through with the exam was even the right priority.

What ultimately helped was breaking the challenge into much smaller units. Rather than thinking about the exam as a whole, I focused on preparing just one topic area per day. I also accepted help from friends for the first time in a very long while — someone would sit with my mother at the hospital while I had a few hours to study.

I passed the exam, and my mother recovered. What I carried forward is a much greater appreciation for asking for help, and an understanding that managing multiple stressors simultaneously requires radical prioritisation rather than trying to maintain normal performance across everything."

---

### Follow-up Question

**"Do you think stressful experiences make people stronger?"**

**Model Answer:**
"I think they can, but it's not inevitable. A lot depends on whether the person has adequate support and resources to process the experience rather than simply survive it. Stress without recovery can be as damaging as the original stressor — what builds resilience, I think, is the reflection that comes after, not the stress itself. So I'd say stressful experiences create the *opportunity* for growth, but that opportunity has to be actively taken."

**Vocabulary notes:** *inevitable, adequate support, resilience, reflection, opportunity for growth*

## Part 3 — Discussion (4-5 minutes)

*The examiner will ask more abstract, discussion-style questions linked to the Part 2 topic. Give extended answers with reasons, examples, and hedging language.*

---

**Question 1:** Why do you think stress and anxiety are more commonly discussed today than in previous generations?

**Model Answer (~80 words):**
"Several factors converge here. Greater psychological awareness means people now have language for experiences that previous generations endured in silence. Social media creates constant comparison and an always-on connectivity that is genuinely unprecedented. The pace of technological and social change is also disorienting for many people. I'd note, though, that increased discussion doesn't necessarily mean increased prevalence — we may partly be measuring and naming something that was always present but never acknowledged or addressed."

---

**Question 2:** Should governments invest more in mental health services? Why?

**Model Answer (~80 words):**
"Unquestionably. The economic case alone is compelling: mental health conditions cost global economies enormous sums through lost productivity and disability claims. Beyond economics, equitable access to psychological care is a matter of basic healthcare provision — there's no logical reason to treat mental health differently from physical health in terms of public funding. In many countries, mental health services are chronically underfunded relative to the scale of need, with serious consequences for individuals and communities alike."

---

**Question 3:** Do you think the relationship between technology and mental health is mostly positive or negative?

**Model Answer (~85 words):**
"I think it's genuinely complicated and resists a simple positive-or-negative verdict. Technology enables real benefits: access to information, mental health apps, peer support communities, and teletherapy for those who lack local services. However, social media platforms in particular appear — based on growing research — to negatively affect adolescents through social comparison, cyberbullying, and algorithmic amplification of distressing content. The honest answer is probably that technology's effect depends heavily on how platforms are designed, how they're used, and who is using them."

---

**Question 4:** How important is work-life balance, and do you think it's achievable in modern society?

**Model Answer (~85 words):**
"It's critically important for sustained wellbeing and long-term productivity — research consistently shows that overwork produces diminishing and eventually negative returns. Whether it's genuinely achievable depends on profession, economic circumstances, and national culture. In high-cost cities, financial pressure makes authentic balance very difficult. Some industry cultures actively stigmatise not working excessive hours. I think balance is achievable in principle but requires deliberate structural choices — both by individuals and through policy — rather than emerging as the natural default of contemporary working life."

---

**Question 5:** Is it possible for medical technology to ever fully eliminate human suffering from disease?

**Model Answer (~80 words):**
"Almost certainly not completely, but the trajectory of progress suggests we may dramatically reduce it. Infectious diseases that were once universally lethal are now preventable or treatable. Genetic medicine offers the prospect of addressing hereditary conditions at their source. However, new diseases continue to emerge, healthcare inequalities mean advances reach some populations far later than others, and ageing produces conditions that are manageable but not eliminable. Full elimination is probably utopian — significant, meaningful reduction is a realistic and worthwhile goal."

---

**Question 6:** What ethical limits should exist on medical and genetic research?

**Model Answer (~85 words):**
"The foundational limits should be informed consent, minimisation of harm, equitable access to benefits, and genuinely independent oversight. In genetics specifically, the most contested boundary is the distinction between therapeutic and enhancement applications. Most ethicists broadly accept using genetic technology to prevent serious disease while remaining sceptical of selecting for traits like intelligence or physical appearance. The concern is less with any individual intervention than with the systemic inequalities that emerge when such technologies become socially stratified by wealth."

---

**Question 7:** Do individuals have a responsibility to look after their own health, or should governments play a larger role?

**Model Answer (~85 words):**
"Both — and I resist framings that pit individual responsibility against government responsibility, because they're genuinely complementary rather than competing. Individuals have real agency over many health behaviours. However, the social determinants of health — income, housing, food environment, working conditions — are primarily shaped by policy, not individual choice. Blaming individuals for health outcomes that are substantially structurally determined is both intellectually inaccurate and morally questionable. Governments have a clear responsibility to create environments where healthy choices are accessible rather than the exclusive privilege of the wealthy."

---

**Question 8:** How might our understanding of the human body and health change in the next fifty years?

**Model Answer (~85 words):**
"Dramatically, I'd expect. Genomic medicine will likely enable personalised treatments matched to individual genetic profiles. Advances in microbiome research — the complex ecosystem of bacteria within us — may reshape our understanding of immunity, mental health, and metabolism. Artificial intelligence applied to medical imaging is already showing diagnostic accuracy that rivals specialists. There may also be fundamental shifts in how we understand ageing — whether it's an inevitable biological process or a modifiable condition. The boundaries between disease prevention and enhancement will become more, not less, contested."

---

**Question 9:** Some people argue that the pursuit of longevity is vanity rather than wisdom. What do you think?

**Model Answer (~85 words):**
"I think that framing creates a false choice. Pursuing longer life in better health isn't inherently vain — for most people it reflects a desire to remain present for relationships and continue contributing meaningfully. The more substantive critique is about resource allocation: investing billions in extending the lives of already-wealthy individuals, while preventable diseases claim millions in poorer countries, reflects genuinely misplaced priorities. Longevity research isn't problematic in itself; the question is who benefits and whether it displaces more urgent global health priorities."

---

**Question 10:** Should wealthy countries be obligated to share their medical research and vaccine technology with developing nations?

**Model Answer (~85 words):**
"I think there are compelling moral and practical arguments for exactly this. Morally, the country of one's birth seems an arbitrary basis for determining access to life-saving medicine. Practically, the COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated that health crises in poorly vaccinated populations don't remain contained — they generate variants that eventually affect everyone. TRIPS Agreement waivers and compulsory licensing mechanisms exist for precisely this reason. The argument that intellectual property rights should be absolute loses credibility when it costs lives at scale."

## Speaking Assessment Criteria Reference

| Criterion | What the examiner assesses |
|---|---|
| Fluency & Coherence | Speaks at length without excessive hesitation; ideas connected logically |
| Lexical Resource | Range and accuracy of vocabulary; ability to paraphrase |
| Grammatical Range & Accuracy | Variety of structures; frequency of errors |
| Pronunciation | Clear delivery; stress and intonation; intelligibility |

**Band 6.5 target:** Speak in sentences, use some complex vocabulary, occasional errors acceptable
**Band 7.0 target:** Extended responses, good range, errors don't impede communication
**Band 7.5+ target:** Natural hedging, academic vocabulary, flexible use of grammar

## Useful Hedging Phrases for Part 3

- "That's an interesting question — I think on balance..."
- "It depends to a significant extent on..."
- "I'd argue that this is more nuanced than it might first appear..."
- "The evidence seems to suggest that..."
- "I'm not entirely certain, but my impression is..."
- "There are strong arguments on both sides, though I lean towards..."
- "That said, I think it's worth acknowledging..."
