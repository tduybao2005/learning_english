# IELTS SPEAKING — TEST 06

**Topics:** Sport & Fitness / Health & Wellbeing / Community & Neighbourhood
**Band Target:** 6.5–8.0
**Duration:** 11–14 minutes total

---

## Part 1 (4–5 minutes)

*The examiner asks short questions about familiar topics. Give natural, extended answers of 2–4 sentences.*

---

### Topic 1: Sport & Exercise

**1. Do you play any sports or exercise regularly?**

Model Answer:
"Yes, I try to stay fairly active. I go for a run about three times a week — nothing competitive, just 30 to 40 minutes around my neighbourhood. I find it really helps clear my head, especially after a long day of studying or working. I also do some light stretching in the evenings, which I started doing after I had some back problems from sitting at a desk too long."

*Key vocabulary: competitive / clear my head / light stretching / back problems*

---

**2. Did you do a lot of sport when you were younger?**

Model Answer:
"Quite a lot, actually. At school I played football with my classmates almost every afternoon — it was just what everyone did. I also went swimming fairly regularly because my parents thought it was an important life skill. Looking back, I think I was more naturally active then because free time automatically meant going outside, whereas now I have to make a deliberate effort to exercise."

*Key vocabulary: life skill / naturally active / deliberate effort / free time*

---

**3. Do you think it is important for children to participate in team sports?**

Model Answer:
"I do, yes — though I think the emphasis should be on participation rather than competition. Team sports teach children things that are very difficult to learn in a classroom: how to collaborate, how to manage disappointment when you lose, how to support teammates who are struggling, and how to communicate under pressure. These are genuinely useful life skills. I'd be wary of pushing children into overly competitive environments too early, though, as that can actually put them off sport altogether."

*Key vocabulary: collaboration / manage disappointment / communication under pressure / overly competitive*

---

**4. Has the way you stay fit changed since you were a child?**

Model Answer:
"Significantly, yes. As a child, exercise was just play — I didn't think of it as 'keeping fit' at all. Now it requires much more conscious effort and planning. I also pay more attention to things like sleep and nutrition than I used to, because I've noticed much more clearly how they affect my energy levels and concentration. In some ways I'm more informed about health now, but I probably move my body less naturally than I did as a child."

*Key vocabulary: conscious effort / nutrition / energy levels / concentration*

---

### Topic 2: Health & Wellbeing

**5. Do you think you have a generally healthy lifestyle?**

Model Answer:
"Reasonably healthy, I'd say, though with room for improvement. I eat fairly balanced meals most of the time, exercise a few times a week, and try to get enough sleep. Where I fall short is probably stress management — I tend to take on too many things at once and find it difficult to fully switch off, which I know isn't good for long-term health. I've been trying to get better at setting boundaries around work and study time."

*Key vocabulary: balanced meals / stress management / switch off / setting boundaries*

---

**6. What do you think is the most important thing a person can do to stay healthy?**

Model Answer:
"If I had to pick one thing, I'd say sleep — it's probably the most underestimated pillar of health. The research on sleep deprivation is quite alarming: consistently getting fewer than seven hours a night is linked to significantly increased risks of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, depression, and even reduced immune function. People often sacrifice sleep in favour of productivity, but the evidence suggests this is deeply counterproductive in the long run."

*Key vocabulary: sleep deprivation / cardiovascular disease / immune function / counterproductive*

---

**7. Do you think people in your country are becoming healthier or less healthy?**

Model Answer:
"It's quite mixed, honestly. On the positive side, there's much greater awareness of nutrition and fitness than there was a generation ago, and gym culture has become genuinely mainstream among younger people in cities. On the other hand, sedentary desk-based work is increasingly the norm, stress levels seem to be rising, and the availability of cheap, highly processed food has increased dramatically. I think the picture varies enormously between urban and rural areas and between different income groups."

*Key vocabulary: mainstream / sedentary / processed food / income groups*

---

**8. How do you manage stress in your daily life?**

Model Answer:
"Honestly, it's something I'm still figuring out. The things that help me most are physical exercise, which gives me a genuine mental reset, and talking to close friends — not necessarily about the stressful thing itself, but just spending time with people I feel comfortable with. I've also found that having a clear end to the working day — closing my laptop and doing something completely different — makes a big difference to how I feel the next morning."

*Key vocabulary: mental reset / comfortable / clear end to the working day / makes a difference*

---

### Topic 3: Community & Neighbourhood

**9. Do you know your neighbours well?**

Model Answer:
"Not as well as I probably should. I know a couple of neighbours by sight and we exchange greetings, but I wouldn't say we have a real relationship. I think this is fairly common in cities — people are busy and tend to move in and out frequently, so there isn't much opportunity to build real connections. I grew up in a smaller town where people knew each other much better, and I do sometimes miss that sense of community."

*Key vocabulary: exchange greetings / sense of community / build connections / frequently*

---

**10. Is your neighbourhood a good place to live? Why?**

Model Answer:
"On balance, yes. It's convenient — there are good transport links, plenty of cafés and shops nearby, and it feels reasonably safe. The main drawback is noise, to be honest. I live near a busy road and the traffic can be quite disruptive, especially at night. I also think it lacks proper green spaces — the nearest park is a 20-minute walk away, which means people don't really use it spontaneously."

*Key vocabulary: convenient / transport links / drawback / spontaneously*

---

**11. What would make your local area a better place to live?**

Model Answer:
"The thing that would make the biggest difference, I think, is more accessible green space — a small park or community garden within walking distance. Research consistently shows that proximity to green space improves mental wellbeing and encourages physical activity. I'd also love better cycling infrastructure, because at the moment the roads feel genuinely dangerous for cyclists. Better community events and markets would also help people feel more connected to where they live."

*Key vocabulary: green space / community garden / cycling infrastructure / community events*

---

**12. Do you think community spirit is stronger in rural areas or in cities?**

Model Answer:
"Generally speaking, I think rural areas tend to have stronger community spirit in the traditional sense — people know each other, support each other through difficulties, and participate in local events. Cities, by contrast, offer a different kind of community: people often find deep connections through shared interests or identities rather than simply geography. An online gaming community or a neighbourhood running club in a city can create very strong bonds, just not in the same place-based way. So I'd say the type of community differs more than the strength."

*Key vocabulary: community spirit / place-based / shared interests / bonds*

---

## Part 2 (3–4 minutes)

**Cue Card:** "Describe a sporting event or competition that you attended or watched that you particularly enjoyed."

You should say:
- what the event was
- where and when it took place
- what happened
- and explain why you found it enjoyable

**Preparation time:** 1 minute

---

### Model Response (~200 words)

"I'd like to talk about a football match I watched about a year ago at a local stadium. It was a national league game between two of the top clubs in Vietnam — I went with my brother and two friends, and it was actually the first time I'd watched a professional football match live rather than on television.

What made it so memorable wasn't even the quality of the football, though the standard was higher than I expected. It was the atmosphere. The stadium was completely packed, and both sets of supporters were singing and chanting throughout the entire match. There was a moment in the second half when the home team scored a brilliant long-range goal, and the eruption of noise from the crowd was unlike anything I'd ever experienced — it was almost physical, like a wave hitting you. We were on our feet for about five minutes just celebrating.

The home team won 2–1, which helped, of course. But what I'll remember most is the shared experience of being in that crowd and feeling part of something much larger than myself. Watching football on a screen is convenient, but it captures almost nothing of what it actually feels like to be there."

*Key vocabulary: atmosphere / supporters / chanting / eruption of noise / shared experience*

---

**Follow-up question:** "Do you think live sport is more enjoyable than watching on television?"

Model Answer:
"For most sports, I think live is a completely different and richer experience, even if the view of the action is sometimes worse. What you gain in atmosphere — the shared emotion, the unpredictability of being there, the physical sensation of crowd noise — more than compensates for the better camera angles you get at home. The exception might be technical sports like snooker or golf, where the broadcast commentary and close-up cameras actually add to your understanding of what's happening."

## Part 3 (4–5 minutes)

*Abstract discussion questions linked to sport, health, and government responsibility.*

---

**1. How important is sport in building national identity and social cohesion?**

Model Answer:
"Sport plays a genuinely significant role in national identity, perhaps more powerfully than most other cultural institutions. Major international competitions like the World Cup or the Olympics create moments of shared national experience that transcend class, region, and political affiliation. However, I think the effect can be both positive and negative — at its best, shared sporting pride builds inclusive national identity, but at its worst, it can amplify nationalism and hostility towards other nations. The key is whether sport is used to celebrate achievement and shared humanity or to demarcate 'us' versus 'them'."

---

**2. Should governments spend public money on elite sport programmes and Olympic teams?**

Model Answer:
"This is genuinely contested territory, and I think a balanced approach is reasonable. Elite sport programmes do have demonstrated benefits: national sporting success raises a country's international profile, can inspire participation in sport among the general population — the so-called 'trickle-down effect' — and contributes to national pride. However, the economic case for very large Olympic programme investments is weak: the evidence that elite medals translate into mass participation is actually quite thin. I would argue that governments should prioritise funding for accessible community sport facilities over elite programmes, as the public health returns are far clearer."

---

**3. How has the commercialisation of sport affected the relationship between clubs and their supporters?**

Model Answer:
"Commercialisation has fundamentally altered the nature of many professional sports, often in ways that supporters find alienating. The most visible example is football, where top clubs are now global media and entertainment brands as much as sporting institutions. Ticket prices have risen to levels that exclude working-class supporters who were historically the core fanbase. Foreign ownership structures can prioritise global brand value over local community connection. At the same time, commercialisation has produced a level of financial investment that has genuinely raised the quality of elite sport. The challenge is whether any of this wealth filters down to grassroots sport, which is where most people actually play."

---

**4. What responsibility should governments take for the physical health of their citizens?**

Model Answer:
"I think government responsibility for public health is real and substantial, though it must be balanced against individual liberty. Governments can create environments that make healthy choices easier: urban planning that encourages walking and cycling, food labelling requirements, taxes on high-sugar products, funding for free exercise facilities, and public health campaigns. These are legitimate uses of state power because the costs of a sedentary, unhealthy population — in healthcare spending, reduced productivity, and human suffering — are borne collectively. The line I'd draw is at measures that feel coercive rather than enabling. Governments should make the healthy choice the easy choice, not criminalise the unhealthy one."

---

**5. Do you think competition in sport teaches values that are useful in other areas of life?**

Model Answer:
"I think sport at its best teaches genuinely transferable values: resilience in the face of failure, the discipline of consistent practice, the ability to perform under pressure, and how to compete fairly while accepting outcomes you don't control. These are clearly applicable beyond sport. However, I think highly competitive environments can also instil problematic values — a win-at-all-costs mentality, an inability to accept loss graciously, or the equation of personal worth with performance outcomes. The values that sport teaches depend enormously on the coaching culture surrounding it, which is why the quality of youth sports coaching matters so much."

---

**6. How has social media changed the world of professional sport?**

Model Answer:
"Social media has transformed professional sport in several dimensions simultaneously. For athletes, it has created direct channels of communication with fans that bypass traditional media, allowing them to control their own narratives but also exposing them to unprecedented levels of online abuse. For sports organisations, it has created enormous new revenue streams through digital content but has also accelerated the news cycle dramatically, meaning that controversies play out in hours rather than days. For fans, it has created richer engagement — real-time statistics, behind-the-scenes content, interactive communities — but has arguably reduced the patience for slow-build narratives that characterised traditional sports broadcasting."

---

**7. Is there too much emphasis on winning in competitive sport, rather than participation?**

Model Answer:
"At the elite level, emphasis on winning is inherent and appropriate — that is what elite competition is for. The more concerning trend is when a results-oriented culture permeates youth and grassroots sport, where the primary value should be enjoyment, physical literacy, and social development. The evidence in sports psychology is fairly consistent: excessive emphasis on winning outcomes in young athletes increases dropout rates, raises anxiety, and reduces long-term participation. Paradoxically, a mastery-oriented culture — focused on personal improvement rather than defeating opponents — tends to produce better long-term athletic development as well as healthier psychological outcomes."

---

**8. How might technology change the way people exercise and stay healthy in the future?**

Model Answer:
"The trajectory seems clear: technology is moving towards increasingly personalised health monitoring and intervention. Wearable devices that already track heart rate, sleep patterns, and activity levels will become more sophisticated, potentially monitoring blood glucose, hydration, and stress biomarkers continuously. This data, combined with AI analysis, could allow for genuinely individualised exercise and nutrition recommendations rather than generic advice. The concern is a two-tier system where those with the resources to access premium health technology gain further advantages over those who cannot, potentially widening existing health inequalities rather than reducing them."

---

**9. What role should schools play in promoting healthy lifestyles among children?**

Model Answer:
"Schools have a crucial role, and I think it extends well beyond the physical education curriculum. The school environment shapes children's relationship with movement, food, and health habits for decades. This means PE lessons that genuinely engage all children — not just those who are already sporty — as well as healthy school meal programmes, active commuting initiatives like walking buses, and health literacy education that gives young people the knowledge to make informed choices. The challenge is that schools are already under enormous pressure from academic demands, and health education is frequently the first thing to be squeezed when curriculum time is tight."

---

**10. Do you think there is a connection between physical health and mental wellbeing?**

Model Answer:
"Absolutely, and the evidence for this connection is now very strong. Regular aerobic exercise has demonstrated antidepressant effects comparable to medication for mild to moderate depression, and it is consistently associated with reduced anxiety, improved cognitive function, better sleep quality, and enhanced self-esteem. The mechanisms are multiple: exercise releases endorphins and neurotransmitters like serotonin and dopamine; it reduces cortisol (the stress hormone); it improves sleep architecture; and for social sports, it provides connection and community. What is perhaps underappreciated is that the relationship runs in both directions — mental health conditions also profoundly affect physical health behaviours and outcomes."

## Speaking Tips for Vietnamese Learners

**Common errors to avoid:**
- "I am very interesting in sport" → "I am very interested in sport"
- "People in my country is..." → "People in my country are..."
- "The sport, it is..." → "Sport is..." (avoid topic-comment structure from Vietnamese)
- "I have went to..." → "I have been to..." / "I went to..."

**Vocabulary for health and sport topics:**
- healthy lifestyle / maintain fitness / physical activity / sedentary behaviour
- mental wellbeing / stress management / work-life balance / mindfulness
- competitive / teamwork / sportsmanship / grassroots sport / elite athlete
- cardiovascular health / immune system / aerobic exercise / endorphins

**Extending Part 3 answers:**
Structure: **Position → Reason → Evidence/Example → Qualification**
Example: "I think [position], because [reason]. For example, [evidence]. However, [qualification]."
