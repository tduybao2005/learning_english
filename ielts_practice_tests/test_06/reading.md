## PASSAGE 1

### The Importance of Sleep for Health

**A** Sleep is one of the most fundamental biological necessities of the human body, yet it remains among the least understood aspects of daily life. Adults in industrialised nations average 6.8 hours of sleep per night — considerably less than the seven to nine hours that sleep scientists recommend for optimal health. Chronic sleep deprivation, defined as consistently obtaining insufficient sleep over an extended period, has been linked to a striking range of adverse health outcomes, including increased risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, obesity, and mental health conditions such as depression and anxiety. The immune system is particularly vulnerable to insufficient sleep: people who sleep fewer than six hours per night are four times more likely to develop a common cold when exposed to the virus than those who sleep seven hours or more. Beyond disease risk, even moderate sleep restriction produces measurable cognitive impairment comparable to legal levels of alcohol intoxication.

**B** The sleep that most people consider a single, unbroken state is actually composed of several distinct stages that cycle repeatedly throughout the night. Sleep scientists distinguish two broad categories: non-rapid eye movement (NREM) sleep and rapid eye movement (REM) sleep. NREM sleep itself has three stages, progressing from light sleep in stages one and two to deep slow-wave sleep in stage three, during which tissue repair and immune system maintenance primarily occur. REM sleep, which typically first occurs approximately ninety minutes after sleep onset, is characterised by vivid dreaming, temporary muscle paralysis, and heightened brain activity that closely resembles the waking state. Research has consistently demonstrated that REM sleep plays a critical role in memory consolidation: during this stage, the brain processes and integrates the day's learning, transferring information from short-term to long-term storage.

**C** The timing and quality of sleep are regulated by two interacting biological systems: the circadian rhythm and homeostatic sleep pressure. The circadian rhythm is an approximately twenty-four-hour internal clock that governs a wide range of physiological processes, including body temperature, hormone secretion, and alertness levels. A central mechanism of this system is melatonin, a hormone produced and released by the pineal gland — a small pea-sized structure located deep in the centre of the brain — in direct response to darkness. As evening light levels fall, melatonin secretion rises steadily, inducing feelings of drowsiness and signalling to the body that sleep is approaching. Melatonin levels peak during the middle of the night and gradually diminish as dawn approaches, preparing the body for waking.

**D** Perhaps the most significant modern disruption to healthy sleep is the pervasive use of electronic screens that emit blue-spectrum light. Blue light wavelengths are particularly effective at suppressing melatonin secretion, because the photosensitive retinal cells that communicate light information to the circadian system are especially sensitive to this portion of the light spectrum. Laboratory studies have demonstrated that two hours of tablet or smartphone use before bedtime can reduce melatonin levels by as much as twenty-two percent and delay the onset of sleep by up to ninety minutes. The proliferation of smartphones and tablets has meant that billions of people worldwide are now routinely exposing themselves to potent circadian disruptors in the hours immediately before they intend to sleep.

**E** Sleep disorders are extraordinarily prevalent in the modern world. Insomnia — defined as persistent difficulty initiating or maintaining sleep despite adequate opportunity — is the most common. Clinicians distinguish between acute insomnia, which is a short-term response to identifiable stressors such as bereavement, examinations, or job loss, and which typically resolves within a few weeks once the stressor passes, and chronic insomnia, which is characterised by sleep difficulties occurring at least three nights per week for a minimum of three months and which often persists without deliberate intervention. Chronic insomnia affects approximately ten percent of the adult population in developed countries and is associated with significantly elevated rates of depression, anxiety, and workplace absenteeism.

**F** Evidence-based recommendations for improving sleep quality — collectively known as sleep hygiene — include maintaining a consistent sleep schedule seven days a week, keeping the bedroom cool and dark, avoiding caffeine after midday, and limiting alcohol consumption, which many people mistakenly believe aids sleep but which in fact suppresses REM sleep and causes frequent night-time awakenings. Regular physical exercise has been shown to improve sleep quality over time, though vigorous exercise within two to three hours of bedtime may paradoxically delay sleep onset by elevating core body temperature and cortisol levels. For those suffering from chronic insomnia, cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) has demonstrated effectiveness superior to sleeping medication in multiple randomised controlled trials and is now recommended as the first-line treatment by leading sleep medicine organisations worldwide.

## PASSAGE 2

### A History of Vaccination

**A** The history of vaccination stretches back far earlier than most people realise. Long before the word "vaccine" existed, populations in China and the Ottoman Empire had observed that deliberately exposing healthy individuals to small amounts of smallpox material could induce a mild infection that protected against the far more lethal form of the disease. This practice, known as variolation, involved introducing material taken from smallpox pustules beneath the skin or into the nasal passages. Historical records indicate that variolation was practised in China as early as the tenth century and was brought to England in the early eighteenth century by Lady Mary Wortley Montagu, who had encountered it during her time in Istanbul. While variolation reduced mortality considerably compared to naturally acquired smallpox, it was not without risk: a small percentage of those who underwent the procedure developed severe illness and died.

**B** The decisive transformation came in 1796, when the English country physician Edward Jenner conducted what is often described as the first controlled vaccination experiment in history. Jenner had noticed that milkmaids in rural Gloucestershire who contracted cowpox — a mild disease transmitted from infected cows — appeared to be consistently protected against smallpox. Working on this observation, Jenner took material from a cowpox lesion on the hand of a dairymaid named Sarah Nelmes and inoculated an eight-year-old boy, James Phipps, with it. Six weeks later, he exposed the boy to live smallpox material; Phipps showed no signs of infection. Jenner published his findings in 1798, proposing the term "vaccine" from the Latin word for cow, *vacca*, and his work would form the scientific foundation upon which all subsequent immunology was built.

**C** It was the French chemist and microbiologist Louis Pasteur who most significantly extended Jenner's principles beyond smallpox. Building on his germ theory of disease — the revolutionary demonstration that infectious illnesses are caused by specific microscopic organisms — Pasteur developed the concept of attenuation: the deliberate weakening of pathogens so that they could stimulate an immune response without causing the disease itself. In 1881, in a celebrated public demonstration at Pouilly-le-Fort, Pasteur vaccinated a group of sheep against anthrax using his attenuated bacterial preparation, then challenged both vaccinated and unvaccinated animals with live anthrax. All vaccinated sheep survived; all unvaccinated sheep died. Five years later, in 1886, Pasteur saved the life of a nine-year-old boy, Joseph Meister, who had been severely bitten by a rabid dog, using an attenuated rabies preparation — the first use of a vaccine in an emergency medical setting.

**D** The twentieth century witnessed an unprecedented acceleration in vaccine development. The discovery of cell culture techniques enabled scientists to grow viruses in the laboratory rather than relying on animal hosts, dramatically reducing production times and costs. Vaccines against diphtheria, pertussis (whooping cough), and tetanus were progressively introduced during the 1920s and 1930s, and their widespread adoption led to dramatic reductions in childhood mortality. The development of yellow fever and influenza vaccines followed in the late 1930s and 1940s. By the second half of the century, immunisation programmes had become a cornerstone of public health policy in most developed nations, and international organisations were beginning to coordinate global vaccination campaigns.

**E** Few achievements in the history of public health match the campaign to eradicate poliomyelitis. Polio, a viral disease that can cause permanent paralysis, particularly in children, reached epidemic proportions in the United States during the 1950s, generating widespread fear. The announcement in April 1955 that Jonas Salk's inactivated poliovirus vaccine had been proven safe and effective in large-scale trials was greeted with nationwide celebration. A decade later, Albert Sabin's oral polio vaccine — which could be administered on a sugar lump without the need for trained medical personnel — transformed the practical feasibility of mass immunisation, particularly in developing countries. The global polio eradication initiative, launched in 1988, has since reduced the number of annual polio cases by more than ninety-nine percent.

**F** Despite the extraordinary success of vaccination programmes, anti-vaccination sentiment has persisted throughout history and has undergone a dramatic resurgence in the twenty-first century. The origins of modern vaccine hesitancy are complex, drawing on longstanding distrust of medical institutions, misunderstandings about immunological science, and in some cases distorted reports of adverse events. A retracted 1998 paper in *The Lancet* that falsely claimed a link between the measles, mumps, and rubella (MMR) vaccine and autism generated a wave of public alarm that measurably reduced vaccination rates in several countries and contributed to renewed outbreaks of measles. The spread of misinformation through social media platforms has amplified anti-vaccination narratives, presenting public health authorities with a communication challenge that scientific evidence alone has repeatedly proven insufficient to resolve.

**G** The COVID-19 pandemic, which emerged in late 2019, presented the global scientific community with an unprecedented challenge: developing a safe and effective vaccine for a novel pathogen within months rather than the usual decade or more. The response produced a landmark technological achievement. Vaccines based on messenger RNA (mRNA) technology — an approach that had been researched for over three decades but had never previously received regulatory approval for human use — were developed, clinically trialled, and authorised within roughly a year of the pandemic's onset. Rather than introducing a weakened pathogen, mRNA vaccines deliver genetic instructions that direct the body's own cells to produce a specific viral protein, triggering an immune response. The speed and efficacy of this development demonstrated the potential of a new paradigm in vaccine technology that is now being applied to other infectious diseases, including HIV, influenza, and malaria.

## PASSAGE 3

### Economic Migration Patterns

**A** International economic migration — the movement of people across national borders primarily in search of employment and improved living standards — is among the most significant demographic phenomena of the modern era. The United Nations estimates that approximately 281 million people, representing 3.6 percent of the global population, currently live outside their country of birth. While migration has occurred throughout human history, the scale, speed, and complexity of contemporary movement have been amplified by globalisation, digital communication, and improved transportation infrastructure. Economists, sociologists, and policymakers debate migration's consequences with considerable intensity, because its effects are far from uniform: they depend heavily on the skills of the migrant, the characteristics of the sending country, the absorptive capacity of the receiving country, and the prevailing policy environment.

**B** Migration scholars distinguish between push factors — conditions in the country of origin that compel people to leave — and pull factors — conditions in the destination country that attract migrants. Push factors encompass a broad spectrum of pressures, including unemployment and underemployment, low wages, political instability, conflict, limited access to education and healthcare, and land insecurity. In recent decades, environmental degradation and climate-induced agricultural failures have emerged as increasingly important push drivers, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa and South and Southeast Asia. The decision to migrate is rarely impulsive; it typically follows careful cost-benefit analysis in which the anticipated gains at the destination are weighed against the financial, social, and psychological costs of departure.

**C** Pull factors operate simultaneously on the other side of the migration equation. Significantly higher wages — often five to ten times the income available at home — represent the most powerful attraction in most cases. Access to better healthcare, education for children, physical safety, and the presence of established diaspora communities that provide social support and employment networks are additional factors that make particular destinations attractive. The geographical distribution of migration flows is not random: they tend to follow well-established corridors shaped by historical colonial relationships, linguistic ties, and pre-existing social networks. The corridor from Mexico to the United States, from South Asia to the Gulf states, and from North Africa to Western Europe represent three of the world's most active migration routes.

**D** One of the most debated consequences of economic migration for sending countries is the phenomenon known as brain drain: the emigration of highly educated and skilled workers whose departure represents a significant loss of human capital for their country of origin. This concern is especially acute for small developing nations that have invested public resources in training doctors, nurses, engineers, and teachers, only to see these professionals depart for higher salaries in wealthier countries. Several Caribbean and Pacific island nations have lost more than forty percent of their university-educated populations to emigration. Sub-Saharan Africa loses an estimated twenty thousand skilled professionals annually to high-income countries, a figure that represents a serious constraint on development prospects, particularly in the health sector.

**E** Against the economic losses of brain drain, however, must be set the substantial economic benefits that many sending countries receive through remittances — the money that migrant workers send back to their families at home. Global remittances reached an estimated 831 billion US dollars in 2022, with low and middle-income countries receiving the large majority of this sum. Remittances represent a significant proportion of GDP in many developing nations: in countries such as Tajikistan, Tonga, and Lebanon, they account for between twenty and fifty percent of national income. Research has consistently found that remittances are more stable than foreign direct investment or overseas development aid, because they continue to flow even during global economic downturns as migrants increase transfers to support family members facing hardship. At the household level, remittances fund food, healthcare, education, and housing improvements, directly reducing poverty rates in recipient communities.

**F** For destination countries, economic migration generates a complex mixture of benefits and costs whose relative magnitude is a matter of ongoing empirical and political debate. On the economic side, migrants fill labour shortages in both high-skilled sectors — such as medicine, engineering, and finance — and low-skilled sectors such as agriculture, construction, food processing, and domestic care. They typically pay more in taxes and social contributions than they draw in public services, particularly in their early working years, providing a fiscal benefit to host-country governments. They also bring diverse perspectives and entrepreneurial energy: immigrants found a disproportionately high share of new businesses and patent applications in many destination countries. However, critics point to wage-depression effects in labour markets where migrants compete directly with native low-skilled workers, as well as pressures on housing, public services, and social cohesion in communities that have received large concentrations of migrants in a short period.

**G** Policy responses to economic migration vary widely. Several destination countries, including Canada, Australia, and New Zealand, operate points-based immigration systems that prioritise applicants with specific skills, work experience, educational qualifications, and language ability. These systems aim to maximise the economic benefit of migration while limiting strain on public services. By contrast, humanitarian migration frameworks operate under different principles, prioritising protection over economic calculation. Bilateral labour agreements between sending and receiving countries have also been used to manage migration flows, typically guaranteeing minimum wages and working conditions for migrants in exchange for cooperation on border management and return of undocumented workers.

**H** Looking ahead, climate change is widely projected to become one of the dominant drivers of both internal and international migration in the twenty-first century. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change estimates that between 216 million and 1.2 billion people could be displaced within their own countries by climate impacts by 2050, with sea-level rise, desertification, extreme weather events, and agricultural disruption being the primary mechanisms. Many of these internally displaced people will subsequently become international migrants if conditions in their home regions deteriorate sufficiently. This projected scale of climate-induced displacement presents governance challenges that existing migration frameworks, designed primarily to manage voluntary economic migration, are not equipped to handle, and demands urgent development of new international legal instruments and funding mechanisms.
