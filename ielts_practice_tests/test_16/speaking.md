# IELTS Speaking — Test 16

**Time allowed: 11–14 minutes (approx.)**

---

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*The examiner asks general questions on familiar topics. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

### Topic 1: Social Media and the Internet

**Question 1.** How often do you use social media in your daily life?

**Model Answer:** I use social media quite frequently — probably several times a day. I mainly use it to keep up with news, stay in touch with friends, and follow topics I am interested in, such as photography and science. I try not to spend too much time scrolling, but it is definitely part of my daily routine.

---

**Question 2.** Which social media platform do you find most useful and why?

**Model Answer:** I would say YouTube is the most useful platform for me personally, because I can learn new things through tutorials and documentaries rather than just consuming entertainment. It has genuinely helped me improve skills like cooking and video editing. The long-form format also feels more educational compared to short-video apps.

---

**Question 3.** Do you think the internet has changed the way people communicate with each other?

**Model Answer:** Absolutely — the internet has transformed communication in ways that would have been unimaginable a generation ago. People can now maintain friendships across different continents with almost no cost or delay. However, I do think face-to-face interaction has become less common as a result, which can sometimes make relationships feel more superficial.

---

**Question 4.** Are there any negative effects of spending too much time online?

**Model Answer:** Yes, there are several. Excessive screen time has been linked to sleep disturbance, reduced attention spans, and increased levels of anxiety, particularly among young people who compare themselves to idealised images on social media. I personally find that taking occasional breaks from technology helps me focus better and feel more present in my day-to-day life.

---

### Topic 2: Sports and Fitness

**Question 5.** Do you do any sports or physical exercise regularly?

**Model Answer:** I try to go for a run three or four times a week, and I occasionally play badminton with friends at weekends. I started running during the pandemic when gyms were closed, and I found it was a great way to clear my head. I am not particularly competitive about it — I do it mainly for the mental health benefits.

---

**Question 6.** Is sport an important part of school education in your country?

**Model Answer:** In my country, physical education is compulsory throughout primary and secondary school, and most schools have facilities for sports such as football, basketball, and volleyball. However, I think the quality of sports education varies quite a lot between urban and rural schools, and it is sometimes treated as less important than academic subjects.

---

**Question 7.** Do you prefer team sports or individual sports?

**Model Answer:** I have a slight preference for individual sports because I enjoy the personal challenge — when I improve my running time or learn a new swimming stroke, the achievement feels entirely my own. That said, I appreciate team sports for the social element they provide, and I find the camaraderie in a team environment motivating in a different way.

---

**Question 8.** Do you think professional athletes are paid too much money?

**Model Answer:** It is a complex question. On one hand, top athletes generate enormous revenues for their clubs and sponsors, so in purely economic terms their salaries reflect their market value. On the other hand, it can seem disproportionate when we compare those earnings to the salaries of teachers or nurses, who arguably contribute more directly to society.

---

### Topic 3: Shopping

**Question 9.** Do you prefer shopping online or in physical stores?

**Model Answer:** I tend to prefer online shopping for convenience — I can compare prices quickly and have items delivered to my door. However, for things like clothes or shoes I still prefer trying them on in a store before buying, since getting the right fit is difficult to judge from a photo or size chart alone.

---

**Question 10.** How has shopping changed in your country over the past decade?

**Model Answer:** Shopping in my country has changed dramatically, largely due to the rise of e-commerce platforms. Ten years ago, most people went to physical markets and shopping centres every week. Now, a significant proportion of everyday purchases — groceries, electronics, clothing — are made online, and many smaller independent shops have closed as a result of the shift.

---

**Question 11.** Do you enjoy shopping for gifts for other people?

**Model Answer:** I actually enjoy it quite a lot, particularly when I find something that feels genuinely personal to the recipient. I find the process of thinking about what someone would like or need quite satisfying. What I find difficult is shopping for people whose tastes I do not know well — I often end up second-guessing myself.

---

**Question 12.** Are there any types of shopping you dislike?

**Model Answer:** I particularly dislike grocery shopping during busy periods, such as weekends or before public holidays. The crowds, queues, and the general chaos of supermarkets at peak times make the experience quite stressful. I have actually switched to ordering groceries online partly for this reason — it saves a considerable amount of time and frustration.

---

## PART 2 — Long Turn

*The examiner gives you a topic card. You have one minute to prepare and should speak for 1–2 minutes.*

---

### Cue Card

**Describe a competition you participated in or watched.**

You should say:
- What the competition was
- When and where it took place
- What happened during the competition
- And explain why the competition was memorable to you.

---

### Model Answer — Part 2

I would like to talk about a public speaking competition I participated in during my second year of university. It was organised by the English department and was held in the main auditorium of the campus, which seated around three hundred people — far more than I had ever spoken in front of before.

The competition required each participant to deliver a prepared five-minute speech on a topic of their choice, followed by a two-minute impromptu response to a question from the judges. I chose to speak about the environmental impact of fast fashion, a subject I had been researching for an assignment at the time. There were twelve competitors in total, representing different faculties across the university.

I remember being extremely nervous in the hours leading up to my turn. My heart was racing as I walked to the podium, and for the first thirty seconds I could barely hear my own voice over the blood rushing in my ears. However, once I found my rhythm and made eye contact with a few friendly faces in the audience, I began to relax and actually enjoy the experience.

I did not win — the prize went to a philosophy student who delivered an extraordinarily confident speech on artificial intelligence. However, I was awarded second place, which exceeded my expectations considerably. What made the competition so memorable was not the result but the realisation that I was capable of addressing a large audience on a complex topic. It gave me a confidence I had not possessed before, and I think it fundamentally changed how I approach situations that require me to speak in public.

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion

*The examiner asks more abstract questions related to the Part 2 topic. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

**Question 1.** Why do you think competition is so prevalent in educational systems around the world?

**Model Answer:** I think competition in education is so widespread partly because it is seen as an efficient mechanism for identifying and rewarding talent. Ranking systems, examinations, and prizes provide clear external benchmarks that help schools, universities, and employers make comparative judgements. There is also a cultural dimension — many societies implicitly value individual achievement and view competitive success as a proxy for merit and capability.

---

**Question 2.** Do you think highly competitive school environments have a negative effect on students' mental health?

**Model Answer:** There is growing evidence to suggest they do, particularly for students who consistently underperform relative to their peers. When a child's self-worth becomes entirely tied to grades and rankings, academic setbacks can trigger anxiety and depression rather than motivating improvement. I think the key is how competition is framed — emphasising personal progress rather than comparison with others reduces much of this psychological pressure.

---

**Question 3.** How important is cooperation as a skill in the modern workplace?

**Model Answer:** Extremely important, in my view. Most significant professional projects — whether in technology, healthcare, or the arts — require teams of people with different expertise working toward a shared goal. The ability to listen, negotiate, delegate, and resolve conflicts constructively is arguably as valuable as individual technical knowledge. Many employers explicitly cite teamwork as a top priority when hiring.

---

**Question 4.** Can you think of any situations where competition between colleagues in a workplace might be harmful?

**Model Answer:** Certainly. When employees compete directly for the same promotion or bonus, they may be tempted to withhold information, undermine colleagues, or prioritise their own visible output over the team's overall success. In environments where trust is essential — such as healthcare teams or research laboratories — this kind of internal rivalry can be genuinely dangerous. A healthier model encourages individuals to compete against their own previous performance rather than against each other.

---

**Question 5.** Do you think children should be encouraged to win at all costs, or is how you play more important?

**Model Answer:** I strongly believe that the manner in which you compete matters enormously, especially for young children who are still forming their values and character. Encouraging a win-at-all-costs mentality from an early age can produce adults who resort to dishonesty or aggression when under pressure. Teaching children to compete with integrity — to respect opponents, accept losses gracefully, and prioritise effort over outcome — cultivates resilience and sportsmanship that will serve them far better in the long run.

---

**Question 6.** Are there any industries or fields where competition has produced particularly positive outcomes?

**Model Answer:** The technology sector is perhaps the most obvious example. Competition between companies like Apple, Google, and Samsung has driven rapid innovation in consumer electronics, significantly improving the quality and affordability of products available to ordinary people. Similarly, competition between pharmaceutical companies has accelerated the development of vaccines and treatments, as we saw during the COVID-19 pandemic. In these contexts, the drive to outperform rivals clearly benefits the broader public.

---

**Question 7.** How do attitudes toward competition differ between generations in your society?

**Model Answer:** I have noticed that older generations in my country tend to view competition as a straightforward virtue — something that builds character and prepares young people for the realities of working life. Younger people, in my observation, are somewhat more ambivalent. There is a growing awareness among millennials and Generation Z of the mental health costs of hyper-competitive environments, and many young professionals actively seek out workplaces that emphasise collaboration and wellbeing over individual rankings.

---

**Question 8.** Should governments regulate the level of competition in educational institutions?

**Model Answer:** I think some degree of governmental oversight is appropriate, particularly to prevent practices that demonstrably harm students — such as excessive standardised testing in primary schools or discriminatory admissions procedures. However, I would be cautious about over-regulation, as some level of academic rigour and benchmarking is necessary to maintain educational standards. A more effective approach might be to fund research into best practices and incentivise schools to adopt evidence-based assessment methods.

---

**Question 9.** In your opinion, is competitive sport a good model for teaching life skills to young people?

**Model Answer:** Yes, I think sport is one of the most effective vehicles for teaching life skills, precisely because the lessons it delivers are tangible and immediate. When a young athlete loses a match, they experience disappointment in a safe, low-stakes context and learn to recover. When their team wins through coordinated effort rather than individual brilliance, they understand the value of collective strategy. These experiences translate directly into resilience, teamwork, and emotional regulation — skills that are invaluable throughout adult life.

---

**Question 10.** Looking at the future, do you think society will become more competitive or more cooperative?

**Model Answer:** I believe the pressures of the twenty-first century — climate change, global health crises, geopolitical instability — will ultimately push societies toward greater cooperation, simply because these challenges cannot be solved by any single nation or individual acting alone. The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated both the fragility of purely competitive approaches and the remarkable things that can be achieved when governments, scientists, and institutions collaborate. That said, I suspect competition will remain a powerful force within societies, even as cooperation becomes more prominent at the international level.
