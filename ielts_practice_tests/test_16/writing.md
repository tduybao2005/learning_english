The line graph below shows the percentage of internet users in a selected country who used four major social media platforms — Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter/X — between 2015 and 2023.

### Model Answer — Task 1

The line graph illustrates changes in the proportion of internet users engaging with four social media platforms — Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter/X — over an eight-year period from 2015 to 2023.

Overall, Facebook remained the most widely used platform throughout the period, although its dominance declined steadily. Instagram and TikTok both showed considerable growth, while Twitter/X remained relatively stable across the entire timeframe.

In 2015, Facebook was used by 72 percent of internet users, far ahead of its nearest rival, Twitter/X, at 24 percent. Instagram accounted for just 22 percent at that point. By 2023, however, Facebook's share had fallen to 55 percent, while Instagram climbed to 47 percent — nearly matching Facebook's figure. TikTok, which was unavailable until approximately 2019, grew with exceptional speed, rising from 8 percent in that year to 35 percent by 2023, making it the fastest-growing platform in the dataset.

Twitter/X followed the most stable trajectory, fluctuating only marginally between 23 and 27 percent across all data points, and ending the period at its lowest recorded share of 23 percent.

In summary, the period witnessed a significant redistribution of social media usage, with newer platforms eroding Facebook's previously unchallenged lead.

## WRITING TASK 2

*You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.*

*Write about the following topic:*

> **Some people believe that children should be taught to be competitive at school. Others think that cooperation is more important than competition. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.**

*Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.*

*Write at least 250 words.*

---

### Model Answer — Task 2

In contemporary education, debate persists over whether schools should cultivate competitive attitudes or emphasise cooperative values. While both approaches offer distinct advantages, I believe that a judicious integration of the two is most beneficial for children's long-term development.

Proponents of competition argue that it is an essential preparation for adult life. The professional world — from corporate employment to academic research — rewards those who can outperform their peers, and exposing children to competitive dynamics at an early age acclimatises them to this reality. Competition can also serve as a powerful motivational tool: when students compete for academic prizes or sporting honours, many exert effort that they would not have invested in a purely cooperative environment. Countries such as South Korea and Singapore, whose educational systems emphasise academic rigour and competitive ranking, consistently achieve high scores in international assessments such as PISA, lending credibility to the competitive model.

Conversely, advocates of cooperation contend that modern workplaces increasingly demand collaborative skills that purely competitive schooling fails to develop. The ability to communicate effectively, resolve conflicts, and contribute to a shared goal is arguably more critical than individual achievement in many professional contexts. Furthermore, highly competitive classrooms can generate anxiety, erode self-esteem in lower-performing students, and discourage the kind of creative risk-taking that drives genuine intellectual growth.

In my view, the most effective approach treats competition and cooperation not as mutually exclusive but as complementary values. Schools can structure competitive activities — science fairs, debating tournaments, team sports — in ways that simultaneously demand cooperation among group members. This balanced framework equips students with both the drive to excel individually and the interpersonal skills to succeed in collaborative environments.

In conclusion, while competition can motivate and prepare students for adult realities, an exclusive focus on it risks neglecting the cooperative capacities that are equally essential. The optimal educational model harnesses both.
