## READING PASSAGE 1

### The Hidden Power of Fictional Worlds

**Paragraph A**

For centuries, critics and philosophers have debated whether reading fiction is a worthwhile intellectual pursuit or merely an indulgence. The question has gained renewed urgency in the digital age, where attention is fragmented and screen-based entertainment competes fiercely for leisure time. Yet a growing body of scientific research suggests that the act of immersing oneself in fictional narratives confers a range of cognitive and emotional benefits that extend far beyond simple entertainment. These advantages touch on empathy, social intelligence, and even neurological development, challenging long-held assumptions about the divide between art and utility.

**Paragraph B**

Central to these findings is the concept of "transportation" — the psychological phenomenon whereby readers become so absorbed in a narrative that they lose awareness of their immediate surroundings. Researchers Melanie Green and Timothy Brock coined this term in 2000 to describe how deep narrative engagement differs fundamentally from superficial reading. When a reader is transported, the boundaries between self and character begin to dissolve. This dissolution, far from being a passive escape, actively recruits neural circuits associated with theory of mind — the capacity to attribute mental states and intentions to other people.

**Paragraph C**

Neuroscientific evidence supports the idea that fiction reading engages the brain in uniquely comprehensive ways. Studies using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) have demonstrated that reading descriptions of physical actions activates the motor cortex, while reading about textures or smells stimulates the corresponding sensory regions. This neural simulation means the brain processes fictional events through many of the same pathways it uses for real experiences, blurring the boundary between imagination and lived reality. Neuroscientist David Comer Kidd and psychologist Emanuele Castano subsequently showed that readers of literary fiction — as opposed to popular genre fiction — demonstrate measurably improved performance on tests of social cognition.

**Paragraph D**

The empathy-enhancing effects of fiction appear particularly robust. A 2013 study by Mar and colleagues found that lifetime exposure to fiction, measured through an author recognition test, was a significant predictor of empathic ability, even after controlling for personality traits such as openness and agreeableness. The mechanism proposed is that fiction provides a low-stakes social simulation environment in which readers practise perspective-taking without the risks or consequences that accompany real-world social interactions. In this sense, novels may function as a kind of moral gymnasium, exercising the emotional muscles needed for compassionate and nuanced human connection.

**Paragraph E**

Beyond empathy, fiction has been shown to influence readers' attitudes and belief systems in measurable ways. Research by Raymond Mar has established correlations between heavy fiction reading and greater tolerance for ambiguity — a trait associated with psychological flexibility and creative problem-solving. Fiction habitually presents morally complex characters and unresolved situations, training the mind to sit comfortably with uncertainty rather than demanding binary resolution. Political scientists have noted that individuals who read broadly across literary traditions tend to exhibit more nuanced views on international affairs, suggesting that fiction fosters a capacity for holding multiple, sometimes contradictory, perspectives simultaneously.

**Paragraph F**

Critics of these findings caution against overstating the case. Not all fiction produces equal benefits; the quality of narrative complexity matters significantly. Studies comparing literary fiction with formula-driven genre fiction consistently find stronger cognitive and empathic effects for the former. Moreover, the direction of causation remains contested: it is plausible that individuals already high in empathy and open-mindedness are simply drawn to fiction, rather than fiction itself producing these qualities. Nevertheless, the converging evidence from psychology, neuroscience, and literary studies makes a compelling case that reading fiction is far more than a guilty pleasure — it is a cognitively demanding, emotionally enriching practice that shapes the human mind in profound and lasting ways.

## READING PASSAGE 2

### Workers Unite: The Rise of Trade Unionism

**Paragraph A**

The emergence of trade unions represents one of the most consequential developments in modern economic history. Born in the crucible of industrial capitalism, these organisations transformed the relationship between labour and capital, establishing principles of collective bargaining that continue to shape employment law and workplace culture across the globe. Their history is neither linear nor triumphant; it is a contested terrain of legislative battles, violent suppression, and gradual, hard-won recognition, punctuated by periods of dramatic expansion and painful retreat.

**Paragraph B**

The earliest precursors of trade unions appeared in Britain during the late eighteenth century, as skilled craftsmen — weavers, shoemakers, and printers — began forming what were called "combinations" to negotiate wages and resist the de-skilling effects of mechanisation. The British Parliament responded to these nascent organisations with the Combination Acts of 1799 and 1800, which effectively criminalised collective worker action. Despite severe legal penalties, including transportation to penal colonies, workers continued to organise clandestinely, demonstrating that the impulse toward collective solidarity could not be permanently suppressed by legislation alone.

**Paragraph C**

The repeal of the Combination Acts in 1824, largely due to the efforts of reformist politician Francis Place, opened a new chapter in labour history. The subsequent decades saw the rapid proliferation of trade societies, though their legal status remained ambiguous and their activities were frequently prosecuted under conspiracy laws. The formation of the Grand National Consolidated Trades Union in 1834, championed by the social reformer Robert Owen, represented an ambitious attempt to unite workers across industries and regions. It collapsed within months due to fierce employer opposition and debilitating internal divisions. The Tolpuddle Martyrs — six Dorset agricultural labourers transported to Australia in 1834 for administering unlawful oaths — became enduring symbols of both working-class resistance and state repression.

**Paragraph D**

Legal recognition arrived gradually and unevenly across different nations. In the United Kingdom, the Trade Union Act of 1871 formally acknowledged unions' right to exist, while the Trade Disputes Act of 1906 granted them immunity from civil liability, effectively protecting strike funds from employer lawsuits. In the United States, progress was slower and more turbulent. The Knights of Labor, founded in 1869, pursued an inclusive strategy that welcomed unskilled workers, women, and Black Americans, though it declined rapidly following the 1886 Haymarket Affair in Chicago. The American Federation of Labor, under the pragmatic leadership of Samuel Gompers, took a more conservative craft-based approach that proved more durable, and the movement gained decisive federal backing with the passage of the National Labor Relations Act of 1935 — also known as the Wagner Act — which guaranteed workers' right to organise and bargain collectively.

**Paragraph E**

The mid-twentieth century marked the high-water period of union influence throughout the industrialised world. In the United Kingdom, trade union membership reached its peak of approximately 13 million in 1979, representing over half the working population. In the United States, union membership as a proportion of the workforce peaked in the mid-1950s at around 35 percent. During this period, unions successfully negotiated substantial wage increases, standardised working hours, paid holidays, and occupational health and safety regulations that became permanently embedded in national employment law. The political influence of unions was equally remarkable: in the UK, the trade union movement played a foundational role in establishing the Labour Party, while in the United States, union political action committees became major financial contributors to Democratic Party campaigns.

**Paragraph F**

The decline of union power began in earnest during the 1980s, driven by a convergence of structural economic and deliberate political forces. The shift from manufacturing to service-sector employment created a workforce that was harder to organise, more geographically dispersed, and less reliant on the collective solidarity that had characterised factory floor culture. Deindustrialisation hollowed out traditional union strongholds in the steel, coal, and textile industries. Simultaneously, governments in Britain and America pursued explicitly anti-union policies: Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher's government enacted legislation restricting secondary picketing and closed shops, while President Ronald Reagan's dismissal of over 11,000 striking air traffic controllers in 1981 sent a powerful signal about the fundamentally changed relationship between the state and organised labour.

**Paragraph G**

Today, trade unions face the challenge of remaining relevant in an era of gig economy labour, zero-hours contracts, and multinational corporations capable of relocating production to lower-wage jurisdictions at short notice. Union density in most Western economies has fallen to historic lows, yet polling consistently shows that workers — particularly younger workers in precarious employment — express support for the principle of collective bargaining. New forms of labour organisation, including app-based digital unions and campaigns such as the "Fight for $15" minimum wage movement in the United States, suggest that the fundamental impulse toward collective action that drove the earliest combinations remains very much alive, even as the forms it takes continue to evolve in response to new economic and technological realities.

## READING PASSAGE 3

### Rewiring the Brain: The Science of Habit Formation

**Paragraph A**

Every day, human beings make hundreds of decisions — what to eat, which route to take to work, how to respond to a colleague's request — yet a substantial proportion of these choices are governed not by deliberate conscious reflection but by deeply ingrained habits. Neuroscientists estimate that up to 45 percent of daily human actions fall into this category: behaviours performed so repeatedly and consistently that they have become virtually automatic, requiring little or no conscious engagement. Understanding the neural architecture that underlies habit formation is far from merely academic — it carries profound implications for public health, education, marketing, and particularly the treatment of addiction and compulsive behaviour.

**Paragraph B**

The brain structure most centrally implicated in habit formation is the basal ganglia — a cluster of interconnected nuclei located deep within the cerebrum. Pioneering research in the early 1990s by Ann Graybiel and colleagues at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology revealed that as behaviours are repeated over time, the neural activity associated with them progressively shifts from the prefrontal cortex — the seat of conscious deliberation, planning, and decision-making — toward the basal ganglia. This process, known as "chunking," allows the brain to compress multi-step behavioural sequences into single, automated units that can be executed without moment-to-moment conscious supervision. Once chunked, habits become extraordinarily efficient, freeing the prefrontal cortex to attend to more demanding cognitive tasks.

**Paragraph C**

The habit loop, a concept popularised by journalist Charles Duhigg in his widely read 2012 book *The Power of Habit*, provides an accessible framework for understanding this behavioural automaticity. The loop consists of three interdependent components: a cue (or trigger, which initiates the behaviour), a routine (the habitual behaviour itself, which may be physical, mental, or emotional), and a reward (the positive outcome that reinforces the behaviour and motivates its repetition). The basal ganglia encode the learned relationship between cue and reward, creating a strong and durable neural pathway that is rapidly activated whenever the cue is encountered in the environment. Over time, the mere anticipation of the cue can trigger cravings for the reward even before the routine begins, making established habits extraordinarily resistant to modification.

**Paragraph D**

Research has clarified, perhaps counterintuitively, that habits are never truly erased from the brain — they are merely suppressed. Studies conducted on laboratory rodents have demonstrated that after a habit has been successfully extinguished through a period of non-reinforcement — for example, by consistently withholding the reward — the neural signature of the original habit remains fully detectable within the basal ganglia. In stressful conditions, or when the original environmental cue is unexpectedly encountered, the dormant habit can re-emerge spontaneously and with full force, a phenomenon researchers have labelled "habit reinstatement." This important finding explains the relapse patterns commonly observed in individuals recovering from addiction, as well as those attempting to maintain long-term dietary or lifestyle changes: old neural pathways lie dormant rather than deleted, remaining primed to reactivate under the right triggering conditions.

**Paragraph E**

A clearer understanding of these neural mechanisms has stimulated considerable interest in developing evidence-based strategies for sustainable habit change. One well-supported approach is "habit stacking," in which a new desired behaviour is deliberately and explicitly anchored to an existing, well-established habit that already has a strong cue-routine-reward pathway. By piggybacking on an existing neural pathway, this technique substantially reduces the cognitive effort and motivational energy required to initiate the new behaviour. A complementary strategy involves what psychologists call "implementation intentions" — specific if-then plans that pre-specify the precise cue, context, and behavioural response (for example: "If it is Monday morning and I have made my coffee, then I will immediately sit down and write for thirty minutes"). Such explicit planning reduces the burden on willpower and spontaneous decision-making.

**Paragraph F**

The societal implications of habit science extend well beyond individual self-improvement programmes. Governments and public health agencies have begun to incorporate insights from "nudge theory" — the strategic redesign of default choice environments to make healthier and more socially beneficial behaviours easier, more prominent, and more automatic — into policy development on issues from organ donation to pension saving to healthy eating. The commercial world has long been ahead of this curve: Duhigg's detailed analysis of Procter & Gamble's early Febreze marketing difficulties illustrates how the product only achieved commercial success once it was repositioned as a pleasurable reward — a satisfying conclusion to an established cleaning routine — rather than as a problem-solving remedy for isolated odour situations. The science of habits thus illuminates a deeply contested terrain where the aspiration for individual autonomy intersects with powerful external forces that shape human behaviour in ways people themselves may not recognise or choose.
