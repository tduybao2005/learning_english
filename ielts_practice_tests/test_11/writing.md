## WRITING TASK 1

*You should spend about 20 minutes on this task.*

**The line graph below shows global average temperatures expressed as degrees Celsius above the pre-industrial baseline from 1880 to 2020. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.**

*Write at least 150 words.*

---

### Global Average Temperature Anomaly (°C above pre-industrial baseline), 1880–2020

| 1900 | +0.1 |

*Note: The period from approximately 1950 to 1980 was characterised by relative temperature stability before the onset of a sustained upward trend.*

---

### Model Answer — Band 7–8

The line graph illustrates the change in global average temperatures, measured in degrees Celsius above the pre-industrial baseline, across a 140-year period from 1880 to 2020.

**Overall**, the data reveal two distinct phases: a long period of relatively modest and fluctuating temperature change lasting from 1880 to approximately 1980, followed by a sharp, continuous rise that accelerated dramatically into the twenty-first century, reaching a peak anomaly of +1.1°C by 2020.

During the initial phase, temperatures remained at a consistent +0.1°C above baseline from 1880 through to 1920, with no detectable warming trend. A first notable rise occurred by 1940, when the anomaly reached +0.3°C. However, this increase did not persist; by 1960 temperatures had returned to +0.1°C, confirming that the mid-twentieth century was characterised by relative climatic stability. Temperatures recovered to +0.3°C again by 1980, still within the same narrow range that had dominated the previous century.

From 1980 onward, the trajectory shifted decisively. The anomaly doubled to +0.6°C over the following two decades, reaching +0.8°C by 2010, +0.9°C in 2015, and a record +1.1°C in 2020. This represents a rise of 0.8°C in just forty years — roughly eight times the increase recorded across the entire preceding century.

In summary, while earlier decades exhibited modest, reversible fluctuations, the graph clearly demonstrates that global warming has intensified significantly since 1980, with the rate of increase accelerating into the most recent decade.

## WRITING TASK 2

*You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.*

**Governments should spend more money funding the arts and cultural programmes rather than investing in sporting events and facilities. To what extent do you agree or disagree?**

*Write at least 250 words.*
*Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.*

---

### Planning Notes (for study purposes)

**Agree side:** Arts develop critical thinking, cultural identity, creativity; museums/theatres accessible to all; arts education benefits long-term economy; cultural heritage is irreplaceable.

**Disagree side:** Sport promotes health, reduces medical costs; major events generate tourism and economic activity; sport unifies society; arts can often raise private funding; elite sports are role models.

**Balanced position:** Both deserve funding, but arts are more systematically underfunded and rely more on public support than sport (which attracts commercial sponsorship).

---

### Model Answer — Band 7–8

Government spending priorities inevitably reflect underlying values about what kind of society a nation aspires to build. The question of whether the arts should receive greater public investment than sport is, in this sense, not merely budgetary but deeply ideological. While I acknowledge the genuine social benefits of sport, I largely agree that the arts and cultural programmes deserve a greater share of public funding, primarily because they are less able to sustain themselves through private and commercial revenue.

The most compelling argument in favour of arts funding is the market failure inherent in cultural production. Commercial entertainment and professional sport attract substantial private investment, corporate sponsorships, broadcasting rights, and ticket revenues that can sustain even high-cost operations. By contrast, symphony orchestras, regional theatres, independent museums, and arts education programmes serving lower-income communities generate insufficient commercial revenue to survive without public subsidy. If governments withdraw or reduce arts funding, it is typically the most culturally valuable but commercially unviable programmes — those serving disadvantaged communities, preserving minority languages, or presenting experimental and challenging work — that disappear first. The economic logic, therefore, favours directing public funds where the market alone cannot provide.

Beyond economics, the arts contribute to societal well-being in ways that are more diffuse but no less significant than sport. Studies in arts education consistently demonstrate that sustained engagement with music, drama, and visual art develops creativity, critical thinking, and emotional intelligence — precisely the competencies most valued in a knowledge-based economy. Moreover, cultural institutions preserve collective memory and national identity in ways that no sporting event can replicate: a national gallery or literary archive serves future generations long after the excitement of a major championship has faded.

This is not to deny that sport deserves public support. Physical activity demonstrably reduces the burden on healthcare systems, and well-planned sporting facilities provide lasting community benefits, particularly in areas lacking recreational infrastructure. The issue is one of balance: sport already attracts private and commercial resources at a scale the arts cannot match, which means the case for supplementary public investment is less urgent.

In conclusion, while both the arts and sport merit government support, I believe arts and cultural programmes have the stronger claim to priority public funding, given their structural inability to generate sufficient market revenue and their unique contributions to education, cultural identity, and civilised public life.
