## PART 1 — Interview Questions (4–5 minutes)

### Topic 1: Reading & Books

**1. Do you enjoy reading? Why or why not?**

> Yes, I genuinely enjoy reading, particularly fiction. I find that a good novel allows me to experience lives and perspectives very different from my own, which I find both relaxing and intellectually stimulating. I try to read for at least thirty minutes before bed each night, and I have noticed that it helps me unwind much more effectively than scrolling through my phone.

**2. What kinds of books do you prefer — fiction or non-fiction?**

> I tend to prefer fiction, especially literary novels and historical fiction. I find that fictional stories often convey deeper truths about human nature than purely factual accounts do. That said, I do read non-fiction occasionally — particularly books about psychology and science — when I want to learn something specific. I think the two genres serve quite different purposes and both have value.

**3. How often do you read?**

> I try to read every day, even if it is only for a short time. On weekdays I usually manage around thirty minutes, but at weekends I sometimes spend several hours with a book if the story has really captured my attention. I keep a small reading log to track the books I finish, which motivates me to stay consistent throughout the year.

**4. Did you read a lot as a child?**

> Yes, I was a very enthusiastic reader as a child. My parents encouraged me by taking me to the local library regularly, and I went through phases of being completely obsessed with certain series. Reading so much when I was young definitely expanded my vocabulary and imagination, and I think it laid the foundation for my academic skills later in life.

### Topic 2: Work and Study Habits

**5. Do you have a daily routine that you stick to?**

> Yes, I have developed a fairly consistent daily routine over the past couple of years. I wake up at roughly the same time each morning, exercise for thirty minutes, and then dedicate the early part of my day to my most demanding tasks — studying or working — before my focus tends to drift. Having a routine has made me noticeably more productive and reduces the amount of mental energy I spend deciding what to do next.

**6. How do you manage your time between work/study and relaxation?**

> I try to use time-blocking — I schedule specific periods for focused work and separate, protected time for rest. I have found that if I do not deliberately plan for relaxation, work tends to expand and fill all available time. I also try to avoid multitasking, as I have read research suggesting it reduces efficiency significantly. Having clear boundaries between study time and personal time helps me be more present in both.

**7. What habits do you have that help you be more productive?**

> A few habits make a big difference for me. First, I write a short to-do list each morning so I always know my priorities. Second, I keep my phone out of sight when I need to concentrate, because notifications are a constant source of distraction. Third, I take short breaks every hour or so, which actually improves my focus over a long working session rather than diminishing it. These may seem simple, but the consistency is what makes them effective.

**8. Is it difficult for you to form new habits?**

> I think forming new habits is genuinely challenging for most people, and I am no exception. The first two or three weeks of trying to establish something new always feel effortful, and there are days when motivation simply is not there. What I have found helpful is attaching the new habit to an existing one — what researchers call habit stacking — and keeping the initial commitment very small so that it feels manageable rather than overwhelming.

### Topic 3: Entertainment and Leisure

**9. What do you usually do to relax after a long day?**

> After a long day I find it most effective to disconnect from screens for a while. I might read a chapter of whatever book I am currently working through, or make a cup of tea and listen to music without doing anything else simultaneously. Occasionally I watch a film or a documentary series. I have noticed that the activities that truly restore my energy are the quieter, slower ones rather than anything digitally stimulating.

**10. Do you prefer entertainment at home or going out?**

> It depends very much on my mood and energy levels. When I am tired or need to recharge, I strongly prefer staying home, where I can control the environment and pace. But when I am feeling sociable and want to experience something new, going out to a concert, an art exhibition, or even just a café with friends is genuinely enjoyable. I think both serve important but different psychological functions.

**11. How has your idea of entertainment changed since you were a child?**

> As a child, entertainment for me was almost exclusively about action and stimulation — video games, sport, anything fast-paced. As I have grown older, I find I gravitate more towards entertainment that also engages my mind or teaches me something: documentaries, literary fiction, live music, or thoughtful conversations. I think that shift reflects a broader change in how I spend my attention and what I find meaningful.

**12. Do you think people today have less free time than previous generations?**

> That is a genuinely complex question. On the one hand, labour-saving technologies should theoretically give us more free time. On the other hand, many people feel more time-pressured than ever, partly because the boundaries between work and personal life have blurred — particularly since smartphones made us constantly reachable. I also think the pressure to use free time "productively" has created a kind of leisure anxiety that previous generations may not have experienced to the same degree.

### Model Response (Band 7–8)

The book I want to talk about is *Man's Search for Meaning* by Viktor Frankl, an Austrian psychiatrist who survived the Nazi concentration camps during the Second World War. I read it about two years ago, somewhat by chance — a friend recommended it when I was going through a difficult period in my own life.

The book is divided into two main parts. The first is Frankl's harrowing personal account of life in Auschwitz and other camps, described with remarkable restraint and humanity. The second part outlines his psychological theory — what he called logotherapy — which is centred on the idea that the primary human drive is not pleasure or power, but the search for meaning. He argues that even in the most extreme suffering, people can choose their attitude and find purpose.

What genuinely changed for me was how I think about difficult circumstances. Before reading this book, I had a tendency to attribute my mood and motivation almost entirely to external conditions — whether things were going well or badly. Frankl's argument forced me to reconsider that. He demonstrated, through the most extreme possible example, that inner freedom remains even when everything else is taken away.

I would absolutely recommend it. It is not a long book — you can read it in a single weekend — but it is extraordinarily dense with insight. I think it is particularly useful for anyone who feels stuck or who is questioning what they are working towards. It is one of those rare books that stays with you long after you have finished it.

**"Do you think the books we read reflect our personality?"**

> I think there is definitely a connection, though it is not entirely straightforward. The books we choose often mirror our existing interests and values, so in that sense they reflect who we already are. But I also believe that meaningful books actively shape and challenge our personality rather than simply confirming it. Perhaps it works in both directions — we are drawn to certain books because of who we are, and those books then push us to grow further.

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion (4–5 minutes)

### 1. Why do you think some people don't enjoy reading?

> There are several possible explanations. First, many people associate reading with obligation — school texts they were forced to study — rather than genuine personal interest, and that negative association can persist into adulthood. Second, modern entertainment options compete very effectively for attention; videos, games, and social media deliver stimulation much faster than a book does. Third, some people may have had limited exposure to books that matched their actual interests. I think if people found the right genre or author at the right moment, far more of them would discover a love of reading.

### 2. How has the internet changed the way people read?

> The internet has transformed reading in deeply contradictory ways. On one hand, it has made more text accessible than at any point in human history — articles, books, research — and has encouraged millions of people who never read newspapers to consume written content daily. On the other hand, reading online tends to be fragmented and superficial; we skim headlines, jump between tabs, and rarely sustain concentration on a single long-form piece. Research suggests that extended exposure to this style of reading may be gradually reducing our capacity for the kind of deep, immersive reading that books demand.

### 3. Do you think reading fiction is as valuable as reading non-fiction?

> I would argue strongly that it is, though in different ways. Non-fiction clearly has the advantage when it comes to conveying specific factual knowledge or professional expertise. But fiction develops something equally important: empathy, emotional intelligence, and the ability to inhabit perspectives radically different from our own. Studies in psychology have found that regular fiction readers tend to perform better on tests of social cognition. I think a society that only values information over imagination loses something essential about what makes us fully human.

### 4. How important are public libraries in modern society?

> I think public libraries remain critically important, even in the digital age — perhaps especially so. They are one of the few genuinely public spaces where anyone, regardless of income, can access knowledge, use computers, and sit in a warm, safe environment without being required to spend money. Libraries also serve communities beyond just lending books: they host educational events, support digital literacy, and provide a refuge for people who are isolated or homeless. The declining funding of libraries in many countries strikes me as a short-sighted political choice with real social costs.

### 5. Do you think habits are more powerful than willpower in shaping behaviour?

> Based on both the research I have encountered and my own experience, I would say yes — habits are generally more powerful and more reliable than willpower. Willpower is a finite resource that depletes under stress and fatigue, whereas habits operate largely automatically, below the level of conscious effort. This is actually an optimistic finding, because it means we do not have to rely on motivation or discipline alone. The practical implication is that if you want to change your behaviour sustainably, you should focus on restructuring your environment and routines rather than trying harder to resist temptation.

### 6. Why is it so difficult for people to break bad habits?

> Breaking bad habits is so difficult because they are encoded in neural pathways that have been reinforced hundreds or thousands of times. The neuroscience literature describes a habit loop: a cue triggers a routine which delivers a reward, and once that loop is established, the brain begins craving the reward whenever it detects the cue. Simply deciding to stop does not interrupt the loop — the craving remains. Effective habit change usually requires either removing the cue from the environment or substituting a different routine that delivers a similar reward, rather than attempting pure elimination.

### 7. How has the understanding of neuroscience changed our approach to education?

> Neuroscience has challenged several long-held assumptions about how learning works, and its influence on educational practice has been genuinely significant. Research on memory consolidation has supported spaced repetition over cramming. Findings about sleep have given scientific weight to arguments against early school start times for adolescents. Understanding the brain's plasticity has shifted focus from fixed intelligence to the idea that effort and strategy can develop ability. However, I would also note that the application of neuroscience to education is sometimes oversimplified or commercially exploited — "brain-based learning" can mean very different things depending on who is using the term.

### 8. Do you think workers today are more productive than previous generations?

> That depends very much on how you define productivity. In terms of economic output per hour, data suggests workers in developed economies are significantly more productive than their counterparts fifty years ago, largely due to technology. However, subjective wellbeing, creativity, and meaningful engagement with work do not necessarily follow the same curve. Many workers report feeling chronically overloaded rather than productively focused. There is also a growing argument that the metrics we use to measure productivity — primarily economic output — fail to capture important forms of human contribution that are harder to quantify.

### 9. Should workplaces invest in helping employees develop good habits?

> I think there is a strong case for it, both ethically and commercially. Employers who support employee wellbeing — through things like structured breaks, access to exercise facilities, or training in focus and stress management — typically see returns in reduced absenteeism and higher engagement. There is also an ethical dimension: given that work occupies the majority of most adults' waking hours, it seems reasonable to expect employers to contribute to, rather than simply consume, their employees' wellbeing. The challenge is ensuring such initiatives are genuine rather than performative gestures that substitute for fair pay and reasonable workloads.

### 10. What role does literature play in building empathy and social understanding?

> Literature has a uniquely powerful capacity to generate empathy because it places readers directly inside the inner lives of others — not just observing them from the outside, but experiencing their fears, desires, and moral dilemmas from the first person. This is something that statistics, news reports, and even documentaries struggle to replicate. When you have spent hours inhabiting the perspective of someone from a different culture, background, or historical period, it becomes harder to dismiss or dehumanise them. I think this is why authoritarian regimes throughout history have consistently sought to control or suppress literature — it is a fundamentally democratising and humanising force.
