# IELTS SPEAKING — TEST 19

**Topics:** Languages & Multilingualism / Travel & Cultural Experience / Traditions & Celebrations
**Band Target:** 6.5–8.0
**Duration:** 11–14 minutes total

---

## Part 1 (4–5 minutes)

*The examiner asks short questions about familiar topics. Give natural, extended answers of 2–4 sentences.*

---

### Topic 1: Languages & Multilingualism

**1. How many languages do you speak?**

Model Answer:
"I speak two with reasonable confidence — Vietnamese, which is my first language, and English, which I've been learning for about ten years now. I also know a very small amount of Chinese from a course I took a couple of years ago, though I'd hesitate to call myself anything more than a complete beginner in that. Learning English has genuinely changed the way I think and access information, so I'm motivated to keep improving."

*Key vocabulary: reasonable confidence / first language / hesitate to call myself / motivated*

---

**2. When did you start learning English?**

Model Answer:
"I started learning English in primary school, as part of the standard curriculum in Vietnam — so probably around age eight or nine. But to be honest, the English I learned at that age was fairly basic and mostly forgotten by the time I reached secondary school. The learning that really mattered happened later, when I started reading and watching content in English for enjoyment rather than for school. That shift from studying English to actually using it for real purposes made an enormous difference."

*Key vocabulary: standard curriculum / secondary school / shift / real purposes*

---

**3. Do you think being bilingual gives people any advantages?**

Model Answer:
"Absolutely, and research backs this up quite strongly. Beyond the obvious advantage of being able to communicate with more people and access more information, bilingualism has been associated with cognitive benefits like better attention control and greater mental flexibility — what linguists call 'metalinguistic awareness.' There's also compelling evidence that being bilingual may delay the onset of dementia in older age. On a more practical level, knowing another language opens professional doors and broadens your cultural perspective in ways that are genuinely hard to quantify but very real."

*Key vocabulary: metalinguistic awareness / cognitive benefits / dementia / cultural perspective*

---

**4. Is the language you speak an important part of your identity?**

Model Answer:
"Very much so, I think — perhaps more than I fully appreciate day to day. Vietnamese is not just the language I communicate in; it's the language I dream in, the language in which certain emotions and family relationships are articulated with a precision that doesn't translate easily into English. The structure and vocabulary of a language shape what's easy to think and what's difficult, which means language is partly constitutive of identity rather than just a tool for expressing a pre-existing self. Losing fluency in your first language, as sometimes happens with immigrant communities, is often experienced as a profound form of loss."

*Key vocabulary: articulated / precision / constitutive / immigrant communities / fluency*

---

### Topic 2: Travel & Cultural Experience

**5. How often do you travel, and where have you been?**

Model Answer:
"Within Vietnam I travel fairly regularly — maybe five or six times a year for trips of various lengths. Internationally I've been to a few countries in Southeast Asia, including Thailand and Cambodia, and once to Japan, which was probably the most culturally striking trip I've taken. I'd like to travel more internationally but the cost and time commitment are real constraints. When I do travel, I try to spend enough time somewhere to get a sense of daily life rather than just seeing the tourist highlights."

*Key vocabulary: culturally striking / constraints / tourist highlights / daily life*

---

**6. Have you ever been to a country with a very different culture?**

Model Answer:
"Japan was probably the most culturally different place I've visited. The contrast with Vietnam was striking in many ways: the formality of interactions, the extraordinary public orderliness — people queuing without being asked, almost no litter anywhere — and the architecture and aesthetic of everyday objects, which seemed to reflect a very different relationship between beauty and function. I also found the food culture fascinating — the level of specialisation and craftsmanship even in a simple bowl of ramen suggested a relationship to cooking that goes well beyond nutrition."

*Key vocabulary: formality / orderliness / aesthetic / specialisation / craftsmanship*

---

**7. What's the most interesting cultural difference you've encountered while travelling?**

Model Answer:
"In Japan, I was struck by the concept of 'tatemae' and 'honne' — roughly, the distinction between one's public face and private feelings, and the way that Japanese social culture carefully manages this distinction in ways that a Vietnamese or Western person might find indirect. It was fascinating because it made me realise that what I'd always assumed was a universal human tendency — to express feelings fairly directly — is actually quite culturally specific. Encountering a different set of social norms that function just as effectively as your own is one of the most intellectually disorienting and enriching things about travel."

*Key vocabulary: tatemae / honne / social norms / intellectually disorienting / enriching*

---

**8. Do you think travelling changes people?**

Model Answer:
"I believe it has the potential to, but it isn't automatic. Travel changes people when they engage genuinely with different ways of living — when they're curious, willing to be uncomfortable, and reflective about what they encounter. But tourism that keeps people inside resorts or constantly comparing things to home can reinforce existing attitudes rather than challenge them. The most transformative travel experiences I've heard about — or had myself — all involve some degree of genuine human connection with people from different backgrounds, not just the consumption of scenery or experiences."

*Key vocabulary: genuinely engage / uncomfortable / reflective / reinforce / transformative*

---

### Topic 3: Traditions & Celebrations

**9. Are there any traditional festivals you particularly enjoy?**

Model Answer:
"Tết — the Vietnamese Lunar New Year — is the one that feels most meaningful to me. It has a dimension that goes beyond celebration: it's the one time of year when almost everyone in the country returns home, when the pace of life genuinely stops, and when family connections are renewed and honoured. There's a ritual quality to it that I find deeply comforting — preparing certain traditional foods, visiting ancestors' graves, giving lucky money to younger relatives — that connects me to something much larger than my individual life."

*Key vocabulary: meaningful / ritual quality / comforting / connect to something larger / lucky money*

---

**10. How important do you think it is to preserve cultural traditions?**

Model Answer:
"I think it's genuinely important, though not at the cost of living traditions becoming mere performance for outsiders. The most valuable traditions are those that continue to serve real social and emotional functions — creating community cohesion, marking important life transitions, transmitting values and stories across generations. When traditions are preserved as museum exhibits or tourist attractions rather than living practices, they lose what made them valuable in the first place. I think the key is finding ways for traditions to adapt to contemporary life rather than either abandoning them entirely or freezing them artificially."

*Key vocabulary: community cohesion / life transitions / transmitting values / adapting / artificially*

---

**11. How have traditions in your family changed compared to your grandparents' generation?**

Model Answer:
"Quite significantly. My grandparents' Tết involved elaborate multi-day preparations — making bánh chưng from scratch, preparing offerings at home altars, and extended family gatherings that would last for days. My family still observes the core practices, but many aspects have been simplified: we might buy bánh chưng from a shop rather than making it ourselves, gatherings are shorter, and some younger relatives are working in cities and can't always make it home. The spirit is similar but the form has condensed considerably, I think because life has just become more compressed."

*Key vocabulary: elaborate / preparations / offerings / home altars / condensed*

---

**12. Do you think your culture's traditions will survive globalisation?**

Model Answer:
"I'm cautiously optimistic. Vietnamese culture has survived over a thousand years of Chinese cultural influence and a century of French colonial impact without losing its distinctiveness — it absorbed influences while retaining a strong cultural core. The challenge of globalisation is different in speed and scale, but I think traditions survive when they meet genuine human needs that aren't met by imported substitutes. Tết, for example, serves the need for family reunion and spiritual connection in ways that no globalised entertainment product can replace. The traditions most at risk are those that were already weakening because they no longer serve a clear function in contemporary life."

*Key vocabulary: cautiously optimistic / distinctiveness / colonial impact / genuine human needs / spiritual connection*

---

## Part 2 (3–4 minutes)

**Cue Card:** "Describe a cultural tradition or festival that is particularly important to you."

You should say:
- what the tradition or festival is
- how and when it is celebrated
- what role you play in the celebration
- and explain why this tradition is meaningful to you

**Preparation time:** 1 minute

---

### Model Response (~200 words)

"I'd like to talk about Tết Nguyên Đán — the Vietnamese Lunar New Year — which is without question the most important festival in Vietnamese culture and, for me personally, the time of year that feels most significant.

Tết typically falls in late January or early February and marks the beginning of the lunar new year. The preparations start at least a week in advance: families clean and decorate their homes with kumquat trees and branches of peach blossoms, prepare special foods like bánh chưng — a square sticky rice cake with mung bean and pork filling — and visit the graves of ancestors to invite their spirits to celebrate with the family. On New Year's Eve, the whole family gathers for a special reunion dinner, and at midnight there are fireworks.

My role in the celebration has grown as I've gotten older. As a child, I was mainly the recipient of lì xì — lucky money in red envelopes from older relatives. Now I help with cooking, join my parents in visiting relatives, and give lucky money to younger cousins.

What makes Tết most meaningful to me is the combination of family reunion, ritual, and the sense of beginning something new. It's one of the few moments when the ordinary pace of life completely stops, and that pause feels genuinely important."

*Key vocabulary: kumquat trees / peach blossoms / bánh chưng / lì xì / ritual / reunion*

---

**Follow-up question:** "Do you think young Vietnamese people are still as interested in traditional festivals as older generations?"

Model Answer:
"I think there's a mixed picture. Many young people genuinely value Tết for the family reunion and the break from routine — those elements feel timeless. What's changing is engagement with the more ritual and spiritual dimensions: visiting temples, performing ancestral rites, observing traditional food customs. These practices are declining among urban young people who may not have grown up with them as living habits. But I also see a counter-trend of young people becoming curious about and proud of Vietnamese cultural heritage, partly as a response to globalisation — a kind of cultural re-rooting that I find genuinely encouraging."

## Part 3 (4–5 minutes)

*Abstract discussion questions on globalisation, language, cultural identity, and democracy.*

---

**1. How is globalisation affecting traditional cultures around the world?**

Model Answer:
"Globalisation has created both remarkable opportunities for cultural exchange and very real pressures toward cultural homogenisation. The most visible dynamic is the global spread of a relatively narrow range of cultural products and practices — predominantly originating in the United States, though increasingly in South Korea and China — that compete with local cultural forms for attention and resources. The concern is not simply that people consume foreign culture, but that the economic logic of global media platforms systematically advantages content with the broadest appeal over culturally specific forms. Local language cinema, regional musical traditions, and indigenous craft practices struggle to compete with the economics of globalised entertainment. Yet I'd note that the picture is not uniformly negative: global connectivity has also created unprecedented opportunities for niche cultural forms to find audiences worldwide."

---

**2. Is it possible to be proud of your own culture without being dismissive of others?**

Model Answer:
"Not only possible, but I'd argue it's the intellectually honest position. Cultural pride rooted in genuine knowledge and appreciation of your own traditions, history, and values is entirely compatible with curiosity and respect for other cultures — in fact, people with deep knowledge of their own culture are often better equipped to appreciate others, because they understand that every culture has developed sophisticated responses to universal human questions about how to live together, mark important transitions, and make meaning. What leads to dismissiveness is usually not cultural pride itself but cultural insecurity combined with ignorance of other cultures. The problem is not loving your own culture; it's the belief that cultural value is a zero-sum competition."

---

**3. Why are so many minority languages disappearing?**

Model Answer:
"Language loss typically follows predictable economic and social dynamics. When a minority language community is also economically marginalised, and when the dominant language offers access to education, employment, and social mobility, parents often make the rational decision to prioritise the dominant language in raising their children. This is both understandable and tragic: understandable because parents naturally want to maximise their children's life opportunities; tragic because each language that disappears takes with it an irreplaceable way of categorising and understanding the world — unique vocabulary, grammatical structures, oral literatures, and cultural knowledge that existed only in that language. By some estimates, roughly half of the world's approximately 7,000 languages may be lost by the end of this century."

---

**4. What can be done to preserve endangered languages and cultures?**

Model Answer:
"Language revitalisation requires interventions at multiple levels simultaneously. Documentation — recording languages through audio, video, and written materials — is urgent but insufficient on its own; a perfectly documented dead language is still a dead language. What sustains a living language is transmission to the next generation, which requires education in the language, economic opportunities that don't require abandoning it, and community contexts in which the language is used for real social functions. The Welsh example — where committed bilingual education policy and deliberate language planning have stabilised and partially reversed the decline of Welsh — demonstrates that revitalisation is possible with sufficient political commitment. But it requires sustained policy effort over decades, not occasional symbolic gestures."

---

**5. Do you think democracy is the best political system?**

Model Answer:
"I think democracy, understood as a system combining competitive elections, rule of law, protection of civil liberties, and accountability of power, remains the most reliable mechanism humans have developed for governing societies without concentrating power dangerously. The core argument isn't that democratic governments are necessarily wise or effective — history provides abundant evidence they can be neither — but that they provide mechanisms for peaceful correction of mistakes and removal of bad leaders, which alternative systems typically lack. Where I'd add nuance is that 'democracy' covers a wide spectrum of actually existing systems, ranging from highly functional to deeply dysfunctional, and the quality of democratic institutions matters as much as their formal existence."

---

**6. Why has democracy been difficult to establish in some countries?**

Model Answer:
"The conditions that make democracy work well — rule of law, independent judiciary, free press, civil society, educated citizenry, cross-cutting social cleavages rather than ethnic or sectarian polarisation, economic development above subsistence level — are not equally present in all societies, and establishing them simultaneously is extraordinarily difficult. Democratic transitions often fail when economic crises create desperation that authoritarian promises exploit, when ethnic or religious divisions make political competition feel existential rather than routine, or when established economic elites use their resources to undermine democratic institutions that threaten their interests. The historical record suggests that democracy consolidates most durably through incremental institution-building over long periods rather than through rapid transitions, which are more likely to produce instability or democratic backsliding."

---

**7. How should countries balance national identity with openness to other cultures?**

Model Answer:
"I think this is a genuinely difficult balance that countries must continuously renegotiate rather than solve permanently. A coherent national identity provides social cohesion, a shared framework for democratic deliberation, and a sense of belonging that most people need. At the same time, closed cultural nationalism tends to produce stagnation and hostility to the outside world, and the historical record of ethnically or culturally exclusive national identities is deeply troubling. The most successful model, I think, is what might be called 'civic nationalism' — a national identity grounded in shared values, institutions, and civic commitments rather than ethnicity or cultural uniformity. This allows for cultural openness and integration of immigrants while maintaining a coherent collective identity."

---

**8. Is the global spread of English positive or negative for cultural diversity?**

Model Answer:
"Both, in ways that are genuinely difficult to disentangle. The positive dimension is real and substantial: English as a global lingua franca enables communication, scientific collaboration, and cultural exchange across language barriers that would otherwise prevent it. A Vietnamese researcher and a Brazilian colleague can collaborate directly without either needing to learn the other's language. The concerning dimension is that the dominance of English creates systematic disadvantages for those who aren't native or near-native speakers in international professional contexts, and it creates pressure on other languages by occupying cultural and economic space that might otherwise sustain them. The critical question is whether English spreads as an additional language for specific functions or begins to displace first languages — the latter being far more culturally damaging."

---

**9. What does "cultural identity" mean in an increasingly globalised world?**

Model Answer:
"Cultural identity has become more consciously constructed and more complex in a globalised world than it probably was for previous generations, for whom cultural identity was largely a given — determined by place, language, religion, and community with relatively little choice involved. Today, especially for educated urban people, cultural identity involves active navigation and composition: drawing on the traditions you've inherited, the global cultural influences you've absorbed, the communities you've chosen, and the values you've deliberately adopted. This can feel destabilising, but it can also be liberating — the possibility of a genuinely hybrid identity that is Vietnamese and globally connected, traditional in some domains and contemporary in others, seems to me richer than any purely static cultural identity could be."

---

**10. How might technology and AI affect cultural diversity in the future?**

Model Answer:
"AI presents both serious threats and interesting possibilities for cultural diversity. On the threatening side, AI systems trained predominantly on English-language data already perform dramatically less well in other languages, which effectively privileges English-language content and disadvantages communities whose cultural production is in less-resourced languages. Large language models and content recommendation algorithms trained on global data may amplify already-dominant cultural forms, accelerating homogenisation. On the positive side, AI translation technology — which is improving rapidly — has genuine potential to make diverse cultural content accessible across language barriers in ways that could actually support cultural diversity by giving smaller cultural communities global reach. The outcome will depend significantly on whether AI development actively prioritises linguistic and cultural diversity or treats it as a secondary consideration."

## Speaking Tips for Vietnamese Learners

**Common errors related to Test 19 topics:**

| Lỗi thường gặp | Cách nói đúng |
|----------------|---------------|
| "I am Vietnamese and I proud of..." | "I am Vietnamese and I'm proud of..." |
| "The democracy is good system" | "Democracy is a good system" |
| "In my country, people is very..." | "In my country, people are very..." |
| "I have learnt English since 10 years" | "I have been learning English for 10 years" |
| "The culture of my country is very unique" | "My country's culture is very unique" / "Vietnamese culture is unique" |

**Useful phrases for talking about culture and identity:**
- cultural heritage / cultural continuity / living tradition vs. preserved tradition
- globalisation / homogenisation / cultural hybridity / lingua franca
- first language / mother tongue / heritage language / code-switching
- democracy / civil liberties / rule of law / accountability / civil society

**Part 2 strategy for Tết / cultural festival topic:**
1. Name the festival and give very brief context (1–2 sentences)
2. Describe the preparations and what happens (3–4 sentences)
3. Describe your personal role in the celebration (2–3 sentences)
4. Explain why it is meaningful — go beyond "because it is traditional" (3–4 sentences)
5. End with a reflection or future-oriented comment (1–2 sentences)
Total target: 1.5–2 minutes of natural speech
