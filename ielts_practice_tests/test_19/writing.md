## WRITING TASK 1

### The Question

The two pie charts below show how people in a particular country spent their leisure time in 2000 and 2020.

**Leisure Time Distribution — 2000**
| Activity | Percentage |
|---|---|
| Watching TV | 35% |
| Socialising | 25% |
| Reading | 15% |
| Sports / Exercise | 12% |
| Hobbies | 8% |
| Online activities | 5% |

**Leisure Time Distribution — 2020**
| Activity | Percentage |
|---|---|
| Online activities | 38% |
| Watching TV / Streaming | 22% |
| Socialising | 15% |
| Sports / Exercise | 12% |
| Hobbies | 8% |
| Reading | 5% |

*Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.*

---

### Band 7–8 Model Answer

The two pie charts compare how people allocated their leisure time in 2000 and 2020, revealing a dramatic shift driven primarily by the rise of digital technology over the intervening two decades.

In 2000, watching television dominated leisure time, accounting for over a third of all recreational activity at 35%. Socialising constituted the second largest share at 25%, while reading represented a noteworthy 15% of leisure time. Sports and exercise claimed 12%, hobbies 8%, and online activities — then in their relative infancy — accounted for a modest 5%.

By 2020, the leisure landscape had transformed fundamentally. Online activities surged to become the single largest category, jumping from just 5% to a commanding 38% — an increase of 33 percentage points and the most striking change across both charts. Watching TV and streaming declined from 35% to 22%, though it retained its position as the second most popular pursuit, perhaps reflecting the partial migration of television consumption onto online platforms. Socialising fell by 10 percentage points to 15%, and reading experienced the steepest proportional decline of any activity, dropping from 15% to just 5%.

Notably, two activities remained entirely unchanged between the two periods: sports and exercise maintained a consistent 12% share, and hobbies held steady at 8%, suggesting that hands-on, physical leisure pursuits proved more resistant to digital disruption than passive or social activities.

Overall, the most significant trend is the extraordinary expansion of online activities at the direct expense of traditional pastimes such as television viewing, socialising, and reading, reflecting the profound impact of the internet and smartphone technology on contemporary leisure habits.

*(203 words)*

---

### Examiner Notes

**Task Achievement (Band 8):**
The response identifies all key features: the dominant trend (online activity surge), the most dramatic numerical shift (+33 percentage points), the decline of traditional activities, and the two unchanged categories. The overview paragraph clearly states the overall pattern, which is a Band 7+ requirement.

**Coherence and Cohesion (Band 8):**
Ideas are logically sequenced from most to least significant. Cohesive devices include "By 2020," "Notably," "Overall," and comparative structures ("while," "though," "perhaps reflecting"). Each body paragraph has a clear focus: 2000 baseline, 2020 changes, and stable activities.

**Lexical Resource (Band 8):**
Notice the variety of reporting language: "dominated," "constituted," "claimed," "surged," "retained," "experienced the steepest proportional decline." The phrase "partial migration of television consumption onto online platforms" demonstrates the ability to interpret data rather than merely describe it.

**Grammatical Range and Accuracy (Band 8):**
Complex structures include: relative clauses ("which is a Band 7+ requirement"), participial phrases ("reflecting the profound impact"), and concessive clauses ("though it retained"). No grammatical errors are present.

**What to avoid (Common Band 5–6 mistakes):**
- Listing all numbers without comparison: "Online activities were 38% in 2020. TV was 22%." (no analysis)
- Writing "increased by 38%" instead of "increased from 5% to 38%" (mathematically incorrect)
- Missing the overview / general statement paragraph
- Using the same verb repeatedly: "increased," "increased," "increased"

## WRITING TASK 2

### The Question

> *Learning a foreign language should be compulsory for all students at secondary school level. To what extent do you agree or disagree?*

*Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.*

---

### Band 7–8 Model Answer

In an increasingly interconnected world, the question of whether foreign language learning should be mandatory throughout secondary education has generated considerable debate among educators, policymakers, and parents alike. While I acknowledge that compulsory language programmes carry genuine costs and practical challenges, I strongly agree that the benefits — cognitive, cultural, and professional — justify making such study obligatory for all secondary school students.

The cognitive case for compulsory language instruction is perhaps the most compelling. Decades of neurological research have established that learning a second language strengthens executive function — the cluster of mental skills encompassing attention, problem-solving, and cognitive flexibility. Bilingual students consistently demonstrate superior performance on tasks requiring mental switching and error monitoring, advantages that generalise well beyond language tasks to mathematics, reading comprehension, and critical thinking. For Vietnamese secondary students, for instance, rigorous English instruction not only opens international academic pathways but simultaneously strengthens the neural architecture underpinning all academic learning.

Beyond individual cognition, foreign language education fosters the intercultural competence that modern societies urgently require. Language is inseparable from culture: learning Mandarin, for example, exposes students to Confucian social philosophy, classical poetry, and business etiquette that are opaque to those who rely solely on translation. In an era of globalisation characterised by both unprecedented cross-cultural collaboration and alarming rises in nationalism and xenophobia, cultivating citizens capable of genuine cross-cultural understanding from adolescence serves a vital social function. Nations whose populations communicate only in their native tongue are increasingly at a diplomatic and commercial disadvantage.

Opponents of compulsory language learning raise legitimate concerns. They argue that forcing disengaged students to study languages they find irrelevant wastes valuable curriculum time better spent on subjects they are motivated to pursue, and that poor teaching quality renders such mandates counterproductive. These objections have merit, but they are arguments for reforming pedagogy and teacher training, not for abandoning the policy entirely. When language instruction is delivered through engaging, communicative methods — immersive storytelling, cultural exchange programmes, and authentic digital content — student motivation follows naturally.

In conclusion, although the implementation of compulsory foreign language study demands careful investment in curriculum design and teacher development, its cognitive, cultural, and professional returns are sufficiently substantial to justify mandatory status at secondary level. The world that today's students will inhabit requires linguistic bridges, not walls.

*(342 words)*

---

### Examiner Notes

**Task Response (Band 8):**
The essay takes a clear position ("I strongly agree") and sustains it throughout. It addresses the topic fully — cognitive benefits, cultural benefits, and counter-arguments with rebuttals. The conclusion echoes the position without merely copying the introduction.

**Coherence and Cohesion (Band 8):**
Each paragraph has one clear controlling idea. Discourse markers ("Beyond individual cognition," "Opponents of compulsory language learning," "In conclusion") guide the reader. Referencing is precise and pronouns are unambiguous.

**Lexical Resource (Band 8):**
Advanced vocabulary used accurately: "executive function," "intercultural competence," "xenophobia," "communicative methods," "linguistic bridges." Collocation is natural: "foster intercultural competence," "cultivating citizens," "underlying pathways."

**Grammatical Range and Accuracy (Band 8):**
Complex grammar: relative clauses ("which opens international academic pathways"), noun clauses ("that modern societies urgently require"), conditional structures ("when language instruction is delivered"), and parallel structures. No errors present.

### Band 6 vs Band 7–8 Comparison

| Feature | Band 6 Response | Band 7–8 Response |
|---|---|---|
| **Position clarity** | "I think learning a foreign language is good" | "I strongly agree that the benefits justify making such study obligatory" |
| **Argument development** | "You can get a better job if you know English" | Explains the cognitive mechanism (executive function, inhibitory control) that produces the benefit |
| **Counter-argument** | Absent or one sentence: "Some people disagree" | Full paragraph with specific objection, acknowledgement of its merit, and direct rebuttal |
| **Vocabulary** | "helpful," "important," "difficult," "learn" | "intercultural competence," "cognitive flexibility," "neural architecture," "commercial disadvantage" |
| **Grammar** | Mostly simple sentences with some errors in complex clauses | Wide range of clause types used accurately and purposefully |
| **Examples** | Generic: "For example, English is useful" | Specific: Vietnamese students, Mandarin and Confucian philosophy, digital content immersion |
| **Conclusion** | Repeats introduction word for word | Introduces the memorable metaphor "linguistic bridges, not walls" to crystallise the argument |
