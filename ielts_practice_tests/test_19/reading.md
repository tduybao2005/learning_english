## READING PASSAGE 1

### "Two Minds in One: The Cognitive Benefits of Bilingualism"

*Questions 1–13*

**Paragraph A**
For centuries, educators and linguists debated whether learning two languages simultaneously was beneficial or detrimental to a child's intellectual development. Early twentieth-century researchers warned that bilingualism confused young minds, supposedly causing cognitive overload and academic underperformance. These fears persisted well into the post-war era, discouraging immigrant families across Europe and North America from passing their native languages on to their children. The tide began to turn in the 1960s, when Canadian researchers Elizabeth Peal and Wallace Lambert published a landmark study demonstrating that bilingual children in Montreal consistently outperformed their monolingual peers on a broad battery of verbal and non-verbal intelligence tests.

**Paragraph B**
The most robust and widely cited advantage of bilingualism relates to what psychologists call executive function — an umbrella term for the higher-order mental processes that govern self-regulation, attention management, and cognitive flexibility. Because bilingual individuals must constantly manage two competing linguistic systems — suppressing one language while activating another — they develop remarkably powerful inhibitory control mechanisms. Neuroimaging studies conducted at institutions including University College London and the Montreal Neurological Institute have confirmed that the brains of bilingual individuals show significantly higher activation in the anterior cingulate cortex and the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, regions directly responsible for conflict monitoring and the selection of appropriate responses under competing conditions.

**Paragraph C**
One of the most striking practical manifestations of this enhanced executive function is the so-called bilingual advantage in task-switching. Researchers at York University in Toronto presented bilingual and monolingual adults with computer-based tasks requiring rapid alternation between different sets of rules. Bilinguals not only responded more quickly but also made significantly fewer errors when switching between task categories, suggesting that a lifetime of managing two grammars and two lexicons trains the brain's switching mechanisms to an extraordinary degree. This advantage has been found to persist across the lifespan, appearing in children as young as seven and remaining measurable in elderly adults well into their eighties.

**Paragraph D**
Perhaps the most medically significant implication of bilingualism concerns the delay of neurodegenerative diseases. Studies tracking thousands of patients across Canada, Spain, and India have found that bilingual individuals develop the symptoms of Alzheimer's disease an average of four to five years later than comparable monolinguals, even when researchers control for variables such as education, occupation, socioeconomic status, and general health. This does not mean bilingualism prevents Alzheimer's — the underlying pathology appears to develop at similar rates — but rather that the bilingual brain builds what neuroscientists term cognitive reserve: a kind of neurological surplus capacity that allows patients to continue functioning normally despite measurable neurological damage. Dr. Ellen Bialystok of York University, who has led much of this research, describes this reserve as analogous to having a larger engine: you can lose some cylinders and still keep driving.

**Paragraph E**
Beyond its neurological benefits, bilingualism appears to enhance social cognition in ways that pure cognitive tests cannot fully capture. Bilingual children demonstrate superior performance on theory-of-mind tasks — tests that measure the ability to understand that other people hold beliefs, desires, and perspectives different from one's own. Researchers speculate this arises because bilingual children must constantly consider which language a conversation partner speaks and whether shared cultural knowledge exists, habitually practising the mental modelling of other minds from an early age. This social-cognitive advantage has significant implications for fields as diverse as diplomacy, conflict resolution, business negotiation, and clinical psychology.

**Paragraph F**
Critics of the bilingual advantage hypothesis argue that much of the research suffers from publication bias — the tendency of journals to publish positive findings while suppressing null results. A 2015 meta-analysis by psychologist Angela de Bruin examined a much wider dataset than earlier reviews and found that the effects were considerably smaller and less consistent than the headline findings suggested. Other researchers have pointed out that many studies confound bilingualism with socioeconomic factors, since immigrant bilingual populations often differ from monolingual comparison groups in ways beyond language alone. The scientific community remains genuinely divided, and leading scholars urge caution about overstating the benefits until larger, pre-registered longitudinal studies can provide clearer answers.

**Paragraph G**
Regardless of where the scientific consensus ultimately lands on the strength of cognitive benefits, bilingualism clearly confers demonstrable real-world advantages. Bilingual individuals command higher salaries in multilingual job markets, demonstrate greater cultural empathy, and possess the practical communication tools to navigate an increasingly interconnected world. For Vietnamese students preparing for international education and careers, the investment in English proficiency represents not merely an academic exercise but a fundamental expansion of one's intellectual and professional horizons. The debate over cognitive advantages, though genuinely unresolved, should not distract from these tangible, well-established benefits that bilingualism unquestionably provides.

## READING PASSAGE 2

### "Power to the People: The Long Road to Modern Democracy"

*Questions 14–26*

**Paragraph A**
The concept of democracy — from the ancient Greek words *demos* (people) and *kratos* (rule or power) — first emerged in the city-state of Athens during the fifth century BCE. The Athenian model, championed by the statesman Cleisthenes and later refined under Pericles, granted male citizens the right to participate directly in legislative assemblies and jury courts. It was, however, a profoundly limited democracy by contemporary standards: women, enslaved people, and non-citizen residents — who together comprised the overwhelming majority of the population — were entirely excluded from political participation. Nevertheless, Athens established a foundational intellectual precedent, demonstrating for the first time in recorded history that governance need not rest solely in the hands of kings, priests, or hereditary aristocracies.

**Paragraph B**
Following the decline of Athenian democracy and the eventual collapse of the Roman Republic, genuine participatory governance largely disappeared from the political landscape of Europe and the Mediterranean world for more than a millennium. The feudal structures that dominated medieval Europe concentrated power in the hands of monarchs, nobles, and ecclesiastical authorities, leaving ordinary citizens with little formal recourse against arbitrary rule. A critical turning point came in England in 1215, when a coalition of rebellious barons forced King John to sign the Magna Carta — a charter that, while primarily protecting noble privileges rather than common people's rights, established the revolutionary principle that the monarch's authority was subject to the rule of law. This document became a touchstone for later reformers across the English-speaking world.

**Paragraph C**
The sixteenth and seventeenth centuries witnessed a dramatic intellectual and political ferment that laid the philosophical groundwork for modern democratic theory. The Protestant Reformation challenged the Catholic Church's monopoly on spiritual and temporal authority, encouraging ordinary individuals to interpret scripture independently and question established hierarchies. Political philosophers including John Locke argued that government derived its legitimacy not from divine right but from the consent of the governed, and that citizens retained the natural right to overthrow rulers who violated their fundamental liberties. These ideas proved explosively influential: they shaped the English Civil War, the Glorious Revolution of 1688, and would eventually cross the Atlantic to inspire the American and French revolutions of the late eighteenth century.

**Paragraph D**
The American Declaration of Independence of 1776 and the subsequent Constitution of 1787 represented an ambitious attempt to translate Enlightenment political philosophy into functioning institutional reality. The American founders drew heavily on Montesquieu's concept of separation of powers — dividing governmental authority between executive, legislative, and judicial branches — as a structural safeguard against tyranny. Yet the American republic was itself riddled with fundamental contradictions: slavery was not only tolerated but constitutionally protected, women were denied political representation, and the democratic franchise was effectively restricted to property-owning white men. The ideals proclaimed in the founding documents would require nearly two centuries of struggle, a devastating civil war, and multiple constitutional amendments before approaching genuine universality.

**Paragraph E**
The French Revolution of 1789 introduced a more radical democratic vision, articulating the principles of liberty, equality, and fraternity as universal human birthright rather than privileges contingent on birth or wealth. However, France's revolutionary democracy proved dramatically unstable, degenerating within years into the Terror under Robespierre and eventually the imperial autocracy of Napoleon Bonaparte. This pattern — democratic revolution followed by authoritarian consolidation — would repeat itself with haunting regularity across the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, from Russia's brief parliamentary experiment crushed by the Bolshevik revolution, to Germany's Weimar Republic, which democratic elections themselves helped dismantle when voters backed the Nazi party in the early 1930s.

**Paragraph F**
The twentieth century saw both the greatest expansion and the most catastrophic failures of democratic governance. Following World War II, a wave of new democracies emerged across Western Europe, assisted by the institutional architecture of the United Nations, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights adopted in 1948, and Marshall Plan economic support that helped stabilise fledgling democratic institutions in war-devastated nations. Decolonisation brought nominal democratic independence to dozens of newly formed states across Africa, Asia, and the Caribbean, though genuine democratic consolidation often proved elusive as former colonial powers departed without establishing the economic foundations or civic institutions that sustaining democracy requires.

**Paragraph G**
Contemporary political scientists distinguish between formal democracy — the existence of elections and constitutional frameworks — and substantive democracy, which requires the genuine protection of civil liberties, an independent judiciary, a free press, robust civil society, and meaningful economic opportunity for citizens to participate effectively in political life. By these more demanding criteria, the democratic project remains incomplete even in established Western democracies, where growing economic inequality, the concentration of media ownership, the influence of corporate money on elections, and the rise of digital disinformation represent genuine threats to democratic health. The twenty-first century challenge is less about establishing new democracies than about sustaining, deepening, and defending the fragile ones already in existence.

## READING PASSAGE 3

### "Computing Beyond Limits: Quantum Technology and Its Future"

*Questions 27–40*

**Paragraph A**
Since the dawn of the digital age in the mid-twentieth century, classical computers have operated on a deceptively simple principle: all information is encoded as binary digits, or bits, each of which exists in one of only two possible states — either 0 or 1. This binary logic underpins everything from the modest pocket calculator to the most powerful supercomputer clusters in existence. For decades, the steady miniaturisation of transistors described by Moore's Law — the empirical observation that transistor density on integrated circuits doubles approximately every two years — delivered consistent performance improvements. However, as transistors approach the scale of individual atoms, quantum mechanical effects begin to interfere with reliable binary operation, and classical computing is approaching fundamental physical limits that no amount of engineering ingenuity can circumvent.

**Paragraph B**
Quantum computing represents a fundamentally different computational paradigm, exploiting the principles of quantum mechanics — the branch of physics governing the behaviour of matter and energy at subatomic scales. The foundational unit of quantum computation is the quantum bit, or qubit, which differs from a classical bit in one crucial respect: rather than occupying either a 0 or 1 state, a qubit can exist in a superposition of both states simultaneously. This is not a metaphor or an approximation but a precise mathematical description of quantum reality: until measured, a qubit genuinely inhabits a blend of 0 and 1, enabling a quantum computer with n qubits to explore 2ⁿ possible computational paths simultaneously rather than evaluating them one at a time. For a system with just 300 qubits, this represents more simultaneous states than there are atoms in the observable universe.

**Paragraph C**
A second quantum property of equal importance is entanglement — a phenomenon Einstein famously and dismissively described as "spooky action at a distance." When two qubits become entangled, measuring the state of one instantaneously determines the state of the other, regardless of the physical distance separating them. This correlation is not transmitted faster than light — no classical information is communicated — but it creates a powerful resource for computation, allowing operations on one qubit to instantaneously influence correlated qubits elsewhere in the system. Quantum entanglement enables quantum computers to coordinate operations across their entire qubit network with a degree of coherence that has no classical analogue, and it forms the backbone of quantum error correction protocols that are essential for building reliable large-scale systems.

**Paragraph D**
The potential applications of large-scale quantum computers are both thrilling and, to those in certain fields, deeply alarming. In drug discovery and materials science, quantum simulation could model molecular interactions at their native quantum scale, potentially enabling the design of new antibiotics, more efficient solar cells, nitrogen-fixing catalysts to replace energy-intensive industrial fertiliser production, and room-temperature superconductors. In optimisation, quantum algorithms could revolutionise logistics, financial modelling, and machine learning in ways that would offer enormous commercial and scientific value. Most controversially, quantum computers running Shor's algorithm — a quantum procedure for factoring large numbers — could theoretically break the RSA encryption that currently protects the vast majority of the world's digital communications and financial transactions.

**Paragraph E**
The cryptographic threat has prompted urgent attention from government agencies and private technology companies worldwide. The United States National Institute of Standards and Technology spent nearly a decade evaluating and standardising post-quantum cryptographic algorithms — encryption methods designed to resist attacks from both classical and quantum computers. In 2024, NIST formally published its first set of post-quantum cryptographic standards, representing a landmark moment in the ongoing effort to future-proof digital security infrastructure. Meanwhile, China, the European Union, and other major technological powers have invested billions in their own quantum research programmes, recognising that quantum advantage in computing and communications represents a potential strategic asymmetry of the highest order.

**Paragraph F**
Despite the excitement surrounding quantum computing, significant engineering challenges remain formidable. Qubits are extraordinarily fragile: any interaction with the external environment — including stray electromagnetic fields, vibrations, or thermal fluctuations — causes decoherence, collapsing the quantum superposition and destroying the computation. Current quantum computers must operate at temperatures approaching absolute zero, approximately 15 millikelvin — colder than the average temperature of deep space — to maintain qubit coherence. Error rates in current systems remain high enough that most calculations require extensive error correction overhead, meaning that the number of physical qubits required to implement a useful, error-corrected logical qubit currently runs into the hundreds or thousands. Google, IBM, and a constellation of well-funded startups are pursuing different hardware approaches — superconducting qubits, trapped ions, photonic systems, and topological qubits — each with distinct advantages and limitations.

**Paragraph G**
Experts disagree sharply about the timeline for practical quantum advantage — the threshold at which quantum computers can solve real-world problems faster than the best classical alternatives. Optimistic projections, often from companies with commercial interests in the field, suggest transformative applications within five to ten years. More sober assessments from academic physicists suggest that fault-tolerant, large-scale quantum computation capable of breaking encryption or simulating complex pharmaceuticals may remain decades away. What is not in dispute, however, is that quantum mechanics provides a genuine and revolutionary computational resource that will, on some timescale, fundamentally alter the landscape of computing, cryptography, and scientific simulation. The question is not whether quantum computing will matter, but when and for whom its benefits will first arrive.
