## PASSAGE 1

**The Rise of the Gig Economy**

Over the past two decades, the nature of work has undergone a profound transformation. Traditional employment models, in which workers are hired on a permanent basis with fixed salaries, benefits, and job security, are increasingly being replaced by short-term contracts, freelance arrangements, and platform-based labour. This emerging model is commonly referred to as the "gig economy," a term that captures the fragmentary and flexible nature of contemporary work.

The gig economy is largely driven by digital platforms such as Uber, Airbnb, Deliveroo, and Upwork, which connect individual service providers directly with customers. These platforms have disrupted established industries by removing the need for traditional intermediaries and allowing workers to offer their services on a flexible, on-demand basis. Proponents argue that this model offers workers unprecedented freedom: the ability to choose their own hours, take on multiple income sources, and maintain a better work-life balance.

However, critics point out that the gig economy comes with significant downsides. Most gig workers are classified as independent contractors rather than employees, which means they are not entitled to the benefits that salaried workers typically receive — such as health insurance, paid leave, pension contributions, and unemployment protection. This lack of legal protection leaves many gig workers in a precarious financial situation, particularly during periods of illness or economic downturn.

Research conducted by the McKinsey Global Institute found that approximately 20–30% of the working-age population in the United States and Europe engage in some form of independent work. While some individuals choose gig work voluntarily and earn above-average incomes, a substantial portion rely on it out of necessity, as they cannot find stable full-time employment. This distinction between voluntary and necessity-driven gig workers is critical for policymakers attempting to design appropriate labour protections.

The gig economy also has broader implications for national economies. Because gig workers often receive income in irregular, project-based payments, tax collection becomes more complex. Governments in many countries have struggled to ensure that platform companies and their workers pay appropriate taxes. Additionally, when large numbers of workers lack stable income, consumer spending may decline, weakening aggregate demand and slowing overall economic growth.

Despite these concerns, the gig economy shows no signs of contracting. Advances in artificial intelligence and automation are expected to accelerate the shift toward flexible labour arrangements. Some economists predict that by 2030, the majority of work in developed economies will be project-based rather than salaried. Whether this represents an opportunity or a threat depends largely on the regulatory frameworks that governments establish in the coming years.

## PASSAGE 2

**Free Trade vs. Protectionism: A Global Debate**

**A.** International trade has long been viewed as an engine of economic growth. By allowing countries to specialise in producing goods and services in which they hold a comparative advantage, free trade promotes efficiency and lowers prices for consumers. Agreements such as the World Trade Organization (WTO) framework, the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA), and the European Union's single market have institutionalised this principle over the past half-century.

**B.** Proponents of free trade argue that open markets stimulate innovation, create employment, and foster diplomatic cooperation between nations. When countries are economically interdependent, the argument goes, they are less likely to engage in military conflict. The post-World War II expansion of global trade is frequently cited as a contributing factor to the longest period of relative peace among major powers in modern history.

**C.** Nevertheless, free trade has not benefitted all segments of society equally. While consumers and export-oriented industries often gain, domestic industries exposed to foreign competition may suffer significantly. Workers in manufacturing sectors that face cheaper imports may lose their jobs. Communities that depend on a single industry can be devastated when that industry collapses under competitive pressure. Critics argue that the gains from trade are frequently concentrated among wealthy shareholders and corporations, while the costs are disproportionately borne by lower-income workers.

**D.** Protectionism — the use of tariffs, quotas, subsidies, and other measures to shield domestic industries from foreign competition — is often presented as the alternative. Historically, many developed nations used protectionist policies during their early industrialisation phases to nurture infant industries and build productive capacity. The United States, Germany, and Japan all employed significant trade barriers in their formative economic periods before embracing liberalisation.

**E.** In recent decades, however, protectionism has made a significant comeback. The election of Donald Trump in the United States led to the imposition of sweeping tariffs on Chinese goods, triggering a tit-for-tat trade war that rattled global supply chains. The UK's withdrawal from the European Union introduced new trade frictions with its largest trading partner. These developments have prompted economists to reconsider whether the relentless pursuit of free trade has ignored legitimate concerns about national sovereignty, security of supply, and labour standards.

**F.** The debate between free trade and protectionism is ultimately not binary. Most economists today acknowledge that some degree of trade management is both inevitable and appropriate. Strategic trade policy — supporting key industries of national importance while maintaining openness in others — is increasingly seen as a pragmatic middle ground. The challenge for governments lies in identifying which industries merit protection and avoiding the political capture of trade policy by powerful lobbying interests.

**G.** Looking ahead, the intersection of trade policy and environmental concerns is likely to reshape the debate further. Carbon border adjustment mechanisms — tariffs applied to imports from countries with weaker environmental regulations — represent a new form of trade barrier justified on climate grounds. As climate policy tightens, the line between legitimate environmental protection and disguised protectionism will become increasingly difficult to draw.

## PASSAGE 3

**Microfinance and Poverty Reduction in Developing Nations**

For much of the twentieth century, access to formal financial services was a privilege reserved for the relatively affluent. Banks required collateral, credit histories, and minimum deposit amounts that effectively excluded the world's poorest populations. This "financial exclusion" perpetuated cycles of poverty: without access to credit, low-income individuals could not invest in small businesses, agricultural improvements, or education, and thus remained trapped in subsistence-level livelihoods.

The concept of microfinance emerged as a response to this structural inequality. Pioneered most prominently by Muhammad Yunus and the Grameen Bank in Bangladesh during the 1970s, microfinance refers to the provision of small loans — commonly known as microcredit — along with savings accounts and insurance products, to individuals who lack access to conventional banking. The Grameen Bank demonstrated that the poor were, in fact, highly reliable borrowers: repayment rates consistently exceeded 95%, challenging long-held assumptions about the creditworthiness of low-income populations.

The model spread rapidly. By the early 2000s, microfinance institutions (MFIs) were operating in over one hundred countries, serving tens of millions of clients. International organisations such as the United Nations and the World Bank endorsed microfinance as a central pillar of development strategy, and the United Nations designated 2005 as the International Year of Microcredit. The Grameen Bank and Muhammad Yunus were jointly awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 2006, bringing unprecedented global attention to the movement.

Despite this institutional enthusiasm, the empirical evidence on microfinance's impact on poverty reduction has proved more mixed than early advocates hoped. A series of rigorous randomised controlled trials conducted in the 2000s and 2010s — in India, Ethiopia, Morocco, Mexico, and Bosnia, among other countries — found that while microfinance did help some borrowers improve their businesses and smooth consumption, it did not, on average, produce substantial increases in income or help households escape poverty. Critics noted that many borrowers used microcredit not for productive investment but for consumption smoothing — meeting immediate household expenses rather than building long-term assets.

Furthermore, microfinance has been implicated in several debt crises. In the Indian state of Andhra Pradesh in 2010, aggressive lending practices by MFIs led to over-indebtedness among poor rural households. Reports emerged of borrowers taking loans from multiple institutions simultaneously to repay existing debts, creating a spiral of indebtedness. Dozens of borrowers reportedly committed suicide under the pressure of debt collectors. This scandal prompted the Indian government to introduce strict regulations on the microfinance sector.

Defenders of microfinance argue that the sector's problems reflect poor implementation rather than a fundamentally flawed concept. Well-governed MFIs that offer fair interest rates, financial literacy training, and flexible repayment schedules have demonstrated measurable positive outcomes for clients. Moreover, proponents argue that the appropriate yardstick for microfinance is not whether it eliminates poverty single-handedly, but whether it expands the financial choices available to the poor — a more modest but arguably more honest benchmark.

The broader lesson from microfinance may be that financial inclusion, while necessary, is insufficient on its own to drive poverty reduction. Access to credit works best when combined with improvements in health infrastructure, educational opportunity, and market access. Countries that have achieved the most dramatic reductions in poverty — such as China, South Korea, and Vietnam — did so primarily through state-led industrial development and export-oriented growth, not microfinance. Microfinance remains a useful tool in the development toolkit, but it is not a silver bullet.
