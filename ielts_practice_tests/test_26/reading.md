## READING PASSAGE 1

**Plate Tectonics: Understanding the Dynamic Earth**

**A**
In 1912, German meteorologist Alfred Wegener proposed one of the most revolutionary ideas in the history of science. His theory of continental drift suggested that the continents had once formed a single supercontinent, which he called Pangaea, and had since slowly drifted apart over millions of years. Wegener observed that the coastlines of South America and Africa appeared to fit together like puzzle pieces, and that identical fossils of ancient organisms had been found on continents now separated by vast oceans. Despite the compelling nature of his observations, the scientific community largely rejected Wegener's hypothesis, primarily because he could not explain the mechanism that would cause continents to move through solid oceanic rock.

**B**
The rejection of Wegener's ideas persisted for several decades until the 1960s, when American geologist Harry Hess proposed the theory of seafloor spreading. Hess suggested that molten rock, or magma, rises from deep within the Earth at mid-ocean ridges — underwater mountain ranges running along the floors of the world's oceans. As this magma solidifies and spreads outward from the ridge, it creates new oceanic crust. This process explained how continents could move without ploughing through solid rock: instead, the ocean floor itself acts as a conveyor belt, carrying the continents along with it. The discovery of mid-ocean ridges through oceanographic surveys provided strong physical evidence for this mechanism.

**C**
Further confirmation came from the study of paleomagnetism — the record of Earth's ancient magnetic field preserved in rocks. As new oceanic crust forms at mid-ocean ridges, iron-rich minerals within the molten rock align themselves with Earth's magnetic field before the rock solidifies. Scientists discovered symmetrical patterns of magnetic striping on either side of mid-ocean ridges, reflecting the fact that Earth's magnetic field has reversed direction many times throughout geological history. These mirror-image patterns provided powerful independent evidence that the seafloor was indeed spreading from the ridges outward.

**D**
The synthesis of continental drift and seafloor spreading gave rise to the modern theory of plate tectonics, which holds that Earth's outer shell — the lithosphere — is divided into several large plates that move relative to one another. These plates interact at three types of boundaries. At divergent boundaries, plates move apart and new crust is created, as seen along mid-ocean ridges and in continental rift zones such as the East African Rift. At convergent boundaries, plates collide; when two continental plates meet, the collision produces mountain ranges such as the Himalayas, formed by the ongoing collision of the Indian and Eurasian plates. When oceanic and continental plates converge, the denser oceanic plate is forced beneath the continental plate in a process called subduction.

**E**
At transform boundaries, plates slide horizontally past one another without creating or destroying crust. The San Andreas Fault in California is a well-known example of a transform boundary, where the Pacific Plate slides northward relative to the North American Plate. This lateral movement builds up enormous stress along the fault line, which is periodically released as earthquakes. Indeed, the distribution of earthquakes and volcanoes around the world closely follows the boundaries between tectonic plates, making plate tectonics one of the most powerful frameworks for understanding and predicting geological hazards.

**F**
Subduction zones, where oceanic plates descend into the mantle, are among the most geologically active regions on Earth. As the subducted plate descends to depths of 100 kilometres or more, the increasing heat and pressure cause the release of water and other volatiles from the plate material. These fluids lower the melting point of the surrounding mantle rock, generating magma that rises through the overlying plate to produce chains of volcanoes known as volcanic arcs. The Pacific Ring of Fire — a horseshoe-shaped zone encircling the Pacific Ocean — represents the most dramatic expression of subduction-related volcanism, accounting for approximately 75 per cent of the world's active volcanoes and 90 per cent of its earthquakes.

## READING PASSAGE 2

**The Ottoman Empire: Power, Culture, and Legacy**

**A**
The Ottoman Empire, one of the most enduring and far-reaching political entities in world history, traces its origins to the late thirteenth century in northwestern Anatolia, in what is now Turkey. Founded around 1299 by Osman I, a leader of a small Turkic principality, the empire gradually expanded through conquest and diplomacy until it controlled a vast territory stretching from southeastern Europe and western Asia to North Africa. At its height, the Ottoman Empire encompassed regions that today include more than twenty modern nations, governing a diverse population of Muslims, Christians, and Jews across multiple continents.

**B**
The reign of Suleiman I, known in the West as Suleiman the Magnificent and in the Ottoman world as Suleiman the Lawgiver, marked the zenith of Ottoman power and prestige. During his rule from 1520 to 1566, the empire expanded into Hungary, Mesopotamia, and along the North African coast, reaching the gates of Vienna in 1529. Suleiman oversaw sweeping legal reforms that codified Ottoman law and streamlined the administration of the empire, earning him the epithet "the Lawgiver" among his own people. His court was renowned for its wealth, sophistication, and patronage of the arts, making Constantinople — the Ottoman capital, formerly known as Byzantium and later renamed Istanbul — one of the most splendid cities in the world.

**C**
The administrative genius of the Ottoman Empire lay partly in its capacity to manage extraordinary ethnic and religious diversity. The millet system granted recognised non-Muslim communities — including Eastern Orthodox Christians, Armenian Christians, and Jews — a significant degree of autonomy in managing their own religious, legal, and educational affairs, while still paying taxes to the Ottoman state. Alongside this, the devshirme system periodically recruited young Christian boys from conquered territories, converted them to Islam, and trained them as soldiers or administrators. The most talented among these recruits could rise to the highest offices of state, including that of Grand Vizier, the empire's chief minister.

**D**
Ottoman cultural achievement found its most visible expression in architecture. The conversion of the Hagia Sophia from a Byzantine cathedral into a mosque in 1453 — following the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople — was among the most symbolic acts of the empire's early centuries. Subsequent sultans commissioned grand mosques that elaborated on and ultimately surpassed the Byzantine architectural tradition. The Süleymaniye Mosque, built under Suleiman's patronage and designed by the master architect Sinan, remains one of the finest examples of classical Ottoman architecture. The Topkapi Palace, home to the sultans for nearly four centuries, served simultaneously as the administrative centre of the empire and a treasury of artistic masterpieces from across the Islamic world.

**E**
The Ottoman contribution to literature and learning was equally significant. Ottoman poets writing in the highly stylised Divan tradition produced works of considerable sophistication that drew on Persian and Arabic literary models while developing a distinctly Ottoman voice. The empire also made important contributions to cartography: the Ottoman admiral and cartographer Piri Reis produced a world map in 1513 that incorporated knowledge from Portuguese, Arabic, and possibly even pre-Columbian sources, demonstrating the remarkable geographical reach of Ottoman intellectual networks. Scientific and mathematical learning flourished in Ottoman madrasas, which trained scholars in theology, philosophy, and the natural sciences.

**F**
The seventeenth century brought the beginning of a gradual but inexorable decline. A combination of factors — including military reverses against European powers, the economic disruption caused by the rise of Atlantic trade routes that bypassed Ottoman-controlled territories, internal corruption, and a series of weak sultans — eroded the empire's strength and territorial extent. Several major conflicts in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries resulted in significant territorial losses, and the empire became increasingly dependent on loans from European financial institutions. By the nineteenth century, the Ottoman state was widely referred to by European statesmen as "the sick man of Europe," reflecting its diminished political and military standing.

**G**
The Ottoman Empire's participation on the losing side of the First World War proved fatal. The Allied occupation of Constantinople following the armistice of 1918 and the subsequent War of Turkish Independence led by Mustafa Kemal Atatürk resulted in the abolition of the Sultanate in 1922 and the proclamation of the Republic of Turkey in 1923. The dissolution of the empire left a complex legacy. New nation-states carved from former Ottoman territories in the Middle East and the Balkans faced profound challenges of political legitimacy and ethnic integration that continue to shape those regions today. Ottoman legal traditions, architectural styles, and cultural practices persisted across many of these successor states, making the empire's influence one of the most enduring in the history of the modern world.

## READING PASSAGE 3

**Nutrition Science and the Transformation of Modern Dietary Understanding**

**A**
Nutrition science — the study of how food and its constituent components affect human health and physiology — is one of the most contested and rapidly evolving fields in biomedical research. At its foundation lie the macronutrients: proteins, fats, and carbohydrates, which provide the body with energy and the raw materials for growth and repair. Alongside these, micronutrients — vitamins and minerals required in smaller quantities — are essential for regulating the biochemical processes that sustain life. For much of the twentieth century, researchers and public health authorities believed that understanding these basic categories was sufficient to formulate sound dietary guidelines. Events of the past five decades have demonstrated that this assumption was profoundly mistaken.

**B**
Few episodes in the history of nutrition have proved more consequential — or more controversial — than the development of low-fat dietary guidelines in the United States and other Western nations during the 1970s and 1980s. Influenced heavily by the epidemiological research of American physiologist Ancel Keys, who identified a correlation between saturated fat intake and rates of coronary heart disease, health authorities recommended that populations reduce their consumption of fat, particularly saturated fat. The food industry responded by producing a proliferation of low-fat products, but to maintain palatability, manufacturers replaced fat with sugar and refined carbohydrates. The unintended consequence was a dramatic increase in sugar consumption at a population level, which many researchers now link to rising rates of obesity and type 2 diabetes.

**C**
What made this dietary shift particularly damaging, critics have argued, was that it occurred partly as a result of deliberate manipulation of the scientific process. Internal documents from the 1960s, obtained by investigative journalists and published in 2016, revealed that the Sugar Research Foundation — a lobby group funded by the sugar industry — had financed nutrition research specifically designed to shift scientific and public attention away from sugar and towards fat as the primary dietary cause of heart disease. The scientists involved downplayed findings that implicated sugar while amplifying those that indicted saturated fat. This episode became a landmark case study in how commercial interests can distort the conduct and communication of nutritional science.

**D**
While the low-fat consensus was faltering, evidence for the benefits of the Mediterranean diet was accumulating. Characterised by high consumption of olive oil, vegetables, legumes, whole grains, fish, and moderate amounts of wine, and relatively low consumption of red meat and processed foods, the Mediterranean dietary pattern was associated in multiple large-scale observational studies with reduced risk of cardiovascular disease, certain cancers, and neurodegenerative conditions such as Alzheimer's disease. The landmark PREDIMED trial, a large randomised controlled study conducted in Spain, found that adherence to a Mediterranean diet supplemented with extra-virgin olive oil or nuts significantly reduced the incidence of major cardiovascular events compared to a low-fat control diet.

**E**
A complementary framework for understanding dietary quality emerged from Brazil, where nutritionist Carlos Monteiro and colleagues developed the NOVA classification system in the early 2000s. Rather than categorising foods by their nutrient composition, NOVA groups foods according to the extent and purpose of their industrial processing. The system identifies four groups: unprocessed or minimally processed foods; processed culinary ingredients such as oils and flour; processed foods; and ultra-processed foods — industrially manufactured products typically containing five or more ingredients, including additives such as emulsifiers, flavour enhancers, and artificial colouring agents that are not ordinarily used in home cooking. Epidemiological evidence has increasingly linked high consumption of ultra-processed foods to adverse health outcomes including obesity, cardiovascular disease, and colorectal cancer.

**F**
One of the most exciting frontiers in contemporary nutrition science concerns the gut microbiome — the vast community of trillions of microorganisms, predominantly bacteria, that inhabit the human digestive tract. Research over the past two decades has established that the composition of the gut microbiome exerts a profound influence on human health, affecting not only digestion and immune function but also mental health through what scientists have termed the gut-brain axis. Diet is the single most powerful determinant of gut microbiome composition: diets rich in diverse plant foods promote a diverse and resilient microbiome, while diets high in ultra-processed foods and low in dietary fibre are associated with reduced microbial diversity and markers of systemic inflammation.

**G**
The accumulation of evidence from these diverse strands of research has prompted a growing movement towards personalised nutrition — the tailoring of dietary recommendations to the individual rather than the population. Proponents argue that individuals vary considerably in their genetic profiles, gut microbiome composition, metabolic responses, and lifestyle factors, meaning that a dietary pattern that produces optimal health outcomes for one person may be inappropriate or even harmful for another. Pioneering studies, including the Israeli Personalised Nutrition Project, have demonstrated that individuals' blood glucose responses to identical foods vary dramatically and can be predicted using algorithms that incorporate gut microbiome data and other biological parameters. While personalised nutrition remains largely in the research phase, its proponents envision a future in which dietary advice is as individualised as a medical prescription.
