# IELTS Academic Speaking — Test 26
**Time allowed: 11–14 minutes (approximately)**

---

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview
*(4–5 minutes)*

The examiner will ask you questions about familiar topics. Answer naturally and develop your responses.

---

### Topic 1: Your Hometown

**1. Where are you from, and what is your hometown like?**

*Model answer:* I'm from Hội An, a small city in central Vietnam. It's a really charming place — it's famous for its well-preserved ancient town, which is actually a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The streets are lined with lanterns and old merchant houses, and the whole area has a very relaxed, historical atmosphere. It's quite a tourist destination these days, but for locals it's also a very community-oriented place to grow up.

**2. What do you like most about your hometown?**

*Model answer:* What I appreciate most is the combination of history and natural beauty. The ancient town is genuinely beautiful, and you have the coast nearby as well, so there's always something to explore. I also like the fact that it's small enough that everyone knows each other — there's a real sense of community that you don't get in bigger cities.

**3. Has your hometown changed much in recent years?**

*Model answer:* Significantly, yes. When I was young, it was a fairly quiet provincial town, but the growth in tourism over the past decade has transformed it quite dramatically. There are far more hotels, restaurants, and souvenir shops than there used to be, and the number of visitors during peak seasons can be quite overwhelming. It's created economic opportunities, but there are also concerns about whether all this development is compatible with preserving the character of the old town.

**4. Would you recommend your hometown as a place to live? Why or why not?**

*Model answer:* I would, with some caveats. It's a wonderful place to grow up and has a lot of cultural richness, but for young professionals it can feel limiting — the job market is heavily skewed towards tourism and hospitality. If you're looking for a more diverse range of career opportunities, you'd probably need to move to a larger city like Da Nang or Ho Chi Minh City. But as a place to raise a family or enjoy a slower-paced life, it's genuinely lovely.

### Topic 2: Food and Diet

**5. What kinds of food do you enjoy eating?**

*Model answer:* I enjoy a fairly wide range of food, but I have a particular fondness for traditional Vietnamese cuisine — dishes like pho, bún bò Huế, and cao lầu, which is actually a local speciality from Hội An. I appreciate food that uses fresh ingredients and complex layering of flavours. I'm also quite interested in Mediterranean food — Greek and Italian dishes, in particular — because I find the use of olive oil, herbs, and fresh vegetables really appealing.

**6. Do you think your diet is healthy? Why or why not?**

*Model answer:* I'd like to think it's reasonably healthy. I try to eat plenty of vegetables, and I cook at home fairly often using fresh ingredients, which I think is better than relying on processed or takeaway food. That said, I'm probably not as disciplined about it as I should be — I have a weakness for sweet things, and I don't always eat as much fruit as I should. It's a work in progress, honestly.

**7. Has your diet changed compared to when you were younger?**

*Model answer:* Yes, quite noticeably. As a child I was a fairly picky eater and tended to prefer simpler, more familiar flavours. As I've got older I've become much more adventurous and genuinely enjoy trying different cuisines. I've also become more conscious of what I eat in terms of nutritional value — I think about things like fibre intake and the balance of different food groups in a way that would have seemed very adult to my younger self.

**8. Do you think people in your country eat healthily in general?**

*Model answer:* Traditionally, Vietnamese cuisine is actually quite healthy — it's largely based on rice, vegetables, fresh herbs, and lean proteins, with relatively little deep-frying compared to some other Asian cuisines. However, eating habits are changing, particularly among younger generations in cities who are eating more processed foods, fast food, and sugary drinks than previous generations did. It's a concern that health professionals are increasingly raising in public discussions.

### Topic 3: History and Culture

**9. Are you interested in history? Why or why not?**

*Model answer:* I am, genuinely. I find history fascinating because it provides a framework for understanding why the world is the way it is today — why certain countries are wealthy, why particular conflicts occur, why cultural practices and institutions take the forms they do. I'm especially interested in the history of civilisations that shaped large parts of the world, like the Roman Empire or the Ottoman Empire, because their legacies are still so visible in contemporary life.

**10. Do you think it is important for young people to learn about their country's history?**

*Model answer:* Absolutely, I think it's essential. Understanding your own country's history gives you a sense of identity and context — it helps you understand the values, struggles, and achievements that shaped the society you live in. It also fosters a kind of critical awareness: if you know how past mistakes were made, you're better equipped to recognise similar patterns in the present. Without that historical grounding, I think people can become quite easily manipulated by simplistic or nationalist narratives.

**11. Are there historical sites or museums in your area that you enjoy visiting?**

*Model answer:* Yes, very much so. Living in Hội An, I'm surrounded by historical sites — the ancient town itself is essentially an open-air museum, with its Japanese merchant houses, Chinese assembly halls, and old bridges. But beyond the buildings, there are also small museums dedicated to local crafts and trades, like silk weaving and lantern making, which give you a real sense of what daily life was like for people here centuries ago. I find those kinds of living heritage experiences more engaging than purely static museum displays.

**12. How do you think modern technology has affected people's interest in history and culture?**

*Model answer:* It's had quite a mixed effect, I think. On the positive side, technology has made historical knowledge far more accessible — you can watch documentaries, explore virtual museum tours, or find academic lectures online that would previously have required a university library. On the other hand, the attention economy tends to reward short, entertaining content over deep engagement, which can reduce complex historical events to oversimplified snippets. So technology has broadened access to history, but it may also have encouraged a somewhat superficial engagement with it.

## PART 2 — Individual Long Turn
*(3–4 minutes: 1 minute preparation + 2 minutes speaking)*

---

**Describe a place in your country that has changed significantly in recent years.**

*You should say:*
- *where this place is*
- *what it was like before the changes*
- *what changes have taken place*
- *and explain how you feel about these changes*

*You will have one minute to prepare. You may make notes if you wish.*

---

### Model Answer (approximately 2 minutes of speech)

The place I'd like to talk about is the waterfront area of Da Nang city, which is located on the central coast of Vietnam, not far from where I grew up. I've been visiting Da Nang since I was a child, so I've witnessed its transformation quite directly over the years.

When I first visited as a young child in the early 2000s, Da Nang was a fairly modest city with a beautiful but underdeveloped beach. The waterfront along My Khe beach was largely undeveloped — there were a few simple seafood restaurants and local vendors, but it was quite raw and natural. The city itself had a slightly rundown quality from decades of post-war economic stagnation.

The changes that have occurred since then have been truly dramatic. The waterfront has been completely transformed into a modern promenade lined with luxury hotels, international restaurants, and recreational facilities. Enormous resort complexes have been built along the coastline, the roads have been massively upgraded, and the city now has an international airport capable of handling millions of visitors annually. New bridges spanning the Han River — including the famous Dragon Bridge, which breathes fire on weekend evenings — have become iconic landmarks in their own right.

My feelings about these changes are genuinely mixed. On one level, I appreciate the economic development — Da Nang has become one of the most liveable and prosperous cities in Vietnam, with good infrastructure and a rising standard of living for its residents. But I also feel a sense of loss for the more authentic, unpolished character the city once had. The beachfront in particular has become heavily commercialised, and it sometimes feels as though it's been designed more for tourists than for the local community. I think this tension between development and preserving a place's soul is one of the great challenges of our time, and Da Nang illustrates it very clearly.

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion
*(4–5 minutes)*

The examiner will ask you questions related to the topic in Part 2. Give extended, well-developed answers.

---

**1. Why do you think cities and towns change so dramatically over short periods of time?**

*Model answer:* There are several driving forces. Economic growth is probably the most powerful — when investment flows into an area, whether from tourism, industry, or real estate development, the physical landscape changes rapidly to accommodate it. Population growth also plays a major role: as more people move to cities seeking work and opportunity, the demand for housing, infrastructure, and services drives rapid construction. And then there are deliberate policy decisions — governments and municipalities often initiate major urban renewal projects to attract further investment or improve the quality of life for residents. When all these forces act together, the pace of change can be extraordinary.

**2. What are the advantages and disadvantages of rapid urban development?**

*Model answer:* The advantages are considerable. Rapid development can lift living standards significantly — improving infrastructure such as roads, water supply, and sanitation; creating employment; and generating the tax revenues that fund public services like healthcare and education. For previously underdeveloped areas, rapid urban growth can mean a genuine improvement in the quality of life for millions of people.

However, the disadvantages are equally real. Rapid development frequently outpaces planning and regulatory frameworks, leading to congestion, pollution, and the destruction of green spaces. It can also displace lower-income communities, who are often pushed to the urban periphery as property values rise. And when it proceeds without adequate attention to cultural heritage, it can erase the historical character of places that took centuries to develop.

**3. Do you think governments have a responsibility to preserve historical buildings and sites?**

*Model answer:* I think they have both a responsibility and a practical interest in doing so. From a moral and cultural perspective, historical buildings and sites are part of the shared inheritance of a society — they embody collective memory and identity in a way that no modern construction can replicate. Once they are demolished, that connection is permanently severed. From a practical standpoint, heritage sites are often major economic assets: they attract tourists, stimulate local economies, and give cities a distinctive identity that makes them more attractive places to live and invest in. So yes, I think preservation should be a priority, though it needs to be balanced thoughtfully against the needs of living communities.

**4. How can cities balance the need for modernisation with the preservation of their historical character?**

*Model answer:* I think the key lies in what planners call "adaptive reuse" — finding new functions for historic buildings and districts rather than demolishing and replacing them. Many successful cities have shown that old warehouses can become art galleries, former industrial zones can be transformed into creative quarters, and historic market buildings can be repurposed as food halls or community spaces. This approach allows cities to evolve economically while maintaining architectural continuity. Zoning regulations that protect the character of historic districts — controlling building heights, facade materials, and the types of businesses that can operate there — also play a crucial role. The challenge is ensuring that these protections are genuinely enforced and not eroded by the pressure of commercial development.

**5. Is it fair to prevent development in areas of natural beauty or environmental significance?**

*Model answer:* This is a genuinely complex question, and I don't think there's a simple answer. On one hand, it can seem unfair to local communities who may have limited economic opportunities and who could benefit significantly from development. Restricting what people can do with land in their region, often in the name of benefiting wider society or future generations, raises real questions of equity.

On the other hand, some natural environments are genuinely irreplaceable — old-growth forests, wetlands, and coral reef systems provide ecological services that no amount of economic development can substitute. There's also a strong argument that the economic value of intact natural environments, through tourism and the ecosystem services they provide, often exceeds what could be generated by their exploitation. So I think the answer depends heavily on context: blanket development restrictions may not always be fair, but for truly exceptional natural environments, they are often both justified and necessary.

**6. Do you think young people today care about preserving historical heritage? Why or why not?**

*Model answer:* I think the picture is more varied than it might appear. There's certainly a segment of young people who are deeply engaged with history and heritage — social media has actually been surprisingly effective at cultivating interest in historical sites and cultural practices among younger audiences. Platforms like Instagram and TikTok have made heritage sites more visible and desirable as travel destinations, and there are many young people who actively advocate for preservation.

That said, I think the dominant cultural currents — the emphasis on novelty, technology, and the future — can make historical preservation seem like a somewhat conservative or backward-looking concern to others. The challenge for heritage advocates is to make the case for preservation in terms that resonate with younger generations: framing historic sites not as relics of the past but as irreplaceable elements of a living cultural identity.

**7. How does the destruction of historical sites affect a country's cultural identity?**

*Model answer:* I think it can be profoundly damaging. Historical sites are physical anchors of collective memory — they give communities a tangible connection to their past and help sustain a sense of shared identity and continuity. When they are destroyed, something intangible but very real is lost: a community's ability to see itself in its own history. This is why the deliberate destruction of cultural heritage in conflict zones — as we've seen in Syria and Iraq — is considered a form of cultural violence. Even when destruction occurs for ostensibly benign reasons, like urban development, the cumulative effect of erasing historical environments can leave communities feeling disconnected from their roots, which can in turn contribute to social fragmentation.

**8. Can tourism be both beneficial and harmful to historical sites? Give examples.**

*Model answer:* Absolutely, and this tension is very visible in practice. Tourism brings the financial resources that fund conservation and restoration — the entry fees, guided tours, and ancillary spending that make the maintenance of expensive historic structures economically viable. Sites like Angkor Wat in Cambodia or the Colosseum in Rome are maintained in large part because they attract millions of paying visitors each year.

However, tourism can also inflict serious damage. Physical wear and tear from millions of visitors erodes fragile stonework, paintings, and artefacts. The commercialisation of heritage areas — the proliferation of souvenir stalls, restaurants, and hotels in and around historic districts — can fundamentally alter the character of a place and compromise its integrity. Overcrowding can make the experience of visiting a heritage site feel far removed from its historical significance. Managing this tension requires careful visitor management, investment in conservation, and a willingness to limit access when necessary.

**9. What role should local communities play in decisions about urban development?**

*Model answer:* I think local communities should play a central and active role, rather than simply being informed of decisions that have already been made elsewhere. The people who actually live in a place have the most direct stake in how it changes — they understand its social fabric, its daily rhythms, and its informal values in ways that external planners often don't. Participatory planning processes, where residents are genuinely consulted and have meaningful input into development decisions, tend to produce outcomes that are more sensitive to community needs and more likely to maintain social cohesion.

The challenge is that genuine community participation is time-consuming and sometimes contentious. There are also equity considerations: if participation processes are not carefully designed, they can end up amplifying the voices of more affluent, educated, or organised community members at the expense of those who are most vulnerable to displacement. So community involvement is essential, but it needs to be inclusive and well-facilitated to be truly meaningful.

**10. Do you think international organisations like UNESCO do enough to protect the world's heritage sites?**

*Model answer:* UNESCO's World Heritage programme has undoubtedly made a significant positive contribution — the designation carries enormous prestige and can catalyse both international attention and financial support for at-risk sites. The "List of World Heritage in Danger" has in some cases prompted urgent conservation efforts that might not otherwise have occurred.

That said, UNESCO's influence is ultimately limited. It has no enforcement powers and relies entirely on the goodwill and cooperation of member states. Countries can and do pursue development projects that threaten heritage sites, and the most UNESCO can do is express concern and apply diplomatic pressure. The reality is that if a national government is determined to prioritise economic development over heritage preservation, UNESCO cannot stop them. So while the organisation plays an invaluable role in setting standards and raising awareness, genuine protection ultimately depends on the political will of individual governments — and that is something international organisations can influence, but not compel.
