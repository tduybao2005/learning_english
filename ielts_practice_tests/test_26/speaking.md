# IELTS Practice Test 26 — Speaking
**Chủ đề: Economics & Globalisation**
**Thời gian: 11–14 phút**

---

## PART 1: Introduction & Interview (~4–5 phút)

**Chủ đề 1: Work & Career (Công việc & Sự nghiệp)**

**1.** Do you work or are you a student?
> I'm currently a university student, though I also do some part-time freelance work on the side. It helps me build practical skills while studying.

**2.** What kind of job would you like to have in the future?
> I'd love to work in international business or economic policy. I'm particularly interested in roles that involve analysing how trade agreements affect developing economies.

**3.** Is job security important to you, or do you prefer flexibility?
> I value both, but if I had to choose, I'd lean toward job security at this stage of my career. Having a stable income makes long-term planning — like saving or continuing education — much easier.

**4.** Would you consider working abroad? Why or why not?
> Absolutely. Working abroad would give me exposure to different business cultures and economic systems that I simply couldn't experience at home. It would also strengthen my language skills significantly.

**Chủ đề 2: Money & Shopping (Tiền bạc & Mua sắm)**

**5.** Do you enjoy shopping? What do you like to buy?
> I wouldn't say I'm a big shopper, but I enjoy browsing for books and tech gadgets. I tend to research purchases quite carefully before spending, especially on more expensive items.

**6.** Do you think people spend money wisely these days?
> Not always. The rise of online shopping and one-click purchasing has made impulsive buying extremely easy. I think many people buy things they don't really need, especially when targeted by clever marketing.

**7.** Is it important to save money? How do you save?
> It's very important, particularly given economic uncertainty. I try to set aside a fixed percentage of any money I earn before spending the rest, which is a simple but effective habit.

**8.** Have prices in your country changed significantly recently?
> Yes, inflation has been quite noticeable over the past couple of years. The cost of food, rent, and transport has all increased, and many people I know are finding it harder to make ends meet.

**Chủ đề 3: Cities & Development (Đô thị & Phát triển)**

**9.** Do you live in a city or a rural area?
> I live in a city, which has its advantages — good transport links, access to education and employment — but also its drawbacks, like noise, congestion, and a high cost of living.

**10.** How has your city changed in the last ten years?
> It has developed enormously. There are many new buildings, shopping centres, and infrastructure projects. The skyline looks completely different from when I was a child.

**11.** Do you think cities in your country are developing too quickly?
> In some ways, yes. Rapid urban development has created significant inequality — gleaming new districts sit alongside areas of persistent poverty. I think development needs to be more inclusive and better planned.

**12.** Would you prefer to live in a big city or a small town in the future?
> Honestly, I think a mid-sized city would suit me best — large enough to offer good career opportunities and cultural activities, but small enough to avoid the worst of urban overcrowding and cost pressures.

## PART 2: Individual Long Turn (~3–4 phút)

### Cue Card

**Describe a job or career you think would be interesting to do in the future.**

You should say:
- What the job is
- What it involves
- What skills or qualifications are needed
- And explain why you think it would be interesting

---

### Bài mẫu (~200 từ)

One career I find genuinely fascinating is that of an international trade economist. This role involves analysing how trade policies — such as tariffs, quotas, and free trade agreements — affect national economies and the welfare of different groups within society.

In practice, economists in this field work for governments, international organisations like the WTO or IMF, think tanks, or large multinational corporations. Their day-to-day work might involve building economic models to predict the effects of a proposed trade deal, writing policy briefs for government ministers, or conducting field research in developing countries to assess how global supply chains affect local workers.

To enter this career, you typically need at least a master's degree in economics or a related field, strong quantitative and analytical skills, and proficiency in statistical software. Language skills are also a significant advantage given the international nature of the work.

What makes this career so interesting to me is the combination of intellectual rigour and real-world impact. Trade policy decisions affect the livelihoods of millions of people — from farmers in Vietnam to factory workers in Germany. The work sits at the intersection of economics, politics, and human welfare, which I find endlessly stimulating. It is a career where you can genuinely contribute to shaping a fairer global economy.

## PART 3: Two-Way Discussion (~4–5 phút)

**Chủ đề: Economic Inequality, Globalisation & the Future of Work**

**1.** Do you think the gap between rich and poor countries is getting wider or narrower?
> The picture is mixed. On one hand, countries like China, India, and Vietnam have seen dramatic reductions in poverty over the past few decades, narrowing the gap in absolute terms. However, relative inequality — the proportional difference in wealth and opportunity — remains enormous and in some respects is growing, particularly when comparing the richest nations with the least developed ones.

**2.** How has globalisation affected workers in developing countries?
> Globalisation has had both positive and negative effects. On the positive side, export-led manufacturing has created millions of jobs and raised wages in countries like Bangladesh, Vietnam, and Mexico. On the negative side, workers in global supply chains often face poor conditions, low pay, and limited labour rights, particularly in industries such as garments, electronics, and agriculture.

**3.** Do you think multinational corporations have a responsibility to pay fair wages globally?
> I believe they do, both ethically and practically. Multinationals that exploit cheap labour in developing countries may gain a short-term cost advantage, but they risk reputational damage and regulatory action. More fundamentally, if workers in developing countries earn higher wages, they become consumers themselves, expanding markets and driving sustainable economic growth that benefits everyone.

**4.** What impact do you think automation will have on employment in the coming decades?
> Automation is likely to be deeply disruptive, eliminating routine and manual jobs while creating new roles that require advanced skills. The central challenge is ensuring that workers displaced by automation have access to retraining and that the productivity gains from technology are broadly shared — not simply captured by shareholders.

**5.** Is it the government's responsibility to reduce economic inequality?
> I think governments have a crucial role, though they cannot act alone. Progressive taxation, social safety nets, investment in public education, and effective regulation of markets are all government responsibilities. However, business, civil society, and international institutions all have parts to play as well. Reducing inequality requires coordinated action across multiple levels.

**6.** How does education affect a country's economic development?
> Education is arguably the most important long-term driver of development. Countries with strong educational systems produce a more skilled, innovative, and productive workforce. Education also empowers individuals to participate in political and economic life, fostering the kind of institutional quality — good governance, rule of law — that underpins sustainable development.

**7.** Do you think free trade agreements are generally beneficial?
> Generally, yes, but with important caveats. Free trade can generate significant economic gains through specialisation and competition. However, the benefits are not automatically shared equally, and poorly designed agreements can harm vulnerable industries and workers. Good trade policy requires complementary measures — retraining programmes, social protection — to ensure the gains are broadly distributed.

**8.** What are the possible negative effects of globalisation on local cultures?
> Globalisation can erode local cultures by flooding markets with standardised global products and media content, reducing demand for locally produced goods and traditional practices. Languages, crafts, and customs may decline as younger generations adopt globally prevalent alternatives. At the same time, globalisation can also facilitate cultural exchange and introduce new ideas that enrich local identities rather than simply replacing them.

**9.** Should wealthy countries do more to help developing economies?
> Absolutely. Wealthy nations have both a moral obligation and a practical interest in supporting development. Increased development aid, technology transfer, fairer trade terms, debt relief, and greater representation for developing countries in international institutions would all make a meaningful difference. Historically, the richest nations benefitted from a global order that was often structured to their advantage; redressing this imbalance is both just and strategically wise.

**10.** How do you think the nature of work will change over the next twenty years?
> Work over the next two decades will likely become more flexible, project-based, and digitally mediated. Remote work, which expanded rapidly during the pandemic, is likely to remain widespread. Artificial intelligence will take over many routine cognitive tasks, raising demand for creativity, critical thinking, and interpersonal skills that machines cannot easily replicate. The challenge for societies is to ensure that these changes benefit all workers, not just those with advanced education and digital access.
