## TASK 1 (20 phút — tối thiểu 150 từ)

**Đề bài:**

The pie charts below show the breakdown of household expenditure in the United Kingdom and Vietnam in 2022.

Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.

---

**[Mô tả biểu đồ — Dữ liệu tham khảo]**

| Hạng mục chi tiêu | Anh (UK) | Việt Nam |
|---|---|---|
| Housing & utilities | 32% | 18% |
| Food & beverages | 18% | 35% |
| Transport | 14% | 12% |
| Healthcare | 10% | 8% |
| Education | 6% | 15% |
| Recreation & leisure | 12% | 5% |
| Other | 8% | 7% |

---

### Bài mẫu (Band 7–8, ~175 từ)

The pie charts compare how households in the United Kingdom and Vietnam allocated their spending across seven categories in 2022.

The most striking difference concerns housing and food. In the UK, housing and utilities represented the single largest expenditure at 32%, while food and beverages accounted for only 18%. The pattern was reversed in Vietnam, where food and beverages dominated household budgets at 35%, whereas housing costs were considerably lower at 18%.

Education spending also differed markedly. Vietnamese households devoted 15% of their budget to education — more than double the UK figure of 6% — reflecting the high cultural priority placed on schooling in Vietnam. Conversely, recreation and leisure spending was substantially higher in the UK (12%) than in Vietnam (5%), suggesting a greater disposable income for non-essential activities.

Transport costs were broadly similar in both countries, at 14% and 12% respectively. Healthcare expenditure was likewise comparable, at 10% in the UK and 8% in Vietnam.

Overall, UK households prioritise housing and lifestyle spending, while Vietnamese households allocate a far greater share of income to food and education.

## TASK 2 (40 phút — tối thiểu 250 từ)

**Đề bài:**

*"Economic growth is the most effective way to reduce poverty in developing countries."*

To what extent do you agree or disagree?

---

### Bài mẫu (Band 7–8, ~280 từ)

Economic growth is widely regarded as a powerful engine of poverty reduction, and there is considerable evidence to support this view. However, growth alone is an insufficient guarantee that the benefits will reach the poorest members of society. I therefore partially agree with the statement, while arguing that the quality and distribution of growth are equally important.

On one hand, sustained economic growth has demonstrably lifted millions of people out of poverty. China's rapid industrialisation between 1980 and 2020 reduced its extreme poverty rate from over 80% to below 1%, primarily through GDP expansion, export-led manufacturing, and rising wages. Similarly, Vietnam's consistent growth over the past three decades has dramatically improved living standards and reduced rural poverty. When economies grow, government tax revenues increase, enabling greater public investment in healthcare, education, and infrastructure — the very services that empower the poor to participate more fully in economic life.

On the other hand, growth that is concentrated among elites or driven by capital-intensive industries can leave the poorest behind. In many sub-Saharan African countries, strong GDP growth has coexisted with stagnant wages and persistent inequality. "Growth without development" — a situation in which national income rises while human development indicators remain low — is a documented phenomenon. Under these conditions, complementary policies are essential: progressive taxation, social protection programmes, land reform, and investment in universal education and healthcare.

In conclusion, economic growth is a necessary but not sufficient condition for poverty reduction. Governments must actively shape the patterns of growth to ensure its benefits are shared broadly. Without deliberate redistribution and institutional investment, even robust economic expansion may fail to reach those who need it most.
