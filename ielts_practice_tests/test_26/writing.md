The maps below show the layout of a small coastal town called Hartfield in 1990 and 2020.

---

### Model Answer — Task 1 (approximately 175 words)

The maps illustrate the transformation of Hartfield, a small coastal town, between 1990 and 2020.

Overall, the town underwent substantial development over the thirty-year period, with significant changes to its residential, commercial, transport, and waterfront areas, while the southern woodland was largely preserved.

The most striking change occurred in the northern district, where open farmland was replaced by a large residential estate of approximately 500 homes. In the town centre, the original market square with its independent shops gave way to a large shopping mall, though the square itself was pedestrianised. On the seafront, the original fishing harbour was converted into a marina serving leisure boats, and the beach was extended with a new promenade.

The eastern edge saw industrial warehouses transformed into a modern technology business park, while the western road was widened from two lanes to a four-lane dual carriageway to accommodate increased traffic. Transport links were also expanded, with additional bus routes and a new light rail station supplementing the original single bus route. Notably, the southern forest remained intact; an eco-park and visitor centre were added to enhance its accessibility.

## WRITING TASK 2

*You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.*

*Write about the following topic:*

**Urban development and modernisation of cities inevitably leads to the destruction of natural environments and historical heritage. To what extent do you agree or disagree?**

*Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.*

*Write at least 250 words.*

---

### Model Answer — Task 2 (approximately 285 words)

The idea that urban progress necessarily comes at the cost of natural environments and historical heritage is one that I partially agree with, though I believe the relationship between modernisation and preservation is more nuanced than this statement implies.

On one hand, there is substantial evidence that rapid urbanisation can be destructive. In many developing nations, the pressure to build new infrastructure, housing, and commercial centres has led to the demolition of historic buildings and the clearance of green spaces. Cities such as Beijing and Cairo have seen ancient neighbourhoods razed to make way for modern developments, erasing centuries of cultural memory in the process. Similarly, the expansion of urban areas into forests, wetlands, and agricultural land destroys irreplaceable ecosystems and contributes to the broader crisis of biodiversity loss.

However, the notion that such destruction is inevitable is contestable. Many cities have demonstrated convincingly that economic growth and preservation can coexist. Amsterdam has maintained its historic seventeenth-century canal district while remaining a thriving, modern European economy. Singapore has invested heavily in incorporating urban greenery into its architecture and infrastructure, achieving worldwide recognition as a "city in a garden." These examples suggest that with sufficient political will, financial investment, and sound planning, urban development can proceed in ways that respect both cultural heritage and ecological value.

Furthermore, modern governance frameworks — including UNESCO's World Heritage Site designation and mandatory national environmental impact assessments — provide legal mechanisms to protect significant natural and historic sites from uncontrolled development. Where these frameworks are rigorously enforced, the most destructive consequences of urbanisation can be effectively prevented.

In conclusion, while urban development does pose a genuine and serious threat to natural and historical assets, I do not believe this destruction is unavoidable. Thoughtful planning, strong governance, and sustained public commitment to heritage can ensure that cities evolve without sacrificing the environments and histories that give them meaning and identity.
