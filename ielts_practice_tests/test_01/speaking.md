# IELTS Academic Speaking — Test 01

**Time allowed: 11–14 minutes (approximately)**

---

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*The examiner will ask you general questions on familiar topics. Answer naturally and extend your responses.*

---

### Topic 1: Work and Study

**1. Do you work or are you a student?**

*Model answer:* I'm currently a full-time student studying Business Administration at university. I'm in my second year, so the workload is quite demanding at the moment. I enjoy most of my subjects, particularly the marketing modules, which I find both creative and practically useful.

**2. What do you enjoy most about your studies?**

*Model answer:* I particularly enjoy working on group projects because they push me to communicate my ideas clearly and to listen to perspectives different from my own. I also appreciate the freedom university gives me to explore topics independently through research and reading, which feels very different from the more structured learning I did at school.

**3. Do you think the subjects you are studying will be useful in the future?**

*Model answer:* I genuinely believe they will be. The analytical and communication skills I'm developing — writing reports, presenting arguments, working in teams — seem applicable to almost any professional environment. Even if I don't end up in a strictly business role, the ability to think critically and present information persuasively will always be valuable.

**4. How do you manage your time between studying and personal life?**

*Model answer:* It's honestly something I've had to work on. I try to plan my week in advance and set specific blocks of time for studying, so I don't end up cramming everything at the last minute. I've found that keeping a simple to-do list and scheduling breaks genuinely helps me stay focused and avoid feeling overwhelmed.

---

### Topic 2: Technology

**5. How often do you use technology in your daily life?**

*Model answer:* Almost constantly, to be honest. My phone is the first thing I check in the morning and one of the last things I look at before sleeping. I use technology for studying, communicating with friends and family, watching films, and navigating around the city. It's deeply embedded in virtually every part of my daily routine.

**6. What kind of technology do you think has changed your life the most?**

*Model answer:* Without a doubt, the smartphone. It has combined so many tools — a map, a camera, a music player, a library, a communication device — into one device that fits in my pocket. I honestly cannot remember the last time I needed a physical dictionary or had to ask a stranger for directions. The convenience is extraordinary.

**7. Are there any negative effects of technology that you have personally experienced?**

*Model answer:* Yes, definitely. I find it very difficult to concentrate for long periods now because I'm so accustomed to switching between apps and checking notifications. There's also a sense that I'm never fully present when I'm socialising — someone always has their phone out. I'm genuinely trying to be more mindful about when and how I use my devices.

**8. Do you think technology will replace teachers in the future?**

*Model answer:* I think it's unlikely to replace teachers entirely, though it will certainly transform the role. Good teachers do far more than simply transmit information — they read the emotional state of a classroom, they build relationships, they inspire curiosity and confidence. Those are fundamentally human skills that I don't believe an algorithm can replicate, at least not in any meaningful sense.

### Topic 3: Reading

**9. Do you enjoy reading? What kinds of things do you read?**

*Model answer:* I enjoy reading when I have the time, though I'm more of a non-fiction reader than a fiction one. I tend to gravitate towards books about history, psychology, and science — the kind that help me make sense of the world I live in. I also read a lot of articles online, which is probably not as deep as reading a full book but at least keeps me engaged with current ideas.

**10. Do you prefer reading physical books or digital content?**

*Model answer:* I have a slight preference for physical books because I find the experience of holding and annotating a book more enjoyable and more memorable. There's research suggesting that people retain information better from physical text than from screens, which I find plausible based on my own experience. That said, for quick reference or news, digital reading is obviously far more convenient.

**11. How has your reading habit changed since childhood?**

*Model answer:* As a child I was a voracious reader of adventure stories and fantasy novels — I could finish a book in a couple of days without any effort. As I've grown older, I read more selectively but perhaps more critically. I'm now less interested in escapism and more interested in ideas that challenge or refine the way I think about things.

**12. Do you think reading is becoming less popular with younger generations?**

*Model answer:* It's a complicated picture. Reading in the traditional sense — sitting down with a book for an extended period — does seem to be declining among younger people. But in another sense, young people today read enormous amounts of text every day through social media, messaging apps, and online platforms. Whether this constitutes "reading" in the way the question implies is genuinely debatable.

## PART 2 — Individual Long Turn

*You will be given a topic card. You have one minute to prepare, then speak for one to two minutes.*

---

### Cue Card

**Describe a teacher who had a significant positive influence on you.**

*You should say:*
- who this teacher was and what subject they taught
- what made this teacher different from others
- how this teacher influenced you specifically
- and explain why you consider this teacher's influence to be significant

---

### Model Response (approximately 90 seconds)

The teacher who has had the most lasting influence on me was my secondary school history teacher, Mr Nguyen — or as we called him among ourselves, the storyteller, because that was fundamentally how he taught. While most of our other teachers used traditional methods — lecturing from a textbook, writing on the board, setting exercises — Mr Nguyen taught almost entirely through narrative. He would begin a lesson by placing us inside a historical moment, describing what people at the time saw, heard, and feared, and only afterwards would he step back and help us analyse why events had unfolded the way they did.

What made him different, I think, was that he took our opinions seriously. He would pose genuinely difficult historical questions — Was this revolution inevitable? Could this war have been avoided? — and then actually listen to our answers rather than waiting to deliver his own. He created an environment in which being curious and being wrong were both acceptable, which was quite unusual in my experience of school.

His influence on me was primarily intellectual. He taught me that understanding history means understanding human motivation — why people make the choices they do under pressure. That kind of thinking has stayed with me and shaped how I approach problems in every other area of my life. I became significantly more interested in reading, in asking "why" rather than just accepting facts, and eventually in studying social sciences.

I consider his influence significant because it fundamentally changed how I learn. He didn't just teach me historical facts — he taught me how to think about information, how to question assumptions, and how to form an argument. Those are skills I use every single day.

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion

*The examiner will ask more abstract questions related to the Part 2 topic.*

---

### Theme: Education and Technology

**1. How has technology changed the way teachers teach in schools today?**

*Model answer:* Technology has transformed teaching in several significant ways. Interactive whiteboards, projectors, and online platforms allow teachers to present information in more visually engaging formats than a blackboard ever permitted. More importantly, digital tools allow students to access resources, submit work, and receive feedback outside classroom hours, extending the learning relationship beyond the physical school day. However, critics argue that technology can also distract students and reduce the quality of in-person engagement between teachers and learners.

**2. Do you think students today have better or worse educational experiences than previous generations?**

*Model answer:* I think the answer depends heavily on what we value in education. In terms of access to information and diversity of learning resources, students today are extraordinarily well-served compared to any previous generation. However, there is a legitimate concern that the depth of learning — the kind that comes from sustained, focused engagement with difficult material — may have diminished in an environment of constant digital distraction and pressure for quick results. Both gains and losses are real.

**3. What qualities make an effective teacher in the modern world?**

*Model answer:* Effective teaching in the modern world still fundamentally depends on the qualities that have always mattered: subject expertise, genuine enthusiasm for the material, and an ability to build relationships with learners. What has changed is that teachers must now also be skilled at navigating technology, maintaining students' attention in a world of competing digital stimuli, and personalising learning to an increasingly diverse range of needs and backgrounds. The ability to inspire critical thinking rather than passive consumption of information is arguably more important than ever.

**4. Is it possible for online education to fully replace traditional classroom learning?**

*Model answer:* Online education has proven remarkably effective for adult learners who are self-motivated and have clear goals — professional training courses, university degrees, and language learning are all well-served by digital platforms. However, for younger learners in particular, the social and emotional dimensions of classroom learning — peer interaction, mentorship, collaborative problem-solving — are not easily replicated online. Research conducted during the COVID-19 pandemic, when millions of children switched to home-based digital learning, suggested that the disruption to social development was at least as significant as the disruption to academic progress.

**5. How important is it for schools to teach critical thinking rather than rote memorisation?**

*Model answer:* Enormously important, particularly in a world where information is more abundant than at any point in history. In an environment where any factual question can be answered by a search engine in seconds, the ability to evaluate sources, identify bias, construct logical arguments, and recognise manipulation is far more valuable than the ability to recall isolated facts. Education systems that continue to prioritise memorisation over reasoning are arguably preparing students for a world that no longer exists. The challenge is that teaching critical thinking is significantly more difficult and resource-intensive than teaching facts.

**6. Should governments allocate more funding to universities or to primary and secondary education?**

*Model answer:* The evidence on returns to investment in education strongly favours early years and primary education. Research in developmental economics has consistently shown that interventions in the early stages of life — quality early childhood education, nutritional support, stable learning environments — produce the highest long-term social returns, both for individuals and for society as a whole. University education benefits individuals significantly, but its social returns are more concentrated. If the goal is to reduce inequality and maximise broad social benefit, the case for prioritising primary and secondary education is compelling.

**7. Do you think private schools provide a better education than state schools?**

*Model answer:* Private schools often have advantages in terms of resources, smaller class sizes, and the social networks they provide access to. However, the evidence that these advantages produce genuinely superior educational outcomes — as measured by skills, intellectual curiosity, or critical thinking — is less clear-cut than is commonly assumed. Many of the apparent advantages of private school education are better explained by the socioeconomic backgrounds of students than by the quality of teaching itself. The most important variable in educational outcomes, the research consistently shows, is the quality of individual teachers, which is not determined by whether an institution is private or state-funded.

**8. In what ways can education systems better prepare young people for the future world of work?**

*Model answer:* Education systems need to place greater emphasis on adaptability, lifelong learning, and transferable skills. The specific technical skills taught in schools today may be obsolete within a decade due to the pace of technological change — particularly with the advance of artificial intelligence. What will remain durable are skills such as complex problem-solving, creative thinking, effective communication, emotional intelligence, and the ability to learn new skills rapidly. Schools would serve students better by cultivating these capacities than by teaching content that may have limited relevance by the time students enter the workforce.

**9. What role should parents play in their children's education?**

*Model answer:* Parents play an irreplaceable role, particularly in the early years when foundational literacy, numeracy, and attitudes towards learning are formed. Research consistently identifies parental involvement — reading to children, discussing ideas at the dinner table, modelling a curiosity about the world — as one of the strongest predictors of educational achievement. However, the nature of that involvement matters: parental pressure that creates anxiety and fear of failure can be actively harmful. The most beneficial parental role is one that supports intrinsic motivation and treats mistakes as opportunities for learning rather than sources of shame.

**10. How might artificial intelligence change education in the next twenty years?**

*Model answer:* Artificial intelligence is likely to have a profound impact on education, though the precise shape of that impact remains uncertain. In the near term, AI tutoring systems that adapt in real time to individual learners' progress and misconceptions have the potential to provide the kind of personalised feedback that only the most dedicated human tutor could previously offer. In the longer term, AI may fundamentally reshape what schools need to teach, rendering certain skills redundant while making others newly critical. The risk is that AI tools are adopted in education primarily as cost-saving measures rather than as genuine enhancements to learning quality, which could exacerbate existing inequalities.
