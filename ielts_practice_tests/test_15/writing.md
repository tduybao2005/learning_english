## WRITING TASK 1

*You should spend about 20 minutes on this task.*

**The table below shows crime statistics for four cities in 2023, measured in incidents per 100,000 population. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.**

*Write at least 150 words.*

---

### Crime Statistics by Type per 100,000 Population (2023)

| Crime Type       | London | Sydney | Tokyo | São Paulo |
|------------------|--------|--------|-------|-----------|
| Theft            | 2,100  | 1,800  | 450   | 3,200     |
| Violent crime    | 850    | 620    | 180   | 1,940     |
| Cybercrime       | 320    | 280    | 510   | 190       |
| Drug offences    | 410    | 390    | 95    | 680       |
| **Total**        | **3,680** | **3,090** | **1,235** | **5,010** |

---

### Band 7–8 Model Answer

The table compares crime rates across four major cities — London, Sydney, Tokyo, and São Paulo — for four categories of offence, expressed as incidents per 100,000 people in 2023.

Overall, São Paulo recorded the highest total crime rate by a considerable margin, while Tokyo had substantially lower figures than the three other cities across almost every category. Theft was the most prevalent crime type in all four locations.

In terms of theft, São Paulo's rate of 3,200 incidents per 100,000 was the highest, nearly seven times greater than Tokyo's figure of 450. London and Sydney fell between these extremes at 2,100 and 1,800 respectively. A similar pattern was observed for violent crime, where São Paulo again led with 1,940 incidents, compared with just 180 in Tokyo. London's violent crime rate of 850 was notably higher than Sydney's 620.

Cybercrime presented a striking reversal of this trend. Tokyo recorded the highest rate of 510 incidents, surpassing London (320), Sydney (280), and São Paulo (190) considerably. This suggests that despite Tokyo's otherwise low crime profile, it faces a disproportionate challenge from digital offences.

Drug offences followed the general pattern, with São Paulo recording the most at 680 and Tokyo the fewest at 95. London and Sydney were broadly comparable at 410 and 390 respectively.

In conclusion, the data reveal that São Paulo experiences the greatest overall crime burden, Tokyo shows exceptional safety except in cybercrime, and the Western cities occupy intermediate positions across most categories.

*(196 words)*

---

### Examiner Notes: Task 1

**Task Achievement (Band 8):** The response covers all four cities and all four crime types, identifies the most significant trends (São Paulo highest overall, Tokyo's anomalous cybercrime position), and provides an accurate overview. Figures are cited selectively and purposefully rather than listed mechanically.

**Coherence and Cohesion (Band 7–8):** Organised logically — overview, then crime-by-crime analysis. Cohesive devices ("A similar pattern," "a striking reversal," "despite") guide the reader efficiently. Paragraph structure is clear.

**Lexical Resource (Band 8):** Strong range of crime vocabulary and comparison language: *"by a considerable margin," "disproportionate challenge," "surpassing," "broadly comparable."* Avoids repetition of the word "high" by using "greatest," "most," "highest."

**Grammatical Range (Band 7–8):** Varied sentence structures including passive constructions (*"was observed"*), concessive clauses (*"despite Tokyo's otherwise low crime profile"*), and comparative/superlative forms used accurately.

**Key Advice for Learners:**
- Always write an overview (2 sentences minimum) identifying the biggest trends — this is the single most important Band 6 → Band 7 step.
- Group comparisons logically (by crime type OR by city); mixing randomly loses coherence marks.
- Never include every number; select the most significant data points.
- Do not begin with "This table shows…" — paraphrase the prompt.

## WRITING TASK 2

*You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.*

**Some people believe that preventing crime through social programmes, education, and community initiatives is more effective than punishing offenders after crimes have been committed. Others argue that strict punishment is necessary to deter criminal behaviour.**

**Discuss both views and give your own opinion.**

*Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.*

*Write at least 250 words.*

---

### Band 7–8 Model Answer

The question of whether society is better served by preventing crime before it occurs or by rigorously punishing those who commit it has long been debated among criminologists, policymakers, and the public. While a strong case can be made for punitive approaches, I am persuaded that well-designed preventative strategies ultimately represent the more effective long-term solution, though both elements have a role to play.

Advocates of strict punishment argue that the threat of significant penalties serves as a deterrent, discouraging potential offenders from engaging in criminal behaviour. The rationale is rooted in classical criminological theory: if individuals calculate that the costs of crime — in terms of imprisonment or financial penalties — outweigh the potential benefits, they will choose to comply with the law. Countries such as Singapore, which combines swift prosecution with severe sentences, are frequently cited as evidence that a tough legal environment can maintain low crime rates. Furthermore, incarceration physically removes dangerous individuals from society during the period of their sentence, preventing repeat victimisation.

However, the evidence base for prevention is arguably more compelling. Research consistently indicates that a disproportionate share of crime is committed by people facing poverty, addiction, poor education, and social marginalisation. Programmes that address these root causes — early childhood education, drug rehabilitation, job training for at-risk youth — have demonstrated measurable reductions in offending rates in multiple international contexts. A well-known example is the Perry Preschool Project in the United States, a longitudinal study spanning decades, which found that high-quality pre-school education for disadvantaged children produced significant reductions in adult arrest rates and yielded economic returns many times the cost of the programme. If crime arises substantially from social conditions rather than from unconstrained rational calculation, then punishment alone — while satisfying a public appetite for justice — cannot address the structural drivers that produce criminal behaviour in the first place.

It is also worth noting that incarceration, particularly in overcrowded or under-resourced prison systems, can itself be a cause of subsequent offending rather than a solution to it. Recidivism rates in countries that invest heavily in rehabilitation — Norway being the most frequently cited example — are substantially lower than in systems focused primarily on punitive confinement, suggesting that how offenders are treated during and after punishment matters as much as the severity of the punishment itself.

In my view, the most effective approach recognises that prevention and punishment are not mutually exclusive but complementary. A society that invests in education, mental health services, and economic opportunity will generate less crime; a functioning justice system that responds proportionately to those who do offend remains necessary nonetheless. The risk of treating punishment as the primary strategy is that it treats crime as an individual moral failing in isolation from the social conditions that make it more likely, and this misdiagnosis inevitably leads to ineffective policy.

In conclusion, while punishment has its place in maintaining order and delivering justice to victims, the weight of evidence suggests that investment in prevention yields greater and more lasting reductions in crime. Societies that acknowledge this are likely to be both safer and more just.

*(519 words)*

---

### Examiner Notes: Task 2

**Task Response (Band 8):** Both views are presented fairly and with supporting evidence. The writer's own position is clearly stated and consistently maintained throughout. The conclusion does not merely repeat the introduction but synthesises the argument. The essay goes beyond surface-level discussion to consider the systemic dimensions of crime (recidivism, root causes).

**Coherence and Cohesion (Band 8):** Five well-structured paragraphs: introduction, punishment view, prevention view (extended), counter-argument within prevention, and conclusion. Discourse markers ("However," "Furthermore," "It is also worth noting," "In my view") are varied and accurate. Each paragraph has a clear controlling idea.

**Lexical Resource (Band 7–8):** Sophisticated vocabulary throughout: *"rationale," "marginalisation," "longitudinal study," "recidivism," "punitive confinement," "complementary."* Phrases like *"disproportionate share,"* *"structural drivers,"* and *"misdiagnosis"* demonstrate high-level lexical precision.

**Grammatical Range (Band 7–8):** Complex structures including conditional sentences (*"If crime arises substantially from…"*), passive voice (*"is committed," "have been committed"*), relative clauses, and concessive constructions (*"while a strong case can be made"*) are deployed accurately. No significant errors.

### Band 6 vs Band 7 Comparison

| Feature | Band 6 Response | Band 7–8 Response |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Position** | "I think both views are important." | Clear position stated and consistently argued |
| **Examples** | "For example, in some countries, punishment is strict." | Specific named examples: Perry Preschool Project, Norway recidivism data, Singapore |
| **Vocabulary** | "crime," "bad," "people," "do wrong things" | "recidivism," "marginalisation," "punitive confinement," "structural drivers" |
| **Grammar** | Mostly simple/compound sentences with some errors | Mix of complex structures; conditionals, passives, concessive clauses used accurately |
| **Development** | Each point mentioned but not fully explained | Each point explained, evidenced, and linked back to the central argument |
| **Cohesion** | "Also," "And," "But" | "However," "Furthermore," "It is also worth noting," "In my view" |
| **Conclusion** | Restates introduction with minimal addition | Synthesises argument; adds nuance ("prevention and punishment are complementary") |

**The single biggest difference between Band 6 and Band 7:** A Band 6 essay *mentions* ideas; a Band 7 essay *develops* them with specific evidence, logical reasoning, and clear links between claims.
