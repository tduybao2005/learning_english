## PART 1 — Interview Questions (4–5 minutes)

### Topic 1: Science & Discovery

**1. Are you interested in science? Why or why not?**

> Yes, I find science genuinely fascinating, particularly when it helps explain everyday phenomena I have always taken for granted. I am especially drawn to fields like neuroscience and physics, where the findings often challenge our intuitive assumptions about reality. I do not have a scientific background professionally, but I read popular science books and podcasts regularly because I believe understanding how the world works makes me a more informed and curious person.

**2. What scientific discovery do you find most fascinating?**

> Without doubt, I find the discovery of DNA's double-helix structure one of the most remarkable achievements in human history. The idea that the blueprint for every living organism is encoded in a spiral molecule contained within each of our trillions of cells is almost impossible to comprehend in its elegance and scale. More recently, I have been captivated by CRISPR gene-editing technology and what it might mean for the future of medicine and human biology.

**3. Did you enjoy science at school?**

> It was mixed, honestly. I loved biology because the content felt immediately relevant to my own life and body. However, I struggled with chemistry, largely because the way it was taught at my school focused heavily on memorisation and calculations rather than conceptual understanding. Looking back, I think a more inquiry-based approach to teaching science would have made a real difference to how engaged I felt, and I suspect that is true for many students.

**4. Do you think science education should be compulsory until age 18?**

> I think some core scientific literacy should be required for all students until at least sixteen, because understanding basic principles of biology, chemistry, and physics is essential for navigating modern life — from making health decisions to evaluating news about climate change. However, I am less convinced that highly specialised science subjects should be compulsory for all students up to eighteen. Young people deserve the freedom to pursue the disciplines that genuinely motivate them, even if those are in the arts or humanities.

### Topic 2: Languages

**5. How many languages do you speak?**

> I speak Vietnamese as my mother tongue and English as a second language, which I have been studying seriously for several years now. I also have a basic working knowledge of Mandarin Chinese from a short course I took at university, though I would not claim to be conversational in it. I am currently focused on improving my English to the level I need for further academic study, but I would love to develop my Mandarin more seriously in the future.

**6. Why did you choose to learn English?**

> Honestly, at first it was not entirely a choice — English is compulsory in Vietnamese schools from a young age, so my early exposure was not particularly voluntary. But as I progressed and began to actually understand the language, I became genuinely motivated by what it unlocked: access to a much wider range of books, academic resources, films, and people. English fluency increasingly feels like a professional and intellectual necessity rather than simply a subject I was taught at school.

**7. Do you think it's easier to learn a language as a child or as an adult?**

> Research strongly supports the idea that children acquire languages more naturally and with better pronunciation, primarily because of neurological plasticity in early development. However, I think adults have some underappreciated advantages — a broader vocabulary base, stronger analytical skills, and more deliberate study strategies. As an adult learner myself, I find that understanding grammar rules explicitly helps me make faster structural progress than simply immersing myself without guidance. So I would say both ages have distinct advantages.

**8. Is English widely used in your country?**

> English is used extensively in Vietnam, particularly in urban areas, professional environments, and education. Most universities offer courses in English and many international companies operating in Vietnam require proficiency. That said, in rural areas or among older generations, English exposure is much more limited. There is growing societal pressure on young Vietnamese people to achieve strong English skills, which has created a thriving private language school industry, particularly for IELTS and TOEFL preparation.

### Topic 3: Happiness & Wellbeing

**9. What makes you happy in everyday life?**

> The things that bring me the most consistent happiness are fairly simple — a good conversation with a close friend, reading something that challenges or surprises me, cooking a meal I am proud of, or spending time outdoors when the weather is pleasant. I have noticed that the activities which reliably improve my mood involve full presence and engagement rather than passive consumption. Research on wellbeing seems to confirm this: connection, accomplishment, and meaning matter far more to sustained happiness than pleasure or comfort.

**10. Do you think people today are happier than previous generations?**

> That is a surprisingly difficult question to answer, because happiness is so subjective and hard to measure historically. Objectively, people in most countries live longer, healthier, and more comfortable lives than previous generations. Yet surveys of subjective wellbeing in many wealthy nations suggest rates of anxiety, loneliness, and depression are rising, particularly among young people. I suspect that material progress and psychological flourishing do not necessarily move in the same direction, and that the social and community structures which historically provided meaning have eroded faster than we anticipated.

**11. How important is work to your happiness?**

> Work is central to my happiness, but I think it is important to be clear about why. It is not the status or income that matters most to me, but rather the sense of purpose, progress, and competence that meaningful work provides. Research consistently shows that people are happiest when they are using their skills to work towards goals they care about — what psychologists call a "flow state." The problem is that many jobs do not offer those conditions, which I believe is one of the most underappreciated sources of widespread unhappiness.

**12. What advice would you give to someone who wants to be happier?**

> I would suggest starting by separating the things that actually influence wellbeing from the things we assume will make us happy. Research suggests that strong relationships, a sense of purpose, regular exercise, adequate sleep, and acts of generosity are among the most reliable predictors of sustained happiness — and none of them require significant money. I would also encourage reducing the habit of comparing yourself to others, particularly on social media, which research links quite strongly to lower life satisfaction and increased anxiety.

## PART 2 — Individual Long Turn (3–4 minutes)

### Cue Card

**Describe a law or rule that you think should be changed.**

You should say:
- what the law or rule is
- why it currently exists
- why you think it should be changed
- and explain what you would replace it with, or how you would change it

---

### Model Response (Band 7–8)

The law I want to discuss is the minimum legal voting age of eighteen in most countries, including my own. I believe this age threshold should be lowered to sixteen in national elections.

Currently, the voting age of eighteen exists primarily for historical reasons — it was tied to the age of military conscription and the assumption that adulthood begins at that point. However, in many countries sixteen-year-olds can legally work, pay taxes, consent to medical treatment, and drive. It seems logically inconsistent to trust them with these responsibilities while excluding them from the democratic process that shapes the laws they live under.

My main argument for lowering the voting age is that sixteen-year-olds are directly affected by political decisions about education, climate change, and economic policy — often more profoundly than older voters, since they will live with the consequences for far longer. Research from countries like Austria and Scotland, where sixteen-year-olds can vote in certain elections, suggests that young voters are engaged, thoughtful, and no less informed than average adult voters.

I would not abolish the current system entirely — I would extend the right to vote to sixteen-year-olds in all national and local elections, and pair this with stronger civic education in schools so that young people have the knowledge and context to participate effectively. I believe this change would make democracy more representative and encourage lifelong civic participation from an earlier age.

### Follow-up Question

**"Do you think young people are generally interested in politics?"**

> It depends on how you define politics. If you mean formal party politics and voting, interest among young people is often lower than among older generations — but I think this reflects disillusionment with institutions rather than indifference to the issues themselves. Young people are often intensely engaged with political questions around climate, inequality, and social justice; they simply express that engagement through protests, social media, and activism rather than through conventional political channels.

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion (4–5 minutes)

### 1. Why is it important for societies to have laws?

> Laws are fundamentally important because they establish the shared framework that makes cooperation and trust possible in a society of millions of strangers. Without enforceable rules, individuals and groups with greater power would simply act in their own interests at the expense of others, and there would be no reliable mechanism to prevent or remedy this. Laws also encode collective moral judgements about fairness, harm, and individual rights, giving those values a practical form that shapes behaviour even when no one is watching. In short, law is one of the foundations of civilised social life.

### 2. Do you think laws in your country are generally fair?

> Vietnam has made considerable progress in developing a more comprehensive legal framework over recent decades, and many laws in areas like contract, property, and criminal procedure reflect international standards. However, I think there are genuine concerns about consistency of enforcement, access to legal representation for ordinary citizens, and the independence of courts from political pressure. Fairness in law depends not only on the content of legislation but on whether it is applied equally and transparently, and in that respect there is meaningful room for improvement in many countries, not just Vietnam.

### 3. Should punishments focus more on rehabilitation or deterrence?

> Evidence from criminology research fairly consistently favours rehabilitation, particularly for non-violent offenders. Countries with the highest rates of incarceration, like the United States, do not demonstrate correspondingly lower crime rates, which suggests that deterrence through punishment has limited effectiveness when social conditions that produce crime remain unaddressed. Rehabilitation programmes that address education, mental health, addiction, and employment dramatically reduce reoffending rates and are generally more cost-effective than long custodial sentences. I think a rational, evidence-based justice system should prioritise rehabilitation while maintaining deterrence for serious offences.

### 4. Who should decide what is legal — elected governments or courts?

> In most democratic systems, these two institutions play complementary rather than competing roles. Elected governments create legislation and respond to public mandates; courts interpret and apply the law, and crucially, check whether legislation violates constitutional rights or principles. Neither should have unchecked power. An entirely parliamentary system with no judicial review risks majority tyranny over minorities, while judicial supremacy without democratic accountability raises legitimate concerns about unelected power. The balance between the two is one of the central challenges of constitutional design, and different countries have resolved it in quite different ways.

### 5. Is it possible for everyone in a society to be happy?

> Universal happiness in any absolute sense is almost certainly impossible — human experience includes grief, loss, failure, and frustration, and no society can eliminate these. But I think it is both possible and morally necessary for societies to create conditions under which the majority of people can live meaningful, dignified, and reasonably satisfying lives. This means addressing material poverty, reducing extreme inequality, building strong public services, and protecting community bonds. The evidence from countries that consistently top global happiness rankings suggests that policy choices make a real difference — this is not simply a matter of individual psychology.

### 6. Why do you think some wealthy countries have high rates of depression?

> This is one of the more perplexing findings in wellbeing research. Several factors seem to contribute. First, affluence often comes alongside the erosion of the strong social networks and community structures that historically provided meaning and belonging. Second, high-income societies frequently create intense competition and comparison, generating chronic stress and inadequacy even among people who are materially comfortable. Third, in many wealthy countries, the individualisation of life — the expectation that you are solely responsible for your own success and happiness — can be psychologically isolating. The science of happiness suggests that social connection and purpose matter far more than income above a comfortable threshold.

### 7. What role should governments play in promoting citizens' wellbeing?

> Governments clearly have a responsibility to provide the material foundations of wellbeing: healthcare, education, safe housing, and economic security. But beyond these basics, the question of how far governments should intervene in more personal dimensions of happiness is genuinely contested. Some argue for what behavioural economists call "nudging" — designing choice environments that make healthy and pro-social behaviours easier without restricting freedom. Others argue that governmental involvement in lifestyle choices risks paternalism. I think governments should focus on structural conditions and safety nets rather than trying to prescribe how citizens should feel or live.

### 8. Do you think the pursuit of happiness has become too individualistic?

> Yes, I think that is a fair and important criticism. In many Western cultural contexts, happiness has been reframed as primarily a personal project — something you achieve through individual choices, mindsets, and self-improvement. This framework ignores the substantial evidence that happiness is fundamentally social and relational, and it places an unfair burden of personal responsibility on people whose unhappiness is largely a product of structural circumstances — poverty, discrimination, insecurity — that individual effort cannot overcome. It also depoliticises suffering in ways that may benefit those with an interest in maintaining those structural conditions.

### 9. How has scientific research changed our understanding of human happiness?

> Happiness research — drawing on psychology, neuroscience, economics, and sociology — has overturned several intuitive assumptions. We now know that people are remarkably poor at predicting what will make them happy, consistently overestimating the impact of major life events like promotions, relationships, or purchases. We know that adaptation means most changes — positive or negative — have a smaller and shorter-lasting effect on wellbeing than we expect. We know that social relationships, autonomy, and purpose are more reliable predictors of life satisfaction than wealth beyond a moderate level. These findings have practical implications not just for individuals but for how governments and organisations should be designed.

### 10. Do you think future generations will have more or less control over their own wellbeing?

> I think the picture is genuinely uncertain, pointing in both directions simultaneously. On the positive side, advances in medicine, neuroscience, and technology may give future people unprecedented tools to understand and manage their mental and physical health. On the negative side, climate change, economic instability, and the erosion of privacy through digital surveillance create powerful forces that limit individuals' agency. I also think there is something potentially worrying about technologies that can manipulate mood, attention, or desire at a neurological level — the capacity to optimise happiness could easily become the capacity to manufacture compliance or passive contentment rather than genuine flourishing.
