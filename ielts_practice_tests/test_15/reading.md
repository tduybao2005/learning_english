## READING PASSAGE 1

**Small Wonders: The Revolutionary World of Nanotechnology**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 1–13, which are based on Reading Passage 1.*

---

**Paragraph A**

Nanotechnology — the manipulation of matter at an atomic and molecular scale — has transitioned from a speculative concept into one of the most consequential scientific fields of the twenty-first century. Working at dimensions between one and one hundred nanometres, researchers are able to engineer materials with properties that differ profoundly from those exhibited at larger scales. A human hair is roughly eighty thousand nanometres wide; a single red blood cell spans approximately seven thousand nanometres. At the nanoscale, the familiar laws of classical physics give way to quantum mechanical phenomena, and the surface-area-to-volume ratio of particles increases so dramatically that chemical reactivity, electrical conductivity, and optical behaviour all change in ways that scientists are only beginning to fully exploit.

**Paragraph B**

The field traces its intellectual origins to a 1959 lecture by physicist Richard Feynman entitled "There's Plenty of Room at the Bottom," in which he envisioned the direct manipulation of individual atoms. Yet the practical tools to realise this vision did not emerge until the 1980s, when the invention of the scanning tunnelling microscope allowed researchers to image and, eventually, to reposition individual atoms on a surface. Since then, nanotechnology has branched into dozens of sub-disciplines, ranging from nanomedicine and nanoelectronics to nanomaterials science and environmental remediation. The pace of progress has been remarkable: the global nanotechnology market, valued at roughly USD 1.76 billion in 2020, is projected to exceed USD 33 billion by 2030, reflecting the breadth of applications now entering commercial deployment.

**Paragraph C**

In medicine, nanotechnology has delivered some of its most dramatic early results. Nanoparticles can be engineered to carry therapeutic payloads — chemotherapy drugs, gene-editing sequences, or diagnostic contrast agents — directly to diseased tissue while largely sparing healthy cells. Liposomal formulations, in which drugs are encased in lipid nanoparticles, have already been approved for treating certain cancers and fungal infections. Researchers at several institutions have demonstrated that iron-oxide nanoparticles can be guided to tumour sites using external magnetic fields, where they then generate localised heat sufficient to destroy malignant cells — a technique known as magnetic hyperthermia. Meanwhile, lab-on-a-chip devices incorporating nanosensors can detect cancer biomarkers in blood at concentrations that conventional assays would miss entirely, potentially enabling diagnoses years before symptoms appear.

**Paragraph D**

The electronics industry has depended on miniaturisation for decades, and nanotechnology has allowed this process to continue beyond the limits of conventional silicon lithography. Carbon nanotubes — hollow cylinders of carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal lattice — conduct electricity with far lower resistance than copper and are mechanically stronger than steel at a fraction of the weight. Transistors built from single carbon nanotubes have been demonstrated operating at switching speeds that silicon devices cannot match. Graphene, a single layer of carbon atoms arranged in the same hexagonal pattern but as a flat sheet rather than a tube, exhibits extraordinary electron mobility and thermal conductivity, making it a candidate material for next-generation transistors, flexible displays, and high-efficiency solar cells. Several major semiconductor manufacturers have already incorporated nanoscale materials into their production pipelines, and prototype all-nanotube processors have been fabricated in university laboratories.

**Paragraph E**

Environmental applications represent another rapidly expanding frontier. Nanoparticles of zero-valent iron have been injected into contaminated groundwater to break down chlorinated solvents through a chemical reaction that renders them harmless. Titanium-dioxide nanoparticles, activated by ultraviolet light, act as photocatalysts that can decompose organic pollutants in water and air, and they are already incorporated into self-cleaning glass and anti-pollution coatings on building surfaces in several cities. Nanofiltration membranes, whose pores are small enough to exclude dissolved salts and heavy metals, are finding application in water purification, offering an energy-efficient alternative to reverse osmosis for communities facing water scarcity. In agriculture, nano-encapsulated fertilisers and pesticides release their active ingredients slowly and in response to specific soil conditions, reducing the total chemical load needed to achieve equivalent crop yields.

**Paragraph F**

Despite its promise, nanotechnology raises legitimate safety concerns that regulatory agencies in many jurisdictions are still grappling with. The same properties that make nanoparticles useful — their tiny size, high reactivity, and ability to penetrate biological membranes — also make their behaviour in living organisms and ecosystems difficult to predict. Studies have shown that certain nanoparticles can cross the blood-brain barrier, accumulate in liver and kidney tissue, and trigger inflammatory responses at concentrations that would be harmless for the same material in bulk form. Silver nanoparticles, widely used as antimicrobial agents in textiles and food packaging, have been detected in wastewater effluents at concentrations that inhibit the nitrifying bacteria essential to sewage treatment. Regulatory frameworks developed for bulk chemicals do not map cleanly onto nanomaterials, and scientists have called for the development of nano-specific toxicology protocols and mandatory disclosure requirements before certain classes of nanoparticle are released into consumer products at scale.

**Paragraph G**

Looking forward, the convergence of nanotechnology with artificial intelligence and synthetic biology is expected to produce capabilities that are genuinely transformative. Self-assembling nanostructures — molecular machines that can organise themselves into complex architectures without external direction — have been demonstrated in the laboratory and may one day enable the construction of nanoscale robots capable of navigating the bloodstream, repairing damaged DNA, or assembling materials atom by atom with zero waste. While critics warn against overstating near-term prospects, the trajectory of discovery in the field suggests that the next two decades will see nanotechnology move from the laboratory bench into everyday life in ways that are difficult to anticipate and impossible to ignore.

## READING PASSAGE 2

**Tongues of Empire: Language, Power, and Colonial History**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 14–26, which are based on Reading Passage 2.*

---

**Paragraph A**

When European powers expanded their empires across Africa, Asia, and the Americas between the fifteenth and twentieth centuries, they carried their languages with them as instruments of governance, commerce, and cultural transformation. Language was never merely a neutral tool of communication; it was a mechanism of power through which colonial administrations classified populations, administered laws, extracted resources, and reshaped identities. Understanding how language functioned within colonial regimes is therefore essential to understanding colonialism itself, as well as the linguistic landscapes of formerly colonised societies that persist into the present day.

**Paragraph B**

The Spanish and Portuguese empires established a template that later colonial powers would adapt and refine. In the Americas, colonial administrators initially allowed missionaries to use indigenous languages for evangelisation, producing the first grammars and dictionaries of Nahuatl, Quechua, and dozens of other languages as by-products of religious conversion. Over time, however, the policy shifted toward forced assimilation. Spanish became the language of legal documents, land titles, and official correspondence, and communities that could not conduct their affairs in the colonial tongue found themselves systematically disadvantaged in disputes over land and labour. By the end of the colonial period, Spanish had displaced or marginalised hundreds of indigenous languages, many of which are today spoken only by elderly community members with no intergenerational transmission.

**Paragraph C**

The British Empire pursued a more pragmatic but ultimately equally transformative linguistic policy. Thomas Babington Macaulay's 1835 "Minute on Indian Education" — in which he famously dismissed all Sanskrit and Arabic scholarship as inferior to a single shelf of good European literature — set the tone for an approach that sought to create, in his words, "a class of persons, Indian in blood and colour, but English in taste, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect." English-medium schools were established across British India, British Africa, and British Southeast Asia, producing generations of administrators and clerks who conducted official business in English while their mother tongues were assigned to the private sphere of home and community. This bifurcation of linguistic life — English for public advancement, vernacular languages for domestic use — became a defining feature of colonial experience across much of the world.

**Paragraph D**

French colonial language policy operated according to a different but equally powerful logic. The doctrine of assimilation, which held that colonial subjects could in principle be elevated to the status of French citizens by adopting French language and culture, gave the French language an explicitly civilising mission. Schools in French West Africa, Indochina, and the Caribbean taught entirely in French, and the few Africans who achieved the status of "évolué" — the evolved or civilised — did so partly by demonstrating mastery of standard Parisian French. Critics of this approach, most notably the Martinican philosopher and poet Aimé Césaire, argued that assimilation represented a form of cultural annihilation, erasing African and Caribbean identities by insisting that the only road to modernity ran through Paris. Césaire's concept of "négritude," developed in the 1930s, was partly a response to and rejection of the assimilationist doctrine, asserting the value and vitality of African cultural and linguistic heritage.

**Paragraph E**

In many regions, colonial powers did not impose a single European language but instead elevated one indigenous language above others as a medium of administration, creating hierarchies within colonised societies that outlasted the colonial period. The British promotion of Swahili as a lingua franca across East Africa, for instance, facilitated colonial administration but simultaneously gave Swahili-speaking communities an advantage over those whose languages were classified as "local dialects." Similar dynamics played out in the Philippines, where Spanish and later American English were superimposed on a diverse linguistic landscape, with Tagalog being promoted to national language status in ways that disadvantaged speakers of Cebuano, Ilocano, and other regional languages.

**Paragraph F**

The consequences of colonial language policies continue to shape political and social life across the post-colonial world. In many African countries, European languages remain the official languages of government, courts, and higher education, despite being spoken at home by only a small minority of the population. This creates a situation in which access to justice, higher education, and formal employment is effectively gated behind fluency in a language that functions as a colonial inheritance. Some scholars argue that this perpetuates a form of epistemic injustice, systematically disadvantaging communities whose knowledge traditions were encoded in non-European languages. Efforts at language revitalisation — the recovery and transmission of endangered indigenous languages — have achieved notable successes in countries including New Zealand, Ireland, and Wales, but these remain exceptions to a global pattern of accelerating language loss.

**Paragraph G**

The relationship between language and power is not, however, a purely one-directional story of imposition and loss. Colonised peoples have consistently appropriated, adapted, and transformed colonial languages, producing hybrid forms that express new identities and resist assimilation into the colonial cultural framework. Creole languages — born in the crucible of slavery and the plantation economy — fused European grammatical structures with African phonological patterns and vocabulary to create fully formed languages with their own literary traditions. Postcolonial literatures in English, French, and Spanish have produced some of the most celebrated and formally innovative writing of the past century, from the novels of Chinua Achebe and Toni Morrison to the poetry of Derek Walcott and Pablo Neruda, demonstrating that former colonial languages can become vehicles for articulating experience and resisting the very power structures that imposed them.

## READING PASSAGE 3

**The Pursuit of Well-Being: What Science Tells Us About Happiness**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 27–40, which are based on Reading Passage 3.*

---

**Paragraph A**

For most of human history, happiness was considered the province of philosophers and theologians rather than scientists. It was Aristotle who introduced the concept of eudaimonia — often translated as "flourishing" or "well-being" — as the highest human good, a state characterised not merely by pleasure but by the full expression of human virtue and potential. The hedonistic alternative, which equated happiness with the experience of pleasure and the absence of pain, was associated with the philosopher Epicurus, who argued that a simple, pleasurable life among friends was the surest path to contentment. These two traditions — eudaimonic and hedonic — have persisted as rival frameworks for understanding happiness and continue to inform scientific research on the subject today.

**Paragraph B**

The emergence of positive psychology as a formal discipline in the late 1990s, championed particularly by the American psychologist Martin Seligman, marked a decisive shift toward the empirical study of what makes life worth living. Prior to this movement, psychology had focused predominantly on pathology — on diagnosing and treating mental illness — rather than on identifying the conditions that produce human flourishing. Seligman's influential PERMA model proposed that well-being consists of five measurable elements: positive emotions, engagement, relationships, meaning, and accomplishment. Each of these elements, he argued, can be cultivated through deliberate practice and is intrinsically valuable in its own right, independent of its contribution to subjective feelings of happiness.

**Paragraph C**

Neuroscientific research has shed considerable light on the biological underpinnings of happiness. Studies using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) have identified the prefrontal cortex and limbic system as central to the experience and regulation of positive emotion. Dopamine, a neurotransmitter associated with reward and motivation, plays a critical role in the anticipation of pleasurable experiences, while serotonin is more closely linked to stable mood and social status. Oxytocin, sometimes called the "bonding hormone," is released during physical touch, social interaction, and acts of generosity, producing feelings of warmth and trust. Notably, research by Richard Davidson and colleagues at the University of Wisconsin has demonstrated that sustained meditation practice can measurably increase activity in left-prefrontal regions associated with positive affect, suggesting that happiness has a degree of neuroplasticity — it can, within limits, be trained.

**Paragraph D**

One of the most counterintuitive findings in happiness research concerns the relationship between wealth and well-being. Early work by economists Richard Easterlin found that, beyond a threshold sufficient to meet basic needs, increases in national income did not translate into increases in reported life satisfaction — a result known as the Easterlin Paradox. More recent large-scale studies using different methodologies have complicated this picture. Research by psychologist Daniel Kahneman and economist Angus Deaton, based on data from over 450,000 Americans, found that emotional well-being — the day-to-day quality of emotional experience — did plateau at an income of around USD 75,000, but that evaluative well-being — life satisfaction assessed more globally — continued to increase with income beyond this point. A subsequent study by Matthew Killingsworth using continuous smartphone data found no plateau even for emotional well-being, suggesting that the relationship between money and happiness may be more linear than previously assumed, though contested.

**Paragraph E**

Social relationships emerge consistently as one of the strongest predictors of well-being across cultures and methodologies. The Harvard Study of Adult Development, one of the longest-running longitudinal studies of human life, tracked the health and well-being of hundreds of men across several decades, beginning in the 1930s. Its most robust conclusion was that the quality of close relationships — more than wealth, fame, or professional achievement — was the single strongest predictor of both health and happiness in later life. Cross-national surveys consistently corroborate this finding: societies with high levels of social trust, civic engagement, and close-knit family networks report higher average life satisfaction, even when controlling for income and other material factors. This evidence suggests that public health strategies focused on combating social isolation may yield greater well-being returns than those focused exclusively on economic growth.

**Paragraph F**

Another significant strand of research focuses on the concept of hedonic adaptation — the tendency of human beings to return relatively quickly to a stable baseline level of happiness following both positive and negative life events. Studies of lottery winners and accident victims have found that, within months to a year of the event, most respondents reported happiness levels not dramatically different from those before the event occurred. This finding, while sometimes interpreted pessimistically, carries an optimistic implication: that human beings possess a remarkable capacity for resilience in the face of adversity. It also points toward the kinds of activities most likely to produce lasting improvements in well-being — not one-time windfalls or dramatic life changes, but regular, varied, and socially embedded practices such as volunteering, physical exercise, and deliberate acts of kindness toward others.

**Paragraph G**

The science of happiness is increasingly being taken seriously by policymakers. Countries including Bhutan, New Zealand, and Scotland have begun supplementing or replacing GDP with broader measures of national well-being in their planning frameworks. Bhutan's Gross National Happiness index, first articulated in the 1970s, has influenced international thinking about the purpose of economic development and inspired the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals framework. Critics caution that subjective well-being measures are vulnerable to cultural and methodological biases, and that the concept of happiness may be too individually and culturally variable to serve as a universal policy target. Nevertheless, the growing consensus that human flourishing involves more than material prosperity represents one of the more hopeful convergences between social science and public policy in recent decades.
