# IELTS SPEAKING — TEST 17

**Topics:** Cities & Urban Life / Photography & Visual Arts / Social Media
**Band Target:** 6.5–8.0
**Duration:** 11–14 minutes total

---

## Part 1 (4–5 minutes)

*The examiner asks short questions about familiar topics. Aim for 2–4 natural sentences per answer.*

---

### Topic 1: Cities & Urban Life

**1. Do you live in a city or a smaller town or village?**

Model Answer:
"I live in a city — a fairly large one, actually. I've lived here for most of my adult life, having moved from a smaller town when I started university. I've got quite used to the pace and convenience of city life at this point, though I do sometimes miss the quietness and sense of community I had growing up."

*Key vocabulary: pace / convenience / sense of community / growing up*

---

**2. What do you like most about where you live?**

Model Answer:
"Probably the variety — there's always something to do, somewhere new to explore, and a huge range of people from different backgrounds. I particularly appreciate the food culture in my neighbourhood; there are restaurants serving cuisines from dozens of different countries within walking distance of my flat. For someone who's curious about the world, that constant exposure to different cultures feels very stimulating."

*Key vocabulary: variety / cuisines / exposure to different cultures / stimulating*

---

**3. Are there enough green spaces and parks in your city?**

Model Answer:
"Honestly, not really — it's one of the things I feel the city falls short on. There are a few parks, but they tend to be quite small and can feel overcrowded at weekends when everyone has the same idea of getting some fresh air. I think urban planners underestimate how much people need accessible greenery for their mental wellbeing, especially in dense residential areas where most people don't have private gardens."

*Key vocabulary: falls short / overcrowded / urban planners / mental wellbeing*

---

**4. Would you prefer to live somewhere else? Why?**

Model Answer:
"Sometimes I fantasise about living somewhere coastal — somewhere with clean air, easy access to nature, and a slower pace of life. I think the city offers so much, but it does take something from you in terms of constant noise, crowding, and the feeling that you always need to be productive. That said, I think I'd miss the cultural energy of a city quite quickly. So perhaps somewhere like a mid-sized coastal city would be the ideal compromise."

*Key vocabulary: fantasise / coastal / pace of life / productive / compromise*

---

### Topic 2: Photography & Visual Arts

**5. Do you enjoy taking photographs?**

Model Answer:
"Very much so — I'd say photography is one of my main hobbies. I find it a fascinating way to engage with the world because it forces you to really look at things: the quality of light, interesting compositions, moments that would otherwise slip by unnoticed. I don't consider myself a particularly technical photographer, but I find the act of looking for things worth photographing genuinely changes how I experience a place or an ordinary day."

*Key vocabulary: compositions / slip by unnoticed / technical / experience a place*

---

**6. What kinds of photos do you take most often?**

Model Answer:
"Mostly street photography and architecture, I think. I'm drawn to the way cities create accidental compositions — the geometry of buildings, the contrast of old and new facades, people caught in interesting moments as they go about their daily lives. I also take a lot of food photos, though I'm slightly embarrassed about that — it's become such a cliché. But honestly, food is aesthetically interesting, and I enjoy the challenge of making it look as good as it tastes."

*Key vocabulary: street photography / facades / cliché / aesthetically*

---

**7. Do you think photography has been changed by smartphones?**

Model Answer:
"Dramatically, yes — both for better and worse. The most significant change is democratisation: virtually everyone now walks around with a high-quality camera in their pocket, which means photography has become accessible to billions of people who would never have used a traditional camera. The result is an extraordinary volume of images being made and shared every day. The downside is that the sheer volume has arguably devalued individual images — when everyone can take a technically good photograph, the threshold for what feels remarkable shifts upward."

*Key vocabulary: democratisation / accessible / devalued / threshold*

---

**8. Is photography a form of art, in your opinion?**

Model Answer:
"I think it absolutely can be, though it isn't automatically. There's a long history of debate about whether photography is 'true' art because the camera does much of the technical work. But I think the artistic choices involved — what to frame, when to press the shutter, how to process an image — are genuinely creative decisions that reflect the photographer's vision and sensibility. Someone like Henri Cartier-Bresson or Sebastião Salgado creates images that are unmistakably artistic. The fact that a tool is involved doesn't make the result less creative."

*Key vocabulary: frame / shutter / sensibility / unmistakably artistic*

---

### Topic 3: Social Media

**9. How much time do you spend on social media each day?**

Model Answer:
"More than I'd like, if I'm honest. I'd estimate about an hour to an hour and a half most days, split across a few different platforms. I've made a few attempts to reduce it — deleting apps from my phone, setting time limits — but I always drift back. I think the challenge is that social media serves several different functions at once: it's how I keep up with news, stay in touch with friends, and follow creative work I find inspiring, so cutting it out entirely feels impractical."

*Key vocabulary: drift back / several functions at once / cutting it out entirely / impractical*

---

**10. What platforms do you use and why?**

Model Answer:
"Mainly Instagram and a couple of messaging apps. I use Instagram primarily for visual inspiration — following photographers, designers, and illustrators whose work I find interesting — rather than for sharing much of my own life. For actually communicating with people I know, I tend to use messaging apps rather than public social media. I think there's an important distinction between social media as a passive interest feed and as a channel for real personal communication."

*Key vocabulary: visual inspiration / passive interest feed / distinction / channel*

---

**11. Do you think social media has changed the way people see themselves?**

Model Answer:
"I think it has had a profound and often troubling effect on self-perception, particularly for younger people. The constant availability of highly curated images of other people's bodies, lives, and achievements creates a comparison environment that simply didn't exist at this scale before. Research consistently links heavy social media use among teenagers with lower self-esteem and higher rates of anxiety and depression. What makes it particularly insidious is that people intellectually understand that social media presents unrealistic highlights, yet the emotional impact of the comparison still occurs."

*Key vocabulary: curated / self-perception / comparison environment / insidious*

---

**12. Have you ever taken a break from social media?**

Model Answer:
"Yes, I took a deliberate break for about three weeks last year. I deleted the apps from my phone and didn't log in on my computer either. What surprised me most was how quickly the compulsion to check faded — after about three days I barely thought about it. I felt more present and less distracted, and I read significantly more than usual. The reason I eventually went back was genuinely practical — there were some professional contacts and community groups I could only reach through those platforms."

*Key vocabulary: deliberate break / compulsion / present / significantly*

## Part 2 (3–4 minutes)

**Cue Card:** "Describe an online community, group, or digital platform that you find particularly useful or interesting."

You should say:
- what it is and what it is used for
- how you discovered or started using it
- what you use it for
- and explain why you find it valuable or interesting

**Preparation time:** 1 minute

---

### Model Response (~200 words)

"I'd like to talk about a photography forum I discovered online about two years ago. It's a discussion community on Reddit — specifically a subreddit dedicated to street and documentary photography — where photographers from around the world share their work, ask for feedback, and discuss the technical and artistic aspects of the genre.

I stumbled across it after searching for advice on editing a particular type of photograph, and I found myself staying for much longer than I'd planned, reading through threads about composition, famous photographers' work, and discussions about the ethics of photographing strangers in public spaces.

What I find most valuable about it is the quality and generosity of the feedback. Unlike general social media where images get a quick 'like' and move on, this community has a culture of thoughtful written critique — people will analyse what works and what doesn't in an image, explain why, and often suggest alternative approaches. I've genuinely improved as a photographer through that feedback loop.

I also appreciate the global nature of it — seeing how photographers in different countries interpret the same genre gives me a much broader perspective than I'd get just from local sources. It's one of the places online where I genuinely feel I'm learning something."

*Key vocabulary: subreddit / documentary photography / composition / thoughtful critique / feedback loop*

---

**Follow-up question:** "Do you think online communities can provide the same sense of belonging as physical communities?"

Model Answer:
"I think they can provide a real sense of belonging, but it's qualitatively different. Online communities can connect people around very specific shared interests or identities in ways that geography makes impossible — someone passionate about a niche art form or a rare medical condition can find their people online when they'd find no one locally. But what online communities typically lack is the physical co-presence, the spontaneous interactions, and the embedded mutual support that characterises the strongest local communities. They're complementary rather than substitutes, I think."

## Part 3 (4–5 minutes)

*Abstract discussion questions on social media, inequality, and urban themes linked to the reading passages.*

---

**1. Should social media companies be held legally responsible for content posted by users?**

Model Answer:
"This is one of the most contested questions in technology regulation, and I think the answer is nuanced. A blanket immunity — as social media companies have historically enjoyed under frameworks like Section 230 in the US — seems increasingly difficult to justify when these platforms actively use algorithmic recommendation systems that amplify harmful content for engagement. They are not passive conduits but active curators. That said, holding companies liable for every individual post would be technically impossible and would likely result in dramatic over-censorship. The most defensible position, I think, is liability for algorithmic amplification of clearly harmful content, combined with meaningful transparency and audit requirements."

---

**2. How has social media changed political discourse and election campaigns?**

Model Answer:
"Social media has fundamentally altered the economics and dynamics of political communication. The positive dimension is disintermediation — politicians and movements can communicate directly with citizens without having their messages filtered through traditional media gatekeepers, which has enabled genuinely grassroots political organising. The concerning dimension is the way social media's engagement algorithms systematically reward outrage, polarisation, and emotional arousal over nuanced argument, because these generate more interaction. Research consistently shows that people on social media are exposed to more extreme content than they seek out deliberately, which appears to be contributing to increasing political polarisation in many democracies."

---

**3. Do you think social media is making economic inequality worse or better?**

Model Answer:
"On balance, I think it's making certain dimensions of inequality worse, though the picture is complex. Social media has created genuine economic opportunity for some — content creators, small businesses, and entrepreneurs who can now reach global audiences without traditional gatekeepers. But the concentration of economic value in a tiny number of platform companies, whose shareholders are predominantly already wealthy, represents an extraordinary transfer of value from users' attention and data to a small group of beneficiaries. The data generated by users collectively is the core asset of these businesses, yet users are not compensated for it. That's a structural inequality that existing regulatory frameworks haven't adequately addressed."

---

**4. Should there be a minimum age requirement for social media platforms?**

Model Answer:
"I think some form of age restriction is justified, though enforcing it effectively is genuinely difficult. The evidence of harm from early social media use — particularly for adolescent girls, among whom rates of anxiety, depression, and eating disorders have risen sharply in the smartphone era — is now substantial enough to warrant a policy response. The counter-argument is that age verification systems can be circumvented and may push young people to less regulated platforms. But 'imperfect enforcement' is not a good argument against having any standard at all; we don't abandon speed limits because some people speed. A more realistic approach might combine age verification with design requirements that reduce the most harmful features for young users."

---

**5. How important is online privacy in the modern world?**

Model Answer:
"Profoundly important, and I think systematically undervalued in public discourse because its erosion happens gradually and invisibly. The surveillance capitalism model — whereby platforms provide free services in exchange for the right to collect, analyse, and monetise extraordinarily detailed data about users' behaviour, preferences, relationships, and even emotional states — represents a transformation in the relationship between individuals and corporations that would have seemed dystopian just two decades ago. Privacy matters not only as an individual right but as a social good: a population under constant commercial surveillance behaves differently — more self-censored, more conformist — in ways that have consequences for democracy and individual autonomy."

---

**6. Do you think people share too much personal information online?**

Model Answer:
"Many people do, yes, though 'too much' is inherently subjective and the harm depends on context. What concerns me most isn't the deliberately shared information — people generally understand, at least abstractly, that they're choosing to share a holiday photo — but the passively collected data that most people are entirely unaware of: precise geolocation history, browsing behaviour, purchase patterns, the metadata attached to messages, and the inferences algorithms can draw from combinations of this data. The gap between what users think they're sharing and what's actually being collected and analysed is enormous, and closing that gap through genuine transparency regulation seems more important than trying to shame individuals for oversharing."

---

**7. Who should regulate social media — governments, companies, or users?**

Model Answer:
"This is fundamentally a question about power, and I think the answer requires all three layers operating in their appropriate domains. Governments must set the baseline legal framework — defining what content is prohibited, establishing data rights and privacy standards, and creating competition rules that prevent monopolistic concentration. Companies should be required to implement these rules and genuinely invest in safety, rather than treating moderation as a PR exercise. Users have a role through collective pressure and informed choices, but placing primary responsibility on individual users to navigate platforms designed by teams of behavioural psychologists is neither fair nor effective. Democratic governance must ultimately provide the framework."

---

**8. How might social media change over the next decade?**

Model Answer:
"Several plausible directions present themselves. Regulatory pressure is likely to increase significantly in Europe, and potentially the US, which could reshape business models in fundamental ways — potentially moving away from advertising-surveillance toward subscription models. AI-generated content is already blurring the line between human and synthetic communication, and this will intensify, creating serious challenges for trust and verification. There's also a possible decentralisation trend, with federated and open-source social platforms gaining users who want more control over their data and communities. Whether any of these trends fundamentally challenges the dominance of existing platforms, or whether network effects keep current incumbents dominant, remains genuinely uncertain."

---

**9. Is the wealth gap between rich and poor growing in your country?**

Model Answer:
"The evidence in most countries, including Vietnam, suggests it is growing, at least in terms of asset wealth even if income inequality in absolute terms shows a more complicated picture. The mechanisms are well-documented: asset price inflation — particularly in property and financial markets — has disproportionately benefited those who already own assets, while wages for many workers have not kept pace with the cost of living. In Vietnam's rapid development context, there has been broad income growth across the spectrum, which is genuinely positive, but the distribution of gains from economic growth has become increasingly skewed toward those with capital and urban property. This has significant implications for social cohesion and intergenerational mobility."

---

**10. What economic policies most effectively reduce inequality?**

Model Answer:
"The research evidence points to a combination of interventions rather than any single policy. Strong public education and healthcare systems that function as genuine equalising forces — ensuring that life outcomes are not entirely determined by the family you're born into — appear consistently across the most equal societies. Progressive taxation, including wealth and inheritance taxes as well as income taxes, redistributes from capital concentration. Robust labour protections and minimum wage standards protect workers' bargaining power. Housing policy is increasingly recognised as crucial: cities that fail to build enough housing generate enormous inequality between owners and renters. The common thread across effective approaches is sustained political commitment over decades, which is harder to achieve than identifying the right policies."

## Speaking Tips for Vietnamese Learners

**Common errors on social media and technology topics:**
- "I access the internet by my phone" → "I access the internet on / through my phone"
- "I download the Facebook" → "I use Facebook" / "I downloaded Facebook" (no article for app names)
- "The social media is..." → "Social media is..." (no article — uncountable concept)
- "I like to share the photo" → "I like to share photos" (plural, no article)

**Useful advanced vocabulary for Part 3:**
- algorithm / algorithmic / curate / curated content
- surveillance capitalism / data privacy / behavioural data
- polarisation / echo chamber / filter bubble / disinformation
- Gini coefficient / wealth gap / intergenerational mobility / asset inflation
- progressive taxation / redistribution / social safety net

**Part 3 strategy — developing abstract answers:**
1. Give your position clearly in the first sentence
2. Explain the main reason in 1–2 sentences
3. Add a nuance, qualification, or counter-consideration
4. End with a broader implication or conclusion
Target length: 6–8 sentences (approximately 80–100 words)
