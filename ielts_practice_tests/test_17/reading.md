## READING PASSAGE 1

### The Urban Oasis: Why Cities Need Green Spaces

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 1–13, which are based on Reading Passage 1.*

---

**A**
For much of human history, cities were defined by their separation from the natural world. Urban planners prioritised efficiency, commerce, and housing density, treating parks and green areas as luxuries rather than necessities. Yet over the past three decades, a growing body of scientific research has fundamentally challenged this assumption. Evidence now suggests that access to green spaces — parks, street trees, community gardens, and urban forests — is not merely aesthetically pleasant but is essential to the physical, psychological, and social well-being of urban populations.

**B**
The physical health benefits of urban green spaces are among the most rigorously documented. Studies conducted across European cities have consistently found that residents living within 300 metres of a substantial park or woodland area report lower rates of cardiovascular disease, obesity, and type-2 diabetes compared with those living in greener-deprived neighbourhoods. The mechanisms behind these findings are multiple. Green spaces encourage physical activity — walking, cycling, and informal sport — in populations that might otherwise lead sedentary lives. Additionally, trees and vegetation absorb particulate matter and other air pollutants, measurably reducing respiratory illness in nearby residents. A landmark 2019 study published in The Lancet found that children growing up near green spaces had stronger lung function than their counterparts in treeless neighbourhoods.

**C**
The mental health dimension of green space access is equally compelling. Researchers at the University of Exeter, using data from over 20,000 participants, demonstrated that people who spent at least 120 minutes per week in natural environments reported significantly better health and psychological well-being than those who spent no time in nature. Urban greenery appears to reduce cortisol — the body's primary stress hormone — and facilitate a state of 'effortless attention' that allows the prefrontal cortex to recover from the demands of directed concentration. This phenomenon, described by psychologist Rachel and Stephen Kaplan as Attention Restoration Theory, has profound implications for urban mental health at a time when anxiety and depression rates are rising steeply in cities worldwide.

**D**
Beyond individual well-being, green spaces serve critical ecological functions within the urban fabric. City trees regulate temperature through a process called evapotranspiration, in which water absorbed by roots is released through leaves as vapour, cooling the surrounding air. In cities where heat island effects can raise temperatures by up to 8°C above rural surroundings, this cooling function is not merely comfortable but potentially life-saving during heatwaves. Urban parks and wetlands also manage stormwater, absorbing rainfall that would otherwise overwhelm drainage infrastructure and contribute to flash flooding. Biodiversity, too, benefits: well-designed green corridors allow insects, birds, and small mammals to move between fragmented habitat patches, supporting populations that would otherwise decline in isolation.

**E**
The social cohesion fostered by shared green spaces is perhaps the most underappreciated of their contributions. Research in the United States found that public housing estates with trees and grass in their communal areas experienced significantly lower levels of aggression and crime than identical blocks set in concrete surroundings. Green spaces create informal meeting points where community bonds are formed across social, ethnic, and generational divides. Community gardens in particular have been recognised as powerful vehicles for social integration, bringing together residents who might otherwise never interact. In cities experiencing rapid demographic change and cultural tension, such spaces can play a genuine role in easing social friction.

**F**
Despite this evidence, green spaces remain inequitably distributed in most cities. Low-income and minority-ethnic neighbourhoods consistently receive less urban greenery than affluent areas — a pattern researchers have termed 'environmental injustice'. In London, for instance, the greenest boroughs have average household incomes more than twice those of the least green. This disparity means that the populations who stand to benefit most from green space access — those experiencing the highest levels of poverty-related stress and health disadvantage — are precisely those who have least access to it. Addressing this imbalance requires not only new park investment but changes to planning legislation that currently permit green space provision to lag in areas of high development pressure.

**G**
Some cities have begun to rethink their relationship with nature in more ambitious ways. Singapore has pursued a 'City in a Garden' strategy, weaving vegetation into the fabric of its buildings and infrastructure rather than confining it to designated park areas. Seoul has removed highways to restore urban streams. Medellín, Colombia, has planted green corridors in its hottest and most deprived districts, reducing local temperatures by several degrees while also addressing historic inequalities. These examples demonstrate that the integration of nature into cities is neither utopian fantasy nor impractical luxury. It is, rather, a prerequisite for cities that aspire to be healthy, resilient, and just.

## READING PASSAGE 2

### Capturing Light: A History of Photography

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 14–26, which are based on Reading Passage 2.*

---

**A**
Photography is often described as one of the defining inventions of the modern age, not merely because it transformed art and commerce, but because it fundamentally altered humanity's relationship with memory, truth, and time. Before photography, visual records of the world were mediated entirely by human interpretation — the painter's hand, the draughtsman's eye. The invention of a process capable of producing an image directly from light itself represented a philosophical as much as a technological revolution, collapsing the distance between experience and representation in ways that earlier generations could scarcely have imagined.

**B**
The origins of photography are rooted in two separate threads of inquiry. The first is the camera obscura — a darkened chamber in which light entering through a small aperture projects an inverted image of the external world onto a screen. Artists and scientists had used this device since at least the sixteenth century to trace accurate outlines of scenes, but the projection could not be preserved. The second thread was the discovery that certain chemical compounds, notably silver salts, darkened when exposed to light. The challenge was to combine these two phenomena: to find a way to fix the transient image created by the camera obscura using light-sensitive chemistry. This was accomplished in the 1820s by French inventor Nicéphore Niépce, whose 'heliographs' required exposure times measured in hours and produced blurred, indistinct images. Nevertheless, the principle was established.

**C**
The first commercially and artistically significant photographic process emerged from the collaboration between Niépce and the theatrical set designer Louis Daguerre. Following Niépce's death in 1833, Daguerre continued their joint research and in 1839 announced the daguerreotype: a process producing sharply detailed images on silver-coated copper plates. The French government purchased the rights to the process and made it freely available to the world, prompting what contemporaries described as a collective frenzy. Within months, daguerreotype studios had opened across Europe and North America. Portraiture, previously the preserve of the wealthy who could afford painted likenesses, became accessible to the middle classes for the first time. The social implications were immediate and profound.

**D**
The daguerreotype had one significant limitation: each image was unique and could not be reproduced. It was the British scientist William Henry Fox Talbot who solved this problem with his calotype process, patented in 1841. The calotype produced a paper negative from which multiple positive prints could be made — the negative-positive principle that would underpin photography for the next 150 years. Although the calotype's images were less sharp than daguerreotypes, the reproducibility of Talbot's process meant that photographs could be published, distributed, and accumulated. The idea of the photographic archive — as a tool of science, journalism, law enforcement, and colonial administration — became practically realisable.

**E**
The following decades saw a rapid succession of technical refinements. The wet collodion process, introduced in 1851, dramatically shortened exposure times and improved image clarity, making photography practical in outdoor and documentary contexts. By the 1860s, photographers such as Mathew Brady were documenting the American Civil War, confronting the public with images of battlefield death that had never been possible to transmit before. The photographic record transformed the way wars — and by extension, governments — were perceived by citizens. For the first time, the reality of armed conflict could not be entirely controlled by state narrators; the camera provided an alternative testimony.

**F**
The invention that truly democratised photography came in 1888, when the American entrepreneur George Eastman introduced the Kodak camera. Priced at $25 and pre-loaded with enough film for 100 exposures, the Kodak required no technical knowledge. Users simply pointed the camera, pressed a button, and returned the entire device to the factory to have the film developed and re-loaded. Eastman's marketing slogan — 'You press the button, we do the rest' — encapsulated a fundamental shift: photography moved from a professional craft to a popular leisure activity practised by millions. The vernacular photograph — the holiday snap, the family portrait, the casual record of ordinary life — was born.

**G**
The transition to digital photography in the 1990s and 2000s represented the most dramatic transformation since the invention of the medium itself. Film, chemistry, and the darkroom were supplanted by sensors, algorithms, and software. The cost of taking additional photographs fell to effectively zero, unleashing an explosion in image production that continues to accelerate. By 2023, an estimated 1.4 trillion photographs were being taken globally each year — more images in a single year than existed in the entire analogue era. The smartphone camera has made image-making a continuous, habitual behaviour for a significant portion of humanity. Whether this abundance enriches or impoverishes our relationship with the visual world remains a question that philosophers, artists, and psychologists are only beginning to address.

## READING PASSAGE 3

### The Widening Gap: Economic Inequality in the 21st Century

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 27–40, which are based on Reading Passage 3.*

---

**A**
Economic inequality is not a new phenomenon. Disparities of wealth and income have characterised human societies since the earliest civilisations, and historians have documented their cyclical rise and fall across millennia. What makes the current era distinctive is not the existence of inequality per se, but its scale, its apparent entrenchment, and the degree to which it contradicts the prosperity narrative that accompanied the globalisation boom of the late twentieth century. The promise of that era — that the rising tide of economic growth would lift all boats — has been scrutinised closely in recent decades and found, by a growing body of researchers, to be at best a partial truth and at worst a deliberate fiction.

**B**
The most influential attempt to quantify and historicise wealth inequality was undertaken by the French economist Thomas Piketty, whose 2013 work Capital in the Twenty-First Century drew on tax records and estate data spanning nearly three centuries to argue that inequality is not the temporary aberration of capitalism but its natural tendency. Piketty's central argument rests on a simple but powerful insight: when the rate of return on capital (r) consistently exceeds the rate of economic growth (g), wealth accumulates disproportionately among those who already possess it. Unless actively counteracted by redistributive taxation or economic disruption, this dynamic produces what Piketty describes as a 'patrimonial capitalism' — a society in which inherited wealth, rather than talent or effort, becomes the primary determinant of economic position.

**C**
The evidence from international data largely supports Piketty's thesis. According to the World Inequality Report, the richest one percent of the global population received 38 percent of all new wealth created between 1995 and 2021. In the United States, the share of national income going to the top 1% rose from approximately 11% in the early 1980s to over 20% by 2015. In developing economies, the picture is complex: while absolute poverty has declined significantly in countries such as China and India, relative inequality — the gap between the richest and the rest — has often widened simultaneously. The fruits of economic growth have not been shared evenly across income groups, even where growth has been impressive in aggregate terms.

**D**
Critics of inequality research raise several important objections. Some economists argue that headline inequality statistics capture a misleading picture because they focus on market income before accounting for redistributive transfers — taxes, benefits, healthcare, and education — which substantially reduce effective inequality in many wealthy nations. Others contend that global inequality, measured across all individuals regardless of nationality, has actually been falling over the past few decades as formerly impoverished nations have grown faster than rich ones. From this perspective, the appropriate unit of analysis is the world as a whole rather than any individual country, and by that measure the trajectory is broadly positive. These objections are not trivial, and serious researchers acknowledge them, but they do not, most scholars argue, fundamentally undermine the evidence of rising inequality within nations.

**E**
The consequences of sustained inequality are documented across multiple dimensions. In terms of economic efficiency, highly unequal societies allocate talent poorly: individuals from disadvantaged backgrounds are prevented by structural barriers — poor schools, inadequate nutrition, limited professional networks — from realising their potential, representing a loss of human capital that reduces overall productivity. The International Monetary Fund, not traditionally an institution associated with concern for social equity, published research in 2014 arguing that high inequality is itself a drag on economic growth and that redistribution, up to a point, actually enhances sustainable economic performance. The growth of an economically precarious middle class also constrains consumer spending in ways that undermine broader economic dynamism.

**F**
Beyond economics, inequality has demonstrable effects on political life. Research by political scientists such as Martin Gilens and Benjamin Page has shown that in the United States, the policy preferences of ordinary citizens have little measurable impact on actual legislative outcomes, whereas the preferences of economic elites are strongly correlated with policy. This suggests that economic inequality converts into political inequality, undermining the formal equality of democratic systems. High inequality has also been associated with reduced social trust, poorer public health outcomes, and higher crime rates — findings synthesised in Richard Wilkinson and Kate Pickett's landmark study The Spirit Level, which compared health and social outcomes across nations and found that more equal societies consistently outperformed unequal ones on a wide range of metrics.

**G**
Policy responses to inequality fall broadly into two camps. Predistributive approaches attempt to address inequality before it arises, through investment in universal education, early childhood programmes, worker bargaining rights, and the regulation of markets that concentrate income and wealth. Redistributive approaches work after the fact, using progressive taxation and social transfer systems to reduce the gap between market outcomes and effective living standards. Most economists argue that the most durable solutions combine elements of both, though political consensus on the appropriate mix remains elusive. The experience of Nordic countries — which have maintained relatively low inequality while achieving strong economic performance — suggests that the trade-off between equality and efficiency is far less severe than its opponents often claim, though the transferability of their institutional models to different cultural and political contexts is a matter of ongoing debate.
