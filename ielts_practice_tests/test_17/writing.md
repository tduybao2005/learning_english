# IELTS Academic Writing — Test 17

**Time allowed: 60 minutes**

---

## WRITING TASK 1

*You should spend about 20 minutes on this task.*

### Task Prompt

The table below shows the percentage of people who owned a smartphone in five countries between 2010 and 2020.

**Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.**

*Write at least 150 words.*

---

**Smartphone Ownership (%) by Country, 2010–2020**

| Country | 2010 | 2013 | 2016 | 2020 |
|---------|------|------|------|------|
| UK | 25% | 55% | 73% | 83% |
| USA | 35% | 62% | 79% | 88% |
| China | 10% | 38% | 67% | 85% |
| India | 4% | 12% | 29% | 54% |
| Nigeria | 3% | 9% | 21% | 39% |

---

### Band 7–8 Model Answer

The table illustrates changes in smartphone ownership across five countries — the UK, USA, China, India, and Nigeria — at four points between 2010 and 2020.

Overall, all five countries experienced substantial growth in smartphone ownership over the decade, though the pace and final levels differed considerably. The USA and UK consistently recorded the highest ownership rates throughout the period, while Nigeria and India remained the lowest, despite notable increases.

In 2010, the USA led all five nations with 35% ownership, closely followed by the UK at 25%. China's rate stood at just 10%, while India and Nigeria had negligible ownership at 4% and 3% respectively. By 2013, rates had risen sharply across the board — the USA reached 62% and the UK 55%, while China saw a particularly dramatic leap to 38%, nearly quadrupling its 2010 figure.

Between 2013 and 2016, China continued its rapid ascent, reaching 67% — almost level with the USA's 79% — reflecting the explosive growth of affordable smartphone manufacturing in that country. By 2020, the USA had achieved the highest rate overall at 88%, marginally ahead of China at 85% and the UK at 83%, indicating that these three nations had largely converged.

India and Nigeria, by contrast, followed a consistently lower trajectory. India grew from 4% to 54% — a remarkable thirteen-fold increase over the decade, yet still leaving more than half the population without smartphones by 2020. Nigeria's growth, while proportionally significant, reached only 39% by 2020, making it the lowest of the five countries throughout the entire period.

*(211 words)*

---

### Examiner Notes

**Task Achievement (Band 8):** The response addresses all key trends — overall pattern, individual country trajectories, and meaningful comparisons. The final data point is not simply listed but is contextualised (convergence between USA, China, UK). The 13-fold increase for India adds analytical value.

**Coherence & Cohesion (Band 8):** Clear paragraph organisation: overview → high-income countries → China's trajectory → lower-income countries. Transition language is varied ("by contrast", "reflecting", "marginally ahead of") and not repetitive.

**Lexical Resource (Band 7–8):** Strong range: *negligible, substantial, dramatic leap, explosive growth, converged, trajectory.* Avoids repetition of basic verbs ("rose", "reached", "stood at", "recorded").

**Grammatical Range & Accuracy (Band 8):** Varied structures: relative clauses, participial phrases ("reflecting the explosive growth"), comparison structures, and passive constructions. No grammatical errors.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- Do NOT begin with "This table shows..." — use a paraphrase of the prompt.
- Do NOT list every single figure without grouping them meaningfully.
- Always include an overview paragraph immediately after the introduction.
- When two countries end at similar levels (USA/China/UK), note the convergence — it shows analytical thinking.

## WRITING TASK 2

*You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.*

### Task Prompt

> **Social media companies should be held legally responsible for harmful content posted on their platforms. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.**

*Write at least 250 words. Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.*

---

### Band 7–8 Model Answer

The question of whether social media corporations should bear legal liability for the damaging content that circulates on their platforms has become one of the most contested issues in technology governance. While there are compelling arguments on both sides, I believe that a form of qualified legal responsibility is both necessary and achievable, provided it is carefully designed.

Those who oppose legal liability for social media companies argue, first, that holding platforms responsible for user-generated content is logically untenable. Just as a telephone company cannot be held liable for threats made over its network, so digital platforms are merely conduits for human communication, not its authors. Extending liability to platforms would, proponents of this view contend, create a chilling effect on free expression: companies would over-censor legitimate speech to avoid legal exposure. Furthermore, given the billions of pieces of content posted daily, genuine pre-emptive moderation at the necessary scale would be economically and technically impossible without significant errors.

On the other hand, there is a persuasive case that the analogy with telephone companies is fundamentally misleading. Unlike neutral telecoms providers, social media platforms actively curate content through algorithmic recommendation systems designed to maximise engagement. When these systems amplify inflammatory, false, or violent material because it generates clicks, the platform is no longer a passive conduit but an active participant in dissemination. Platforms have also demonstrated that when sufficiently motivated — as by advertiser pressure — they can enforce content standards with considerable speed and effectiveness, suggesting that claims of impossibility are selective rather than genuine.

My own view is that an intermediate model, sometimes called 'conditional liability', offers the most defensible path. Under such a framework, platforms would be immune from liability for content they are genuinely unaware of, but would lose that immunity if they failed to act promptly and transparently upon receiving credible complaints. This mirrors the approach already embedded in European Union legislation under the Digital Services Act, which has begun to demonstrate that accountability and free expression can coexist. The key is specificity: legal frameworks must define 'harmful content' with precision to prevent regulatory capture and political misuse, and penalties must be proportionate to platform size to avoid inadvertently protecting large incumbents from smaller competitors.

In conclusion, while absolute liability would threaten free expression and practical operability, the complete absence of accountability allows predictable and documented harms to persist. A targeted, process-based legal responsibility — centred on what platforms know and what they do in response — represents the most justifiable and workable solution to this genuinely difficult problem.

*(403 words)*

---

### Examiner Notes

**Task Achievement (Band 8):** Both views are presented with substantive, specific arguments — not generic statements. The writer's own position is clearly stated and defended with a concrete policy example (EU Digital Services Act). The response goes well beyond "there are two sides" to offer a nuanced synthesis.

**Coherence & Cohesion (Band 8):** Logical four-paragraph structure: introduction → view 1 → view 2 → own position and conclusion. Discourse markers are sophisticated: "not merely a passive conduit but an active participant", "mirrors the approach already embedded in".

**Lexical Resource (Band 8):** Sophisticated vocabulary used accurately: *untenable, chilling effect, conduit, algorithmic recommendation, dissemination, conditional liability, regulatory capture, proportionate.* No awkward collocations or forced formal language.

**Grammatical Range & Accuracy (Band 8):** Full range of complex structures: conditional sentences, nominalisations ("the extension of liability"), passive voice, relative clauses, and concessive constructions ("while..."). Near-error-free.

### Band 6 vs Band 7–8 Comparison

| Feature | Band 6 Example | Band 7–8 Example |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| Thesis | "I think social media companies should be responsible for bad content." | "A form of qualified legal responsibility is both necessary and achievable, provided it is carefully designed." |
| Argument support | "People can be hurt by harmful content online." | "When algorithmic systems amplify inflammatory material to maximise engagement, the platform is no longer a passive conduit but an active participant in dissemination." |
| Counter-argument | "However, some people think it is too difficult." | "Given the billions of pieces of content posted daily, genuine pre-emptive moderation at the necessary scale would be economically and technically impossible." |
| Vocabulary | "bad", "harmful", "social media sites", "people" | "damaging content", "legal liability", "digital platforms", "user-generated content" |
| Conclusion | "In conclusion, I believe social media companies should be more responsible." | "A targeted, process-based legal responsibility — centred on what platforms know and what they do in response — represents the most justifiable and workable solution." |
| Word count | ~260 | ~400 |

**Key upgrades from Band 6 → 7:**
1. Replace vague words ("bad", "harmful") with precise vocabulary.
2. Use a real-world example (specific legislation, named research, actual company behaviour).
3. State your position with nuance — avoid binary thinking.
4. Use concessive structures ("while X is true, Y is the stronger consideration") to show sophisticated reasoning.
5. Develop each paragraph to at least 80 words with evidence and explanation.
