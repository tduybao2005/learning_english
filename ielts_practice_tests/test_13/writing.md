## WRITING TASK 1

*You should spend about 20 minutes on this task.*

The pie chart below shows the proportion of household energy consumption by category in a European country in 2022.

*Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.*

*Write at least 150 words.*

### Task 1 Data

| Category | Percentage |
|---|---|
| Heating | 42% |
| Appliances | 23% |
| Water heating | 18% |
| Lighting | 10% |
| Cooking | 7% |

---

### Model Answer — Task 1

The pie chart illustrates how household energy consumption was distributed across five categories in a European country in 2022. Overall, thermal uses dominated total consumption, with heating alone accounting for the largest proportion, while cooking represented the smallest share.

Heating was by far the most significant category, comprising 42 per cent of total household energy use. Appliances constituted the second largest share at 23 per cent, a figure that reflects the proliferation of electrical devices in modern homes. Water heating accounted for a further 18 per cent, meaning that these three categories combined consumed over four-fifths of all household energy.

Lighting and cooking, by contrast, played a considerably smaller role. Lighting accounted for 10 per cent of consumption, while cooking represented only 7 per cent — the lowest proportion among all five categories. Notably, even though cooking is a daily necessity in every household, its energy share was roughly half that of lighting.

These figures suggest that heating and appliance use are the primary drivers of household energy demand in this European country, a finding with significant implications for the targeting of energy efficiency policy.

## WRITING TASK 2

*You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.*

*Write about the following topic:*

**Some people think that space exploration is a waste of money and that governments should spend funds on more urgent needs. To what extent do you agree or disagree?**

*Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.*

*Write at least 250 words.*

---

### Model Answer — Task 2

Space exploration has long attracted both passionate advocates and vocal critics, and the question of whether public funds should be directed toward it or toward more immediate social needs is genuinely contested. While I acknowledge the strength of the opposing view, I broadly disagree with the proposition that space exploration constitutes a waste of money.

Critics argue that the billions of dollars spent annually on agencies such as NASA, the European Space Agency, and their equivalents represent resources that could instead be directed toward healthcare, education, poverty alleviation, or climate change mitigation. In a world where hundreds of millions of people still lack access to clean water, reliable food supplies, or basic medical care, this moral objection deserves serious engagement. The opportunity cost of a single rocket launch could theoretically fund thousands of schools or hospitals in developing nations.

Nevertheless, the economic returns from space investment are substantial and are frequently underestimated by its critics. Technologies developed specifically for space missions have found widespread civilian applications: GPS navigation, weather forecasting satellites, medical imaging equipment, water purification technology, and solar panels all trace significant aspects of their development to publicly funded space research programmes. These innovations have generated enormous commercial value and improved the quality of life for billions of people worldwide.

Furthermore, space exploration addresses questions of long-term human survival that extend beyond immediate social policy. Research into asteroid detection, planetary science, and the habitability of other celestial bodies represents prudent civilisational planning. While such scenarios may seem remote, responsible governments should consider risks across timescales longer than a single electoral cycle.

In conclusion, although the critique that space funding competes with urgent earthly priorities has genuine merit, the technological returns and long-term strategic value of space exploration more than justify its costs. Rather than treating these as mutually exclusive priorities, governments should seek adequate funding for both.
