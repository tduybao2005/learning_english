# IELTS Speaking — Test 13

**Total time: 11–14 minutes**

---

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*The examiner will ask you general questions on familiar topics. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

### Topic 1: Environment

**Question 1:** Do you think people in your country are concerned about environmental issues?

**Model Answer:** I think awareness has grown considerably in recent years, particularly among younger people. Many of my classmates take recycling and energy saving seriously, and environmental topics regularly appear in the news. However, I feel that concern does not always translate into action, especially when behavioural changes are costly or inconvenient.

---

**Question 2:** What do you do personally to help protect the environment?

**Model Answer:** I try to use public transport instead of taking taxis whenever possible, and I am careful about reducing food waste at home. I also avoid single-use plastics by carrying a reusable water bottle. These are small steps, but I believe that collective individual action can make a meaningful difference over time.

---

**Question 3:** Do you think governments or individuals are more responsible for solving environmental problems?

**Model Answer:** I think both have a role to play, but governments carry greater responsibility because only they can implement systemic change. Regulations limiting carbon emissions or mandating renewable energy are far more impactful than individual choices alone. That said, public pressure from environmentally conscious citizens is often what motivates governments to act in the first place.

---

**Question 4:** Have attitudes toward the environment changed compared to previous generations?

**Model Answer:** Definitely. My parents' generation rarely discussed the environment as a daily concern, whereas my generation has grown up with climate change as an urgent, documented reality. Concepts like carbon footprints, renewable energy, and biodiversity loss are now part of everyday conversation in ways that would have been unusual thirty years ago.

---

### Topic 2: Food and Cooking

**Question 5:** Do you enjoy cooking? Why or why not?

**Model Answer:** I enjoy cooking when I have enough time, which unfortunately is not always the case. I find it satisfying to prepare a meal from scratch, particularly dishes from my home region. During busy periods, however, I tend to rely on simpler options, which I sometimes feel guilty about given how much more nutritious home-cooked food usually is.

---

**Question 6:** How has the way people eat changed in recent years?

**Model Answer:** People seem to eat out far more frequently than previous generations did, and food delivery apps have made it even easier to avoid cooking at home. There is also much greater awareness of dietary health, with many people adopting vegetarian, vegan, or low-carbohydrate diets that would have been uncommon a generation ago.

---

**Question 7:** Is it important for young people to learn to cook?

**Model Answer:** I believe it is very important. Cooking is a fundamental life skill that promotes independence, healthier eating habits, and an appreciation of food culture. Young people who cannot cook tend to rely heavily on processed or takeaway food, which is typically less nutritious and more expensive in the long run.

---

**Question 8:** Are traditional foods in your culture in danger of being forgotten?

**Model Answer:** To some extent, yes. Globalisation has brought international cuisines into every city, and younger generations sometimes prefer foreign food to traditional local dishes. However, I think there is also a growing counter-trend, with chefs and food writers working hard to document and revive traditional recipes that might otherwise be lost.

---

### Topic 3: Hobbies

**Question 9:** What hobbies do you have, and how long have you been doing them?

**Model Answer:** My main hobby is photography, which I have been practising for about three years. I particularly enjoy taking photographs of landscapes and urban architecture. I also read quite widely, especially non-fiction about history and science. Both hobbies give me a way to relax and learn something new at the same time.

---

**Question 10:** Do you think hobbies are important for mental health?

**Model Answer:** Absolutely. Having an absorbing hobby provides a genuine mental escape from the pressures of study or work, which I think is essential for psychological wellbeing. Research consistently shows that creative and physical pursuits reduce stress levels and can even improve concentration and productivity in other areas of life.

---

**Question 11:** Have your hobbies changed as you have got older?

**Model Answer:** Yes, quite significantly. When I was younger I was more interested in outdoor sports and competitive activities. As I have grown older, I have been drawn to quieter, more reflective pursuits — photography, reading, and occasionally writing. I think this reflects a broader shift in how I prefer to spend my personal time.

---

**Question 12:** Do you think people have enough free time to pursue hobbies in modern life?

**Model Answer:** In many cases, probably not. Modern working and study schedules leave many people with very little leisure time, and when they do have free time they often feel too exhausted to invest in a hobby that requires sustained effort. I think this is a genuine quality-of-life issue that societies should take more seriously.

---

## PART 2 — Individual Long Turn

*The examiner will give you a topic card. You will have 1 minute to prepare and should speak for 1–2 minutes.*

---

### Cue Card

**Describe a natural landscape or outdoor place that you find beautiful.**

You should say:
- where this place is and how you know it
- what it looks like and what natural features it has
- what you like to do when you are there
- And explain why you find this place particularly beautiful or special.

---

### Model Answer — Part 2

The place I would like to describe is a coastal headland about two hours from where I grew up. My family used to take trips there during summer when I was a child, and I have returned several times since as an adult, so I know it well across different seasons.

The landscape is dramatic and varied. The headland itself rises steeply from the sea, with cliffs of dark weathered rock that drop sharply into the water below. At the base of the cliffs, the sea breaks in wide arcs of white foam, particularly when there is a strong wind blowing in from the ocean. Behind the cliff edge there is a stretch of open grassland where wildflowers grow in spring and early summer — small purple and yellow flowers that contrast vividly with the grey stone and the deep blue of the water.

When I visit, I usually spend most of my time walking along the cliff path and sitting at various points to take photographs. The changing light — particularly in the late afternoon when the sun begins to descend — creates remarkable shadows across the rock faces and gives the sea a golden quality that I find very difficult to capture adequately with a camera, though I keep trying.

What makes this place particularly special to me is the combination of physical scale and emotional stillness it produces. Standing at the edge of the headland, with the ocean stretching to the horizon in every direction, gives me a powerful sense of perspective. Whatever concerns or pressures I have brought with me seem to diminish when confronted with something so vast and indifferent to everyday human affairs. I find that deeply refreshing, and I try to visit at least once a year if I can.

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion

*The examiner will ask you questions related to the topic from Part 2. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

**Question 1:** Do you think people today have enough contact with nature?

**Model Answer:** In many parts of the world, probably not. Rapid urbanisation means that a growing proportion of the global population lives in cities where access to green space or natural environments is limited. I think this disconnection from nature has real consequences for mental health and overall wellbeing, and it is something that urban planners and governments should address more proactively.

---

**Question 2:** Why do you think natural environments are important for human beings?

**Model Answer:** Natural environments provide both psychological and physiological benefits that are difficult to replicate in built environments. Research in environmental psychology has shown that spending time in nature reduces cortisol levels, lowers blood pressure, and improves mood and concentration. Beyond individual wellbeing, contact with nature also tends to foster a sense of environmental responsibility, which is increasingly important given the urgency of climate change.

---

**Question 3:** How has climate change affected natural landscapes in your country?

**Model Answer:** In my country, the effects are becoming increasingly visible. Coastal erosion has accelerated due to rising sea levels and more intense storm activity. Seasonal patterns have shifted noticeably, with winters becoming shorter and summers more intensely hot. Some plant and animal species that were once common are becoming rarer, while invasive species from warmer climates are beginning to establish themselves in areas where they were previously absent.

---

**Question 4:** Who do you think bears the greatest responsibility for protecting the natural environment — governments, businesses, or individuals?

**Model Answer:** I think governments and large corporations bear the greatest responsibility, simply because the scale of environmental damage caused by industrial activity far exceeds what individual behaviour can address. However, individuals play a crucial role in generating the political will for governments to act — through voting, campaigning, and making purchasing decisions that reward environmentally responsible businesses.

---

**Question 5:** Do you think economic development and environmental protection can be balanced?

**Model Answer:** Yes, I believe they can, though achieving that balance requires political courage and long-term thinking that is often in short supply. The evidence increasingly shows that many forms of sustainable development — renewable energy, circular economy models, sustainable agriculture — can be economically competitive with their carbon-intensive alternatives. The real challenge is overcoming the short-term economic interests of industries that profit from the current status quo.

---

**Question 6:** Should wealthier countries do more to help developing nations tackle environmental problems?

**Model Answer:** Absolutely, and I think there is a strong moral case for it. Wealthy industrialised nations are historically responsible for the majority of cumulative greenhouse gas emissions, yet developing nations — which have contributed far less to the problem — often suffer its most severe consequences. Financial transfers, technology sharing, and debt relief are among the mechanisms by which wealthier nations can begin to address this imbalance more fairly.

---

**Question 7:** How important is environmental education in schools?

**Model Answer:** Extremely important. The decisions that today's young people will make as adults — as voters, consumers, and potentially as policymakers — will determine whether the world's environmental trajectory improves or deteriorates. If schools provide a rigorous grounding in ecological science and sustainability, the next generation will be far better equipped to make informed choices. I think environmental education should be integrated across multiple subjects rather than confined to a single class.

---

**Question 8:** Do you think technology will be able to solve most environmental problems?

**Model Answer:** Technology will certainly be part of the solution — renewable energy, carbon capture, and precision agriculture are already making measurable progress. However, I am sceptical of the view that technology alone can solve environmental problems without changes in consumption patterns and governance. Technological solutions can be undermined if political will or public behaviour does not support their adoption, so I think innovation and systemic change must work together.

---

**Question 9:** Are international agreements on climate change effective?

**Model Answer:** They have produced some meaningful progress. The Montreal Protocol successfully addressed the ozone depletion crisis, demonstrating that global coordination is possible, and the Paris Agreement has raised ambition on emissions reduction across many countries. However, implementation is often inconsistent, and the pace of change remains far below what the science suggests is necessary. Strengthening enforcement mechanisms and making commitments legally binding would significantly improve their effectiveness.

---

**Question 10:** Do you think future generations will experience a better or worse natural environment than we have today?

**Model Answer:** I genuinely believe the answer depends on decisions made in the next ten to twenty years. If current trends continue without significant policy intervention, future generations will likely inherit a world with more extreme weather, greater biodiversity loss, and more limited access to intact natural landscapes. However, I also see reasons for cautious optimism: renewable energy adoption is accelerating, environmental awareness is growing, and many young people are deeply committed to change. The outcome is genuinely uncertain, but not yet determined.

---
