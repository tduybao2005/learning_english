# IELTS SPEAKING — TEST 04

**Topics: Food & Cooking | Family Background | Languages Spoken**

**Total test time: 11–14 minutes**

---

## PART 1 (4–5 minutes)
*The examiner will ask you questions about yourself and familiar topics.*

---

### Section A: Food & Cooking (4 questions)

**Q1. Do you enjoy cooking?**

**Model Answer:**
Yes, I do — though I would describe myself as an enthusiastic amateur rather than a skilled cook. I find cooking a very satisfying activity, particularly at the end of a long day of studying, because it involves a clear process with a tangible result. I have a small repertoire of dishes I make reasonably well — mostly Vietnamese home cooking — and I am gradually trying to expand it by attempting more complex recipes on weekends.

---

**Q2. Has the food that people in your country eat changed much in recent years?**

**Model Answer:**
Considerably, I think. When I was growing up, family meals were predominantly traditional Vietnamese food — rice, soup, grilled or braised fish and meat. Over the past decade, international cuisine has become much more accessible and popular, particularly in cities. Japanese food, Korean dishes, Western-style fast food, and various types of street food fusion have all become very common, especially among younger people. I think this reflects both greater economic prosperity and much broader exposure to other cultures through social media and travel.

---

**Q3. Do you prefer eating at home or eating out at restaurants?**

**Model Answer:**
I have a genuine preference for both in different contexts, which I realise is not a very decisive answer. At home, I appreciate being able to control what goes into the food and the relaxed environment. At a restaurant, I enjoy the experience of being served something I would not easily be able to make myself, and the social dimension of sharing a meal in a new environment. If I had to choose, I would probably say eating at home with family is more meaningful, while eating out with friends is more enjoyable in terms of atmosphere.

---

**Q4. Do you think it is important to know how to cook?**

**Model Answer:**
I think it is genuinely valuable, yes, for several reasons. Practically speaking, being able to cook gives you control over your own nutrition and budget — relying entirely on restaurants or takeaway food is considerably more expensive and makes it difficult to maintain a healthy diet. But beyond the practical arguments, I think cooking is also a cultural skill: many of the foods that define Vietnamese identity and family life require active knowledge to make well, and that knowledge risks being lost if younger generations do not learn it.

---

### Section B: Family Background (4 questions)

**Q5. Can you tell me a little about your family?**

**Model Answer:**
Of course. I come from a family of four — my parents and one younger sibling. We are originally from a relatively small city in the north of Vietnam, though my parents moved to Hanoi before I was born for work reasons. My father works in engineering and my mother is a teacher, so education has always been emphasised quite strongly in our household. We are quite a close-knit family in the sense that we have dinner together most evenings and visit extended family fairly regularly during holidays.

---

**Q6. Has your family background influenced the values you hold today?**

**Model Answer:**
Very much so, I think. The emphasis my parents placed on education and on perseverance through difficulty has shaped the way I approach my studies and, more broadly, challenges in my life. My mother, in particular, modelled a kind of patient, consistent effort — rather than relying on natural talent — that I have tried to internalise as my own working philosophy. I am also aware that my family's background has given me certain privileges and advantages that not everyone has, and I try to remain conscious of that.

---

**Q7. Are family structures in your country changing compared to previous generations?**

**Model Answer:**
Yes, quite significantly. The traditional Vietnamese family model involved multiple generations living together in a single household, with grandparents playing an active role in raising children and family decisions being made collectively. This is becoming less common, particularly in urban areas, where younger couples increasingly live independently in smaller apartments and where work schedules leave less time for extended family engagement. I think this change is partly driven by urbanisation, rising property prices, and changing expectations among younger people about personal autonomy.

---

**Q8. How important do you think family is compared to friendships?**

**Model Answer:**
I find this quite a difficult question to answer neatly, because I think the comparison is somewhat artificial — they serve different functions in a person's life and the absence of either would leave a different kind of gap. Family provides a sense of continuity, unconditional belonging, and shared history that friendships rarely replicate in the same way. Friendships, on the other hand, are chosen rather than inherited, which gives them a different kind of meaning. I would say both are essential to wellbeing, and I would be wary of a life that had one without the other.

---

### Section C: Languages Spoken (4 questions)

**Q9. Apart from your first language, do you speak or study any other languages?**

**Model Answer:**
Yes — I am currently studying English, which is clearly evident from our conversation today. I have been studying it for several years and have reached a level where I can engage comfortably with written and spoken material in most everyday and academic contexts, though I am still working on developing a more natural and fluent spoken register. I have also studied some basic Chinese in school, though my proficiency there is quite limited and I would not describe myself as a speaker in any meaningful sense.

---

**Q10. Do you think being multilingual is an advantage in today's world?**

**Model Answer:**
Absolutely — I think it is one of the most versatile advantages a person can develop. The most obvious benefit is professional: in many fields, particularly international business, diplomacy, academia, and technology, English proficiency opens doors that would otherwise remain closed. But beyond the professional dimension, knowing another language also gives you access to literature, humour, relationships, and cultural perspectives that are not fully translatable. There is a well-known idea that each language you speak represents a different way of seeing the world, and I find that a compelling one.

---

**Q11. At what age do you think it is best to start learning a foreign language?**

**Model Answer:**
The research I have come across suggests that earlier is generally better for acquiring native-like pronunciation and grammatical intuition, though older learners can make faster initial progress in vocabulary and grammar because they have more cognitive tools available. My own experience of beginning English in a fairly structured way as a child and continuing through adolescence and now as an adult gives me the impression that consistent, motivated exposure over a long period matters more than the precise starting age. I think the quality and variety of exposure — reading, speaking, listening to authentic materials — is at least as important as starting young.

---

**Q12. Is there any language other than English that you would like to learn in the future?**

**Model Answer:**
Yes — I have a genuine interest in learning Japanese. I find the language both linguistically fascinating, given its very different grammatical structure from Vietnamese and English, and practically appealing given the strong economic and cultural ties between Vietnam and Japan and the richness of Japanese literature, film, and popular culture. I am also aware that learning a third language after having already learned a second is often somewhat faster, as the learner has already developed the metacognitive tools for language learning in general.

## PART 2 (3–4 minutes)

### TASK CARD

**Describe your favourite meal or a particularly memorable dining experience.**

You should say:
- what the meal or experience was
- where it took place and who you were with
- why it was memorable or significant
- and explain what made the food or experience so enjoyable

---

### Model Answer (approx. 200 words)

The meal I want to describe took place about two years ago, at a restaurant I had not been to before — a small, fairly unassuming pho restaurant in the Old Quarter of Hanoi, where I was visiting with my family during the Tet holiday period. I have eaten pho hundreds of times in my life, as most Vietnamese people have, but this particular meal was genuinely unlike any previous version of the dish I had experienced.

What made it memorable was partly the quality of the broth — which had clearly been simmered for many hours and had a depth and clarity of flavour that most quick-service restaurants do not achieve — and partly the context. We had been walking through the city for most of the morning, it was cold in a way that Hanoi only occasionally is in January, and sitting down with my parents and sibling in a warm, narrow room packed with other families was one of those experiences where the food, the environment, and the company all combined into something that felt more meaningful than the sum of its parts.

I think the memory has stayed with me because it was such a simple experience. There was nothing elaborate or expensive about it, and yet it felt completely satisfying in a way that more ambitious meals sometimes fail to achieve.

---

**Follow-up question:** "Do you think food is an important part of Vietnamese cultural identity?"

**Model short answer:**
Very much so — I think food in Vietnam carries an unusual amount of cultural weight. Many of the most significant family and social moments are organised around shared meals, and the regional diversity of Vietnamese cuisine — the difference between northern, central, and southern cooking styles — reflects genuine historical and cultural differences between these regions. When Vietnamese people travel abroad, one of the most commonly reported experiences of homesickness is specifically a craving for Vietnamese food, which suggests it represents something more than mere sustenance.

### Theme: Food Culture, Globalisation, Food Security, and Dietary Choices

---

**Q1. How has globalisation affected food cultures around the world?**

**Model Answer:**
The effects have been complex and somewhat contradictory. On one hand, globalisation has made an extraordinary range of cuisines accessible in places where they were previously unavailable, enriching the dietary experience and cultural exposure of people in many parts of the world. On the other hand, the global spread of fast food chains and processed food products has in many regions contributed to a homogenisation of diet and a decline in traditional culinary knowledge and practice. Research consistently links increased consumption of ultra-processed foods to higher rates of obesity, diabetes, and cardiovascular disease, suggesting that the globalisation of food has significant public health consequences.

---

**Q2. Do you think traditional cuisines are at risk of disappearing?**

**Model Answer:**
I think specific traditional techniques and dishes are genuinely at risk, particularly those that require considerable skill and time to prepare — recipes that were historically made by grandmothers and passed down through direct observation are not well adapted to survival in a world where fewer people cook from scratch and where recipe transmission has largely moved online in simplified form. At the same time, I think there is a growing counter-movement: in Vietnam and elsewhere, there is increasing cultural and commercial interest in authentic traditional food, driven partly by tourism and partly by a nostalgic cultural revaluation among younger urbanites.

---

**Q3. What are the main causes of global food insecurity in your view?**

**Model Answer:**
I think the causes operate at several levels. At the most structural level, global food insecurity is not primarily a production problem — the world currently produces more than enough calories to feed its entire population — but a distribution problem, driven by inequality in purchasing power, inadequate infrastructure for storage and transport in low-income regions, political instability, and the diversion of food crops to animal feed or biofuels. Climate change is an increasingly significant additional driver, affecting the reliability of harvests in many of the regions where food insecurity is already most severe.

---

**Q4. Should wealthy countries do more to address food insecurity in poorer countries?**

**Model Answer:**
Yes, I think they have both a moral responsibility and a practical interest in doing so. The moral argument is straightforward: when preventable malnutrition causes suffering and death at a scale that is well within the collective capacity of wealthy nations to reduce, the failure to act is difficult to defend. The practical argument is that food insecurity drives migration, political instability, and conflict in ways that eventually affect the security and economy of wealthy nations too. However, I would add that the form of assistance matters enormously: aid that undermines local agricultural markets and creates dependency is arguably worse than no aid at all.

---

**Q5. How do individual dietary choices affect the environment?**

**Model Answer:**
Substantially — though the degree varies considerably depending on which aspect of diet we are discussing. The most significant dietary driver of environmental impact is meat consumption, particularly beef, which requires vastly more land, water, and energy per unit of nutrition than plant-based alternatives, and which generates significant greenhouse gas emissions through both land use change and the methane produced by cattle. Research suggests that a global shift towards plant-rich diets could reduce agricultural land use by more than 75% while still providing adequate nutrition. However, this shift involves complex trade-offs with cultural traditions, economic systems, and the livelihoods of farming communities in many parts of the world.

---

**Q6. Do you think governments should regulate what people eat for health reasons?**

**Model Answer:**
I think there is a legitimate role for government policy in shaping the food environment — through regulation of food labelling, restriction of advertising of unhealthy products to children, taxation of sugar-sweetened beverages and ultra-processed foods, and subsidisation of healthy foods — while being cautious about more directly prescriptive interventions. The evidence on taxes targeting specific unhealthy products is reasonably strong: the sugar tax introduced in the UK in 2018 was associated with meaningful reductions in sugar content in soft drinks. However, there is also a legitimate concern that such policies can be regressive — falling more heavily on lower-income households who rely more on cheaper processed foods.

---

**Q7. Is there growing interest in vegetarian or vegan diets in your country?**

**Model Answer:**
Yes, though from a relatively low base by international comparison. Vietnam has a long tradition of Buddhist vegetarian practice, so the concept of meat-free eating is culturally familiar, but the recent growth is driven more by health and environmental concerns among urban, educated younger people than by religious practice. Plant-based meat products from brands such as Beyond Meat and various local equivalents have become available in supermarkets in major cities over the past few years, suggesting there is a commercial market developing. I think the trend is real but still quite niche in terms of the broader population.

---

**Q8. How do you think food production will need to change to feed a growing global population?**

**Model Answer:**
I think it will need to change in several intersecting ways simultaneously. On the supply side, there is significant potential in precision agriculture technology — using sensors, AI-based monitoring, and targeted application of inputs to increase yields while reducing waste and environmental impact. Alternative protein sources, including cultivated meat grown from cell cultures and insect-based protein, may eventually play a significant role in reducing the land and resource demands of meeting global protein needs. On the demand side, reducing food waste — which currently accounts for approximately one third of all food produced globally — and shifting consumption patterns in wealthier countries towards lower-impact foods are probably the two changes with the greatest near-term impact.

---

**Q9. What role does food play in social and family life in Vietnam?**

**Model Answer:**
A very central one, I would say — more so than in many cultures I am aware of. Family meals in Vietnam are not simply occasions for nutrition; they are the primary daily context for family communication, the sharing of news, and the maintenance of relationships. The act of eating together carries social meaning. Festive occasions such as Tet, weddings, and death anniversaries are almost entirely organised around the preparation and sharing of specific foods, many of which have symbolic significance. I think this means that food in Vietnam functions as a primary language of care and belonging in a way that goes well beyond its material content.

---

**Q10. Do you think people today have a healthy relationship with food?**

**Model Answer:**
I think this varies enormously between individuals and contexts, and that both problematic extremes are visible in modern life. On one side, there is significant overconsumption of ultra-processed, nutritionally poor food in many populations, driven by the convenience, cost, and deliberate engineering of palatability in the food industry. On the other side, there is growing evidence of highly anxious, restrictive, or obsessive relationships with food, particularly among younger women, that cause significant psychological harm. A genuinely healthy relationship with food — one characterised by enjoyment, variety, moderation, and social connection, without excessive guilt or anxiety — seems to be something that many people find difficult to maintain in the current food environment.
