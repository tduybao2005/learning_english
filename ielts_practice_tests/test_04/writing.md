## TASK 1 (20 minutes | Minimum 150 words)

**The two maps below show the town of Riverside in 1990 and 2020. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.**

---

**MAP DATA: Riverside**

**1990:**
- North: Farmland (large area)
- Centre: Single bridge crossing the river
- Centre: Fishing village (on the riverbank)
- East bank: Fish processing factory
- South: Market square

**2020:**
- North: Residential development (replacing farmland)
- North bank: Riverside promenade (new)
- Centre, East, West: Three bridges total (two additional bridges added east and west of original)
- Centre: Expanded town centre (replacing fishing village)
- East bank: Technology park (replacing fish processing factory)
- South: New shopping centre (added south of market square; market square retained)

---

### Model Answer — Band 7.5 (approx. 175 words)

The two maps illustrate how Riverside underwent significant transformation over the three decades between 1990 and 2020, developing from a small fishing community into a more urbanised and economically diversified town.

The most dramatic change was in the northern area of town: the extensive farmland that characterised this zone in 1990 was entirely replaced by a residential development by 2020, suggesting substantial population growth over the period. On the east bank, the fish processing factory — a defining feature of Riverside's original economic identity — was demolished and replaced by a technology park, signalling a fundamental economic transition away from traditional primary industries.

River crossing capacity was expanded considerably: whereas Riverside had only a single central bridge in 1990, two additional bridges were constructed to the east and west by 2020. A riverside promenade was also developed along the northern bank, converting former agricultural land into a public leisure amenity. In the south, a new shopping centre was added to serve the expanding population, though the original market square remained intact.

Overall, the maps depict a town that grew substantially and modernised its economy while retaining some elements of its original character.

## TASK 2 (40 minutes | Minimum 250 words)

**"University education should be free for all students and paid for entirely by the government. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"**

**Write at least 250 words. Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.**

---

### Model Answer — Band 7.5 (approx. 275 words)

The question of whether university education should be fully government-funded is one that divides opinion across political, economic, and ethical lines. While I strongly support making higher education genuinely accessible to all qualified students regardless of financial background, I do not believe that making it entirely free for all students represents the most equitable or fiscally sustainable approach in all national contexts.

The most compelling argument for free university education is its potential to eliminate the financial barriers that currently prevent talented individuals from disadvantaged backgrounds from accessing higher education at all. In countries with high tuition fees — notably the United States and the United Kingdom — the burden of student debt has reached levels that many economists describe as a generational financial crisis, and evidence suggests that fee levels do deter some prospective students from lower-income households from applying. Countries such as Germany and Norway, which provide tuition-free higher education, demonstrate compellingly that the model is operationally viable when supported by sufficient public investment.

However, I would argue that entirely free university education is not necessarily the most equitable use of public funds, even from a progressive perspective. University graduates typically earn substantially more over their working lives than non-graduates: requiring graduates to contribute to the cost of their education through income-contingent loan arrangements — repaid only once earnings exceed a defined threshold — represents a fair and progressive distribution of costs. Australia's Higher Education Contribution Scheme (HECS), which operates precisely on this principle, is widely regarded as an effective and equitable model. Public funds that would otherwise be absorbed by blanket tuition subsidies for degree students could instead be directed towards early childhood education, vocational training, and community programmes with potentially higher returns for social mobility among those who do not attend university.

In conclusion, I support making university education accessible through means-tested grants and income-contingent loans rather than universal free tuition, as this approach more effectively balances individual benefit with collective fairness and fiscal responsibility.

- **Task Response:** Clear, qualified position; both perspectives acknowledged; specific countries used as evidence (Germany, Norway, Australia); conclusion answers the question directly
- **Coherence and Cohesion:** Clear three-paragraph body structure; effective use of discourse markers (however, in conclusion, even from a progressive perspective); logical sequencing
- **Lexical Resource:** Range of precise vocabulary (income-contingent, progressive, fiscal, accessible, equitable, threshold); AWL items used appropriately; no significant colloquial register
- **Grammatical Range and Accuracy:** Mix of complex and compound sentences; passive voice and conditional forms used; accurate subordinate clauses
- **To improve to Band 8:** Could develop the counterargument paragraph more; could acknowledge Vietnam's specific context more directly; could integrate a concessive clause structure more fluently
