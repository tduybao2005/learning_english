## READING PASSAGE 1

**"The Science and Philosophy of Organic Farming"**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 1–13, which are based on Reading Passage 1.*

---

**Paragraph A**

Organic farming is defined by what it prohibits as much as by what it promotes. Certified organic agricultural producers are required to abstain from the use of synthetic pesticides, synthetic chemical fertilisers, and genetically modified organisms (GMOs), instead relying on natural inputs, biological pest control, and soil management practices that work with rather than against natural ecological processes. Certification systems have been established by numerous regulatory bodies to ensure compliance and to provide assurance to consumers: the European Union operates under Regulation EC 834/2007, the United States administers the USDA Organic programme, and Vietnam has developed the VietGAP quality assurance framework for domestic producers. Globally, the organic food and beverage market reached an estimated value of $120 billion in 2020, with the majority of consumption concentrated in North America and Europe, where consumer awareness and purchasing power have most strongly supported premium-priced organic products.

**Paragraph B**

One of the most consistently documented benefits of organic farming is its positive long-term impact on soil health. Conventional intensive agriculture, which relies heavily on synthetic inputs and mechanised tillage, tends over time to reduce the diversity and abundance of soil microorganisms, compact the soil structure, and diminish the capacity of the land to support future cultivation without increasing chemical inputs. Organic farming practices — including crop rotation, cover cropping, the use of compost and animal manures, and the avoidance of synthetic inputs — build and sustain soil microbiome diversity, support earthworm populations that physically aerate and fertilise the soil, and foster mycorrhizal networks: the intricate underground fungal systems that connect plant root systems and facilitate the exchange of water and nutrients between plants and the soil. These biological networks are increasingly recognised by soil scientists as foundational to long-term agricultural productivity, and their maintenance is argued by advocates of organic farming to represent a form of investment in future food security.

**Paragraph C**

The ecological benefits of organic farming extend significantly beyond the soil. Research consistently finds that organic farms host substantially more species of plants, insects, and birds than their conventionally farmed equivalents, even when the surrounding landscape is controlled for. The absence of synthetic pesticides — in particular, the class of insecticides known as neonicotinoids, which have been linked in multiple studies to the collapse of bee populations through the impairment of navigation and reproduction — is considered a major factor in this biodiversity advantage. The significance of this finding extends beyond ecological sentiment: pollinators, particularly bees, provide essential services to global food production, with an estimated 75% of the world's food crops depending to some degree on animal pollination. The decline of pollinator populations therefore represents not merely an ecological loss but a direct threat to food security, lending additional urgency to the debate about the long-term sustainability of conventional agricultural practices.

**Paragraph D**

The question of whether organic food is nutritionally superior to conventionally produced food is one of the most contested in the scientific literature, and the evidence remains genuinely equivocal. Some studies, including a large meta-analysis published in the British Journal of Nutrition in 2014, have found that organic crops contain modestly higher concentrations of certain antioxidant compounds and lower concentrations of pesticide residues and toxic metals. However, a landmark review commissioned by the UK Food Standards Agency in 2009 examined the available evidence base and concluded that there was insufficient evidence to support confident claims that organic food provides meaningful health benefits relative to conventional food. Researchers note that the nutritional composition of both organic and conventional produce varies enormously depending on variety, growing conditions, season, and post-harvest handling, making direct comparison inherently difficult. The scientific consensus, to the extent one exists, appears to be one of "not proven" rather than "disproven."

**Paragraph E**

The economic landscape of organic farming presents a complex set of challenges and trade-offs that complicate any straightforward advocacy of wider adoption. Organic yields — the quantity of produce generated per unit area of land — are on average 19–25% lower than those achieved by equivalent conventional farms, a gap that has significant implications for the economics of individual farms and for the global food supply equation. To remain economically viable with lower output, organic producers typically charge premium prices for their products — prices that, by definition, make organic food less accessible to lower-income consumers and concentrate its consumption among more affluent households. This pricing structure raises uncomfortable questions about equity: if organic farming is genuinely better for environmental and health outcomes, the burden of its higher costs falls disproportionately on those least able to bear it, unless substantial public subsidy supports the transition. Debates about whether governments should redirect agricultural subsidies from conventional to organic production remain politically contentious in most countries.

**Paragraph F**

Despite these current economic constraints, many proponents of organic farming are cautiously optimistic about its long-term scalability. Advances in precision agriculture — using sensor technology, satellite imaging, and AI-based crop monitoring — are beginning to offer organic producers tools to manage their crops more efficiently and reduce losses from pests and disease without resorting to synthetic chemistry. Consumer demand for organic products continues to grow globally, creating market incentives for investment in organic production research and infrastructure. Some agricultural researchers argue that the current yield gap between organic and conventional farming partly reflects decades of underinvestment in organic-specific crop breeding and agronomy, and that targeted research investment could substantially narrow it. The ultimate challenge — whether organic practices could sustainably feed a global population projected to reach 10 billion by 2050 — remains genuinely open, and the honest answer depends significantly on contested assumptions about dietary patterns, food waste, and the distribution of agricultural resources.

## READING PASSAGE 2

**"The Ancient Silk Road: Commerce, Culture, and Contagion"**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 14–26, which are based on Reading Passage 2.*

---

**Paragraph A**

The term "Silk Road" is in many respects a misleading simplification. Coined by the German geographer Ferdinand von Richthofen in 1877, the term suggests a single, clearly defined route, while in reality the Silk Road was a complex and shifting network of overland and maritime trade routes connecting the civilisations of East Asia, Central Asia, South Asia, the Middle East, and the Mediterranean basin. These routes were operational, in various combinations and configurations, from approximately the 2nd century BCE until the 15th century CE — a span of over fifteen hundred years. The network also had a significant maritime dimension, known as the Maritime Silk Road, which operated through ports along the coasts of India, the Persian Gulf, the Arabian Peninsula, and East Africa, and was in some periods more commercially significant than its overland counterpart.

**Paragraph B**

Silk gave the network its modern name and was among the most valuable commodities to travel it, moving primarily from China westward to Central Asia, Persia, and eventually the Roman and Byzantine Mediterranean, where it commanded extraordinary prices among wealthy buyers. However, silk was far from the only commodity in transit. Moving eastward from the Mediterranean and the Middle East, merchants carried glassware — a technology that Chinese craftsmen could not match in quality — as well as gold, silver, and semi-precious stones. Other goods that crossed the network in various directions included spices, cotton textiles, ivory, porcelain, paper, and, eventually, the technologies of gunpowder and the magnetic compass. Silk itself also served a currency function at certain times and in certain markets, being used directly as payment for goods, services, and even military alliances.

**Paragraph C**

The Silk Road was never merely a channel for the movement of physical goods: it was one of the most consequential vectors for the transmission of ideas, religions, artistic traditions, and technologies in human history. Buddhism, originating in the Indian subcontinent, spread along Silk Road routes through Central Asia to reach China, Japan, and Korea, where it became a defining element of cultural life. Islam, emerging in the Arabian Peninsula in the 7th century CE, spread rapidly along trade networks both westward and eastward, reaching sub-Saharan Africa, Central Asia, and the maritime trading ports of Southeast Asia within a few centuries. The meeting of Greek and Buddhist artistic traditions in the region of Gandhara — in modern Pakistan and Afghanistan — produced a distinctive sculptural style that combined Hellenistic figural forms with Buddhist iconography, illustrating the depth at which cultural fusion could occur along these routes. Musical instruments, board games, culinary ingredients, and agricultural crops also moved along the Silk Road, reshaping the cultural lives of peoples far removed from the points of origin.

**Paragraph D**

The Silk Road's role in cultural transmission was not, however, exclusively benign. The network also served as a channel for the transmission of disease, most devastatingly in the case of the bubonic plague — caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis and carried by fleas on rats, which were themselves passengers in merchant caravans. In 1346, Mongol forces besieging the Genoese trading port of Caffa on the Black Sea are reported to have catapulted plague-infected corpses over the city walls — an early example of biological warfare. The disease reached Constantinople and Sicily by 1347 and spread with catastrophic speed through a European population that had no prior immunity. The resulting pandemic — known in subsequent centuries as the Black Death — is estimated to have killed between 30% and 60% of Europe's population over the following decade, one of the most devastating demographic events in recorded human history. Historians debate whether the pandemic originated in Central Asia or elsewhere, but the role of Silk Road trade routes in facilitating its westward spread is not seriously contested.

**Paragraph E**

One of the less celebrated but highly significant forms of transmission along the Silk Road was the movement of manufacturing technologies. Papermaking — invented in China and long a Chinese technological advantage — was transmitted to the Islamic world in the 8th century CE following the Battle of Talas, at which Chinese papermakers captured by Arab forces were reportedly put to work establishing paper mills at Samarkand, in modern Uzbekistan. From the Islamic world, papermaking technology reached Europe over subsequent centuries, dramatically reducing the cost of writing materials and enabling the expansion of literacy and learning. This technological transmission preceded Gutenberg's printing press by several centuries, meaning that the Islamic world had access to paper as a cheap and versatile writing medium long before European civilisation did — a fact with significant implications for the relative development of literacy and scholarship in the medieval period.

**Paragraph F**

A fundamental characteristic of Silk Road commerce that is often overlooked in popular accounts is that it functioned primarily as a relay trade system rather than as a route along which individual merchants travelled from one end to the other. In practice, very few traders completed the entire overland journey from China to the Mediterranean; goods passed through a succession of intermediary hands, each operating within a specific regional segment of the network. Among the most commercially skilled and widely travelled of these specialist intermediary merchants were the Sogdians — an Iranian-speaking people from the region around Samarkand whose merchant communities established networks across Central Asia and into China. Maritime trade was similarly handled by specialist communities, with Malay traders dominating certain ocean routes and Arab merchants others. Key hub cities — including Samarkand, Dunhuang, and the Persian port of Hormuz — served as points at which goods were exchanged between merchants from different regions, making multilingual capability and knowledge of diverse local customs essential commercial skills.

**Paragraph G**

The legacy of the Silk Road continues to generate contemporary relevance. UNESCO has designated Silk Road heritage sites across multiple countries as part of an effort to recognise and preserve the shared cultural heritage of the routes and to promote cross-cultural understanding. More contentiously, China's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), launched in 2013 by President Xi Jinping, has explicitly positioned itself as a 21st-century revival of the Silk Road concept — a vast programme of infrastructure investment, trade facilitation, and economic cooperation linking China to Central Asia, Europe, Africa, and Southeast Asia. Critics of the BRI, including numerous economists and foreign policy analysts, question whether the initiative genuinely serves the mutual economic benefit of all participating nations or primarily advances Chinese geopolitical and economic interests by creating financial dependencies among recipient countries.

## READING PASSAGE 3

**"Language Acquisition: Nature, Nurture, and the Critical Period"**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 27–40, which are based on Reading Passage 3.*

---

**Paragraph A**

The question of how human children acquire language — a process of extraordinary speed and apparent ease that typically results in mastery of a highly complex grammatical system within four to five years, without formal instruction — has been one of the most productive and contentious debates in twentieth and twenty-first century linguistics. Two broad theoretical traditions have historically dominated the debate. The nativist tradition, most influentially associated with the American linguist Noam Chomsky, holds that humans are endowed with an innate, biologically determined capacity for language that is specific to our species and that differs qualitatively from all other forms of learning. The behaviourist tradition, associated most closely with the psychologist B.F. Skinner, argued that language is acquired through the same general mechanisms of reinforcement and conditioning that govern all learned behaviour. A third approach — the social-interactionist perspective, drawing on the work of Lev Vygotsky and Jerome Bruner — emphasises the indispensable role of social interaction, caregiving relationships, and the linguistic environment in shaping language development.

**Paragraph B**

Chomsky's account of language acquisition centres on the concept of a Universal Grammar — an innate set of abstract principles and parameters that he argued all human languages share at a deep structural level, and that the child does not need to learn but rather activates and configures through exposure to the specific language of their environment. The most compelling argument in favour of this position is what Chomsky termed the "poverty of the stimulus": the observation that children routinely and spontaneously acquire complex grammatical rules — including rules for forming questions, embedding subordinate clauses, and applying grammatical transformations — that they have never been explicitly taught and that the input they receive does not contain sufficient information to explain by induction alone. A frequently cited piece of supporting evidence is the systematic nature of children's grammatical errors: when children say "goed" instead of "went" or "mouses" instead of "mice," they are not imitating adult errors (adults do not make these errors) but rather applying a logical rule — over-regularisation — that reveals the existence of an underlying grammatical system being actively constructed.

**Paragraph C**

The behaviourist account of language acquisition was most comprehensively articulated by B.F. Skinner in his 1957 book Verbal Behavior, in which he argued that language is shaped by operant conditioning: children produce vocalisations that are selectively reinforced by caregivers, and through this process of differential reinforcement, adult-like linguistic behaviour is gradually shaped. This account was subjected to a widely influential and devastating critique by Chomsky in a 1959 review, in which he argued that behaviourist principles were fundamentally inadequate to explain the productivity, creativity, and grammatical complexity of natural language. Chomsky's critique did not fully resolve all questions about language development, but it effectively ended behaviourism's dominance in linguistics and psycholinguistics, redirecting the field towards cognitive and nativist frameworks for the subsequent decades.

**Paragraph D**

One of the most influential and empirically contested concepts in language acquisition research is the critical period hypothesis, first formally articulated by the neurologist Eric Lenneberg in 1967. Lenneberg proposed that there is a biologically determined window during which language acquisition proceeds naturally, efficiently, and with native-like outcomes, and that this window closes at or around the onset of puberty, after which language learning — particularly the acquisition of native-like grammatical competence and phonological accuracy — becomes substantially more difficult. Evidence for the critical period hypothesis comes from multiple sources. Studies of feral children — individuals who grew up in extreme social isolation during childhood and were consequently denied normal linguistic input — provide some of the most striking data: the case of Genie, a girl discovered in California in 1970 at the age of thirteen, having been kept in near-total isolation and denied access to language, is particularly well-documented. Despite years of intensive language teaching and supportive care following her discovery, Genie was never able to achieve the grammatical complexity and spontaneity that characterise native-language competence. Additional evidence comes from clinical research on cochlear implants: children who receive implants early in life, within the critical period, typically achieve substantially better language outcomes than those who receive implants later.

**Paragraph E**

The acquisition of two or more languages presents its own fascinating complexities. Children who are exposed simultaneously to two languages from birth — so-called simultaneous bilinguals — acquire both with native-like proficiency and without apparent cognitive cost, treating each as a distinct linguistic system from very early in development. A behaviour frequently observed in bilingual children — and sometimes misinterpreted as evidence of confusion or delay — is code-switching: the fluid alternation between two languages within a single conversation or even within a single sentence. Research has consistently demonstrated that code-switching is not evidence of confusion but rather a sophisticated and rule-governed communicative skill that reflects high proficiency in both languages and sensitivity to social context. Beyond the acquisition process itself, there is substantial research interest in the cognitive consequences of bilingualism, including evidence that bilingual individuals show advantages in measures of executive function, attention control, and task-switching, and that lifelong bilingualism may delay the onset of certain neurodegenerative conditions including dementia.

**Paragraph F**

Specific Language Impairment (SLI) — a condition in which children fail to develop normal grammatical competence despite having typical levels of general intelligence, normal hearing, and adequate access to language input — provides important evidence for the biological substrate of language acquisition. Children with SLI show persistent and characteristic difficulty with grammatical morphology — particularly with the marking of tense and agreement — that is not explained by environmental factors, suggesting that language acquisition relies on specific cognitive and neural mechanisms rather than on general intellectual ability alone. Molecular genetics has contributed significantly to understanding SLI: the FOXP2 gene, a transcription factor involved in the development of the neural circuits underlying language and speech, has been identified as a significant contributor to some forms of language impairment when mutated, providing molecular-level evidence for the biological basis of human language capacity.

**Paragraph G**

The question of whether adults can achieve native-like proficiency in a second language — and how this compares to child language acquisition — has generated substantial debate and genuinely contradictory findings. Exceptional cases of adults achieving very high levels of second language proficiency clearly exist and are well documented. However, the balance of research suggests that adults, even highly motivated and extensively exposed individuals, characteristically retain a detectable foreign accent in a second language, even when their grammatical and lexical competence is otherwise very high. This persistent phonological non-nativeness is consistent with the critical period hypothesis's application to phonology. However, it is important to note that adult language learning is not simply a deficient version of child language acquisition: adults bring to the task a sophisticated meta-linguistic awareness, a broad conceptual knowledge base, and more developed strategic learning skills that can produce different and in some respects advantageous acquisition pathways, even if the ultimate attainment level for most learners falls short of full native-like competence.

**Paragraph H**

The research findings from language acquisition have been extensively, though not always carefully, applied to educational policy. Evidence for the critical period has been widely cited to support the introduction of foreign language instruction at younger ages in school curricula, on the grounds that younger learners will benefit from the greater plasticity of the developing language acquisition system. Similarly, research on bilingualism has been invoked to support policies aimed at maintaining heritage languages among immigrant communities, rather than focusing exclusively on acquisition of the majority language of the host society. However, researchers caution that the translation from laboratory findings to educational practice involves many additional variables, and that the quality of instruction, the richness of the linguistic environment provided, the level of engagement of parents and caregivers, and the social status attached to the languages involved may all have larger practical effects on language learning outcomes than the age of initial exposure alone.
