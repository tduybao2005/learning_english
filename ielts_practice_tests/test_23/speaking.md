# IELTS Academic Speaking — Test 23

**Time allowed: 11–14 minutes (approximately)**

---

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*The examiner will ask you general questions on familiar topics. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

### Topic 1: Social Media

**Question 1.** Do you use social media? Which platforms do you use most often?

**Model Answer:** Yes, I use social media fairly regularly. The platforms I use most are Instagram and YouTube — Instagram mainly for keeping up with friends and following accounts related to topics I'm interested in, and YouTube for educational content and entertainment. I tend to avoid platforms that feel more focused on argument or political debate, as I find them more draining than enjoyable.

---

**Question 2.** How much time do you spend on social media each day?

**Model Answer:** Probably around one to two hours on a typical day, though it can be more if I'm watching longer videos. I've become more conscious of my screen time in the past year or so, and I try to avoid picking up my phone during meals or in the hour before I go to sleep. I notice I feel less anxious on days when I use it less, which is quite telling.

---

**Question 3.** Do you think social media has changed the way people communicate?

**Model Answer:** Enormously, yes. People can now maintain contact with hundreds of acquaintances simultaneously in a way that was simply impossible before, and long-distance friendships are much easier to sustain. On the other hand, I think it has also changed the *quality* of some communication — interactions can become more performative and less genuine when there's an audience involved. It's a trade-off that I don't think society has fully come to terms with yet.

---

**Question 4.** Are there any aspects of social media you find concerning?

**Model Answer:** A few, actually. The one I think about most is the effect on younger teenagers — there's quite a lot of research suggesting that heavy social media use, particularly around images and social comparison, is linked to anxiety and low self-esteem in adolescents. I'm also concerned about the spread of misinformation, because the platforms are designed to spread engaging content quickly, and false or misleading information tends to be emotionally engaging almost by definition.

---

### Topic 2: Water and the Environment

**Question 5.** Do you think people in your country are conscious of how much water they use?

**Model Answer:** Honestly, I think awareness has grown in recent years, but it's still not as high as it probably should be. Water feels abundant in daily life — you turn on the tap and it's there — so it's easy to take it for granted. I notice that when there's a period of drought or water restrictions, people become much more conscious very quickly, but that awareness tends to fade once the immediate problem passes.

---

**Question 6.** What do you do personally to conserve water?

**Model Answer:** I try to take shorter showers and I always turn the tap off while brushing my teeth. I'm also fairly careful about only running the washing machine with a full load. These are small things individually, but I think the cumulative effect matters. I'd like to do more — I've read that dietary choices, particularly reducing meat consumption, can have a surprisingly large impact on personal water footprints, and that's something I'm thinking about more seriously.

---

**Question 7.** Do you think water scarcity will become a serious global problem in the future?

**Model Answer:** I think it already is a serious problem in many parts of the world — regions like the Middle East and parts of sub-Saharan Africa are already under extreme water stress. Whether it becomes a *global* crisis visible to people in water-rich countries depends on how quickly climate change alters precipitation patterns and how effectively we manage the resources we have. The projections I've read suggest the outlook is genuinely concerning without significant policy change.

---

**Question 8.** Do you think governments do enough to protect the environment?

**Model Answer:** Generally speaking, I don't think most governments do enough, though the picture varies considerably from country to country. The challenge is that environmental protection often requires short-term economic costs for long-term diffuse benefits, which is a difficult political sell. I'm cautiously more optimistic than I was a few years ago because renewable energy has become economically competitive with fossil fuels in many markets, which reduces the trade-off somewhat. But the pace of change still seems too slow relative to the scale of the problem.

---

### Topic 3: Daily Routines

**Question 9.** Could you describe your typical morning routine?

**Model Answer:** My mornings are fairly structured. I get up at around six-thirty, drink a glass of water before anything else, and then spend about thirty minutes reading — usually a physical book rather than my phone, which I find is a much calmer way to start the day. After that I have breakfast, get ready, and try to leave the house by eight o'clock. I've found that mornings where I stick to that routine are noticeably more productive than ones where I don't.

---

**Question 10.** Do you prefer a structured routine or a more flexible daily schedule?

**Model Answer:** I generally prefer a structured routine for the morning and working hours, and more flexibility in the evenings. The structure in the morning reduces the number of decisions I have to make early in the day, which I find genuinely helpful. But in the evenings I like to be spontaneous — meeting friends on short notice, going for a walk if the weather is good, or reading for as long or as short as I feel like. That combination seems to work well for me.

---

**Question 11.** Has your daily routine changed significantly in recent years?

**Model Answer:** Quite a lot, actually. A few years ago my routine was much more chaotic — I was going to bed late, sleeping in when I could, and fitting work and study around social activities rather than the other way around. As I've got older I've become more deliberate about my habits, particularly around sleep and exercise, and I've noticed a real difference in my energy levels and ability to concentrate. I think I underestimated for a long time how much those fundamentals affect everything else.

---

**Question 12.** Do you think having a fixed routine is important for productivity?

**Model Answer:** I think it's important for most people, though not necessarily identical for everyone. A routine reduces what psychologists sometimes call 'decision fatigue' — the mental energy spent choosing what to do next — and it creates a reliable container for focused work. That said, some people genuinely thrive with more spontaneous, fluid schedules, particularly those in creative fields where rigid structure can feel stifling. The key is probably understanding your own patterns well enough to design a routine — or non-routine — that serves your particular way of working.

## PART 2 — Long Turn

*The examiner will give you a task card. You have one minute to prepare, then speak for 1–2 minutes.*

---

### Cue Card

**Describe a time when you read or shared important information online.**

*You should say:*
- what the information was about
- where you found it
- who you shared it with or why it was important to you
- **And explain** what effect it had on you or others.

---

### Model Answer — Part 2

I'd like to talk about a time a couple of years ago when I came across an article about water stress and global freshwater scarcity that I found genuinely eye-opening and ended up sharing quite widely.

I found it on a science journalism website I follow — one that specialises in translating academic research into accessible language. The article was summarising findings from a major UN report on freshwater resources, and the core claim was something I hadn't fully appreciated before: that although water covers most of the planet's surface, only about three per cent is freshwater, and much of even that tiny fraction is locked in glaciers or deep underground aquifers. The part that really struck me was the projection that global demand for freshwater would exceed sustainable supply by forty per cent within the decade.

I shared it with a group chat of friends and also posted it on my social media. I remember feeling almost surprised by how engaged people were — it's not exactly a sensational topic, but the numbers were stark enough that several people commented saying they'd had no idea the situation was that precarious. One friend who studies environmental science told me the projections were actually somewhat conservative.

As for the effect on me personally — I think it genuinely shifted the way I think about water. Before reading that article, I knew water conservation was vaguely important, but it felt abstract. Seeing the specific statistics made it concrete and urgent in a way that has actually changed some of my daily habits. I'm more deliberate about water use now than I was before, which isn't something I can say about every piece of information I've read online.

*(~270 words)*

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion

*The examiner will ask discussion questions related to the Part 2 topic. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

**Question 1.** Do you think social media platforms have a responsibility to control the type of information shared on them?

**Model Answer:** Yes, I do, to a significant degree. Platforms are not passive pipes — they actively make editorial choices through their algorithms about which content to amplify and which to suppress. That involvement creates a corresponding responsibility. The challenge is defining where editorial responsibility ends and censorship begins, which is a genuinely difficult line to draw. But I think the current situation, where platforms disclaim responsibility while exerting enormous influence over what billions of people see, is clearly unsustainable.

---

**Question 2.** Should governments be able to regulate what people post on social media? Why or why not?

**Model Answer:** Governments already regulate speech in most contexts — defamation laws, incitement prohibitions, consumer protection rules — and I see no principled reason why those regulations should not extend to social media. The question is more about proportionality and due process than whether regulation is legitimate at all. I am concerned about governments using regulatory power to suppress legitimate political opposition or journalism, which does happen. But that risk argues for carefully designed regulation with robust appeal mechanisms, not for no regulation at all.

---

**Question 3.** How do you think social media companies could be made more accountable for the content on their platforms?

**Model Answer:** Several mechanisms seem promising. Algorithmic transparency requirements — forcing companies to disclose how their recommendation systems work and what content they amplify — would allow independent scrutiny. Liability reforms that remove the complete immunity platforms currently enjoy in some jurisdictions for third-party content would create financial incentives to moderate more carefully. And independent auditing of content moderation practices, similar to the way financial institutions are audited, would reduce the self-policing problem. None of these is a complete solution, but together they would create significantly more accountability than exists at present.

---

**Question 4.** What role do you think schools should play in teaching young people about using social media safely?

**Model Answer:** A very substantial one, in my view. Digital literacy — the ability to evaluate sources critically, recognise manipulation techniques, understand how algorithms shape what you see, and manage your own online presence responsibly — is as fundamental a skill as reading and arithmetic for anyone growing up today. Schools are well-placed to provide that foundation in a structured way that families cannot always provide, particularly in households where parents are less digitally literate than their children. I'd argue it should be a compulsory component of the curriculum from secondary school onwards, at minimum.

---

**Question 5.** Do you think older generations are more vulnerable to online misinformation than younger people?

**Model Answer:** Research does suggest that older adults are somewhat more likely to share false information on social media, which some researchers attribute to lower familiarity with the platforms' conventions and less experience with evaluating online sources. But I'd be cautious about overgeneralising — younger people are by no means immune to misinformation, particularly when it aligns with their existing beliefs or comes from sources they trust within their social networks. The determinants of susceptibility are probably more about critical thinking habits and media literacy than age per se.

---

**Question 6.** How has social media changed the way news is reported and consumed?

**Model Answer:** Dramatically. The news cycle has accelerated enormously — stories break on social media well before traditional outlets can cover them, which creates pressure for speed that often comes at the expense of accuracy and context. Citizen journalism has genuinely expanded the range of voices and events that receive coverage, which I think is largely positive. On the other hand, the business models of traditional journalism have been severely disrupted, and many local news organisations that provided accountable coverage of local government and community affairs have collapsed, leaving significant gaps in democratic oversight that social media has not filled.

---

**Question 7.** Do you think social media creates more connections between people or more divisions?

**Model Answer:** Probably both, simultaneously and in different ways. Within communities of interest — people who share a hobby, a health condition, a political cause, or a cultural identity — social media creates connections that would simply not have been possible before. But between those communities, the algorithmic tendency to sort people into distinct information environments can reinforce existing differences and reduce the exposure to genuinely differing perspectives that a shared information landscape used to provide. Whether it produces more connection or more division overall probably depends on how you weight those very different types of social interaction.

---

**Question 8.** What do you think are the long-term consequences of young people growing up with social media?

**Model Answer:** That's one of the big open questions of our time. The research suggests real risks around adolescent mental health, attention spans, and the development of face-to-face social skills, particularly given the timing effects — social media became ubiquitous during the formative years of people who are now in their teens and twenties. But it's also possible that this generation is developing new capacities for navigating online environments, building distributed communities, and curating information that older generations lack. It's probably too early to draw definitive conclusions, and the outcomes will depend partly on how platform design and regulation evolve over the next decade.

---

**Question 9.** Do you think technology companies have done enough to protect users' mental health?

**Model Answer:** Not remotely, in my assessment. The fundamental business model — maximising time spent on the platform to maximise advertising revenue — creates a structural incentive to optimise for engagement rather than user wellbeing, and these two objectives frequently conflict. Some companies have introduced features like screen time dashboards and content warnings, but these are fairly superficial interventions that don't address the underlying algorithmic dynamics. Meaningful protection would require changing the incentive structure, which is unlikely without regulatory pressure. The comparison I keep returning to is tobacco: the industry produced evidence for decades that its products were safe, and ultimately it required government intervention to force change.

---

**Question 10.** In your opinion, what would a healthier relationship between society and social media look like?

**Model Answer:** I think it would involve several things simultaneously. At the platform level, algorithms that optimise for user-defined goals rather than pure engagement time — allowing people to say, for instance, that they want to spend no more than an hour per day or avoid certain types of content. At the regulatory level, minimum age requirements with real enforcement, transparency about algorithmic amplification, and data privacy protections that give users genuine control. And at the individual and educational level, widespread digital literacy that helps people use these tools with intention rather than simply being used by them. None of that is technologically impossible — it's primarily a question of political will and incentive design.
