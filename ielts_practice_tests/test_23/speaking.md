# IELTS PRACTICE TEST 23 — SPEAKING

**Tổng thời gian:** 11–14 phút

---

## PART 1 (4–5 phút)

*Giám khảo sẽ hỏi bạn về bản thân và các chủ đề quen thuộc.*

---

### Chủ đề 1: The Environment

**Q1. Are you personally concerned about climate change?**

Yes, very much so. I follow news about rising temperatures and extreme weather quite closely, and I think the frequency of floods and heatwaves in recent years makes it impossible to ignore. It feels like something that affects everyone, not just people in distant countries.

**Q2. Do you do anything in your daily life to help protect the environment?**

I try to. I recycle, I use reusable bags instead of plastic ones, and I take public transport most days instead of driving. I know these are small steps, but I believe they add up, especially when many people make the same effort.

**Q3. Has your attitude towards the environment changed as you have grown older?**

Definitely. When I was younger, I didn't think about it much at all. As I've read more and seen the visible changes — less snow in winter, more intense storms — I've become much more aware and genuinely worried about what kind of planet we are leaving for future generations.

**Q4. Are environmental issues discussed much in your country?**

Yes, they are increasingly prominent. Climate change features regularly in news programmes and political debates. Schools are incorporating environmental topics into their curricula, and there are growing youth movements pushing for stronger government action.

---

### Chủ đề 2: Daily Habits

**Q5. Do you think small daily habits can make a real difference to the environment?**

I think they can, though their impact is limited without systemic change. If millions of people collectively reduce their energy consumption or choose plant-based meals more often, the cumulative effect is significant. The challenge is reaching that critical mass of behavioural change.

**Q6. How do you try to save energy at home?**

I make sure to turn off lights when I leave a room, I keep the thermostat at a moderate level rather than overheating the house, and I recently switched to LED bulbs throughout my home. I also unplug appliances when they are not in use, since standby power consumption can add up.

**Q7. Do you think people in your country are becoming more environmentally conscious?**

Gradually, yes. I see more solar panels on rooftops than I did five years ago, and supermarkets have cut down on single-use plastic noticeably. However, I think economic pressures often take priority, and not everyone can afford environmentally friendly products or electric vehicles.

**Q8. Would you consider buying an electric car in the future?**

Absolutely, if prices continue to fall. The main barriers right now are the upfront cost and the limited charging infrastructure in some areas. But as technology improves and the grid becomes greener, electric vehicles seem like the obvious choice.

---

### Chủ đề 3: Technology

**Q9. Do you think technology can solve the problem of climate change?**

I think technology is a crucial part of the solution, but not the whole answer. Renewable energy, battery storage, and electric transport are genuinely transformative innovations. At the same time, technology cannot substitute for changes in behaviour and policy — we need all three working together.

**Q10. Are there any technologies you use that you think are environmentally beneficial?**

My smartphone app that tracks my carbon footprint is one I find quite useful — it makes me more conscious of the impact of different choices. I also use an app to plan the most efficient public transport routes, which reduces unnecessary car journeys.

**Q11. What new technologies do you think will be most important for fighting climate change?**

I am particularly excited about advances in green hydrogen — using renewable electricity to split water into hydrogen and oxygen — which could decarbonise heavy industries like steel and shipping that are difficult to electrify. Long-duration battery storage is also vital for making solar and wind power fully reliable.

**Q12. Do you think social media helps or hinders environmental awareness?**

Both, honestly. On one hand, it spreads information quickly, connects activists globally, and has helped movements like the school climate strikes gain enormous momentum. On the other hand, it also circulates misinformation and can reduce complex issues to slogans, making nuanced debate harder.

## PART 2 (3–4 phút)

*Bạn có 1 phút để chuẩn bị, sau đó nói trong 1–2 phút.*

---

**Cue Card:**

Describe an occasion when you did something to help the environment.

You should say:
- what you did
- when and where this happened
- who you did it with
- and explain how you felt about what you did

---

**Model Answer (~200 từ):**

I would like to talk about a community tree-planting event I participated in about two years ago. It was organised by a local environmental group in the park near my neighbourhood, and it took place on a Saturday morning in early spring when the weather was perfect — cool but sunny, which made working outdoors very pleasant.

I went along with three friends who had also seen the announcement on social media. When we arrived, there were around fifty volunteers of all ages, from primary school children with their parents to retired residents. Each group was given a plot of land, some saplings, and basic tools, and we spent about three hours digging holes, placing the young trees carefully, and watering them in.

What struck me most was the sense of shared purpose. Everyone seemed genuinely motivated, and there was a lot of laughter and conversation between people who had never met before. By the end of the morning, we had planted over two hundred trees.

Emotionally, I felt a deep sense of satisfaction — something more tangible than pressing a 'donate' button or signing an online petition. Watching those small saplings go into the ground and knowing they would grow for decades made the whole effort feel meaningful and lasting. I have since returned to that park and noticed how much the trees have grown, which brings me a quiet sense of pride every time.

## PART 3 (4–5 phút)

*Câu hỏi trừu tượng và thảo luận sâu hơn về chủ đề.*

---

**Q1. Do you think individuals or governments should take greater responsibility for protecting the environment?**

Both bear responsibility, but in different proportions. Individuals can influence culture and signal demand, but they lack the power to restructure energy systems or set emissions regulations. Governments have the legislative authority and financial resources to mandate and incentivise the structural changes that are necessary at scale. So while individual effort is valuable, the heavier burden rightly falls on governments and the businesses they regulate.

**Q2. Why do some people choose not to change their behaviour even when they know about climate change?**

There are several reasons. Cost is a significant barrier — environmentally friendly choices such as organic food, electric vehicles, or solar panels often carry a price premium that lower-income households cannot afford. There is also the problem of psychological distance: climate change can feel like a future or distant threat, reducing the urgency people feel to act today. Finally, some people feel that their individual actions are insignificant relative to the scale of the problem, which can lead to a sense of hopelessness or apathy.

**Q3. How effective are international agreements such as the Paris Agreement in addressing climate change?**

They are important symbolically and provide a framework for accountability, but their effectiveness is limited by the fact that they are largely non-binding. Countries set their own targets and face no legal penalty for missing them. The Paris Agreement has, however, normalised the expectation that all nations — including developing ones — should have climate commitments, which represents genuine progress. Strengthening enforcement mechanisms and increasing financial support for developing nations to transition to clean energy would make such agreements more effective.

**Q4. Should richer countries pay more to help developing nations deal with climate change? Why?**

Yes, and this is a matter of both fairness and practicality. Wealthy nations are historically responsible for the vast majority of accumulated greenhouse gas emissions, having industrialised over many decades without restriction. Developing countries, which have contributed far less to the problem, often face the most severe consequences — including rising sea levels, droughts, and extreme weather events. It is therefore both morally just and strategically wise for richer countries to provide financial and technical assistance, since climate change ultimately has no borders.

**Q5. What do you think are the biggest obstacles to global cooperation on climate change?**

National self-interest is probably the greatest obstacle. Governments prioritise their own short-term economic interests, particularly when climate policies may increase energy costs or threaten jobs in fossil fuel industries. Geopolitical tensions between major emitters — the United States, China, and the European Union — also complicate cooperation. Additionally, there is a fundamental tension between the long-term nature of climate change and the short-term electoral cycles that shape political decision-making.

**Q6. Do you think younger generations are more environmentally conscious than older ones?**

Generally, I believe so, though the distinction is not absolute. Younger people have grown up with climate change as a defining issue of their time, and surveys consistently show they rate it as one of their top concerns. Movements such as Fridays for Future, led by young activists, reflect a genuine sense of urgency among that generation. However, it is also true that many older people are deeply committed environmentalists, and that economic circumstances shape behaviour across all age groups.

**Q7. How might climate change affect global food security in the coming decades?**

The impacts are likely to be severe and uneven. Rising temperatures will reduce crop yields in already warm regions, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia, where food insecurity is already a major challenge. Changing precipitation patterns will cause some regions to experience more frequent droughts while others face flooding. Meanwhile, ocean acidification and warming will disrupt fisheries that billions of people depend on for protein. The combination of these factors could trigger food price spikes, migration, and conflict, making climate change as much a food security issue as an environmental one.

**Q8. Can businesses genuinely be environmentally responsible, or is profit always the priority?**

Businesses can be genuinely responsible, but systemic incentives matter enormously. Where regulation creates a level playing field — for example, through carbon taxes or strict emissions standards — companies compete on sustainability rather than who can externalise the most environmental costs. There are also reputational and commercial incentives: consumers and investors increasingly favour companies with credible environmental records. However, without regulation, voluntary corporate sustainability commitments often remain superficial, driven more by marketing than by meaningful change.

**Q9. What role should education play in addressing climate change?**

A central one. If young people understand the science of climate change, its causes, consequences, and possible solutions, they are better equipped to make informed decisions as consumers, voters, and future policymakers. Education can also foster the kind of systems thinking needed to appreciate the complexity of climate solutions — recognising, for example, that individual actions are connected to broader social and economic structures. Schools that incorporate environmental literacy across all subjects, not just science, help cultivate a generation that is both knowledgeable and motivated to act.

**Q10. Do you think it is already too late to prevent serious climate change, or is there still hope?**

I believe it is too late to prevent all serious impacts — we are already experiencing them in the form of more frequent extreme weather events, rising sea levels, and ecosystem disruption. However, it is not too late to prevent the worst outcomes. The difference between 1.5°C and 3°C of global warming is enormous in terms of human suffering, biodiversity loss, and economic damage. Every fraction of a degree avoided represents lives saved and ecosystems preserved. So while pessimism is understandable, I think hope — combined with urgency — is a more useful response than despair.
