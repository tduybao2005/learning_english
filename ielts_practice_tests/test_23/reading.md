### Renewable Energy Sources

The world's energy landscape is undergoing a dramatic transformation. For most of human history, civilisations relied on burning wood, coal, oil, and natural gas to generate heat and electricity. These fossil fuels, while abundant and cheap, release enormous quantities of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases when combusted. Scientists now overwhelmingly agree that this accumulation of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere is the primary driver of contemporary climate change. In response, governments, businesses, and communities around the globe are investing heavily in renewable energy — sources of power that are naturally replenished and produce little or no direct carbon emissions.

Solar power is one of the most promising renewable technologies. Photovoltaic (PV) cells, which convert sunlight directly into electricity, have fallen in price by more than 90 per cent over the past two decades. Today, utility-scale solar farms occupy vast tracts of desert and semi-arid land, while rooftop panels bring electricity generation directly to homes and businesses. The amount of solar energy reaching the Earth's surface in one hour is sufficient to satisfy global human energy demand for an entire year, though capturing and storing that energy efficiently remains a significant engineering challenge.

Wind energy is another rapidly expanding sector. Modern wind turbines stand up to 200 metres tall and can generate electricity even in relatively light breezes. Offshore wind farms benefit from stronger, more consistent winds than those found inland, and their development has accelerated along the coastlines of Europe, North America, and increasingly Asia. Critics sometimes raise concerns about the visual impact of turbines on landscapes and the potential threat they pose to birds and bats; however, studies suggest these impacts are modest compared with those caused by conventional power plants.

Hydroelectric power — generating electricity from flowing water — has long been the world's largest source of renewable electricity. Large dams capture the energy of rivers, while run-of-river systems and small-scale micro-hydro installations serve remote communities without the need for large reservoirs. Though hydropower is reliable and well-established, new dam construction is controversial because it often involves flooding valleys, displacing communities, and disrupting aquatic ecosystems.

Geothermal energy harnesses heat stored within the Earth. In regions with volcanic activity — Iceland, New Zealand, Kenya, and parts of the western United States — steam or hot water extracted from underground reservoirs can drive turbines to produce electricity or be used directly to heat buildings. Geothermal plants operate continuously, regardless of weather conditions, giving them a significant advantage over solar and wind installations.

Finally, bioenergy — energy derived from organic materials such as agricultural waste, wood chips, and purpose-grown energy crops — plays a complex role in the renewable portfolio. While burning biomass releases CO₂, proponents argue that this carbon was recently absorbed by the plants themselves, making the process roughly carbon-neutral over time. However, critics point out that land devoted to energy crops could otherwise be used for food production or left as natural habitat.

Transitioning to a predominantly renewable energy system will require not only new generating capacity but also upgraded electricity grids, large-scale energy storage solutions, and changes in consumer behaviour. Nevertheless, the costs of renewable technologies continue to fall, and the pace of deployment is accelerating worldwide.

## PASSAGE 2

### The Effects of Ocean Acidification

**A.** The world's oceans have long served as a crucial buffer against the worst impacts of climate change. Since the Industrial Revolution, they have absorbed roughly 30 per cent of all carbon dioxide emitted by human activity — a service that has significantly slowed the pace of atmospheric warming. Yet this absorption comes at a cost. When CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it reacts with water molecules to form carbonic acid, which then breaks down and releases hydrogen ions. This process lowers the pH of ocean water — a phenomenon known as ocean acidification. Since pre-industrial times, average ocean surface pH has fallen from approximately 8.2 to 8.1, a shift that, while seemingly small, represents a roughly 26 per cent increase in hydrogen ion concentration.

**B.** The consequences for marine life are far-reaching and largely detrimental. Many sea creatures — including corals, oysters, mussels, clams, sea urchins, and certain species of plankton — build their shells and skeletons from calcium carbonate. In more acidic conditions, calcium carbonate dissolves more readily, making it harder for these organisms to form and maintain their protective structures. Laboratory experiments have demonstrated that many shell-forming species produce thinner, weaker shells under elevated CO₂ conditions, leaving them more vulnerable to predators and physical damage.

**C.** Coral reefs are perhaps the most visible casualty of ocean acidification. Home to roughly 25 per cent of all marine species despite covering less than one per cent of the ocean floor, coral reefs are among the most biodiverse ecosystems on Earth. Corals build reefs from calcium carbonate skeletons secreted by tiny animals called coral polyps. As ocean acidity rises, the rate at which corals can calcify decreases, while the rate at which existing reef structures dissolve increases. Combined with the thermal bleaching events driven by rising water temperatures, acidification poses an existential threat to coral ecosystems worldwide.

**D.** Fish and other non-calcifying marine organisms are not immune to these changes. Research has shown that elevated CO₂ levels can impair the sensory abilities of fish, making it harder for them to detect predators and locate suitable habitats. Some studies have found that larval fish exposed to acidified water show altered behaviour, such as being attracted to rather than repelled by the smell of predators — a potentially fatal shift. Additionally, the base of many marine food webs consists of calcifying plankton species; their decline could trigger cascading disruptions throughout entire ocean ecosystems.

**E.** The economic implications are substantial. Coastal and island communities that depend on coral reef tourism and fishing face declining revenues as reefs degrade. The global shellfish industry, which produces millions of tonnes of oysters, mussels, and clams annually, is already reporting production losses linked to more acidic coastal waters. Some aquaculture operations on the Pacific coast of North America have experienced dramatic failures of oyster larvae in hatcheries, a problem they have traced directly to the influx of corrosive, CO₂-enriched water from the open ocean.

**F.** Researchers are exploring several potential responses. Some scientists advocate for reducing CO₂ emissions as the only long-term solution, arguing that partial measures merely delay the inevitable. Others are investigating whether locally alkaline conditions — created by adding materials such as crushed limestone to specific reef zones — could buy time for vulnerable ecosystems. Aquaculture operators have begun monitoring seawater chemistry in real time and adjusting hatchery conditions accordingly. Yet the scientific consensus remains that meaningful action to curb CO₂ emissions is the only way to arrest ocean acidification at a global scale.

### Geoengineering: Promise or Peril?

As the global community struggles to curb greenhouse gas emissions rapidly enough to avert the most dangerous consequences of climate change, a growing number of scientists and policymakers are turning to a controversial set of ideas collectively known as geoengineering — deliberate, large-scale interventions in the Earth's natural systems designed to counteract global warming. While proponents argue that geoengineering offers a vital last resort when conventional mitigation proves insufficient, critics warn that it may introduce new and unpredictable risks, distract from the urgent task of reducing emissions, and raise profound ethical questions about who has the right to alter planetary systems that affect every person on Earth.

Geoengineering proposals generally fall into two broad categories. The first, known as solar radiation management (SRM), aims to reduce the amount of sunlight reaching the Earth's surface, thereby cooling the planet without addressing the underlying cause of warming. The second category, carbon dioxide removal (CDR), seeks to extract CO₂ directly from the atmosphere and store it permanently, thereby tackling the root cause of climate change but typically operating on much longer timescales.

Among SRM techniques, stratospheric aerosol injection (SAI) has attracted the most attention. The concept draws inspiration from large volcanic eruptions: when a major volcano such as Mount Pinatubo erupted in 1991, it ejected millions of tonnes of sulphur dioxide into the stratosphere, forming reflective particles that temporarily cooled the global climate by approximately 0.5°C over the following year. Scientists propose that aircraft could continuously inject similar reflective particles — sulphur dioxide, calcium carbonate, or other substances — into the stratosphere to achieve a sustained cooling effect. Modelling studies suggest this could be done relatively cheaply and quickly, at least compared with the costs of eliminating fossil fuel use. However, SAI would do nothing to reduce ocean acidification, since atmospheric CO₂ concentrations would continue to rise. Furthermore, a sudden halt to an SAI programme — whether due to political breakdown, funding failure, or conflict — would cause rapid "termination shock," in which temperatures would rebound sharply, with potentially catastrophic consequences for ecosystems that had adapted to artificially cooled conditions.

Marine cloud brightening (MCB) is another SRM approach under investigation. By spraying fine particles of sea salt into low-lying marine clouds, scientists hope to increase the reflectivity of those clouds, bouncing more sunlight back into space. Unlike SAI, MCB would be regional and reversible, its effects dissipating within days if the spraying were stopped. Critics, however, worry that altering cloud patterns could disrupt rainfall, affecting agriculture in distant regions.

On the CDR side, direct air capture (DAC) technology uses chemical processes to extract CO₂ directly from ambient air, compressing and storing it underground or using it in industrial processes. DAC plants currently operate in Iceland, the United States, and Canada, but at present they remain extremely expensive and energy-intensive, removing only modest quantities of CO₂ annually. Afforestation and reforestation — planting trees on a massive scale — represent a more traditional CDR approach and enjoy broad public support, though scientists caution that the land area required to make a meaningful dent in atmospheric CO₂ is immense, and that forests can release their stored carbon through drought, fire, or disease.

Enhanced ocean iron fertilisation is a CDR technique that involves adding iron compounds to iron-deficient areas of the ocean to stimulate the growth of phytoplankton, which absorbs CO₂ through photosynthesis. When the phytoplankton die, they sink, carrying carbon to the deep ocean floor. Small-scale experiments have confirmed that iron fertilisation does increase phytoplankton growth, but whether enough carbon is actually transported to the deep ocean to make a significant climate impact remains uncertain, and side effects — such as the creation of oxygen-depleted dead zones — could be severe.

Governance is perhaps the most intractable challenge surrounding geoengineering. Unlike reducing emissions — an action that each nation can pursue independently — deploying SRM would affect the entire planet, influencing rainfall patterns, monsoon systems, and agricultural yields across national borders. Any nation powerful enough to deploy these technologies could theoretically do so unilaterally, with consequences that fall disproportionately on countries that had no say in the decision. No international legal framework currently exists to regulate geoengineering research or deployment, and negotiations over such a framework have repeatedly stalled.
