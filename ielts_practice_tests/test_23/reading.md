### Global Water Scarcity

**A** Water is fundamental to all life on Earth, yet access to safe, clean freshwater is becoming one of the most pressing challenges of the twenty-first century. Of all the water on our planet — the vast majority of which fills the world's oceans — only three per cent is classified as freshwater. Of this small fraction, approximately two per cent is locked away in polar ice caps, glaciers, and deep underground aquifers that are effectively inaccessible for human use. This means that less than one per cent of Earth's total water supply is readily available for drinking, agriculture, and industrial processes. As the global population continues to grow and resource demands intensify, the gap between available freshwater and human need is widening at an unprecedented rate.

**B** The agricultural sector is by far the dominant consumer of freshwater globally, accounting for approximately 70 per cent of all freshwater withdrawals worldwide. Irrigation systems that sustain food production for billions of people draw vast quantities of water from rivers, lakes, and underground aquifers, many of which are being depleted far more rapidly than they can be replenished. Industrial processes, including manufacturing, energy production, and mining, account for a further 20 per cent of total water use, while domestic consumption — drinking, cooking, and sanitation — constitutes the remaining ten per cent. Population growth compounds this problem significantly: the United Nations projects that global demand for freshwater will exceed sustainable supply by 40 per cent before the year 2030.

**C** Beyond consumption pressures, the contamination of existing freshwater supplies represents a serious and growing threat. Agricultural runoff carrying fertilisers, pesticides, and livestock waste seeps into rivers and groundwater systems, rendering once-potable water unsafe for human use. Industrial discharge introduces heavy metals, solvents, and synthetic chemicals into waterways, while in many developing nations, inadequately treated sewage flows directly into rivers and coastal zones. Climate change adds a further dimension of complexity to the crisis: shifting precipitation patterns disrupt the predictability of rainfall in both temperate and tropical regions, while rising temperatures accelerate glacial retreat and increase evaporation rates, further reducing the volume of accessible freshwater.

**D** The concept of the water stress index provides a useful framework for understanding regional variations in water availability. A region is considered to be under water stress when its annual water withdrawals exceed 25 per cent of total renewable freshwater resources; when this threshold reaches 75 per cent, a region is classified as being under severe water stress. By this measure, the Middle East stands out as the world's most acutely water-stressed region: countries such as Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and Yemen withdraw water at rates that far exceed what their natural systems can renew, relying heavily on non-renewable fossil groundwater reserves that have accumulated over thousands of years. Sub-Saharan Africa, while possessing significant water resources in some areas, faces severe localised scarcity driven by a combination of poverty, inadequate infrastructure, and increasingly erratic rainfall.

**E** Technological innovation has provided partial answers to the challenge of water scarcity. Desalination — the process of removing salt and other dissolved minerals from seawater or brackish water to produce potable water — has been adopted at scale in water-scarce coastal nations, particularly across the Gulf states of the Middle East. The largest desalination plants, located in Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates, can produce millions of litres of freshwater per day. However, desalination remains expensive and highly energy-intensive, with significant environmental costs including the disposal of highly concentrated brine back into marine ecosystems. As a result, it is not a viable solution for landlocked or economically developing nations.

**F** Conservation technologies offer a more accessible and broadly applicable set of responses. Drip irrigation — a method that delivers water directly to the root zones of crops through networks of tubes and emitters — has been shown to reduce agricultural water use by up to 50 per cent compared with conventional flood irrigation. Rainwater harvesting systems, which collect and store rainfall from rooftops and catchment areas, are increasingly being deployed in both urban and rural settings across water-scarce regions. Greywater recycling, which treats and reuses wastewater from sinks, showers, and washing machines for non-potable purposes, can meaningfully reduce domestic water demand. Collectively, these approaches point toward a more sustainable and equitable management of the world's most vital resource.

### The Gutenberg Press and the Information Revolution

**A** Before Johannes Gutenberg's transformative invention in the mid-fifteenth century, the production of written texts in Europe was an extraordinarily labour-intensive and expensive process. Books and manuscripts were copied by hand, predominantly by scribes working in monastic scriptoria — the dedicated writing rooms of religious establishments. A single Bible might take a skilled monk more than a year to complete, and the resulting volume would command a price equivalent to a craftsman's annual wages. Literacy, accordingly, was confined almost entirely to the clergy, the nobility, and a narrow stratum of educated merchants. Access to books was so limited that most universities kept their volumes chained to reading desks to prevent loss. The intellectual life of Europe was consequently circumscribed by the sheer physical scarcity of written material.

**B** Around 1450, Gutenberg, a goldsmith from Mainz, Germany, devised a system of moveable type that would fundamentally change this situation. Drawing on his metallurgical expertise, he developed individual letter blocks cast in a durable lead alloy that could be arranged into pages of text, inked, and pressed onto paper or vellum with a modified screw press. The resulting Gutenberg Bible, completed around 1455, is estimated to have been produced in a print run of approximately 180 copies — a figure that seems modest by modern standards but represented a revolutionary multiplication of supply relative to the hand-copying tradition. The speed and consistency of mechanical printing allowed for the faithful reproduction of identical texts at a scale previously unimaginable.

**C** The technology spread with remarkable rapidity across Europe. Within twenty years of Gutenberg's first print run, printing presses had been established in Italy, France, Switzerland, and England. By the end of the fifteenth century — just half a century after the invention — historians estimate that more than 20 million books had been printed across the continent, a volume that dwarfed the entire manuscript output of the preceding millennium. The commercial potential of the new medium attracted entrepreneurs and investors, and competition between printers drove down the cost of books, making them progressively more accessible to the emerging urban middle classes.

**D** The printing press arrived at a moment of profound religious tension in Europe, and it rapidly became the decisive tool of the Protestant Reformation. When Martin Luther nailed his 95 Theses to the door of the Castle Church in Wittenberg in 1517, he initiated what might otherwise have remained a localised theological dispute. The printing press transformed it into a continent-wide movement: within weeks, printed copies of Luther's arguments had spread to every major city in the German-speaking world, and within months, they had reached audiences across Western Europe. The ability to disseminate heterodox religious ideas faster than ecclesiastical authorities could suppress them fundamentally altered the balance of power between Rome and its critics.

**E** The printing press also catalysed the Scientific Revolution by enabling the systematic sharing of experimental findings, mathematical proofs, and theoretical frameworks across national boundaries. Before print, scholars relied on hand-copied manuscripts that were prone to transcription errors and available only to those with access to particular libraries. The printed book allowed scientists such as Copernicus, Galileo, and Newton to build on one another's work with a reliability and speed that manuscript culture could not support. Standardised printed editions ensured that scholars in different countries were reading identical texts, enabling meaningful intellectual exchange for the first time.

**F** Among the less remarked but highly consequential effects of printing was its role in the standardisation of vernacular languages. Before the press, written European languages existed in countless regional dialects and orthographic variations; a document produced in one region might be scarcely intelligible to a literate reader from another part of the same country. As printers sought to maximise their markets, they favoured linguistic forms that enjoyed the widest possible readership, gradually converging on standard written forms of languages such as English, French, and German. This process accelerated the development of national linguistic identities and laid the groundwork for modern standard languages.

**G** Contemporary scholars frequently draw parallels between the Gutenberg press and the internet. Both technologies fundamentally disrupted established gatekeepers of information — the Church and the manuscript trade in the fifteenth century, traditional media corporations and governments in the twenty-first. Both enabled an explosion of information production that outpaced the capacity of existing institutions to regulate or curate it. And both generated anxieties about misinformation, social fragmentation, and the erosion of shared intellectual norms. Whether the internet's long-term social effects will prove as transformative as those of the printing press remains, as yet, an open question.

### Social Media and Adolescent Mental Health

**A** The rapid proliferation of social media platforms over the past two decades has produced one of the most significant shifts in adolescent behaviour ever recorded. Surveys conducted across multiple high-income nations consistently find that teenagers aged thirteen to seventeen spend an average of three to four hours per day on social media platforms — a figure that excludes other forms of screen-based activity such as gaming, streaming, and online schooling. In some demographic groups, particularly among urban teenagers in the United States and the United Kingdom, reported daily usage exceeds five hours. This scale of engagement, unprecedented in human adolescent development, has prompted growing concern among psychologists, public health researchers, and policymakers about its effects on the mental health of young people.

**B** Large-scale epidemiological studies have documented significant correlations between high levels of social media use and elevated rates of anxiety and clinical depression among adolescents. The relationship is particularly pronounced among adolescent girls: a landmark longitudinal study published in the journal *JAMA Psychiatry* found that girls who used social media platforms for more than three hours daily were approximately twice as likely to report symptoms of depression as those who used such platforms for fewer than two hours. Boys showed a weaker but still statistically significant association. The timing of these findings is notable: rates of adolescent anxiety and depression in many high-income countries began rising sharply around 2012, coinciding with the widespread adoption of smartphones and the migration of social media use from desktop computers to pocket devices.

**C** Researchers have proposed several mechanisms by which social media may harm mental health. The phenomenon of social comparison — the tendency to evaluate one's own life, appearance, and achievements against those of others — is significantly amplified by social media, where users are exposed to highly curated and typically idealised representations of their peers' lives. Psychologists have linked this constant exposure to upward social comparison to diminished self-esteem, body dissatisfaction, and heightened feelings of inadequacy, particularly among young women who encounter an unrelenting stream of filtered images. The algorithmic design of most major platforms, which privileges emotionally engaging content, tends to amplify extreme or aspirational content, reinforcing social comparison dynamics.

**D** Cyberbullying constitutes a second major mechanism of harm. Unlike traditional forms of bullying, which are bounded by physical space and school hours, cyberbullying can follow its victims into their homes and persist throughout the night. Research suggests that adolescents who experience cyberbullying are at significantly elevated risk of depression, self-harm, and suicidal ideation. The relative anonymity afforded by some platforms reduces the social inhibitions that might otherwise discourage aggressive behaviour, while the public and permanent nature of online posts can magnify the humiliation experienced by victims.

**E** A third pathway through which social media may impair adolescent wellbeing is the disruption of sleep. The habitual use of screens in the hours before bedtime — particularly the checking of social media notifications — has been shown to delay sleep onset and reduce total sleep duration. The blue light emitted by screens suppresses melatonin production, physiologically impeding the transition to sleep. Chronic sleep deficits in adolescents are strongly associated with impaired concentration, emotional dysregulation, increased risk of anxiety and depression, and diminished academic performance.

**F** Not all researchers, however, accept that social media is predominantly harmful to young people. Proponents of a more balanced view argue that digital platforms offer significant benefits, particularly for adolescents who feel isolated, marginalised, or underrepresented in their offline communities. Online communities provide a sense of belonging and peer support that can be especially valuable for young people navigating questions of identity, sexuality, or chronic illness. Research has also shown that positive online interactions — defined as exchanges characterised by mutual support, encouragement, and shared interest — are associated with improved mood and reduced feelings of loneliness.

**G** The question of whether social media causes mental health problems or merely correlates with them remains a subject of genuine scientific debate. Critics of the causal argument point out that existing research is largely observational; it is plausible that adolescents who are already prone to anxiety and depression are more likely to engage heavily with social media, rather than the reverse. Nonetheless, growing public concern has prompted regulatory responses in several jurisdictions. In 2023, the Australian government passed legislation imposing a minimum age of sixteen for social media platform access, making it one of the world's most restrictive regulatory frameworks for adolescent digital use. Researchers and policymakers continue to debate whether such measures represent an effective and proportionate response to a genuinely emerging public health problem.
