## TASK 1 (20 phút — tối thiểu 150 từ)

**Đề bài:**

The line graph below shows changes in CO₂ emissions (in metric tonnes per capita) for three countries — Australia, Germany, and India — between 2000 and 2020.

Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.

**[Mô tả biểu đồ — Line graph]**

| Năm | Australia | Germany | India |
|-----|-----------|---------|-------|
| 2000 | 17.5 | 10.5 | 1.1 |
| 2005 | 18.3 | 9.8 | 1.4 |
| 2010 | 17.8 | 9.2 | 1.7 |
| 2015 | 16.4 | 8.5 | 1.9 |
| 2020 | 15.2 | 7.8 | 2.1 |

---

**Model Answer (Band 7–8, ~175 từ):**

The line graph illustrates per-capita CO₂ emissions in Australia, Germany, and India over a twenty-year period from 2000 to 2020.

Overall, emissions in Australia and Germany followed a downward trend throughout the period, while India's figures rose steadily, though they remained considerably lower in absolute terms.

In 2000, Australia recorded the highest per-capita emissions at 17.5 tonnes, followed by Germany at 10.5 tonnes. India's figure was markedly lower, standing at just 1.1 tonnes. By 2005, Australia had reached its peak of 18.3 tonnes before declining consistently, ending the period at 15.2 tonnes in 2020. Germany demonstrated a more gradual but equally sustained fall, dropping from 10.5 to 7.8 tonnes over the same interval.

In contrast, India's emissions climbed continuously from 1.1 to 2.1 tonnes per capita — nearly doubling across the two decades — reflecting the country's rapid industrial expansion. Despite this increase, India's 2020 figure remained far below those of both Australia and Germany.

In summary, all three countries showed notable shifts in their emission profiles, with the two developed nations reducing output while India's grew, highlighting divergent development paths.

## TASK 2 (40 phút — tối thiểu 250 từ)

**Đề bài:**

Some people argue that individuals can do little to tackle climate change and that it is governments and businesses that must act. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

---

**Model Answer (Band 7–8, ~280 từ):**

The question of who bears primary responsibility for addressing climate change is one of the most debated issues of our era. While I acknowledge that individual efforts play a role, I largely agree that substantive, systemic change requires decisive action from governments and corporations rather than relying predominantly on personal choices.

The scale of climate change demands solutions that individuals simply cannot deliver on their own. Even if every person in a country reduced their carbon footprint dramatically by cycling to work, eating less meat, and switching to renewable energy at home, these efforts would be dwarfed by the emissions generated by a handful of fossil fuel companies, heavy industries, and international aviation. Research has shown that just one hundred corporations are responsible for more than 70 per cent of global emissions. Expecting individual consumers to offset the decisions of such powerful entities through lifestyle changes alone is neither realistic nor equitable.

Governments possess the legislative and financial tools needed to drive change at scale. They can introduce carbon taxes to make polluting activities more expensive, set binding emissions targets for industries, invest in renewable energy infrastructure, and fund public transport systems that reduce private car use. Similarly, businesses — particularly those in energy, manufacturing, and agriculture — have the capacity to redesign entire supply chains and production processes in ways that individuals cannot influence directly.

That said, individual action remains valuable. Consumer choices send signals to the market, and civic engagement pressures governments to adopt bolder policies. Voting for environmentally committed parties and supporting green businesses can amplify systemic change.

In conclusion, while individual responsibility should not be dismissed, governments and businesses must lead the response to climate change through bold policy and structural transformation. Personal action alone is insufficient to meet the scale of the challenge.
