## WRITING TASK 1

*You should spend about 20 minutes on this task.*

The two pie charts below show the distribution of the world's water supply and the breakdown of global freshwater withdrawals by sector.

**Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.**

*Write at least 150 words.*

---

### Chart Data

**Chart 1 — Earth's Total Water Supply**

| Category | Percentage |
|---|---|
| Saltwater (oceans) | 97% |
| Freshwater (total) | 3% |

**Chart 2 — Global Freshwater Withdrawals by Sector**

| Sector | Percentage |
|---|---|
| Agriculture | 70% |
| Industry | 20% |
| Domestic use | 10% |

---

### Model Answer — Task 1 (Band 7–8)

The two pie charts illustrate the composition of the world's total water supply and the distribution of global freshwater withdrawals across three sectors.

Overall, the data reveal a striking scarcity of accessible freshwater and a pronounced dominance of the agricultural sector in its consumption.

As the first chart shows, the vast majority of water on Earth — 97% — is saltwater held in the oceans, leaving only 3% as freshwater. This already limited supply is further reduced by the fact that much of it is locked in glaciers and polar ice caps, rendering it inaccessible for most practical purposes.

Turning to the second chart, agriculture accounts for by far the largest share of freshwater withdrawals at 70%, dwarfing the contributions of both industry and domestic use. Industry is responsible for a considerably smaller proportion at 20%, while domestic use represents the smallest category at just 10%.

Taken together, the charts highlight a critical imbalance: a resource that is extraordinarily scarce globally is consumed predominantly by a single sector, underscoring the urgency of more efficient water management in agriculture.

*(174 words)*

---

### Examiner Notes

- **Task Achievement:** Both charts are accurately described with an effective overview identifying the key contrasts — the 97/3 saltwater–freshwater split and agriculture's dominant 70% share of withdrawals. Key figures are cited precisely and comparisons are clearly drawn.
- **Coherence & Cohesion:** The response moves logically from an overview to chart-by-chart analysis and a brief synthesis. Cohesive devices ('Overall', 'As the first chart shows', 'Turning to the second chart', 'Taken together') guide the reader effectively without feeling mechanical.
- **Lexical Resource:** A range of precise vocabulary is deployed: 'composition', 'pronounced dominance', 'dwarfing', 'inaccessible', 'underscoring', 'imbalance'. Synonyms such as 'distribution'/'breakdown' and 'withdrawals'/'consumption' avoid repetition naturally.
- **Grammatical Range & Accuracy:** A variety of structures is used — passive voice ('is held', 'is consumed'), participial phrases ('rendering it inaccessible'), and comparative constructions ('by far the largest share', 'considerably smaller'). Accuracy is maintained throughout.

## WRITING TASK 2

*You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.*

*Write about the following topic:*

> **Social media has had a largely negative effect on society. To what extent do you agree or disagree?**

*Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.*

*Write at least 250 words.*

---

### Model Answer — Task 2

The rapid proliferation of social media platforms over the past two decades has prompted widespread debate about their impact on society. While I acknowledge that social media carries real risks, particularly for young people's wellbeing, I disagree that its effects are *largely* negative. The picture is more nuanced: the harm depends heavily on how platforms are designed and how they are used.

The case for negative effects is well-supported by evidence. Research has linked heavy social media use among adolescents — especially girls — to rising rates of anxiety, depression, and poor self-image, driven by social comparison and exposure to idealised content. Cyberbullying, which platforms can amplify on a massive scale, poses a genuine threat to vulnerable individuals. More broadly, the algorithmic prioritisation of outrage and sensationalism has contributed to the spread of misinformation, political polarisation, and the erosion of shared civic discourse in many societies.

However, social media also provides considerable social benefits that should not be dismissed. For isolated individuals — those living with disabilities, belonging to minority communities, or residing in remote areas — online networks can provide vital human connection and a sense of belonging that might otherwise be unavailable. Social movements, from climate activism to campaigns for civil rights, have been organised and amplified through these platforms in ways that were simply not possible before. For small businesses and independent creators, social media has democratised access to audiences that were previously the exclusive preserve of large corporations and media institutions.

The critical issue, in my view, is platform design and governance rather than social media itself. Algorithms optimised purely for engagement time inevitably promote harmful content; regulatory intervention and design reforms — such as restricting recommendation algorithms for minors — could preserve the genuine benefits while mitigating the harms. Social media, like most powerful technologies, is a tool whose effects are shaped by the choices of those who build and regulate it.

In conclusion, while the harms of social media are real and warrant serious attention, characterising its overall societal effect as *largely* negative fails to account for the meaningful connections, opportunities, and civic benefits it has enabled. A more accurate verdict is that it is a transformative force whose net impact depends on policy, design, and individual practice.

---

### Examiner Notes

- **Task Response:** The essay takes a clear, well-qualified position — disagreeing with the statement while genuinely acknowledging the evidence for harm. Both sides are developed with concrete examples (adolescent mental health, cyberbullying, misinformation vs. connectivity, social movements, small businesses). The pivot to platform design adds analytical depth beyond a simple agree/disagree.
- **Coherence & Cohesion:** Five well-structured paragraphs flow logically: introduction with position, body 1 (harms), body 2 (benefits), body 3 (key mediating factor), conclusion. Discourse markers ('However', 'The critical issue', 'In conclusion') and referencing ('these platforms', 'its effects') ensure smooth cohesion throughout.
- **Lexical Resource:** Precise and varied vocabulary: 'proliferation', 'algorithmic prioritisation', 'civic discourse', 'polarisation', 'mitigating', 'democratised', 'exclusive preserve', 'transformative force'. Collocations are natural and accurately deployed.
- **Grammatical Range & Accuracy:** Complex structures are handled confidently — passive voice, conditional clauses ('could preserve...while mitigating'), relative clauses, and nominalisations. Italics are used for rhetorical emphasis (*largely*, *largely*). Errors are absent or negligible.
