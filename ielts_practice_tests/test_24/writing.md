## WRITING TASK 1

*You should spend about 20 minutes on this task.*

The diagram below shows the four stages of the monarch butterfly's lifecycle.

**Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.**

*Write at least 150 words.*

---

### Lifecycle Stages

| Stage | Name | Description | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Egg | Laid individually on milkweed leaves; tiny, ribbed, pale yellow | 3–5 days |
| 2 | Larva (Caterpillar) | Hatches and feeds voraciously on milkweed; grows through 5 instars; distinctive black, yellow, and white stripes | 10–14 days |
| 3 | Pupa (Chrysalis) | Caterpillar forms a bright green chrysalis with gold spots; complete metamorphosis occurs inside | 8–15 days |
| 4 | Adult Butterfly | Emerges with orange and black wings; feeds on nectar; migrates up to 4,500 km to overwintering sites in Mexico | 2–6 weeks (summer) or up to 8 months (migratory generation) |

---

### Model Answer — Task 1

The diagram illustrates the four sequential stages that make up the lifecycle of the monarch butterfly, a cyclical process that begins with the egg and culminates in the adult butterfly, which subsequently reproduces to restart the cycle.

The process is initiated when a female monarch lays tiny, pale yellow eggs individually on the leaves of milkweed plants. Each egg hatches within three to five days. In the second stage, the larva — commonly known as the caterpillar — emerges and feeds voraciously on milkweed foliage, passing through five developmental phases called instars and developing its characteristic black, yellow, and white striped pattern. This larval stage lasts between ten and fourteen days, making it the longest of the first three stages.

Subsequently, the caterpillar transforms into a pupa, or chrysalis. A bright green casing with distinctive gold spots encases the insect while complete metamorphosis takes place internally, a process that spans approximately eight to fifteen days. Once this transformation is complete, the adult butterfly finally emerges, bearing the species' iconic orange and black wings. Following this, the adult feeds on flower nectar and, depending on the generation, either lives for two to six weeks during summer or, in the case of the migratory generation, survives for up to eight months — the longest adult lifespan — to undertake the journey of up to 4,500 kilometres to overwintering sites in Mexico.

## WRITING TASK 2

*You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.*

*Write about the following topic:*

Some economists argue that reducing economic inequality is essential for social stability. Others believe that a degree of inequality is necessary to motivate people to work hard and innovate.

**Discuss both views and give your own opinion.**

*Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.*

*Write at least 250 words.*

---

### Model Answer — Task 2

The question of how much economic inequality a modern society should tolerate has long been a subject of vigorous debate among economists and policymakers. While one school of thought holds that significant disparity is a prerequisite for entrepreneurial drive and innovation, a compelling counter-argument insists that inequality beyond a certain threshold undermines the social foundations that sustain productive economies.

Proponents of inequality as a motivational force argue that differential rewards are essential in market systems. If individuals who invest longer in education, work harder, or take greater entrepreneurial risks are not materially better rewarded than those who do not, the incentive to acquire skills, invest capital, or launch new ventures is significantly diminished. Silicon Valley is frequently cited as evidence that steep income gradients can drive transformative innovation, with the prospect of substantial financial reward attracting risk-takers and fostering a culture of productivity.

Nevertheless, the case for actively reducing inequality is supported by a growing body of empirical research. Studies consistently show that societies with wider income gaps tend to produce worse outcomes across a range of social indicators — including life expectancy, educational attainment, and social mobility — compared with more equal ones. Political instability is another documented consequence: when citizens perceive the economic system as fundamentally unjust, trust in democratic institutions erodes and political polarisation intensifies, as evidenced by the populist movements that swept through numerous countries during the 2010s.

In my view, while a moderate degree of inequality is both inevitable and arguably beneficial in stimulating effort and innovation, the levels observed in many contemporary economies far exceed any productive threshold. The priority for governments should be to guarantee genuine equality of opportunity — through quality education, accessible healthcare, and fair labour markets — rather than accepting extreme disparities of outcome as economically necessary.
