## TASK 1

### Đề bài

*The bar chart below shows the levels of job satisfaction among workers in five different professions in 2023. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.*

**[Bar chart — % of workers reporting high job satisfaction in 2023]**

| Profession | High Satisfaction (%) |
|---|---|
| Teaching | 72 |
| Healthcare | 68 |
| Engineering | 55 |
| Finance | 41 |
| Retail | 34 |

*Viết ít nhất 150 từ.*

---

### Bài mẫu (Band 7–8, ~175 từ)

The bar chart illustrates the percentage of workers who reported high levels of job satisfaction across five professions in 2023.

Overall, satisfaction levels varied considerably between professions, with teaching recording the highest figure and retail the lowest. White-collar professions associated with public service or technical expertise tended to score more highly than commercial roles.

Teaching had the greatest proportion of satisfied workers, at 72%, followed closely by healthcare at 68%. These two professions stood notably apart from the remaining three, with a gap of over ten percentage points between healthcare and the next-ranked profession.

Engineering occupied the middle position with 55% of workers expressing high satisfaction, suggesting a moderately positive outlook among technical professionals. By contrast, finance and retail reported considerably lower levels, at 41% and 34% respectively. Retail workers were the least satisfied overall, with fewer than two in five workers rating their job satisfaction as high — less than half the proportion seen in teaching.

In summary, the data suggests a clear divide between professions with strong social purpose and those in more commercially driven sectors, with the former demonstrating markedly higher levels of workforce satisfaction.

## TASK 2

### Đề bài

*"Some psychologists believe that the key to a happy life is to focus on what we have rather than what we want. Do you agree or disagree?"*

*Viết ít nhất 250 từ. Trình bày quan điểm của bạn và giải thích lý do.*

---

### Bài mẫu (Band 7–8, ~280 từ)

The idea that contentment comes from appreciating what we already possess, rather than perpetually chasing what we lack, has a long history in both philosophical tradition and modern psychology. I largely agree with this view, though I believe a complete picture of happiness requires a degree of balance between gratitude and aspiration.

A growing body of psychological research supports the notion that gratitude and present-focused thinking are strongly linked to well-being. Studies in positive psychology consistently show that individuals who practise gratitude — consciously acknowledging what they are thankful for — report higher levels of life satisfaction, stronger social relationships, and lower rates of depression. Moreover, the phenomenon known as hedonic adaptation reveals that acquiring new possessions or achieving desired goals rarely produces lasting happiness; people tend to return quickly to their baseline emotional state regardless of what they gain. This suggests that endlessly pursuing the next goal is, in many cases, a futile strategy for happiness.

However, completely abandoning desire and ambition carries its own risks. The PERMA model developed by Martin Seligman identifies Accomplishment as one of five pillars of well-being, indicating that goal-directed behaviour and a sense of progress contribute meaningfully to a fulfilling life. A person who never strives for anything beyond their present circumstances may experience stagnation, boredom, or a lack of purpose. Moderate ambition, pursued with self-awareness and without anxiety, can be a healthy and motivating force.

In conclusion, while the evidence strongly supports the psychological benefits of gratitude and present-moment awareness, I believe the healthiest approach combines appreciation for what one has with mindful, balanced aspiration for growth. The key lies not in eliminating wants, but in ensuring that unmet desires do not eclipse recognition of existing blessings.
