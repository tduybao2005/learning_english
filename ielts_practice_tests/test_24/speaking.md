# IELTS Academic Speaking — Test 24

**Time allowed: 11–14 minutes (approximately)**

***

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*The examiner will ask you general questions on familiar topics. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

### Topic 1: Animals and Nature

**Question 1.** Do you enjoy spending time in natural environments such as parks, forests, or the countryside?

**Model Answer:** Yes, I find it genuinely restorative. I live in a fairly urban area, so when I do get the chance to spend time outdoors — whether it's a local park or somewhere more remote — I notice an immediate difference in how I feel. There's something about being around trees, open sky, and birdsong that makes it easier to think clearly and switch off from everyday pressures. I try to make time for it at least once a week if I can manage it.

---

**Question 2.** Are you interested in wildlife and animals?

**Model Answer:** Quite interested, yes. I wouldn't call myself an expert, but I do follow news about wildlife conservation and I find animal behaviour genuinely fascinating. I recently read about the monarch butterfly's migration — the fact that it travels thousands of kilometres to specific forests in Mexico, guided in part by the position of the sun — and I found that almost unbelievable. The more you learn about what animals are capable of, the more impressive the natural world becomes.

---

**Question 3.** Do you think people in your country spend enough time in nature?

**Model Answer:** Honestly, probably not. People's lives have become increasingly centred around screens and urban environments, and I think the time many people spend outdoors has been shrinking steadily. It's a concern not just for wellbeing but also because people who have little contact with nature tend to feel less personally invested in protecting it. If you've never seen a migratory bird or a healthy river ecosystem, it's harder to care deeply when those things are threatened.

---

**Question 4.** Have attitudes towards the natural environment changed in recent years in your country?

**Model Answer:** Yes, I think there has been a genuine shift, particularly among younger generations. Environmental issues — climate change, deforestation, biodiversity loss — have moved from the margins of political debate to become central concerns. More people seem to be changing their consumer habits, choosing products with lower environmental footprints, and supporting conservation organisations. That said, I think the change in attitudes hasn't always translated into a matching change in government policy or corporate behaviour, which is frustrating.

---

### Topic 2: Money and Finance

**Question 5.** How important is money to you personally?

**Model Answer:** I think about it mainly as a means to an end rather than something I value for its own sake. Having enough money to cover my needs and avoid financial stress matters enormously — financial insecurity can affect your health, your relationships, and your ability to make long-term decisions. Beyond a certain level of security, though, I don't think accumulating more wealth brings proportionally more happiness. Research seems to support that idea fairly strongly.

---

**Question 6.** Do you think it is important to teach children about money and financial management?

**Model Answer:** Absolutely, and I think it's something many education systems do poorly. Basic financial literacy — understanding how to budget, save, avoid high-interest debt, and invest — should be taught explicitly at school rather than being left to chance. People who grow up without that knowledge are far more vulnerable to financial difficulties in adulthood. The fact that financial literacy levels vary so strongly along socioeconomic lines also feeds into broader patterns of inequality.

---

**Question 7.** Do you prefer to save money or spend it?

**Model Answer:** My natural tendency is to save, which I think comes partly from growing up in a household where money was tight at times. I'd rather have a financial cushion and forgo some immediate pleasures than spend freely and feel anxious about the future. That said, I recognise there's a balance — being excessively frugal to the point of not enjoying life doesn't make much sense either. I try to be intentional rather than impulsive about spending.

---

**Question 8.** Do you think people today are more focused on making money than people in the past?

**Model Answer:** It's an interesting question. I think the pressure to earn more has intensified in many places because the cost of living — particularly housing in cities — has risen faster than wages over recent decades. So people may appear more money-focused, but perhaps what's really changed is that economic security now requires more active effort to maintain. At the same time, social media has amplified comparisons with others, which can make people feel dissatisfied with what they have and more driven to earn more.

---

### Topic 3: Ambitions and Goals

**Question 9.** Do you have a clear idea of what you want to achieve in life?

**Model Answer:** I have a general sense of direction rather than a rigid plan. I want to do work that I find meaningful and that contributes something useful, maintain close relationships with people I care about, and have enough financial security to live without constant worry. I've found that overly specific life plans tend to become outdated fairly quickly as circumstances change, so I try to stay flexible about the specific path while keeping the broader values in mind.

---

**Question 10.** Do you think ambition is always a positive quality?

**Model Answer:** Not always, no. Ambition in the sense of being motivated to improve, achieve, and contribute is clearly valuable — it drives innovation and personal development. But ambition that is purely self-serving and indifferent to others can cause real harm. Some of the greatest damage to institutions and communities has been done by highly ambitious individuals who prioritised their own advancement above ethical considerations. The most admirable version of ambition seems to me to be one that's directed towards something larger than personal success alone.

---

**Question 11.** Has your idea of success changed as you have grown older?

**Model Answer:** Very much so. When I was younger I think I associated success mainly with conventional markers — academic achievement, career advancement, a certain level of income. As I've gotten older, I've come to value things that are harder to quantify: having work that feels purposeful, relationships that are genuinely deep, and some sense that I'm living in accordance with my own values rather than simply meeting external expectations. I think that shift is fairly common, and is probably a sign of some kind of maturity.

---

**Question 12.** What motivates you most when working towards a long-term goal?

**Model Answer:** Probably a combination of genuine interest in the goal itself and a clear sense of progress. If I find the end objective intrinsically interesting — not just externally rewarding — I can sustain effort over a long period even when progress is slow. Breaking large goals into smaller milestones also helps, because being able to see concrete progress along the way prevents the whole endeavour from feeling abstract and discouraging. I also find that explaining a goal to someone else and being accountable to them increases my commitment considerably.

---

### Cue Card

**Describe a documentary or science programme that you found interesting.**

*You should say:*
- *what the documentary or programme was about*
- *when and where you watched it*
- *what you found out or learnt from it*
- *and explain why you found it particularly interesting*

---

### Model Answer — Part 2

The documentary I'd like to talk about is one I watched a couple of years ago on a streaming platform — it was about animal migration, and specifically it traced the journey of the monarch butterfly from its summer habitats in North America to the forests of central Mexico where it spends the winter.

I came across it by chance on a Sunday evening when I wasn't looking for anything in particular. The film was beautifully shot — it used aerial footage that showed vast clouds of orange and black butterflies descending on the oyamel fir trees in the mountains of Michoacán state. The visual impact was extraordinary; it looked almost unreal, as though the trees themselves had come alive in a kind of slow orange wave.

What I found particularly fascinating was the navigation element — the fact that individual butterflies use the position of the sun as a kind of compass, adjusting their heading as the sun moves across the sky throughout the day. Given that the butterflies making the southward journey have never been to Mexico before — they are the great-grandchildren of the butterflies that left the previous spring — the precision of their navigation seemed almost miraculous.

The documentary also dealt with the very real threats the migration faces: deforestation in Mexico reducing the available overwintering habitat, climate change shifting the timing of milkweed growth in North America, and widespread use of herbicides eliminating the plants on which larvae depend. That contrast between something so spectacular and so fragile made the whole programme feel genuinely urgent rather than just visually impressive.

I recommend it to anyone with even a passing interest in the natural world.

*(~290 words)*

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion

*The examiner will ask you questions related to the Part 2 topic, inviting you to discuss more abstract ideas. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

**Question 1.** Do you think economic inequality is a serious problem in the world today?

**Model Answer:** Yes, I think it's one of the defining problems of our era. The gap between the wealthiest and poorest individuals — both within countries and between countries — has widened considerably over recent decades, and the consequences are far-reaching: unequal access to healthcare, education, and political influence, as well as the social tensions that come when large portions of the population feel the system is not working for them. The fact that we have simultaneously seen extraordinary technological progress and rising inequality suggests the benefits of that progress are not being shared in a sustainable or just way.

---

**Question 2.** What do you think are the main causes of the growing gap between rich and poor?

**Model Answer:** I think several forces have converged. Technological change — particularly automation and the shift towards digital, knowledge-based work — has created huge rewards for those with the right skills while displacing workers in routine occupations. Globalisation has added to this by relocating manufacturing to lower-wage countries, reducing employment and bargaining power in industrialised economies. At the same time, policy choices have played a significant role: many governments have reduced taxes on high incomes and capital gains while allowing trade union membership and the real value of minimum wages to decline. These aren't natural forces — they are partly the result of deliberate political decisions that could, in principle, be reversed.

---

**Question 3.** Some people argue that inequality is necessary to motivate innovation and hard work. Do you agree?

**Model Answer:** There's a legitimate kernel of truth in that argument — differential rewards do provide incentives, and markets that offer no reward for extra effort or risk-taking tend to produce inefficiency. But I think it becomes a rationalisation when used to justify the extreme levels of inequality we currently see. The marginal difference in motivation between earning, say, a million dollars and a hundred million dollars seems very small, while the social costs of that additional concentration of wealth — in terms of political influence, educational opportunity for others, and democratic fairness — are substantial. Most of the innovation that has genuinely transformed human welfare, from the internet to vaccines, has also depended heavily on public funding and collaborative research rather than purely on individual financial reward.

---

**Question 4.** What effects can high levels of inequality have on society?

**Model Answer:** The documented effects are quite wide-ranging. At the individual level, research consistently shows that people in more unequal societies have shorter life expectancies, higher rates of mental illness, and lower levels of educational attainment on average. At the community level, high inequality tends to correlate with lower social trust and weaker civic participation. Politically, extreme inequality often fuels populism and political polarisation, because people who feel economically marginalised become more susceptible to anti-establishment messages. Some economists also argue that very high inequality reduces long-run economic growth by suppressing consumer demand and underinvesting in human capital at the lower end of the income distribution.

---

**Question 5.** What role do you think technology plays in creating or reducing economic inequality?

**Model Answer:** Technology's relationship with inequality is genuinely complicated and cuts both ways. On the one hand, automation and artificial intelligence are displacing workers in many middle-income jobs, accelerating wage polarisation between high- and low-skill workers. The wealth generated by the most successful technology companies is also extraordinarily concentrated in the hands of a small number of shareholders and founders. On the other hand, technology has democratised access to information, education, and markets in ways that can potentially help people in lower-income situations to improve their prospects — through online learning, digital financial services, or platforms that allow small producers to reach global markets. Whether technology ultimately reduces or amplifies inequality seems to depend heavily on the policy environment in which it operates rather than on the technology itself.

---

**Question 6.** Do you think governments are doing enough to address the wealth gap?

**Model Answer:** In most cases, no, though the picture varies considerably by country. The Nordic nations — Sweden, Denmark, Norway — have maintained relatively compressed income distributions through high levels of public investment, strong labour market institutions, and progressive taxation. Many other developed economies have allowed inequality to widen over decades while implementing only modest corrective measures. Part of the challenge is political: those who benefit most from the current distribution of wealth also tend to have disproportionate political influence, which creates structural resistance to redistributive policies. I think effective action probably requires not just higher taxes on income and wealth but also long-term investment in education, healthcare, and housing that addresses inequality of opportunity rather than just inequality of outcome.

---

**Question 7.** Should there be an upper limit on how much wealth any individual can accumulate?

**Model Answer:** It's a fascinating and genuinely contested question. Strict wealth caps face significant practical challenges — jurisdictional arbitrage, legal complexity, and the difficulty of defining and measuring wealth consistently — but I do think there are strong arguments for more aggressive progressive taxation at very high wealth levels, as well as tighter restrictions on the inheritance of large fortunes. The core concern is less about the precise number than about the political power that vast concentrations of wealth confer. When a small number of individuals can effectively shape media coverage, fund political campaigns, and lobby for favourable regulation, the formal equality of democratic citizenship becomes somewhat hollow.

---

**Question 8.** Is it possible to have a society that is both innovative and highly equal?

**Model Answer:** I think the evidence suggests yes, and several countries demonstrate it in practice. The Nordic economies are among the most productive, technologically advanced, and innovative in the world, yet they maintain some of the lowest inequality levels among developed nations. Japan similarly has a highly innovative economy with relatively compressed wages. What these examples suggest is that the trade-off between equality and dynamism is not as stark or inevitable as free-market advocates sometimes claim. Strong public institutions, universal education, and collaborative rather than purely individualistic business cultures seem capable of sustaining innovation without requiring extreme inequality as a precondition.

---

**Question 9.** How might climate change and inequality be related?

**Model Answer:** They're deeply intertwined in several ways. First, the people who are least responsible for carbon emissions — those in lower-income countries and the poorer members of wealthier societies — tend to be most vulnerable to climate impacts such as extreme heat, flooding, and food insecurity. Second, the wealth and political influence of fossil fuel interests has arguably delayed the policy responses needed to address climate change. Conversely, the transition to green energy creates enormous opportunities, but ensuring that the economic benefits of that transition reach workers and communities displaced from fossil fuel industries is a significant equity challenge. Addressing both climate change and inequality simultaneously is complicated, but the two challenges are connected enough that solutions to one often need to account for the other.

---

**Question 10.** Do you think the next generation will face more or less inequality than the current one?

**Model Answer:** Difficult to say with confidence, but I lean towards thinking inequality could worsen before it improves, particularly if the economic disruption caused by artificial intelligence and automation is as significant as many analysts predict. The potential for AI to automate not just manual tasks but a wide range of cognitive and professional work could put significant downward pressure on wages across many sectors while generating enormous profits for a small number of firms and shareholders. Whether this translates into lasting higher inequality will depend enormously on how governments respond in terms of taxation, education, and social support. There are reasons to be cautiously optimistic — public awareness of inequality is higher than it has been for decades, and there is growing political pressure for redistributive policy — but I wouldn't be complacent about the outcome.
