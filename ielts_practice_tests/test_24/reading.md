## PASSAGE 1: The Psychology of Habit Formation

### Bài đọc

Every day, humans perform hundreds of actions almost automatically — brushing their teeth, taking a particular route to work, reaching for coffee first thing in the morning. These are habits, and understanding how they form has become one of the most significant areas of modern psychology. Researchers now believe that habits account for roughly 40 to 45 percent of daily human behaviour, making them a dominant force in how we live our lives.

A habit is essentially a learned behaviour that has become automatic through repetition. When an action is repeated consistently in the same context, the brain begins to encode it as a routine. Neuroscientists describe this process as "chunking" — the brain groups a sequence of actions into a single automatic unit, freeing up cognitive resources for other tasks. This is why experienced drivers can navigate familiar roads while holding a conversation, whereas a learner driver must concentrate intensely on every movement.

The habit loop, a concept popularised by journalist Charles Duhigg in his 2012 book The Power of Habit, describes the three-part structure of every habit: the cue, the routine, and the reward. The cue is a trigger — a time, place, emotion, or person — that tells the brain to begin the habit. The routine is the behaviour itself, and the reward is what the brain receives as a result, reinforcing the loop and increasing the likelihood that the habit will be repeated. Duhigg argues that to change a habit, one must identify the cue and reward while substituting a new routine in between.

Research has challenged the common belief that it takes 21 days to form a new habit. A 2010 study by Phillippa Lally and colleagues at University College London found that, on average, it takes 66 days for a new behaviour to become automatic — though the range varied considerably, from 18 to 254 days. The speed of habit formation depends on the complexity of the behaviour, the individual's motivation, and how consistently the behaviour is performed in the same context.

Not all habits are equally difficult to break. Simple habits tied to strong sensory cues — such as reaching for a cigarette when smelling smoke — are often more persistent than complex habits. This is because sensory cues directly activate automatic responses in the brain's basal ganglia, a region associated with procedural learning and habit formation. The prefrontal cortex, which governs deliberate decision-making, has limited influence once a habit is deeply ingrained.

Understanding habit formation has important implications for public health. Many chronic diseases — including obesity, type 2 diabetes, and heart disease — are linked to habitual behaviours such as poor diet, physical inactivity, and smoking. Health programmes that apply principles of habit psychology, such as implementation intentions (specific plans linking a cue to a desired behaviour), have shown promising results in promoting healthier lifestyles.

Ultimately, habits are not character flaws or signs of weakness; they are the brain's efficient response to a complex world. Recognising this empowers individuals to approach behaviour change with patience, strategy, and self-compassion rather than relying on willpower alone.

## PASSAGE 2: Social Conformity and Group Pressure

### Bài đọc

**Section A**
Human beings are fundamentally social creatures. From early childhood, we learn that fitting in with the group — whether family, peers, or community — carries significant rewards, and standing apart can bring discomfort or exclusion. This drive to conform is not merely a social nicety; it is a powerful psychological force that shapes opinions, decisions, and behaviours in ways that individuals often fail to recognise.

**Section B**
The scientific study of conformity was brought into sharp focus by the work of Solomon Asch in the 1950s. In his now-famous experiments, Asch asked participants to match the length of a line on one card to the correct line on another card. The task was simple, with an obvious correct answer. However, when Asch placed participants in a group where confederates — actors working with the researcher — unanimously gave a clearly wrong answer, approximately 75 percent of participants conformed to the incorrect answer at least once. Asch concluded that social pressure could lead people to deny the clear evidence of their own senses.

**Section C**
Subsequent researchers have identified two primary types of conformity. Normative conformity occurs when individuals comply with group norms to gain social acceptance or avoid rejection, even when they privately disagree. Informational conformity, by contrast, occurs when individuals look to others for guidance in ambiguous situations, genuinely believing that the group possesses more accurate information. The distinction matters practically: normative conformity often produces public compliance without genuine attitude change, whereas informational conformity can lead to lasting shifts in belief.

**Section D**
One of the most influential factors governing conformity is group size. Research suggests that conformity increases as group size grows, but only up to a point — typically around three to five members. Beyond that threshold, additional members contribute little to social pressure. Equally important is unanimity: if even one member of the group publicly disagrees, the pressure on the individual to conform drops dramatically. This "ally effect" suggests that social support, however minimal, can be sufficient to enable independent thought.

**Section E**
The consequences of conformity extend well beyond laboratory settings. In organisational contexts, a phenomenon known as groupthink can emerge when the desire for harmony within a team overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives. The term was coined by social psychologist Irving Janis, who studied decision-making failures such as the Bay of Pigs invasion. Groupthink, Janis argued, is characterised by an illusion of invulnerability, collective rationalisation, and pressure on dissenters to remain silent. Such dynamics can lead highly intelligent groups to make catastrophically poor decisions.

**Section F**
More recent research has explored conformity in digital environments. Online communities, social media platforms, and review sites all create new contexts for social influence. Studies have found that users are significantly more likely to rate a product positively if they first see that others have already done so — a pattern described as "herding." Algorithms that amplify popular content may therefore compound conformity effects, creating self-reinforcing opinion bubbles that are resistant to correction by new information.

**Section G**
Despite its pervasive influence, conformity is not inevitable. Individuals with strong personal values, high self-esteem, and what psychologists call an "internal locus of control" — the belief that they control their own outcomes — are more resistant to social pressure. Education in critical thinking and awareness of social influence mechanisms has also been shown to reduce conformity in experimental settings, suggesting that understanding the psychology of conformity is itself a step towards greater autonomy.

## PASSAGE 3: The Science of Happiness

### Bài đọc

For most of human history, happiness was considered the province of philosophy and religion — a matter of wisdom, virtue, or divine grace. The idea that happiness could be studied scientifically, measured objectively, and deliberately cultivated was largely foreign to pre-modern thought. That changed dramatically in the late twentieth century, when positive psychology emerged as a formal discipline, and researchers began applying the rigorous methods of empirical science to one of humanity's oldest questions: what makes a good life?

The field was formally launched in 1998 when Martin Seligman, then president of the American Psychological Association, made the scientific study of human flourishing a research priority. Together with colleagues such as Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi and Christopher Peterson, Seligman developed frameworks for understanding well-being that moved beyond the mere absence of illness. The most influential of these is Seligman's PERMA model, which identifies five core elements of well-being: Positive emotions, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, and Accomplishment.

Neuroscientific research has shed considerable light on the biological basis of happiness. Studies using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) have shown that positive emotional experiences are associated with heightened activity in the left prefrontal cortex, while negative emotions tend to activate the right prefrontal cortex. Neuroscientist Richard Davidson has proposed that individuals differ in their "affective style" — a relatively stable tendency toward positive or negative emotion — which is shaped by both genetic inheritance and early life experience. Crucially, Davidson's research suggests that the brain retains a degree of plasticity throughout life, meaning that deliberate mental training, such as mindfulness meditation, can shift affective style in a more positive direction.

A landmark study in the science of happiness is the work of psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Angus Deaton, who analysed data from a Gallup survey of over 450,000 Americans. Their 2010 findings suggested that emotional well-being — day-to-day feelings of happiness, stress, and sadness — increases with income up to an annual salary of approximately $75,000 (in 2010 values), beyond which additional income has little effect on emotional experience, though it continues to influence individuals' overall life evaluations. This research sparked intense debate and prompted subsequent studies with differing conclusions, underscoring the complexity of the relationship between wealth and happiness.

Social relationships consistently emerge as one of the strongest predictors of happiness across cultures and throughout the life course. The Harvard Study of Adult Development, one of the longest-running studies of human happiness, tracked hundreds of participants over more than 75 years and found that the quality of close relationships — rather than wealth, fame, or professional achievement — was the single most important factor determining life satisfaction in old age. Director Robert Waldinger summarised the findings as follows: "Good relationships keep us happier and healthier. Period."

Cultural context shapes both the experience and expression of happiness. In individualistic societies such as the United States and Australia, happiness is often associated with personal achievement, excitement, and high-arousal positive emotions. In collectivist societies such as Japan and South Korea, happiness is more frequently expressed through calm contentment, social harmony, and fulfilment of relational duties. Researchers have therefore cautioned against applying findings from Western samples universally, arguing that any complete science of happiness must be attentive to cultural variation.

Perhaps the most practically significant finding of happiness research is that humans dramatically overestimate the impact of future events — both positive and negative — on their long-term well-being. Psychologist Daniel Gilbert has called this tendency "impact bias." People believe that winning the lottery will make them permanently happier, or that a disability will make them permanently miserable; in reality, both lottery winners and individuals who have acquired disabilities tend to return to their baseline level of happiness within a relatively short time. This phenomenon, known as hedonic adaptation, suggests that the pursuit of happiness through the acquisition of goods, status, or experiences may be fundamentally misguided — and that sustainable well-being is more likely to be found in the cultivation of relationships, meaning, and engagement with daily life.
