# IELTS SPEAKING — TEST 05

**Overview:** The IELTS Speaking test is 11–14 minutes, conducted as a face-to-face interview.
- Part 1: 4–5 minutes — general questions on familiar topics
- Part 2: 3–4 minutes — individual long turn (cue card + 2-minute talk)
- Part 3: 4–5 minutes — two-way discussion on abstract topics

---

## Part 1 (4–5 minutes)

### Topic 1: Entertainment & Media

**Question 1:** What kind of films do you enjoy watching?

**Model Answer:**
"Well, I tend to gravitate towards psychological thrillers — there's something about the suspense and the way they make you question everything that I find incredibly engaging. I particularly enjoy films like *Parasite* or *Inception* because they work on multiple levels simultaneously. That said, I also appreciate the occasional comedy when I just want to switch off after a stressful day."

---

**Question 2:** How often do you go to the cinema?

**Model Answer:**
"To be honest, not as often as I'd like. I probably go once a month or so. The experience of watching something on a big screen with surround sound is really something you can't replicate at home, so I try to save it for films that truly deserve that treatment — big blockbusters or visually spectacular films especially."

---

**Question 3:** Do you prefer watching films at home or in a cinema?

**Model Answer:**
"That really depends on the type of film. For action-packed blockbusters or visually stunning films, the cinema wins hands down. But for something more dialogue-driven or intimate, I actually prefer watching at home where I can pause and discuss scenes with friends. I also appreciate being able to rewind if I miss something important."

---

**Question 4:** Has the way you watch films changed in recent years?

**Model Answer:**
"Absolutely. With streaming platforms now offering so much quality content, I find myself watching far more series and documentaries than traditional films. It's become much more convenient, though I do sometimes miss the ritual of going out to the cinema — there's a social element to it that you just can't recreate on your sofa."

---

### Topic 2: Music

**Question 5:** What kind of music do you enjoy listening to?

**Model Answer:**
"My tastes are fairly eclectic, to be honest. I gravitate towards indie rock and jazz — I love the improvisational quality of jazz and the raw energy of indie bands. I find that different genres suit different moods. When I'm working, I tend to put on something instrumental so it doesn't distract me, but when I'm exercising, I need something with a strong beat."

---

**Question 6:** Do you enjoy attending live music events?

**Model Answer:**
"I absolutely do — there's something uniquely electric about live performances that recordings simply cannot capture. The energy of a crowd, the slight imperfections of a live show, the physical vibration of the sound — all of these make the experience far more immersive than listening at home. I try to attend concerts a few times a year when I can afford to."

---

**Question 7:** How does music affect your mood?

**Model Answer:**
"Enormously, actually. I think music is one of the most powerful mood regulators available to us. When I'm feeling anxious or stressed, putting on something calm and melodic can genuinely shift my emotional state within minutes. On the other hand, an upbeat track can completely transform my energy levels if I'm feeling sluggish. I think most people experience this even if they don't consciously acknowledge it."

---

**Question 8:** Do you think it is important for children to learn a musical instrument?

**Model Answer:**
"I think it's highly beneficial, though I wouldn't say it's essential for everyone. Learning an instrument teaches discipline, patience, and creative expression, and research suggests it also supports mathematical and linguistic development in the brain. That said, forcing a child who has no interest in music to practise daily can create negative associations with learning. The key is probably to introduce music in an enjoyable, low-pressure way."

---

### Topic 3: Social Activities

**Question 9:** How do you typically spend time with your friends?

**Model Answer:**
"It varies a lot depending on the season and how busy everyone is. In warmer months, we tend to meet up outdoors — going for walks, having picnics, or exploring different parts of the city. In winter, we gravitate more towards indoor activities like board games, cooking together, or watching films. I think the activity matters less than the quality of conversation, honestly."

---

**Question 10:** Do you prefer socialising in large groups or with one or two close friends?

**Model Answer:**
"Definitely the latter — I find one-on-one conversations or small groups far more meaningful. In large social settings, I find it difficult to have genuinely deep conversations because the dynamic tends to stay quite surface-level. With close friends, you can really discuss ideas, share vulnerabilities, and feel a sense of real connection. That said, larger gatherings can be fun occasionally, particularly for celebrations."

---

**Question 11:** Do you think your social habits have changed as you've got older?

**Model Answer:**
"Quite significantly, yes. When I was younger, I was much more focused on having a large social circle and going out frequently. As I've matured, I've come to value quality over quantity when it comes to friendships. I'm now much more selective about how I spend my social time, and I find I'm far more fulfilled by a quiet dinner with a close friend than a large party."

---

**Question 12:** Has technology changed the way people socialise?

**Model Answer:**
"Without question. Social media and messaging apps have made it incredibly easy to stay in touch with people across distances, which is genuinely wonderful. However, I do think there's a risk that digital communication can become a substitute for face-to-face interaction rather than a supplement to it. When people are texting at a dinner table rather than talking to each other, technology has arguably diminished the quality of social connection."

---

## Part 2 — Cue Card (3–4 minutes)

**TASK CARD:**

> **Describe a film or book that had a significant impact on you.**
>
> You should say:
> - what the film or book was
> - what it was about
> - how it affected or changed you
> - and explain why it made such a strong impression on you

**Preparation time:** 1 minute
**Speaking time:** 1–2 minutes

---

### Model Response (~200 words):

"I'd like to talk about a book called *The Alchemist* by Paulo Coelho, which I read when I was about eighteen and which genuinely changed the way I thought about my future.

The story follows a young Andalusian shepherd named Santiago who dreams of finding treasure near the Egyptian pyramids. What makes the book so remarkable is that it's not really about the treasure at all — it's about the journey and the concept of following your 'Personal Legend,' which is essentially the idea that each of us has a unique destiny that we should pursue with courage.

What struck me most was the simplicity of the book's philosophy. It made me realise that I had been living too cautiously, always prioritising security over genuine passion. After reading it, I made the decision to pursue a career in a field that genuinely excited me rather than simply choosing the most financially stable option.

I think the reason it left such a lasting impression is that it arrived at exactly the right moment in my life, when I was trying to figure out who I wanted to be. I would wholeheartedly recommend it to anyone who feels stuck or uncertain about their direction."

---

**Follow-up Question:** "Do you think books affect people differently at different stages of life?"

**Model Answer:**
"I think that's absolutely true. The same book can mean completely different things depending on when you read it. Life experiences give you different lenses through which to interpret stories. For example, a novel about parenthood might feel quite abstract to a teenager but profoundly moving to someone who has just had a child. That's what makes great literature so enduring — it continues to reveal new layers with each reading."

---

### Part 2 Strategy Tips:

- **Use all 1 minute of preparation time** — make brief notes under each bullet point
- **Don't memorise a script** — use your notes as prompts, speak naturally
- **Extend naturally** by adding: feelings, reasons, comparisons, examples
- **Aim for 1 minute 30 seconds minimum** — the examiner will stop you at 2 minutes
- **Useful openers:** "I'd like to talk about...", "The one that comes to mind is...", "Without hesitation, I'd choose..."

## Part 3 — Two-Way Discussion (4–5 minutes)

### Questions on: Media, Arts & Cultural Influence

---

**Question 1:** How important is it for governments to support the arts and cultural industries?

**Model Answer (80 words):**
"I think government support for the arts is crucial, precisely because cultural industries often cannot sustain themselves through commercial revenue alone. Museums, theatres, and independent film projects serve an important social function — preserving heritage, fostering critical thinking, and providing citizens with avenues for creative expression. Without public funding, these institutions risk becoming accessible only to the wealthy. That said, governments should be careful to support arts broadly rather than favouring particular styles or ideologies, as artistic freedom is fundamental to genuine cultural vitality."

---

**Question 2:** Do you think social media has had a positive or negative impact on the film and music industries?

**Model Answer (75 words):**
"It's genuinely mixed. On the positive side, social media has democratised access — independent artists can now reach global audiences without a record label or distribution deal. Viral moments can launch careers overnight. However, the attention economy that social media creates has also shortened cultural attention spans. Albums and films that don't generate instant engagement are often overlooked, which can discourage the creation of complex, challenging work that requires time to appreciate. So the benefits and costs are substantial."

---

**Question 3:** Should there be censorship of films and other media? Why or why not?

**Model Answer (85 words):**
"This is a genuinely difficult question. I believe some form of content regulation is necessary — for example, protecting children from graphic violence or exploitation. However, state-imposed ideological censorship of films or literature is extremely dangerous because it suppresses legitimate political and social commentary. History shows us that many of the world's most important artistic works — from Orwell's novels to politically charged films — were censored by authoritarian regimes. A free society should trust adults to critically engage with challenging content rather than shielding them from it."

---

**Question 4:** How does cinema influence cultural attitudes and social norms?

**Model Answer (78 words):**
"Cinema is enormously powerful as a cultural force. Films shape how we perceive gender roles, racial groups, and social relationships — often in ways audiences don't consciously recognise. For example, decades of Hollywood films reinforced certain stereotypes about particular nationalities, and audiences absorbed these narratives as reflections of reality. On the positive side, films can also challenge prejudice and expand empathy — films depicting LGBTQ+ experiences or the realities of poverty have played significant roles in shifting public attitudes in many countries."

---

**Question 5:** Is it important for children to be exposed to literature and art from an early age?

**Model Answer (72 words):**
"Absolutely, and I think this is underappreciated in many education systems that prioritise STEM subjects. Early exposure to literature develops vocabulary, empathy, and narrative thinking — skills that underpin success in almost every field. Art education develops visual intelligence, creativity, and the ability to communicate ideas non-verbally. There is also growing neuroscientific evidence that engagement with arts from an early age supports broader cognitive development. The idea that arts education is a 'luxury' is, in my view, deeply mistaken."

---

**Question 6:** Do you think streaming services are changing what kinds of stories get told in film and television?

**Model Answer (82 words):**
"Definitely, and in rather complex ways. On the positive side, streaming platforms have given voice to stories and perspectives that mainstream Hollywood rarely told — we've seen a dramatic increase in international productions, stories centred on women and minorities, and genre-defying narratives. However, streaming companies are also driven by data, which means they sometimes commission content based on what algorithms predict will perform well. This can lead to a proliferation of formulaic content and a hesitation to fund truly experimental or challenging projects."

---

**Question 7:** How has the way people consume music changed in the last twenty years?

**Model Answer (77 words):**
"The transformation has been radical. Twenty years ago, most people purchased physical albums or downloaded individual songs. Today, streaming dominates — people access virtually unlimited music for a small monthly fee. This has both democratised access and fundamentally changed listening habits. Many argue that streaming has encouraged 'playlist culture,' where songs are consumed as isolated tracks rather than as part of a cohesive artistic whole. This has arguably pressured artists to frontload the catchiest elements of their songs rather than allowing ideas to develop organically."

---

**Question 8:** Do you think music can serve as a tool for social or political change?

**Model Answer (80 words):**
"Historically, music has been one of the most powerful vehicles for social and political movements. Folk music was central to the American civil rights movement; protest songs shaped anti-war sentiment in the 1960s and 70s; hip-hop has been a persistent voice for marginalised urban communities. Music reaches people emotionally in ways that political speeches or journalism sometimes cannot. However, its impact depends heavily on context — music amplifies existing movements but rarely creates them alone. It works best as one component of a broader cultural and political conversation."

---

**Question 9:** Are traditional art forms at risk of dying out in the digital age?

**Model Answer (76 words):**
"Some are certainly under pressure, yes. Traditional crafts, classical music performance, and folk art forms struggle to attract young audiences when competing with the immediacy and spectacle of digital entertainment. However, I'd resist the idea that this is inevitable. We've seen remarkable revivals — vinyl records have made a comeback, handcrafted products are thriving through platforms like Etsy, and traditional dance forms find new audiences on social media. Digital technology can, paradoxically, help preserve and promote cultural heritage when used thoughtfully."

---

**Question 10:** Is it the responsibility of artists to reflect the values of their society, or should they challenge those values?

**Model Answer (84 words):**
"I strongly believe that art's highest function is to challenge rather than merely reflect. When artists simply mirror existing values, they produce pleasant decoration but rarely create the kind of work that expands human understanding or drives social progress. The most significant artistic works throughout history have been provocative — they made audiences uncomfortable, questioned assumptions, and proposed alternative ways of seeing the world. Of course, this must be balanced with artistic integrity rather than provocation for its own sake. Challenge without substance becomes mere spectacle."

---

### Part 3 Strategy Tips:

- **Take 2–3 seconds to think** before answering — say "That's an interesting question" or "Let me think about that"
- **Structure longer answers:** position → reason 1 → reason 2 → concession/nuance → conclusion
- **Use hedging language:** "I would argue that...", "It seems to me that...", "To some extent..."
- **Avoid one-sentence answers** — examiner will keep prompting; aim for 60–90 seconds per answer
- **Show analytical thinking:** compare, contrast, give historical examples, acknowledge complexity
