# Bài Nói IELTS - Test 28: Education & Learning

---

## PART 1: Câu hỏi ngắn

### Chủ đề 1: Education & School (Giáo dục & Trường học)

**1. Did you enjoy school when you were younger?**

Yes, for the most part I did. I particularly enjoyed classes where the teacher was passionate about the subject, as that enthusiasm was contagious. However, I found some exam-focused lessons quite stressful.

**2. What was your favourite subject at school, and why?**

My favourite subject was literature. I loved how it encouraged me to interpret texts critically and explore different perspectives. Discussing novels and poems felt much more engaging than memorising facts.

**3. Do you think the education system in your country prepares students well for adult life?**

It prepares students academically, but I think there are some gaps. Life skills such as financial literacy, critical thinking, and emotional resilience are not always covered deeply enough in the standard curriculum.

**4. How important do you think homework is for students?**

Homework can reinforce what is learned in class, but I think the volume matters. Excessive homework reduces time for creative play and rest, which are just as important for a child's development.

### Chủ đề 2: Reading & Books (Đọc sách)

**5. How often do you read books?**

I try to read for at least thirty minutes every evening. I find that reading before bed helps me unwind and also expands my vocabulary in both Vietnamese and English.

**6. Do you prefer physical books or e-books?**

I prefer physical books because I enjoy the tactile experience of turning pages and annotating in the margins. That said, e-books are much more convenient when travelling.

**7. What kind of books do you enjoy reading most?**

I tend to gravitate towards non-fiction, particularly books about history and psychology. I find them genuinely useful for understanding the world, though I also enjoy a well-crafted novel from time to time.

**8. Did reading play an important role in your education?**

Absolutely. Reading widely exposed me to vocabulary and sentence structures that formal lessons alone could not provide. It also helped me develop a more nuanced understanding of complex topics across different subjects.

### Chủ đề 3: Goals & Ambitions (Mục tiêu & Tham vọng)

**9. What are your main goals for the next five years?**

In the next five years, I hope to achieve professional qualifications in my field, improve my English to a proficient level, and travel to at least three new countries. I want to grow both personally and professionally.

**10. How do you stay motivated when working towards a long-term goal?**

I break large goals down into smaller, manageable milestones and track my progress weekly. Celebrating small wins keeps my motivation high, and I also remind myself regularly of why the goal matters to me.

**11. Do you think it is important to have clear goals in life?**

Yes, I do. Without clear goals, it is easy to drift and spend time on activities that do not contribute to meaningful growth. Goals give direction and help prioritise how we spend our time and energy.

**12. Have your ambitions changed since you were a child?**

Significantly. As a child I wanted to be a scientist, but as I grew older I became more interested in communication and education. I think our ambitions naturally evolve as we learn more about ourselves and the world.

**Bài nói mẫu (khoảng 200 từ):**

The teacher who has had the greatest influence on my life is Mr Minh, my secondary school English teacher. I first met him when I was around thirteen years old, and from the very first lesson it was clear that he was unlike any other teacher I had encountered.

What set Mr Minh apart was his absolute conviction that language learning should be joyful rather than mechanical. Instead of drilling grammar rules in isolation, he brought authentic English songs, films, and newspaper articles into the classroom. He encouraged us to make mistakes and celebrated effort over perfection, which created an atmosphere where students felt genuinely safe to experiment with the language.

His influence on me was profound because he did not just teach English — he taught me how to love the process of learning itself. He showed me that curiosity, persistence, and a willingness to engage with difficulty were more valuable than any textbook. I still carry that lesson with me today.

Long-term, Mr Minh is the reason I pursued further English study and why I am sitting the IELTS examination now. He convinced me that language is a window onto other cultures and ways of thinking, and that conviction has shaped my academic and professional choices ever since.

## PART 3: Câu hỏi trừu tượng

**1. How has the role of teachers changed in recent decades?**

Teachers have shifted from being the sole source of knowledge to becoming facilitators and guides. With information now freely available online, their role is less about transmitting facts and more about helping students develop critical thinking, evaluate sources, and apply knowledge in meaningful contexts. This requires a quite different set of skills than traditional teaching.

**2. Do you think technology will eventually replace teachers?**

I do not believe technology will replace teachers entirely, because teaching is fundamentally a human relationship. Artificial intelligence can deliver content efficiently, but it cannot replicate the emotional attunement, adaptability, and mentorship that an effective teacher provides. Technology is better understood as a powerful tool that supports, rather than substitutes for, human instruction.

**3. What are the main differences between education systems in developed and developing countries?**

The most significant differences tend to involve resource availability, teacher quality, and curriculum relevance. Developed countries generally benefit from well-trained teachers, modern facilities, and evidence-based curricula, while many developing nations still struggle with overcrowded classrooms and under-resourced schools. However, some developing nations have achieved impressive outcomes by prioritising teacher training and community engagement.

**4. Should university education be free for all students?**

There are strong arguments on both sides. Free university education reduces inequality and ensures that talent, rather than wealth, determines who accesses higher learning. However, the financial burden on governments is considerable. A compromise — such as income-contingent loans or heavily subsidised tuition — may offer a more sustainable balance between accessibility and fiscal responsibility.

**5. How important is vocational training compared to academic education?**

Both are essential, and I think the traditional hierarchy that places academic education above vocational training is outdated. Skilled tradespeople — electricians, nurses, technicians — are just as vital to a functioning society as university graduates. Countries like Germany and Switzerland, which invest heavily in dual vocational and academic pathways, consistently produce both high employment rates and strong economies.

**6. What do you think is the purpose of education?**

Education serves multiple purposes simultaneously: it equips individuals with skills and knowledge, socialises children into civic life, reduces inequality by broadening opportunity, and fosters the intellectual curiosity that drives innovation and cultural progress. No single purpose adequately captures its full significance, which is why debates about education policy are so complex and contentious.

**7. How does lifelong learning benefit individuals and society?**

For individuals, lifelong learning maintains cognitive agility, increases adaptability in a changing job market, and contributes to personal fulfilment. For society, a population that continues to learn is more innovative, more civically engaged, and better equipped to address collective challenges such as climate change and technological disruption. Cultures that normalise continuous learning tend to be more resilient overall.

**8. Is it better to study abroad or remain in your home country?**

Both options have distinct advantages. Studying abroad exposes students to new cultures, languages, and perspectives, which can be enormously broadening. However, studying at home keeps students connected to their communities, is typically more affordable, and allows them to develop expertise relevant to their own national context. The best choice depends on the individual's goals, resources, and field of study.

**9. Should governments decide what subjects universities offer?**

Governments have a legitimate interest in ensuring that higher education serves national development goals, but direct control over academic content risks stifling intellectual freedom and innovation. A preferable model might involve governments providing incentives for programmes aligned with national needs — in healthcare, engineering, or sustainability — while leaving universities the autonomy to develop their own academic offerings.

**10. Do you think the gap between academic knowledge and real-world application is a serious problem?**

Yes, it is a genuine and persistent challenge. Graduates frequently report feeling unprepared for the practical demands of their professions, despite years of study. Bridging this gap requires closer collaboration between universities and industries, more embedded work placements, and curricula that are regularly reviewed against current professional standards. The good news is that many institutions are already moving in this direction.
