# IELTS Academic Speaking — Test 28

**Time allowed: 11–14 minutes**

***

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*The examiner will ask you general questions on familiar topics. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

### Topic 1: Nature and Wildlife

**Question 1.** Do you enjoy spending time in nature? Why or why not?

**Model Answer:** Yes, very much. I find being outdoors — whether in a forest, by the sea, or even in a well-kept park — genuinely restorative in a way that indoor environments rarely are. There is something about the combination of open space, natural sounds, and the unpredictability of wildlife that clears my head and reduces stress. I try to get outside at least once a week, even when life gets busy, because I always feel better for it.

---

**Question 2.** Have you ever seen a wild animal in its natural habitat? What was the experience like?

**Model Answer:** Yes, I was lucky enough to see a family of deer in a forest during a hiking trip a couple of years ago. What struck me most was how quietly they moved — they appeared from between the trees without any warning and were gone just as quickly. It was a brief encounter, but it felt quite special precisely because it was unplanned and unscripted. That sense of observing something that has nothing to do with human activity is quite rare and, I think, quite precious.

---

**Question 3.** Are people in your country generally aware of environmental issues such as wildlife protection?

**Model Answer:** Awareness has grown significantly in recent years, particularly among younger people, partly driven by documentary series and social media coverage of issues like deforestation and endangered species. However, I think there is still a gap between awareness and action — many people understand that biodiversity is under threat in the abstract but don't necessarily change their behaviour or support relevant policies in any consistent way. Education and well-designed policy incentives would probably do more than awareness campaigns alone.

---

**Question 4.** Do you think it is important for children to learn about nature and wildlife? Why?

**Model Answer:** I think it is extremely important, and if anything it has become more so as children spend less time outdoors than previous generations. Developing a relationship with the natural world from an early age seems to build both environmental concern and a broader sense of curiosity and wonder. Research suggests that children who regularly spend time in nature are also healthier and better at concentrating. If we want future generations to value and protect biodiversity, giving them direct, enjoyable experience of it seems far more effective than any amount of classroom instruction.

---

### Topic 2: Energy Use at Home

**Question 5.** How do you use energy at home, and do you try to reduce your consumption?

**Model Answer:** Like most people, I use energy for heating, cooling, cooking, lighting, and charging various devices. I have made some efforts to reduce consumption — switching to LED lighting throughout the flat, being more careful about turning off standby appliances, and avoiding using the tumble dryer when I can hang clothes outside instead. They are small changes, but I think individual habits, multiplied across millions of households, do add up to something meaningful at scale.

---

**Question 6.** Are people in your country becoming more interested in renewable energy for their homes?

**Model Answer:** Definitely. There has been noticeable growth in rooftop solar panels in residential areas over the past five years or so, and some energy suppliers now market specifically green tariffs that allow households to pay a premium for electricity from renewable sources. The main barrier still seems to be upfront cost — solar installation in particular requires a significant initial investment that many households cannot easily afford, even if the long-term savings make it financially sensible.

---

**Question 7.** Do you think governments should do more to encourage households to use cleaner energy?

**Model Answer:** I think governments have both the responsibility and the tools to do this more effectively than they currently do in most countries. Subsidies for solar panels and heat pumps, mandatory energy-efficiency standards for new buildings, and time-of-use electricity tariffs that reward flexible consumption during periods of high renewable generation are all measures that have proven effective where they have been well implemented. The argument that individuals should make these choices voluntarily overlooks the fact that the infrastructure and pricing signals that shape choices are themselves the product of policy decisions.

---

**Question 8.** How do you think energy use in homes will change in the next 20 years?

**Model Answer:** I expect it will change significantly. More homes will likely generate some of their own electricity through rooftop solar, and battery storage systems will allow households to use that electricity around the clock rather than just during daylight hours. Electric vehicles will become the norm, turning the car into a large mobile battery that can interact with the home's energy system. Heating will increasingly shift from gas boilers to heat pumps, which are far more efficient even if they require upgrades to insulation. Overall, the home energy system will become much more interactive and digitally managed than it is today.

---

### Topic 3: Languages

**Question 9.** How many languages do you speak, and how did you learn them?

**Model Answer:** I speak two languages — my native language and English. My native language was acquired naturally through childhood immersion, in the way all first languages are. English I learnt through formal schooling initially, but the real breakthrough came when I started consuming media — films, podcasts, and online content — in English without relying on subtitles or translation. The combination of structured grammar study and large quantities of authentic input turned out to be far more effective for me than formal lessons alone.

---

**Question 10.** Do you think it is important to preserve languages that are spoken by only a small number of people?

**Model Answer:** I genuinely do, though I recognise it is a complex issue. A language is not simply a communication tool — it encodes a particular way of understanding the world, a body of oral literature, and ecological and cultural knowledge that has accumulated over centuries. When a language disappears, all of that is lost in a way that no translation can fully recover. At the same time, communities facing economic marginalisation often have understandable reasons for shifting to more widely spoken languages, and we should be careful not to romanticise heritage languages in ways that ignore the real constraints people face.

---

**Question 11.** Have you ever tried to learn a third language? What was the experience like?

**Model Answer:** I tried to learn Spanish for about a year and made reasonable progress up to a conversational level, but I found it difficult to sustain the required practice once I was no longer in a structured course. The vocabulary stuck fairly well, but without regular opportunities to use the language in real conversation, my fluency plateaued and gradually declined. The experience taught me that consistent, authentic use matters far more than any number of grammar exercises, and that language learning requires a long-term commitment rather than a short intensive effort.

---

**Question 12.** Do you think translation technology will make learning foreign languages less important in the future?

**Model Answer:** It will probably reduce the practical necessity of learning a language for basic transactions — travel, business communication, reading foreign websites — and I think that is largely a positive development that lowers barriers. But I would argue that the deeper benefits of language learning cannot be replicated by technology. Understanding a language from the inside — its humour, its ambiguities, its cultural resonances — gives you access to a way of thinking that a translation, however accurate, cannot fully convey. For people who want genuine cross-cultural understanding rather than merely the ability to exchange information, language learning will remain valuable regardless of how good translation becomes.

### Model Answer — Part 2

I would like to talk about a visit I made to a national park about three years ago, which turned out to be one of the most memorable natural experiences I have had.

The park is a large protected area of mixed forest and wetland, home to a wide variety of birds, mammals, and reptiles. I went during early autumn, which turned out to be an ideal time — the summer crowds had thinned, the light was extraordinary, and the animals were more active than they tend to be in the heat of midsummer. I was with a small group of friends, one of whom is a keen birdwatcher and served as an informal guide throughout the trip.

The moment that impressed me most happened on our second morning. We were walking along a path beside a slow-moving river when we spotted a large bird of prey — later identified as an osprey — hovering almost motionlessly above the water for what felt like a full minute. Then it folded its wings and plunged almost vertically into the river, emerging a few seconds later with a fish gripped in its talons. It happened so quickly and so perfectly that none of us said anything for a long moment afterwards.

What impressed me about the experience was not just the spectacle itself but the sense it gave me of a world that operates entirely on its own logic and on its own timescale, with no reference to human concerns. In everyday life it is easy to feel that the natural world is something distant, something we observe on screens rather than encounter in person. That osprey dive reminded me, in a visceral way, that it is not.

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion

*The examiner will ask discussion questions related to the Part 2 topic. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

**Question 1.** Why do you think biodiversity conservation has become such an important issue in recent decades?

**Model Answer:** I think several factors have converged. Scientific evidence for the scale of species loss has become impossible to ignore — the statistics on extinction rates, collapsing insect populations, and declining vertebrate numbers are deeply alarming, and that evidence has filtered through to public awareness. Alongside that, there is a growing understanding that biodiversity underpins the ecosystem services that human economies depend on: pollination, water purification, climate regulation. The issue has shifted from being seen as a niche concern of naturalists to a mainstream economic and security issue, which has given it a very different kind of political urgency.

---

**Question 2.** Do you think governments are doing enough to protect endangered species?

**Model Answer:** In most cases, I would say no. While there are genuine conservation successes — the recovery of certain raptor populations in Europe, or the stabilisation of some whale stocks following international hunting bans — the overall trajectory of biodiversity loss has not been reversed. International frameworks like the Convention on Biological Diversity set targets, but the mechanisms for enforcing them are weak, and national governments face strong economic incentives to prioritise development over conservation in the short term. The gap between ambitious commitments and actual outcomes is, unfortunately, very wide.

---

**Question 3.** Some people argue that conservation efforts should focus on economically valuable species rather than all species equally. What do you think?

**Model Answer:** I understand the pragmatic appeal of that argument — resources are limited, and prioritising species that provide measurable benefits to humans might seem like a rational allocation of effort. But I think it misunderstands how ecosystems work. Keystone species whose removal causes cascading collapse are often not the most economically conspicuous ones — the humble sea otter, for example, turns out to be critical to the health of entire kelp forest ecosystems. More broadly, we simply do not know enough about ecological interdependencies to confidently identify which species are expendable, which suggests that broad-based conservation is the more prudent approach even from a purely utilitarian standpoint.

---

**Question 4.** What role can ordinary individuals play in protecting biodiversity?

**Model Answer:** Quite a significant one, I think, though perhaps not in the ways usually suggested. Individual consumer choices — reducing meat consumption, avoiding products linked to deforestation, choosing sustainably sourced materials — can collectively exert real pressure on supply chains and markets. Beyond consumption, political engagement matters: voting for governments with credible conservation policies, supporting environmental organisations, and holding businesses accountable through civil society pressure are probably more impactful than individual lifestyle adjustments alone. And there is real value in maintaining and restoring biodiversity in domestic gardens, urban spaces, and community land — small habitats can be surprisingly important for pollinators and birds.

---

**Question 5.** Do you think nuclear energy should play a larger role in addressing climate change?

**Model Answer:** I think it should be part of the response, yes, though the debate is more nuanced than either its strongest proponents or opponents tend to acknowledge. The case for nuclear is clear: it produces very low lifecycle carbon emissions, it can generate electricity reliably around the clock, and countries like France have demonstrated that a grid based predominantly on nuclear can be low-carbon and stable. The case against rests on legitimate concerns about accident risk, the unresolved challenge of waste disposal, and the high and often unpredictable construction costs. My view is that the urgency of climate change justifies keeping existing nuclear plants operating, and in some countries, building new ones — particularly where renewables face geographic or grid constraints.

---

**Question 6.** How should society handle the risks associated with nuclear power?

**Model Answer:** Through a combination of robust regulation, transparent communication, and technological investment. Regulatory oversight needs to be genuinely independent from both government and industry, with internationally agreed safety standards and meaningful consequences for non-compliance. Public communication needs to be honest about both risks and benefits — the evidence suggests that nuclear energy's actual safety record per unit of energy generated compares favourably with fossil fuels, but that fact is rarely communicated clearly. On technology, investment in Generation IV designs that incorporate passive safety features and produce less long-lived waste seems entirely justified. The worst outcome would be to phase out nuclear prematurely, replacing it with fossil fuels rather than renewables, simply because of public anxiety that is not proportionate to the evidence.

---

**Question 7.** How might the development of nuclear fusion change the global energy landscape?

**Model Answer:** If fusion is successfully commercialised, it could be transformative — potentially providing an almost limitless supply of low-carbon energy from hydrogen isotopes that are widely available and produce far less long-lived radioactive waste than fission. The ITER project in France is designed to demonstrate the scientific feasibility of net energy gain from fusion, which would be a critical milestone. However, there is a significant difference between demonstrating feasibility in a research setting and building commercially viable power stations, and that gap has proved wider and more difficult to close than optimists predicted several decades ago. I think fusion is genuinely promising, but it would be unwise to rely on it to solve our near-term energy and climate challenges, for which we need to deploy existing technologies more rapidly and at greater scale.

---

**Question 8.** Why do you think some languages are disappearing much faster than others?

**Model Answer:** The key factors are the size of the speaker community, the economic status of the language, and the political context in which it is spoken. Languages with very small speaker populations are inherently more vulnerable, since the loss of a few key intergenerational transmitters can be catastrophic. Economic incentives are enormously powerful: if switching to a dominant language dramatically improves a family's educational and employment prospects, parents often make that choice pragmatically, and within a generation the heritage language may no longer be passed on at home. Political history also matters greatly — communities whose languages were suppressed or stigmatised by colonial or authoritarian governments often internalised negative attitudes towards their own languages that are very difficult to reverse.

---

**Question 9.** What can be done to support communities trying to revitalise an endangered language?

**Model Answer:** Several things, and the evidence from successful cases like Welsh and Māori suggests that the most important are creating genuine domains of use — contexts where the language is not merely studied but actively lived in. Welsh-medium schools, where children are educated entirely in Welsh, have been critical in producing a new generation of fluent speakers. Broadcasting — television and radio programmes in the language — provides both models of contemporary usage and a signal that the language is a living medium of modern life, not a museum artefact. Legal recognition and official status, where the state uses the language in government and public services, matters too. Financial support for language documentation, literature, and artistic production helps create the cultural infrastructure without which revitalisation efforts tend to stall.

---

**Question 10.** Do you think the world would lose something important if a large number of languages became extinct over the next century?

**Model Answer:** Absolutely, and the loss would be both practical and profound. On a practical level, many languages encode knowledge about ecosystems, medicinal plants, navigation, and weather patterns that has been accumulated over millennia and has no exact equivalent in written scientific literature. When a language disappears, that knowledge becomes far more difficult to access and use. On a deeper level, each language represents a distinct way of perceiving and articulating human experience — its loss narrows the range of concepts and perspectives available to our species as a whole. I find it telling that linguists often compare the extinction of a language to the destruction of a library, because in many ways that is exactly what it is: an irreversible loss of accumulated human intelligence that future generations will never have the opportunity to benefit from.
