## PASSAGE 1: The Benefits of Bilingual Education

*(Khoảng 500 từ — Độ khó: Dễ)*

In recent decades, bilingual education has gained considerable attention from educators, policymakers, and parents alike. This approach to schooling, which involves teaching students in two languages simultaneously, has been shown to offer a range of academic, cognitive, and social advantages that extend well beyond simple language acquisition.

One of the most frequently cited benefits of bilingual education is its positive effect on cognitive development. Research conducted at universities in Canada and Spain has consistently demonstrated that children who learn in two languages develop stronger executive functions — the mental skills that help with planning, attention, and problem-solving. This so-called "bilingual advantage" appears to arise from the constant mental exercise of switching between two linguistic systems, which strengthens the brain's ability to focus and filter out irrelevant information.

Academically, bilingual students often outperform their monolingual peers in standardised tests, particularly in reading comprehension and mathematical reasoning. A landmark study published in the Journal of Educational Psychology found that bilingual students scored an average of 15% higher in reading assessments by the age of ten compared to students educated in a single language. Educators attribute this advantage to the heightened metalinguistic awareness that bilingual learners develop — an understanding of how language itself works, which transfers across subjects.

Socially, bilingual education prepares children for an increasingly globalised world. Students who are comfortable in two languages typically show greater cultural empathy and adaptability. In multicultural societies, schools that offer instruction in both the majority language and a community or heritage language have been found to reduce social divisions and foster a stronger sense of inclusion among minority groups. Parents from immigrant communities frequently report that bilingual programmes help their children maintain strong connections to their cultural roots while also integrating successfully into the mainstream society.

Critics of bilingual education sometimes argue that dividing instructional time between two languages may slow the development of proficiency in either tongue. However, longitudinal research suggests that any initial lag in vocabulary development is temporary. By the time bilingual students reach secondary school, they typically demonstrate native-level fluency in both languages, and the cognitive gains they have accumulated far outweigh the early disadvantages.

Another concern raised by opponents is the cost of implementing bilingual programmes, which require specially trained teachers, bilingual materials, and administrative oversight. While these expenses are real, proponents counter that the long-term economic benefits — including higher earning potential for bilingual graduates and reduced costs associated with language barriers in healthcare and legal settings — justify the initial investment.

In conclusion, the evidence in favour of bilingual education is compelling. Whether viewed through a cognitive, academic, or social lens, teaching children in two languages appears to equip them with skills and perspectives that serve them throughout their lives.

## PASSAGE 2: Online Learning: Revolution or Illusion?

*(Khoảng 650 từ — Độ khó: Trung bình)*

**A.** The rise of online learning platforms has fundamentally altered the landscape of education. With millions of courses now available at the click of a button, enthusiasts argue that digital education is democratising knowledge on an unprecedented scale. Yet beneath the optimism lies a more complicated reality — one in which access, quality, and outcomes vary enormously.

**B.** Online learning first gained significant traction in the early 2000s with the emergence of learning management systems used by universities to supplement traditional lectures. The real turning point, however, came with the launch of Massive Open Online Courses, or MOOCs, around 2012. Platforms such as Coursera, edX, and Udemy began offering courses from elite institutions to anyone with an internet connection, promising to disrupt the traditional model of higher education.

**C.** Proponents of online learning celebrate its flexibility. Students can study at their own pace, fitting education around employment and family commitments. This is particularly valuable for adult learners who cannot attend full-time campus programmes. Distance education has also removed geographical barriers, enabling individuals in remote or underserved regions to access courses previously available only to those in major cities or wealthy countries.

**D.** However, research on the effectiveness of online learning presents a more nuanced picture. Studies conducted by educational psychologists at Stanford and MIT found that while students enrolled in online courses report high initial satisfaction, completion rates are alarmingly low — frequently below 10% for MOOCs. The absence of face-to-face interaction, peer pressure, and structured scheduling appears to undermine the motivation that many learners need to persist through difficult material.

**E.** The digital divide further complicates the egalitarian promise of online education. While proponents claim that the internet levels the playing field, reliable high-speed access remains unavailable to large populations in developing nations and even in rural areas of wealthy countries. Furthermore, meaningful participation in online learning requires not just connectivity but also digital literacy — a skill set that is unevenly distributed across age groups and socioeconomic backgrounds.

**F.** Quality assurance is another significant challenge. The barrier to creating and publishing online courses is low, resulting in a marketplace flooded with content of wildly varying quality. Without the rigorous peer review and institutional accreditation that govern traditional academia, learners may struggle to distinguish high-quality instruction from superficial or even misleading material. Employers, too, have been slow to recognise online credentials with the same respect accorded to traditional degrees from established universities.

**G.** Despite these limitations, online learning has carved out an important role in continuing professional development and lifelong learning. Working professionals regularly use online platforms to update technical skills, particularly in fast-changing fields such as data science, software development, and digital marketing. In these contexts, the flexible, modular format of online courses aligns well with the needs of learners who already possess strong self-discipline and a clear sense of purpose.

## PASSAGE 3: The Purpose of Higher Education: Skills or Knowledge?

*(Khoảng 750 từ — Độ khó: Khó)*

For much of the twentieth century, the dominant philosophy underpinning higher education was rooted in the classical tradition: the purpose of a university was to cultivate the intellect, to expose students to great bodies of knowledge, and to produce well-rounded citizens capable of critical thought. Today, that vision is being vigorously contested. As tuition fees rise, student debt accumulates, and labour markets grow more competitive, a powerful chorus of voices argues that universities must justify their existence by delivering measurable vocational outcomes.

The instrumentalist case for skills-focused education is not without merit. Graduates entering a workforce characterised by rapid technological change require concrete competencies — data analysis, coding, project management, communication — that traditional humanities programmes have not always prioritised. Employers frequently complain that graduates are intellectually sophisticated but lack the practical readiness to contribute immediately to an organisation. A survey conducted by the Confederation of British Industry found that over 60% of employers believed universities were not adequately preparing graduates for the modern workplace.

Yet the critics of vocational narrowing raise equally compelling objections. They argue that reducing higher education to a training programme fundamentally misunderstands what universities are for. Knowledge pursued for its own sake — whether in philosophy, history, pure mathematics, or literature — develops mental capacities that are ultimately more adaptable than any specific technical skill. The ability to analyse complex problems, construct coherent arguments, and synthesise information from diverse sources is precisely what distinguishes a university-educated individual from someone who has undergone narrow technical instruction.

Empirical evidence on this question is mixed. Studies tracking the career trajectories of graduates over several decades suggest that those with broadly academic backgrounds — including humanities and social sciences — often achieve comparable long-term earnings and career advancement to those who pursued ostensibly more vocational degrees. The difference tends to manifest in the short term: STEM and professional graduates typically enter employment more quickly and at higher starting salaries, while humanities graduates may take longer to establish themselves but show resilience and upward mobility over time.

The tension between skills and knowledge also reflects deeper questions about who bears the cost of higher education. When universities are primarily state-funded, there is a strong argument for prioritising the public good — educating citizens with broad horizons and civic sensibility. When students themselves shoulder significant financial burdens through tuition fees and loans, their demand for demonstrable return on investment is understandable, if not always well-served by a strictly vocational curriculum.

A growing number of educators and institutions have begun to advocate for a synthesis — what some call the "liberal-professional" model — that integrates rigorous academic content with practical experience. Internships, project-based learning, interdisciplinary programmes, and industry partnerships are increasingly embedded in degree structures previously regarded as purely theoretical. This hybrid approach aims to preserve the intellectual breadth valued by traditionalists while addressing the legitimate employability concerns of students and employers alike.

Nevertheless, fundamental disagreements persist. Those who hold a humanistic view of education worry that market pressures will steadily erode the space for scholarship that has no immediate commercial application, impoverishing intellectual life and ultimately society. Conversely, vocational advocates contend that an educated elite pursuing knowledge for its own sake — financed by public or private debt — represents an indulgence that modern economies can ill afford.

What is clear is that higher education faces a genuinely difficult challenge: to remain faithful to its deepest traditions while also evolving to meet the demands of a world that has changed profoundly since those traditions were established. Whether that challenge is best met by choosing between skills and knowledge, or by seeking a more ambitious integration of both, remains one of the most consequential debates in contemporary education policy.
