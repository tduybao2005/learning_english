### Biodiversity Loss and the Sixth Mass Extinction

**A** Earth is experiencing what biologists now widely describe as the sixth mass extinction — the most rapid disappearance of species since the asteroid impact that eliminated the non-avian dinosaurs approximately 66 million years ago. Under natural conditions, the background extinction rate is estimated at between one and five species per year across the entire planet. Today, however, current rates of species loss are estimated at 100 to 1,000 times above this background level, an acceleration that has prompted the United Nations to place biodiversity loss alongside climate change as one of the defining crises of our era.

**B** Scientists have identified five principal drivers of this unprecedented decline, often summarised under the acronym HIPPO: Habitat destruction, Invasive species, Pollution, Population (meaning overexploitation of wildlife), and climate change. Of these, habitat destruction remains the most pervasive threat, responsible for the loss or severe degradation of natural ecosystems on every continent. The conversion of forests, wetlands, and grasslands to farmland, urban infrastructure, and industrial sites has eliminated or fragmented the habitats upon which vast numbers of species depend. Invasive organisms — those introduced beyond their native range — represent a second formidable threat, frequently outcompeting native wildlife for food and territory or introducing novel pathogens to which indigenous species have no immunity.

**C** To measure and communicate the scale of this crisis, the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) maintains the Red List of Threatened Species, the world's most comprehensive inventory of biodiversity health. The Red List assigns species to one of seven standardised categories: Least Concern, Near Threatened, Vulnerable, Endangered, Critically Endangered, Extinct in the Wild, and Extinct. The most recent assessments indicate that more than 44,000 of the approximately 157,000 species evaluated are classified as threatened with extinction — more than 28 per cent of all those assessed.

**D** Central to understanding why species loss is so ecologically damaging is the concept of keystone species — organisms whose presence in an ecosystem exerts a disproportionately large influence on ecological structure relative to their numerical abundance. Sea otters, for instance, feed voraciously on sea urchins; when otter populations collapse, sea urchin numbers explode and the urchins devastate the kelp forests that provide habitat for hundreds of other species. Similarly, the reintroduction of wolves to Yellowstone National Park in the United States triggered a trophic cascade: wolf predation reduced elk numbers and altered elk grazing behaviour, allowing riverside vegetation to recover and ultimately changing the course and hydrology of rivers.

**E** The loss of species carries profound consequences for human welfare through the degradation of ecosystem services — the benefits that healthy natural systems provide at no direct cost to society. These include the pollination of food crops by insects, the purification of freshwater by wetlands, the regulation of local climate by forests, and the sequestration of carbon dioxide by soils and ocean ecosystems. Estimates of the annual economic value of global ecosystem services exceed $125 trillion, underscoring that biodiversity is not merely an aesthetic or ethical concern but a critical foundation of economic life.

**F** In response to the accelerating crisis, conservation organisations and governments have invested in rewilding — the large-scale restoration of natural ecosystems through the reintroduction of ecologically significant species. Europe's Rewilding Network, which now operates across several European countries, has pursued projects to restore European bison, lynx, and Eurasian beaver to landscapes from which they had been absent for centuries. While such rewilding initiatives show considerable promise in recovering ecological function and resilience, scientists emphasise that they cannot substitute for the primary prevention of habitat loss.

### Nuclear Energy: Risks and Potential

**A** Nuclear power is generated through the process of fission — the splitting of heavy atomic nuclei, typically uranium-235, to release large quantities of energy in the form of heat. Inside a reactor, neutrons strike uranium fuel rods, triggering a controlled chain reaction in which each splitting event releases additional neutrons that sustain further fissions. Control rods, made from neutron-absorbing materials such as boron or cadmium, are raised or lowered to regulate the reaction's intensity. The heat produced converts water into high-pressure steam, which drives turbines connected to electrical generators, producing electricity in the same fundamental way as coal-fired or gas-fired power stations.

**B** One of nuclear energy's most significant operational advantages is its high capacity factor — the ratio of actual electricity output to the maximum possible output over a given period. Modern nuclear power stations routinely achieve capacity factors of 90 per cent or more, enabling them to generate electricity almost continuously for months at a time. Solar and wind installations, by contrast, typically achieve capacity factors of around 25 per cent, since their output depends on the availability of sunlight or wind. This reliability makes nuclear a valuable source of baseload power — the continuous minimum level of electricity supply that grids must always maintain regardless of fluctuations in demand.

**C** Despite its technical credentials, nuclear energy has been marked by serious accidents that have shaped public attitudes and policy decisions for decades. The Three Mile Island accident in the United States in 1979 resulted in a partial core meltdown, though it caused no confirmed radiation casualties. The Chernobyl disaster in Ukraine in 1986 was far more severe: an unstable reactor design and serious operator errors during a safety test caused a catastrophic explosion and fire that spread radioactive contamination across much of Europe. The Fukushima Daiichi accident in Japan in 2011 was triggered by a massive tsunami that disabled the plant's cooling systems, causing three reactor cores to melt down. All three accidents substantially reduced public confidence in nuclear energy, and several countries, including Germany, subsequently committed to phasing out their nuclear programmes entirely.

**D** Perhaps the most intractable challenge associated with nuclear energy is the management of radioactive waste. The fission process produces highly radioactive by-products — including isotopes such as caesium-137 and plutonium-239 — that must be isolated from the environment for extraordinarily long periods. The half-life of plutonium-239, for example, is approximately 24,000 years. Current proposals for long-term storage focus on deep geological repositories in stable rock formations, but no country has yet opened a permanent facility for high-level waste, and the political and technical obstacles to doing so remain formidable.

**E** In response, several nations have invested in Generation IV reactor designs — advanced concepts intended to improve upon existing technology across the dimensions of safety, efficiency, and waste management. These reactors are designed to operate on a broader range of fuel types, including spent fuel from current plants, and to produce substantially smaller quantities of long-lived radioactive waste. Some designs incorporate passive safety features that cause the reactor to shut down automatically without operator intervention or powered safety systems, addressing one of the principal criticisms directed at older reactor generations.

**F** An even more ambitious prospect is nuclear fusion — the joining of light atomic nuclei rather than the splitting of heavy ones. Fusion is the process that powers the sun and uses hydrogen isotopes known as deuterium and tritium as fuel. These materials are abundant, and fusion produces far less long-lived radioactive waste than fission. The ITER project, a collaboration involving 35 nations currently under construction in southern France, aims to demonstrate that a fusion reaction can release more energy than is required to initiate it. ITER is a research facility rather than a power station, however, and commercial fusion electricity remains a longer-term aspiration rather than an imminent prospect.

**G** Notwithstanding the challenges of safety, waste management, and construction cost, many climate scientists and energy analysts argue that nuclear power must play a meaningful role in the global shift to low-carbon electricity. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change has identified it as one of a portfolio of technologies — alongside renewables, carbon capture, and energy efficiency — that will likely be needed to limit global warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius. Nuclear's high capacity factor, very low lifecycle carbon emissions of around 12 grams of CO₂ per kilowatt-hour, and ability to deliver stable dispatchable power make it a potentially valuable complement to variable renewable sources such as solar and wind.

### The Origins and Evolution of Language

**A** Among the most distinctive features of the human species is language — a system of communication so elaborate and generative that no other animal has evolved anything remotely comparable. One of the foundational questions in linguistics is whether this capacity is primarily a cultural achievement, transmitted from one generation to the next through exposure and socialisation, or whether it reflects an innate biological endowment present in all humans from birth. The linguist Noam Chomsky advanced the latter view with his influential theory of universal grammar, proposing that all humans are born with a set of innate grammatical principles that constrain the possible form of any natural language and explain why children acquire language with extraordinary speed and uniformity.

**B** Chomsky's nativist account rests partly on the observation that children achieve grammatical competence far more rapidly than could be explained by the linguistic input they receive — a phenomenon he termed the 'poverty of the stimulus'. Even children exposed to incomplete or inconsistent language reliably develop grammatically sophisticated speech. Furthermore, despite their surface diversity, all known languages share deep structural properties — including a subject-predicate distinction and the capacity for recursive embedding — which Chomsky attributed to a shared biological inheritance. Critics of the nativist position, however, argue that these apparent universals can be explained by general cognitive capacities and the practical communicative demands that all human beings share, rather than by a language-specific biological module.

**C** A related puzzle concerns the evolutionary origins of language itself. Because language leaves no direct fossil record, scholars must reconstruct its emergence from indirect evidence, including comparative anatomy, archaeology, and the study of primate communication. Several competing proto-language theories have been proposed. The 'gestural' hypothesis suggests that language evolved initially from manual and facial gestures before shifting to the vocal modality. The 'musical' hypothesis proposes that language precursors were melodic social vocalisations serving to cement group bonds. The 'cognitive' hypothesis ties the emergence of language to advances in tool manufacture and social intelligence that occurred in early Homo sapiens. Each account has its advocates, and the debate shows no sign of imminent resolution.

**D** Sign languages provide some of the most compelling evidence for the biological basis of human language capacity. Contrary to early assumptions that sign languages were rudimentary or incomplete systems, research since the 1960s has demonstrated that natural sign languages — including American Sign Language, British Sign Language, and Israeli Sign Language — possess the same structural complexity as spoken languages, with fully developed phonology (based on hand shapes, locations, and movements), morphology, and syntax. Perhaps most striking is the case of Nicaraguan Sign Language, which emerged spontaneously among deaf children in Nicaraguan schools during the 1970s and 1980s, developing over successive cohorts of learners into a fully complex grammatical system with no external linguistic model. This suggests that the human capacity for language is so deeply embedded in neurology that it will emerge naturally wherever the social conditions for its development are present.

**E** Despite the richness of human linguistic diversity, a profound threat now hangs over much of the world's linguistic heritage. Linguists estimate that of the approximately 7,000 languages currently spoken worldwide, roughly half may be extinct by the end of the twenty-first century. The rate of loss is alarming: one language is estimated to die every 14 days, as the final fluent speakers of minority languages age and pass away without replacement. The principal causes are the economic and social pressures that lead communities to abandon heritage languages in favour of dominant national or international languages that offer superior educational and employment opportunities. When a language disappears, it takes with it an irreplaceable body of ecological knowledge, oral literature, and ways of perceiving the world that no other language can fully replicate.

**F** In response to this crisis, linguists, governments, and communities have pursued language revitalisation — deliberate programmes of documentation, education, and institutional promotion aimed at reversing decline or restoring near-extinct languages to everyday use. Welsh provides one of the more encouraging examples in Europe: sustained government investment in Welsh-medium education, broadcasting in Welsh, and the language's official status alongside English has reversed a long decline, and Welsh now has more than 800,000 speakers, with the highest rates of use found among younger age groups. Māori in New Zealand has similarly benefited from a network of immersion schools known as kura kaupapa Māori and the establishment of a dedicated Māori television channel, contributing to a significant increase in the number of active young speakers. Critics note, however, that these success stories remain the exception rather than the rule, and that most endangered languages continue to decline despite revitalisation efforts.

**G** Perhaps the most philosophically intriguing question in the study of language is whether it shapes thought itself. The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis — named after the anthropologist Edward Sapir and his student Benjamin Lee Whorf — proposes that the language a person speaks influences, or even determines, the way they perceive and categorise reality. Whorf's analysis of the Hopi language led him to argue that Hopi speakers conceptualised time fundamentally differently from English speakers. Strong versions of the hypothesis — which hold that language determines thought — are now largely rejected by contemporary linguists as excessively deterministic. Weaker versions, however, which propose only that language influences rather than determines perception, continue to attract experimental support: studies have found, for instance, that speakers of languages with a richer vocabulary for colour distinctions are faster at discriminating between closely related colours than speakers whose languages draw fewer distinctions.
