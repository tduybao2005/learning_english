## TASK 1

**Đề bài:**

*The bar chart below compares the average years of schooling in six countries in 1990 and 2020. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.*

**Dữ liệu biểu đồ (mô phỏng):**

| Quốc gia / Country | 1990 (years) | 2020 (years) |
|---|---|---|
| Japan | 10.5 | 13.4 |
| Germany | 10.2 | 13.1 |
| Brazil | 5.8 | 8.9 |
| India | 4.1 | 6.7 |
| Nigeria | 3.2 | 5.3 |
| South Korea | 9.8 | 13.9 |

---

**Bài mẫu (Band 7–8, khoảng 175 từ):**

The bar chart illustrates the average number of years spent in schooling across six countries in 1990 and 2020, revealing a universal upward trend over the thirty-year period.

South Korea recorded the highest figure in 2020 at 13.9 years, a significant rise from 9.8 years in 1990. Japan and Germany followed closely, both reaching approximately 13 years by 2020, compared to just over 10 years three decades earlier.

Among developing nations, Brazil demonstrated the most notable improvement, increasing from 5.8 to 8.9 years. India also made substantial progress, climbing from 4.1 to 6.7 years, while Nigeria, despite remaining the lowest-ranking country overall, grew from 3.2 to 5.3 years — an increase of over two years.

In terms of relative change, all six countries showed meaningful growth, though the gap between the highest and lowest performers persisted. South Korea, Japan, and Germany consistently outperformed the remaining three countries, and the absolute difference between the highest and lowest values narrowed only marginally between 1990 and 2020.

Overall, global educational attainment improved considerably, with East Asian nations leading the way.

## TASK 2

**Đề bài:**

*"Universities should focus on giving students the practical skills needed for employment rather than providing access to knowledge for its own sake. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"*

---

**Bài mẫu (Band 7–8, khoảng 280 từ):**

The role of universities in modern society has become a matter of intense debate. While some argue that higher education must prioritise employability and practical competence, I believe that a purely vocational focus would impoverish academic life and that a balanced approach is far more beneficial.

Those who advocate for skills-focused universities point to the realities of a competitive labour market. Graduates today face rapidly evolving industries where technical proficiency in areas such as data analysis, digital communication, and project management is often essential. Employers frequently report that university leavers lack workplace readiness, suggesting that academic institutions have drifted too far from practical concerns.

However, reducing universities to training centres would be a profound mistake. The pursuit of knowledge for its own sake — in philosophy, history, literature, and pure science — cultivates critical thinking, intellectual flexibility, and the capacity to analyse complex problems. These qualities are arguably more durable and transferable than any specific technical skill, which may become obsolete as technology evolves. Furthermore, a society populated by narrowly trained specialists, without citizens capable of broad ethical reasoning and civic engagement, risks losing the very foundations of democratic life.

The strongest position, in my view, lies in integration rather than opposition. Leading universities already embed internships, project-based learning, and industry collaboration into rigorous academic programmes. This "liberal-professional" model demonstrates that employability and intellectual depth are not mutually exclusive but mutually reinforcing.

In conclusion, while practical skills are undeniably valuable, universities that abandon knowledge for its own sake would betray their most important function. The goal should be graduates who are both intellectually accomplished and professionally capable — not one at the expense of the other.
