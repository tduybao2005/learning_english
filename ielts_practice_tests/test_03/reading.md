## READING PASSAGE 1

**"The Science of Physical Exercise and Human Health"**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 1–13, which are based on Reading Passage 1.*

---

**Paragraph A**

Physical exercise is among the most comprehensively studied and robustly validated health interventions available to individuals and public health systems worldwide. The World Health Organization (WHO) recommends that adults engage in at least 150 to 300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week, or an equivalent combination of moderate and vigorous activity. Despite the clarity of this guidance and the overwhelming body of evidence supporting it, global statistics reveal a persistent failure to translate this knowledge into behaviour: one in four adults worldwide fails to meet minimum activity guidelines. Sedentary behaviour — characterised by prolonged periods of physical inactivity — has now been classified as a leading cause of premature death, contributing to the global burden of chronic disease in a manner that public health authorities describe as a worldwide epidemic.

**Paragraph B**

Aerobic exercise — activities that elevate heart rate and increase oxygen consumption, such as running, cycling, swimming, and brisk walking — provides well-documented cardiovascular benefits. Regular aerobic exercise reduces resting heart rate, lowers blood pressure, and improves the heart's mechanical efficiency at pumping blood through the circulatory system. Studies consistently associate regular aerobic activity with a 35% reduction in the risk of heart disease and a 30–40% reduction in the risk of stroke. One key measure of cardiovascular fitness frequently used by exercise scientists is VO2 max — the maximum amount of oxygen the body can use during intense exercise — which declines naturally with age but can be significantly improved and maintained through consistent aerobic training, even among older adults.

**Paragraph C**

Resistance training — exercises that work muscles against external loads, such as weight training, resistance bands, or bodyweight exercises such as press-ups and squats — offers distinct physiological benefits that complement those of aerobic activity. Regular resistance training increases muscle mass and strength, improves bone density (thereby reducing the risk of osteoporosis and fractures in later life), and elevates the basal metabolic rate, meaning the body burns more calories at rest even when not exercising. This last benefit is particularly significant given the global prevalence of obesity and metabolic disease. Research indicates that muscle mass declines at approximately 3–5% per decade after the age of 30 in sedentary adults, a degenerative process — known as sarcopaenia — that consistent resistance training can substantially slow and in some cases partially reverse.

**Paragraph D**

Physical exercise also profoundly affects mental health through several intersecting neurobiological mechanisms. Exercise triggers the release of endorphins — neurochemicals produced in the brain and nervous system that produce feelings of wellbeing and reduce the perception of pain. More significantly from a neurological perspective, aerobic exercise promotes neuroplasticity: the brain's fundamental ability to form, reorganise, and strengthen neural connections in response to experience and activity. Exercise additionally stimulates neurogenesis — the formation of new neurons — particularly in the hippocampus, the brain region central to memory formation and learning. Multiple large-scale clinical trials have found that regular exercise is as effective as antidepressant medication in treating mild to moderate depression, with the considerable additional benefit of producing no pharmaceutical side effects and contributing simultaneously to physical health.

**Paragraph E**

Despite the range and scale of these documented benefits, physical inactivity remains a formidable global public health challenge. The WHO estimates that physical inactivity costs the global economy approximately $54 billion annually in direct healthcare expenditure and a further $14 billion in lost productivity resulting from illness and premature death. A significant proportion of this inactivity is not simply a matter of individual motivation or choice: structural and environmental factors — including long working hours, car-dependent urban planning, the nature of desk-based employment, and the proliferation of sedentary screen-based entertainment — create conditions in which physical activity requires deliberate effort rather than emerging naturally from daily life. Accordingly, simply advising individuals to "exercise more" has proven largely ineffective as a standalone public health intervention; evidence indicates that structural and environmental changes to built environments and working conditions produce more durable and equitable increases in population activity levels than individually targeted behavioural campaigns.

**Paragraph F**

Barriers to exercise are not uniformly distributed across society, and it is important for public health policy to recognise the structural nature of inactivity. Cost of gym memberships, access to safe outdoor environments, time pressure arising from multiple employment or caregiving responsibilities, and physical disability all disproportionately affect lower-income populations, the elderly, and those living in car-dependent rural or suburban areas with limited public infrastructure. Public health interventions that have demonstrated meaningful success include subsidised community sport and recreation programmes, investment in cycling lanes and pedestrian infrastructure, employer wellness initiatives that incorporate movement breaks and flexible hours, and active transport policies that incentivise walking and cycling for short journeys. Wearable technology — including smartwatches and fitness trackers — has emerged as an additional tool in supporting behaviour change, with evidence suggesting that step-count monitoring and the gamification of physical activity can help sustain behavioural change over time, though this approach appears primarily effective among individuals who are already motivated to be more active.

## READING PASSAGE 2

**"The Printing Press and the Knowledge Revolution"**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 14–26, which are based on Reading Passage 2.*

---

**Paragraph A**

The invention of moveable type printing by Johannes Gutenberg around 1440 in Mainz, Germany, is widely regarded as one of the most consequential technological developments in human history — a shift that fundamentally altered the relationship between knowledge, power, and society. Gutenberg's press, which adapted the screw-press technology familiar from winemaking and olive oil production to the printing of text on paper, was not, strictly speaking, a wholly original invention. Block printing and moveable type had been practised in China and Korea for several centuries prior to Gutenberg's work, and elements of his innovation drew on techniques already in use across Europe for printing on fabric and for the production of playing cards. What Gutenberg achieved, and what distinguishes his contribution, was the development of a mechanically efficient, commercially viable, and reproducible system for producing printed books in European languages at a scale and speed previously unimaginable — enabling an unprecedented acceleration in the production, distribution, and, ultimately, the democratic accessibility of written material.

**Paragraph B**

The contrast between the printing press and the preceding manuscript tradition was dramatic in almost every dimension. Before Gutenberg's invention, books were produced by hand — a process so extraordinarily laborious that a single manuscript could take a trained scribe months or even years to complete, and was consequently available only to the wealthiest institutions such as monasteries, royal courts, and universities, and to the most affluent individuals. Gutenberg's press, by contrast, could produce approximately 3,600 pages per working day. Within fifty years of its introduction, printing presses had been established in more than 270 European cities, producing an estimated 20 million books — a figure that exceeded the total number of books that had existed in all of Europe's libraries and collections combined in the period immediately before the press's invention. The transformation in the availability of texts was, by any measure, revolutionary.

**Paragraph C**

One profound but frequently overlooked consequence of the printing press was its significant role in standardising written language across Europe. Manuscripts had varied enormously in their spelling, grammar, punctuation, and vocabulary depending on the region, time period, and individual copyist who had produced them — variations that reflected both the diversity of European vernacular languages and the absence of any centralised mechanism for enforcing consistency. Printers, working with fixed moveable type and producing thousands of identical copies of a text for wide distribution, were compelled to make consistent and deliberate choices about spelling and grammar before committing text to the press. This process, applied across the continent's growing print trade, effectively helped to establish and stabilise standard written forms of vernacular languages. Ironically, these developments contributed significantly to the eventual displacement of Latin as the dominant written language of educated Europeans: printers found it substantially more commercially viable to produce texts in the spoken languages of their paying customers than in a classical language accessible only to a scholarly elite.

**Paragraph D**

The printing press played a pivotal role in the Protestant Reformation, one of the most consequential political and religious upheavals in European history. In 1517, the German theologian Martin Luther is traditionally said to have posted his 95 Theses — a list of detailed grievances against the theological and financial practices of the Catholic Church — on the door of the Castle Church in Wittenberg. What might under earlier technological conditions have remained a relatively localised theological dispute was, within weeks, amplified by the printing press into a continent-wide controversy. Printed copies of Luther's theses spread rapidly across Germany and into neighbouring countries, reaching audiences that no manuscript tradition could have served with comparable speed or reach. The practical impossibility for Church authorities to intercept and suppress the circulation of Lutheran texts printed at hundreds of locations across multiple jurisdictions was a decisive factor in the Reformation's momentum. Luther himself recognised and celebrated the role of print in this transformation, writing that printing was "God's highest and extremest act of grace, whereby the business of the Gospel is driven forward."

**Paragraph E**

The scientific revolution of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries was similarly facilitated and accelerated by the printing press and the culture of shared, reproducible knowledge that it made possible. The great figures of the revolution — Copernicus, Galileo, Vesalius, and Newton — could disseminate their discoveries and arguments to an international audience of scholars across Europe without relying on the slow, error-prone, and unreliable chain of handwritten correspondence and personal contact. The publication of Isaac Newton's Principia Mathematica in 1687 — a single printed work that could be simultaneously distributed to scholars in London, Paris, Amsterdam, and beyond — accelerated the consolidation and acceptance of the new scientific method in ways that would have been practically impossible within a manuscript culture.

**Paragraph F**

The printing press also provoked concerted efforts at censorship and information control from the moment its power to shape public opinion became apparent. Both secular and ecclesiastical authorities recognised with considerable alarm that the free circulation of printed material fundamentally threatened their ability to control information and manage the beliefs of their subjects and congregations. The Catholic Church's response included the establishment of the Index Librorum Prohibitorum — the List of Prohibited Books — in 1559, a formally maintained catalogue of works that Catholics were forbidden to read or own. Secular rulers throughout Europe established licensing regimes requiring official approval before any work could be printed and distributed. Despite the seriousness of these measures and the genuine penalties imposed for violations, the practical impossibility of monitoring and controlling the output of hundreds of independent printing establishments scattered across numerous competing political jurisdictions meant that such censorship was rarely, if ever, fully effective.

**Paragraph G**

Historians and cultural commentators have frequently drawn parallels between the social and intellectual impact of the printing press and that of the internet, identifying both as revolutionary communication technologies that dramatically democratised access to information and simultaneously destabilised existing structures of intellectual and institutional authority. Both the press and the internet enabled previously marginalised or excluded voices to reach large audiences at relatively low cost; both provoked intense anxiety among established institutions — political, religious, and cultural — about the loss of control over information. Whether the internet will, in the long run, produce transformations as profound, as durable, and as comprehensively world-altering as those generated by Gutenberg's press remains, of course, a question whose answer lies entirely in the future.

## READING PASSAGE 3

**"Artificial Intelligence in Healthcare"**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 27–40, which are based on Reading Passage 3.*

---

**Paragraph A**

Artificial intelligence is transforming healthcare at a pace and with a breadth of application that many practitioners, researchers, and policymakers consider simultaneously exciting and alarming. From the analysis of diagnostic medical imaging to the acceleration of drug discovery, the personalisation of treatment protocols, and the automation of administrative tasks, AI systems are demonstrating capabilities that in specific, well-defined tasks frequently exceed those of experienced human clinicians. Yet the technology also raises profound and as yet unresolved questions about clinical accountability, algorithmic bias, data privacy, and the fundamental nature of the doctor-patient relationship — questions whose answers will shape the trajectory of AI adoption in medicine for decades to come.

**Paragraph B**

One of the most technically mature and clinically deployed applications of AI in healthcare is diagnostic imaging analysis. Deep learning algorithms, trained on millions of labelled medical images, can detect patterns associated with cancer, cardiovascular disease, and neurological conditions with a speed and consistency that individual human radiologists are unable to match, particularly when operating under the time pressures typical of busy clinical environments. In a landmark 2019 study published in the journal Nature Medicine, researchers reported that a deep learning model outperformed the average radiologist in detecting breast cancer from mammography images, achieving both fewer false positives and fewer false negatives than the human comparators. Similar advances have since been reported in the automated detection of diabetic retinopathy from retinal scans, skin cancer from photographic images, and certain forms of lung cancer from CT imaging data.

**Paragraph C**

The application of AI to the process of drug discovery represents perhaps the most potentially transformative development in the entire field of AI-enabled medicine. The traditional process of identifying a promising drug compound and bringing it from initial discovery through preclinical testing, clinical trials, and regulatory approval to clinical availability typically takes ten to fifteen years and costs, on average, over $2 billion. AI systems can screen millions of molecular compounds through computational simulation — a process known as in silico screening — identifying candidates for further testing at a small fraction of the time and cost associated with traditional physical screening methods. The most celebrated recent example of AI's impact on this process is AlphaFold, a system developed by Google DeepMind, which in 2020 provided highly accurate predictions of protein three-dimensional structures. The prediction of protein folding — the process by which a protein's sequence of amino acids determines its final three-dimensional shape — had confounded biochemists for more than five decades and is fundamental to understanding how diseases operate at a molecular level and how drugs can be designed to interact with specific biological targets.

**Paragraph D**

Personalised medicine — the tailoring of medical treatments to the specific characteristics of individual patients based on their genetic profile, lifestyle factors, medical history, and other personal data — represents another major frontier for the application of AI. By analysing large and complex datasets that combine genetic information, electronic health records, diagnostic imaging data, and historical treatment outcomes, AI systems can identify patterns and correlations that would be invisible to human analysis at the same scale and speed, thereby guiding more precise and potentially more effective treatment decisions. In oncology, AI-assisted tumour profiling and genetic analysis has already begun to influence the selection of chemotherapy protocols in clinical settings, in some cases improving treatment outcomes for specific patient subgroups by more precisely matching therapeutic approaches to the genetic characteristics of individual tumours.

**Paragraph E**

Surgical robotics represents a more established — if more technically narrow — application of AI and automation within clinical settings. Systems such as the da Vinci Surgical System employ robotic arms that can be controlled either directly by surgeons working at a remote console or, in emerging implementations under active research, partially guided by AI systems to assist with specific aspects of a procedure. The claimed benefits of robotic-assisted surgery include smaller and more precise incisions, reduced intraoperative blood loss, lower rates of certain complications, shorter hospital stays, and faster patient recovery. However, such systems are expensive to purchase, operate, and maintain; they require specialised surgeon training that takes considerable time to acquire; and they are currently available primarily to well-funded hospitals in wealthy countries, creating an additional dimension of global healthcare inequality.

**Paragraph F**

Alongside the considerable promise that AI holds for healthcare, a range of significant ethical challenges and practical limitations have emerged that require serious and sustained attention. Algorithmic bias is among the most pressing of these concerns: AI systems trained predominantly on data from particular demographic, geographic, or socioeconomic groups may perform substantially less accurately — sometimes dangerously so — when applied to patients from other backgrounds. Studies have documented meaningful disparities in the diagnostic performance of some dermatological AI tools across patients with different skin tones, raising serious concerns about the potential for AI-assisted healthcare to exacerbate, rather than reduce, the healthcare inequalities that already exist between different populations. The question of legal and clinical accountability when an AI system contributes to a diagnostic error, a delayed diagnosis, or a harmful treatment recommendation also remains largely unresolved in the legal frameworks of most jurisdictions.

**Paragraph G**

Regulatory frameworks for AI medical devices are evolving, though the pace of regulation has generally struggled to match the speed of technological development. The US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has developed specific regulatory pathways for evaluating and approving AI-based medical software, making a significant distinction between what it terms "locked" algorithms — which, once deployed, function in a fixed and identical manner on all subsequent inputs — and "adaptive" algorithms, which continue to learn from new data and may change their behaviour after initial regulatory approval and deployment. The latter category presents particular challenges for regulators: a system that was assessed and approved as safe and effective at the time of its authorisation may subsequently alter its decision-making patterns as it processes new clinical data. International regulatory harmonisation in this domain remains limited, creating significant complexity for companies seeking approval for AI healthcare products across multiple national markets simultaneously.

**Paragraph H**

Looking ahead, the majority of clinical and technological experts anticipate that AI will function primarily as a sophisticated tool to augment, complement, and extend human clinical judgement rather than to replace it across the broad range of tasks that constitute medical practice. The emerging consensus envisions AI systems handling high-volume, pattern-recognition-intensive tasks — population-level screening, continuous patient monitoring, preliminary diagnostic triage — thereby freeing clinicians to devote more time and attention to the complex decision-making, nuanced patient communication, and holistic care coordination that remains the distinctive province of human medicine. Perhaps most significantly for the long-term prospects of global health equity, AI-assisted diagnostics has the potential to bring specialist-level diagnostic capability to primary care settings in low-resource regions that currently lack access to specialist clinicians, offering a technological pathway to addressing some of the enormous disparities in healthcare quality and outcomes that currently exist between wealthy and low-income countries.
