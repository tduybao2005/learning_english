## TASK 1 (20 minutes | Minimum 150 words)

**The two pie charts below show household energy consumption by category in 2000 and 2020. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.**

---

**DATA: Household Energy Consumption**

| Category    | 2000 | 2020 |
|-------------|------|------|
| Heating     | 42%  | 35%  |
| Cooling     | 18%  | 25%  |
| Appliances  | 23%  | 20%  |
| Lighting    | 12%  |  8%  |
| Other       |  5%  | 12%  |

---

### Model Answer — Band 7.5 (approx. 175 words)

The two pie charts compare household energy use across five categories in 2000 and 2020, revealing a gradual diversification in consumption patterns over the twenty-year period.

In both years, heating accounted for the largest share of household energy. However, its proportion declined from 42% in 2000 to 35% in 2020, likely reflecting improvements in building insulation and heating technology efficiency. Conversely, energy used for cooling increased from 18% to 25%, possibly reflecting both rising ambient temperatures and the greater affordability and prevalence of air conditioning systems during this period.

Appliance use fell slightly from 23% to 20%, while lighting saw the sharpest proportional decline of all categories — from 12% to just 8% — a trend consistent with the widespread adoption of energy-efficient LED lighting technology across this period. The "Other" category was the only one to grow substantially, rising from 5% to 12%, potentially capturing increased energy demand from devices such as electric vehicles, home charging systems, or smart home technology not previously in widespread residential use.

Overall, while heating remained the dominant category in both years, energy consumption became noticeably more evenly distributed across categories by 2020.

## TASK 2 (40 minutes | Minimum 250 words)

**"Artificial intelligence will eventually replace most human jobs. Is this a positive or negative development?"**

**Write at least 250 words. Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.**

---

### Model Answer — Band 7.5 (approx. 275 words)

The prospect of artificial intelligence automating a broad range of human occupations is one of the most contested economic debates of our era. In my view, while the efficiency gains and productivity benefits brought by AI-driven automation are undeniable, the social consequences of widespread job displacement are likely to be profoundly negative unless accompanied by significant policy intervention and investment in social support systems.

The primary concern is the scale and speed of potential disruption. Unlike previous technological revolutions — industrialisation and computerisation — which displaced employment in one sector while simultaneously creating new categories of work in others, AI's capabilities span an exceptionally broad range of cognitive tasks. Jobs in fields as diverse as accounting, legal research, medical diagnosis, customer service, logistics, and even creative writing are already being partially automated. Economists Carl Benedikt Frey and Michael Osborne have estimated that up to 47% of current occupations in the United States could be susceptible to automation within the next two decades. For many workers, particularly those in lower-paid, routine, and procedural occupations, the transition may be deeply and personally damaging.

Proponents of AI automation argue that it will ultimately generate new industries and new categories of work, as every previous technological revolution has done. They point to historical evidence that automation has consistently raised living standards in the long run. This optimism may be valid over sufficient timescales, but it tends to underestimate the human cost of transition — particularly for older workers and those without the resources, flexibility, or educational access required to retrain for emerging roles.

In conclusion, AI-driven job displacement is, on balance, a negative development unless it is accompanied by robust social safety nets, sustained public investment in retraining programmes, and redistributive policies — such as shortened working weeks or broader sharing of automation productivity gains — that ensure the economic benefits of technological progress are widely shared rather than concentrated among a small elite. Technology without equitable policy is simply disruption.
