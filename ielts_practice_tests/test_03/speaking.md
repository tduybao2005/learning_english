# IELTS SPEAKING — TEST 03

**Topics: Technology & Work | Career Plans | Free Time**

**Total test time: 11–14 minutes**

---

## PART 1 (4–5 minutes)
*The examiner will ask you questions about yourself and familiar topics.*

---

### Section A: Technology & Work (4 questions)

**Q1. Do you use technology a lot at work or in your studies?**

**Model Answer:**
Yes, quite extensively. Most of what I do in my studies is done on a laptop — writing assignments, researching topics online, and attending online classes. I also use apps on my phone to organise my schedule and set reminders. I think it would be very difficult to function as a student today without a certain level of digital literacy.

---

**Q2. Has technology changed the way people work in your country over the past ten years?**

**Model Answer:**
Absolutely, I think the change has been enormous. Ten years ago, most office work was quite paper-heavy, and meetings always happened in person. Now, a lot of communication happens via messaging apps and video calls, and many people work remotely for at least part of the week. The pandemic really accelerated changes that might otherwise have taken much longer to occur.

---

**Q3. Is there any type of technology you find difficult or frustrating to use?**

**Model Answer:**
Honestly, yes — I find certain government administrative websites quite frustrating to navigate. They often seem to be designed without thinking carefully about the user experience, so simple tasks like submitting a form can take a disproportionate amount of time and effort. I also find software that constantly requires updates and restarts slightly irritating when I am in the middle of important work.

---

**Q4. Do you think young people today rely too much on technology?**

**Model Answer:**
That is a question I think about quite a lot. In some respects, yes — I notice that many people, including myself at times, find it difficult to be in a quiet environment without checking a phone. However, I think it is worth questioning whether this is necessarily a problem, or whether it simply reflects the fact that technology now enables a genuinely richer range of activities than were previously available in a moment of spare time.

---

### Section B: Career Plans (4 questions)

**Q5. What kind of career would you like to have in the future?**

**Model Answer:**
I am currently drawn to working in the technology industry — ideally in a role that involves both analytical thinking and communication with people, such as product management or data analysis. I find it motivating to work on problems that have a real impact on users' lives, and the technology sector seems to offer that combination more consistently than most other fields at this stage in its development.

---

**Q6. Did your family's background influence your career goals?**

**Model Answer:**
To some degree, yes. My parents both worked in relatively stable, traditional professions, and they have always emphasised the importance of financial security and professional credibility. That background has definitely shaped my inclination towards careers in established industries. However, I have also been influenced by reading about entrepreneurs and innovators, so I would say my ambitions are a combination of both practical security and a desire to build something meaningful.

---

**Q7. Do you think it is important to love your job, or is earning a good salary more important?**

**Model Answer:**
I think this is one of those questions where the honest answer is "both matter, and they are not always in conflict." If I had to choose, I would say that finding genuine meaning and interest in your work is probably more important for long-term wellbeing — there is a substantial body of psychological research suggesting that intrinsic motivation produces greater satisfaction than purely financial reward. But I also recognise that financial security is foundational; it is difficult to find meaning in work when you are worried about paying your bills.

---

**Q8. Have your career plans changed since you began studying?**

**Model Answer:**
Yes, considerably. When I first started my studies, I had a quite narrow sense of what I wanted to do, based largely on what I thought was expected of me. Through studying, meeting people from different backgrounds, and learning about fields I was previously unaware of, I have developed a much broader picture of what is possible and what I might find genuinely fulfilling. My current direction is quite different from what I envisioned at the start.

---

### Section C: Free Time (4 questions)

**Q9. What do you typically do in your free time?**

**Model Answer:**
My free time is fairly varied. I read quite a lot — mostly non-fiction, particularly books about history, science, or economics. I also exercise regularly, which I find essential for maintaining my energy and concentration during the study week. Occasionally I cook, which I enjoy as a way of switching off from screen-based activities and producing something tangible and enjoyable at the end of the process.

---

**Q10. Do you think people in your country have enough leisure time?**

**Model Answer:**
Honestly, I do not think so — at least not for working adults and university students. Working hours in Vietnam tend to be quite long by international comparison, and the pressure to study and demonstrate academic achievement leaves young people with relatively little unstructured time. I think this is beginning to change among younger generations, who increasingly value work-life balance, but it remains a significant cultural and structural challenge.

---

**Q11. Has your use of free time changed as you have got older?**

**Model Answer:**
Yes, quite noticeably. When I was younger, almost all of my free time was spent on video games or watching television, and I had very little interest in reading or physical activity. As I have got older, I have come to appreciate quieter, more intellectually engaging activities — reading, thinking, having meaningful conversations. I think this reflects a gradual shift in what I find genuinely restorative and stimulating, rather than simply entertaining.

---

**Q12. Is it important for people to have hobbies outside of work or study?**

**Model Answer:**
I believe it is very important, for several reasons. Hobbies provide a sense of identity and competence that is independent of professional or academic performance, which is valuable for maintaining a stable sense of self-worth. They also provide opportunities for social connection, stress reduction, and the development of skills that may not have direct professional applications but that enrich life in other ways. Research on wellbeing consistently suggests that having meaningful activities outside of work is strongly associated with life satisfaction.

## PART 2 (3–4 minutes)
*Read the task card, prepare for 1 minute, then speak for 1–2 minutes.*

---

### TASK CARD

**Describe a piece of technology you consider essential to your daily life.**

You should say:
- what it is
- how you use it in your daily routine
- how your life would be different without it
- and explain why it is so essential to you

---

### Model Answer (approx. 200 words)

The piece of technology I would describe as truly essential to my daily life is my smartphone. I am aware that this is probably not a particularly original choice, but when I actually reflect on how I use it and what it enables, the depth of its integration into my daily routine is quite striking.

In practical terms, I use it from the moment I wake up — for my alarm, to check the day's schedule, and to read any messages that arrived overnight. Throughout the day, I use it to navigate between locations using maps, to look up information while studying, to communicate with classmates and family through messaging apps, and, in the evenings, sometimes to read long-form articles or listen to podcasts.

Without it, I think my life would be substantially more effortful in logistical terms — navigating unfamiliar places, coordinating with people, and accessing information quickly would all require more time and planning. More significantly, perhaps, the smartphone connects me to a network of people and resources that makes me feel both informed and connected to a wider world.

What makes it so essential, I think, is not any single function but the way it integrates so many previously separate tools — map, dictionary, camera, calendar, telephone — into a single seamlessly accessible device.

---

**Follow-up question:** "Do you think there is any disadvantage to being so dependent on your smartphone?"

**Model short answer:**
Yes — I think the main disadvantage is that constant availability creates an expectation of immediate response that can feel quite pressure-laden. There is also some evidence that the design of social media applications in particular is deliberately engineered to be habit-forming in ways that are not always in users' best interests. I try to set boundaries around my phone use, though I am not always successful.

## PART 3 (4–5 minutes)
*The examiner will ask you further questions related to the Part 2 topic.*

---

### Theme: Automation, the Future of Work, and AI Ethics

---

**Q1. Do you think most jobs could eventually be automated by artificial intelligence?**

**Model Answer:**
I think many jobs could be partially automated, but very few will be entirely replaced in the short to medium term. AI tends to be most effective at well-defined, repetitive, pattern-recognition tasks — which describes a significant proportion of the work done in data processing, manufacturing, customer service, and certain professional services. However, tasks that require genuine empathy, complex ethical judgement, or creative problem-solving in novel situations remain much more difficult to automate reliably. The more realistic future is one of augmentation rather than wholesale replacement.

---

**Q2. Which types of jobs do you think are most at risk from automation, and which are safest?**

**Model Answer:**
The roles most at risk are those involving routine, predictable, and highly procedural tasks — data entry, basic customer service, toll collection, certain aspects of legal document review, or radiological image analysis. The roles that appear most resilient are those requiring sustained interpersonal connection, physical dexterity in unpredictable environments, and highly contextual creative judgement — such as therapists, social workers, skilled trades like plumbing in complex environments, and teachers who genuinely adapt to individual learners. The boundary, though, is shifting as AI capabilities improve.

---

**Q3. What responsibility do governments have in managing the impact of automation on workers?**

**Model Answer:**
I believe governments have a substantial and somewhat urgent responsibility in this area. At a minimum, they need to invest significantly in retraining and reskilling programmes that are genuinely accessible to workers who are displaced — not simply available in principle but affordable and practically achievable for working adults with families. Beyond that, there are broader questions about whether the productivity gains from automation should be taxed and redistributed, and whether working-hours norms should be reduced to share the benefits of increased productive efficiency more widely across the population.

---

**Q4. Some people argue that we should slow down the development of AI to protect jobs. Do you agree?**

**Model Answer:**
I find that argument understandable in its motivation but ultimately impractical and probably counterproductive. Technological development is to a significant degree globally distributed, and a decision by one country to slow AI development would largely succeed only in disadvantaging that country's economy relative to others who continue to advance. A more effective approach is to accelerate the development of policy frameworks, social safety nets, and educational systems that ensure societies can adapt to technological change with as little human cost as possible, rather than attempting to halt the technology itself.

---

**Q5. How do you think AI might change education in the future?**

**Model Answer:**
I think the most significant potential change is the move towards genuinely personalised learning pathways. Currently, education systems are largely designed to teach a common curriculum to large groups at roughly the same pace, which means the system works well for students near the median but less well for those who are significantly ahead or behind. AI tutoring systems could, in principle, provide every student with an experience that adapts continuously to their individual level, pace, and learning style — something that a single teacher with thirty students cannot feasibly do. Whether this potential will be realised equitably is a different and more uncertain question.

---

**Q6. Are there tasks or decisions that you believe should never be delegated to an AI system?**

**Model Answer:**
Yes, absolutely. I think decisions that carry significant consequences for human life and dignity — criminal sentencing, complex medical diagnoses where the stakes are very high, decisions about child welfare, or determinations of refugee status — should always involve meaningful human oversight and final accountability. The issue is not that AI cannot contribute useful analysis to these decisions; it often can. The problem is one of accountability: when an AI system makes a consequential error, it is currently very difficult to establish clear responsibility, and the people affected have limited means of challenge or redress.

---

**Q7. Do you think AI will make the world more or less equal in the future?**

**Model Answer:**
I think the honest answer is that it depends entirely on how societies choose to develop and deploy it. AI has genuine potential to reduce certain inequalities — for example, by making expert diagnostic or legal services accessible to people in low-resource regions who currently lack access to them. But it also has demonstrated potential to exacerbate inequalities, particularly if AI systems trained predominantly on data from wealthy, Western populations perform less accurately for others, or if the economic gains from automation accrue primarily to technology companies and their shareholders rather than being redistributed more broadly.

---

**Q8. How has the widespread use of technology changed social relationships, in your view?**

**Model Answer:**
This is something I think about quite a lot. My observation is that technology has simultaneously expanded the reach of social connection — I can maintain relationships with people across different cities and countries in a way that would have been much more difficult twenty years ago — while, for some people, substituting a kind of shallow, low-effort digital connection for the deeper, more effortful forms of in-person relationship that seem to be most strongly associated with genuine wellbeing. I think the net effect depends heavily on how individuals choose to use the tools available to them.

---

**Q9. Do you think AI systems can ever be truly creative?**

**Model Answer:**
This is a genuinely philosophically interesting question, and I am not sure the answer is straightforward. Current AI systems can produce outputs that are indistinguishable from human creative work in some domains — text, images, music — and in doing so they clearly demonstrate something that functions like creativity in its outward form. Whether there is genuine intentionality, meaning, or experience underlying that output is much less clear, and I suspect the answer depends largely on how you define creativity. If creativity is defined purely by the novelty and quality of the output, then AI is already creative. If it requires conscious intention and the desire to communicate something meaningful, the answer is much less obvious.

---

**Q10. How do you personally try to balance technology use with offline life?**

**Model Answer:**
Imperfectly, if I am honest. I have tried setting specific time periods in the evening during which I do not look at my phone, and I generally try to avoid bringing devices into the bedroom. I also find that physical exercise, cooking, and reading physical books provide a natural counterbalance to screen time. But I think the broader cultural challenge is that the default environment — the design of the apps, the expectation of constant availability, the nature of remote study and work — pushes heavily towards more technology use, and meaningful resistance requires deliberate and sustained effort.
