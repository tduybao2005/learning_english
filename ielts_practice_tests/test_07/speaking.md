# IELTS SPEAKING — TEST 07

**Topics: Environment & Nature | Science & Discovery | Personal Habits**

*Total duration: 11–14 minutes*

---

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*Duration: 4–5 minutes*
*The examiner will ask you general questions on familiar topics.*

---

### Topic A: Environment & Nature

**Question 1:** Do you spend much time outdoors or in nature?

**Model Answer:**
Yes, I do try to spend time outdoors when I can, though I have to admit it is not as often as I would like. I usually go for a walk in a local park on weekends, which helps me clear my head after a busy week. Being around trees and open sky genuinely makes me feel calmer and more refreshed. I think living in a city means you have to make a deliberate effort to connect with nature.

---

**Question 2:** Are people in your country generally concerned about environmental issues?

**Model Answer:**
I think awareness has increased a great deal in recent years, especially among younger people who grew up hearing about climate change. However, there is often a gap between awareness and action — people know environmental problems exist but do not always change their daily behaviour significantly. In Vietnam, there is growing concern about air pollution in big cities like Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City, and about plastic waste in rivers and coastal areas, which are very visible problems that people encounter every day.

---

**Question 3:** Have you ever done anything to help protect the environment?

**Model Answer:**
On a personal level, I try to reduce single-use plastic — I carry a reusable bottle and try to avoid plastic bags when shopping. I also try to be conscious about electricity use at home. I have participated in a local community clean-up event once, which felt meaningful. I know these are small gestures compared to the scale of the problem, but I believe individual habits matter and can influence people around you.

---

**Question 4:** Do you think climate change affects daily life in your country?

**Model Answer:**
Absolutely, and increasingly so. Vietnam is particularly vulnerable to climate change because of its long coastline and its dependence on agriculture and fisheries. Flooding in cities like Ho Chi Minh City during the rainy season has become more severe and more frequent. Farmers in the Mekong Delta, which is one of the world's most important rice-producing regions, are increasingly dealing with saltwater intrusion into farmland as sea levels rise. So for many Vietnamese people, climate change is not an abstract future threat — it is a present reality affecting livelihoods.

---

### Topic B: Science & Discovery

**Question 5:** Are you interested in science?

**Model Answer:**
I would say I have a general curiosity about scientific topics, though I am not a scientist by training. I find areas like biology, space exploration, and medicine particularly fascinating — especially when new discoveries challenge things we thought we knew. I read science news articles fairly regularly and enjoy documentary programmes that explain complex scientific ideas in accessible ways. I think science is important not just for specialists but for everyone who wants to understand the world around them.

---

**Question 6:** What scientific discovery do you think has had the greatest impact on human life?

**Model Answer:**
That is a difficult question to answer because there are so many candidates — vaccines, antibiotics, electricity, the internet. But if I had to choose one, I might say vaccines, because they have prevented an almost incomprehensible amount of human suffering. Diseases like smallpox, which killed hundreds of millions of people throughout history, have been completely eradicated through vaccination. The COVID-19 pandemic reminded us dramatically how quickly new vaccines can be developed with modern technology and how much lives depend on them.

---

**Question 7:** Do you follow news about scientific discoveries or space exploration?

**Model Answer:**
Yes, I find space exploration genuinely exciting. The idea that humans might eventually travel to Mars within my lifetime is extraordinary when you think about it. I followed the James Webb Space Telescope launch closely because the images it produced were simply breathtaking — looking back billions of years to the early universe. I also find the search for signs of life beyond Earth philosophically compelling. Whether we are alone in the universe is perhaps the biggest unanswered question humanity faces.

---

### Topic C: Personal Habits

**Question 8:** Do you have any habits that you would like to change?

**Model Answer:**
Honestly, I spend too much time on my phone — scrolling through social media or watching short videos in the evening when I could be reading, exercising, or sleeping. I am aware it is a habit that is deliberately engineered by app designers to be as addictive as possible, which makes it harder to break through willpower alone. I have tried setting screen time limits, which helps to some extent, but I have not fully cracked it yet.

---

**Question 9:** How do you usually spend your evenings?

**Model Answer:**
My evenings vary quite a bit. On weeknights, I usually have dinner with my family, do a bit of studying or reading, and try to go to bed at a reasonable hour. If I have finished work or studying earlier than usual, I might watch a film or a series on a streaming platform. At weekends I sometimes meet friends for coffee or dinner. I am trying to be more intentional about how I use my evenings rather than just letting them disappear into screen time.

---

**Question 10:** Do you think people's daily habits have changed because of technology?

**Model Answer:**
Enormously so. The smartphone has fundamentally altered how people spend their time in ways that would have been impossible to predict even twenty years ago. We sleep less, read fewer books, and have shorter attention spans on average. But technology has also made some habits more accessible — exercise tracking apps encourage people to be more physically active, and language learning apps make it much easier to study a language consistently. I think the impact of technology on habits is genuinely mixed — it depends entirely on how consciously and intentionally you use it.

---

**Question 11:** Is it easy or difficult for you to form new habits?

**Model Answer:**
I find it quite difficult, to be honest. I know from things I have read that forming a genuine habit requires consistent repetition over a fairly long period — research suggests anywhere from a few weeks to several months depending on the behaviour. The hardest part for me is getting through the initial stage when the new behaviour does not yet feel natural or rewarding. I have had more success with habits I attach to existing routines — for example, studying vocabulary while having my morning coffee — than with habits that require carving out entirely new time in my day.

---

**Question 12:** Do you think habits formed in childhood are particularly important?

**Model Answer:**
I think they are very influential, yes. Habits formed early in life tend to become deeply embedded because they are practised thousands of times before adulthood, and they shape how we relate to things like food, physical activity, reading, and social interaction. Research on child development suggests that early experiences of reading with parents, for example, have a lasting positive effect on literacy and a love of learning. That said, I also believe adults can change habits with sufficient motivation and the right strategies, so childhood patterns are not deterministic.

## PART 2 — Individual Long Turn

*Duration: 3–4 minutes (including 1 minute preparation)*

*Read the task card below. You have 1 minute to prepare. Then speak for 1–2 minutes.*

---

### Task Card

**Describe an environmental problem in your area or country that particularly concerns you.**

You should say:
- what the problem is
- what causes it
- how it affects the local community or the environment
- and explain what you think should be done to address it

---

### Model Response (~200 words)

The environmental problem that concerns me most in Vietnam is air pollution, particularly in Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City. On bad days, a thick haze hangs over the city and you can actually smell the pollution when you step outside. The air quality index frequently reaches levels that health authorities describe as "unhealthy" or even "very unhealthy" for sensitive groups like children and the elderly.

The causes are multiple and interconnected. Millions of motorbikes and cars release exhaust fumes in cities where traffic congestion is chronic. Factories and industrial zones on the outskirts of cities contribute significant emissions. And seasonal agricultural burning of rice straw in surrounding provinces sends smoke drifting into urban areas, making certain months much worse than others.

For the local community, the health impacts are real and serious. Respiratory illnesses, including asthma and bronchitis, are common. People wear face masks not only as a pandemic precaution but as a year-round protection against pollution. Parents worry about letting their children play outside on bad air quality days.

I think the solutions need to operate at multiple levels. In the short term, stronger enforcement of industrial emission standards would help. In the medium term, investment in public transport — expanding the metro system and making buses more convenient — would reduce motorbike dependency. Longer term, transitioning to electric vehicles and cleaner energy sources is essential.

---

### Follow-up Question

**Do you think individuals or governments bear greater responsibility for solving environmental problems?**

**Model Answer:**
I think this is a genuine both/and situation rather than an either/or choice. Governments have the tools that individuals do not — they can set regulations, impose carbon prices, fund public transport, and ban harmful products. These structural interventions are far more powerful than individual behaviour change alone. However, governments respond to social pressure and public opinion, so individual choices and civic engagement — voting, advocating, boycotting — matter enormously in shaping political will. Waiting for either side to act alone risks nothing getting done.

## PART 3 — Two-way Discussion

*Duration: 4–5 minutes*
*The examiner will ask questions related to the Part 2 topic.*

---

### Discussion Theme: Climate, Environment, and Human Choices

---

**Question 1:** Why do you think many people are concerned about climate change but do not significantly change their behaviour?

**Model Answer:**
This gap between concern and action is one of the most studied puzzles in behavioural science, and I think it comes down to a combination of factors. Climate change is a diffuse, long-term threat that does not trigger the immediate emotional responses that motivate behaviour change the way more immediate dangers do. There is also a collective action problem — people feel their individual behaviour makes no meaningful difference when others are not making the same changes. And many low-carbon choices, like using public transport or eating less meat, require genuine sacrifice of convenience. So knowledge and concern are simply insufficient on their own to drive behaviour change at scale without structural supports.

---

**Question 2:** Should governments introduce laws to force individuals to adopt more environmentally friendly behaviours?

**Model Answer:**
I think there is a strong case for certain forms of regulatory intervention, particularly for behaviours where the environmental cost is invisible to the individual — burning fossil fuels being the obvious example. Carbon taxes or cap-and-trade systems make this cost visible and create financial incentives to change. Mandating energy efficiency standards for appliances and buildings is similarly effective and relatively uncontroversial. However, I am more cautious about overly prescriptive laws that tell people what they can and cannot eat or buy, because these tend to generate political backlash that undermines the broader sustainability agenda. Incentives often work better than prohibitions.

---

**Question 3:** Do you think wealthier countries have a greater responsibility to address climate change than developing nations?

**Model Answer:**
Yes, I strongly believe so, and for two interconnected reasons. First, wealthy countries have historically generated the overwhelming majority of cumulative greenhouse gas emissions since industrialisation — they built their prosperity on carbon-intensive development pathways. Second, they have far greater financial and technological resources to adapt and to invest in clean alternatives. The principle of "common but differentiated responsibilities" is enshrined in international climate agreements for precisely these reasons. That said, developing nations must also pursue low-carbon development pathways rather than simply replicating the carbon-intensive industrialisation of the past, ideally with significant financial and technological support from wealthier countries.

---

**Question 4:** How important is biodiversity loss compared to climate change as an environmental challenge?

**Model Answer:**
I think biodiversity loss is arguably as serious as climate change and considerably less discussed in mainstream public conversation. The two crises are deeply interrelated — climate change accelerates habitat loss and species extinction, while intact ecosystems like forests help regulate the climate. But biodiversity loss has its own catastrophic potential independent of climate change: losing the insects that pollinate food crops, or the fish that provide protein for hundreds of millions of people, would have devastating consequences for food security and human welfare. The problem is that biodiversity loss is even harder to reverse than climate change — you cannot bring back an extinct species — so it may demand even greater urgency.

---

**Question 5:** Do you think technology will be sufficient to solve the world's environmental problems?

**Model Answer:**
Technology is clearly a necessary part of the solution — renewable energy, electric vehicles, more efficient agricultural practices, and carbon capture technologies will all be essential. But I am sceptical that technology alone is sufficient. The underlying driver of most environmental problems is economic systems that externalise environmental costs and reward resource consumption. Without changing those incentive structures through policy and governance, new technologies tend to get absorbed into existing patterns of growth rather than displacing them. Solar panels are cheaper than ever, but global energy demand has grown so rapidly that total fossil fuel consumption is still increasing. Technology and systemic change need to work together.

---

**Question 6:** How can education help address environmental challenges?

**Model Answer:**
Education can play a genuinely important role, particularly in building the environmental literacy that allows citizens to understand the challenges they face and make informed political and consumer choices. Environmental education that begins young — teaching children to value nature, understand ecological systems, and think about their place in the natural world — tends to produce adults with stronger pro-environmental values and behaviours. Education also develops the critical thinking skills needed to evaluate evidence and resist misinformation, which is particularly important given the organised efforts by some interest groups to create doubt about climate science. However, education alone cannot compensate for insufficient policy action and perverse economic incentives.

---

**Question 7:** What role do international organisations play in addressing environmental challenges?

**Model Answer:**
International organisations like the United Nations Environment Programme, the IPCC, and the frameworks established by the Paris Agreement and the Convention on Biological Diversity play a crucial role in providing shared scientific assessments, facilitating international negotiations, and establishing common frameworks and targets. They are essential for problems that are inherently global in nature — no single country can solve climate change or biodiversity loss alone, and coordination is needed to prevent free-rider problems where countries benefit from others' environmental efforts without contributing themselves. That said, international organisations are constrained by the political will of their member states and have limited enforcement powers, which is why domestic political action remains so important alongside international cooperation.

---

**Question 8:** Is sustainable development genuinely possible, or does all economic development inevitably damage the environment?

**Model Answer:**
I believe sustainable development is possible in principle, though achieving it in practice is extraordinarily challenging and requires fundamental changes to how we measure economic success. The concept of GDP growth as the primary measure of national progress is problematic because it does not account for the depletion of natural capital or the environmental costs of production. Countries like Bhutan have experimented with Gross National Happiness as an alternative metric. The "doughnut economics" framework developed by economist Kate Raworth proposes measuring success by whether societies meet the social needs of all people while staying within planetary environmental boundaries. These alternative frameworks suggest that a different kind of economy — one that thrives without necessarily growing — is conceptually possible, even if the political path towards it remains unclear.

---

**Question 9:** How might climate change affect migration patterns in the coming decades?

**Model Answer:**
Climate change is already influencing migration and is widely expected to become one of the most significant drivers of population movement in the coming decades. Rising sea levels threaten low-lying coastal areas and small island states, making them literally uninhabitable. Increasing frequency of droughts and floods destroys agricultural livelihoods in vulnerable regions, pushing people to migrate to cities or across borders. Some projections suggest that tens or even hundreds of millions of people could be displaced by climate-related factors by 2050, with particularly severe impacts in South Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and low-lying coastal regions. Current international law does not recognise climate refugees as a protected category, which creates a significant legal and humanitarian gap that will need to be addressed.

---

**Question 10:** Do you feel optimistic or pessimistic about the future of the environment?

**Model Answer:**
I find myself somewhere between cautious optimism and realistic concern, which probably sounds evasive but reflects genuine uncertainty. On the optimistic side: the cost of renewable energy has fallen far faster than most projections anticipated, public and political awareness of environmental issues has increased dramatically, and there are genuinely impressive examples of ecological recovery when human pressure is reduced — the recovery of certain whale populations, the rewilding of previously degraded landscapes. On the pessimistic side: global emissions are still rising, biodiversity loss is accelerating, and the pace of policy change remains far slower than the science demands. I think the outcome genuinely depends on choices that societies make in the next ten to fifteen years, which means this is a period of extraordinary importance.
