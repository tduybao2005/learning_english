## TASK 1

**Time recommended: 20 minutes**
**Minimum words: 150**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on this task.*

**The two bar charts below compare male and female employment by sector as a percentage of total employed workers in each group in a particular country.**

**Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.**

---

**DATA:**

| Sector | Male (%) | Female (%) |
|---|---|---|
| Agriculture | 8 | 6 |
| Manufacturing | 28 | 18 |
| Construction | 15 | 2 |
| Services | 46 | 72 |
| Other | 3 | 2 |

---

### Model Answer — Band 7.5 (~175 words)

The bar charts compare the sectoral distribution of male and female employment as a proportion of total workers in each group in a particular country.

The most striking difference between male and female employment patterns is in the service sector, where women are far more heavily concentrated: 72% of all employed women work in services, compared to 46% of men. Manufacturing presents the reverse pattern, employing 28% of men but only 18% of women. Construction shows the sharpest gender disparity of all sectors — 15% of employed men work in construction compared to just 2% of women.

Agriculture employs broadly similar proportions of both genders, at 8% for men and 6% for women, making it the most balanced sector across the two groups. The "Other" category is negligible for both, at 3% and 2% respectively.

Overall, the data reveals a clear pattern of occupational gender segregation. Traditionally male-dominated sectors such as construction and manufacturing employ proportionally far more men, while the service sector — which encompasses education, healthcare, retail, and public administration — employs a significantly larger proportion of the female workforce.

## TASK 2

**Time recommended: 40 minutes**
**Minimum words: 250**

*You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.*

**Write about the following topic:**

> *Social media has more negative effects than positive effects on individuals and society.*
>
> *Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*

**Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.**

---

### Model Answer — Band 7.5 (~275 words)

The question of whether social media does more harm than good has become one of the defining debates of the digital age. While I acknowledge that social media platforms have genuine and important social value, I am inclined to believe that their negative effects, on balance, outweigh their benefits — particularly for younger users.

Those who defend social media point to its unparalleled capacity to connect people across distances, facilitate social and political movements, and democratise the creation and sharing of information. Platforms have played a documented role in organising civic activism — from the Arab Spring uprisings to international climate campaigns — and allow diaspora communities to maintain cultural connections with their homelands across vast geographic distances. For individuals in remote or marginalised communities, access to social media can provide a genuine lifeline to information, emotional support networks, and economic opportunity.

Nevertheless, a growing body of research links heavy social media use to negative mental health outcomes, particularly among adolescents. Studies by psychologist Jean Twenge and others have documented significant correlations between the rise of smartphone-based social media use and increasing rates of depression, anxiety, and reported loneliness among teenagers in multiple high-income countries. The psychological mechanisms appear to involve social comparison — the exposure to curated, idealised representations of others' lives that generate feelings of inadequacy — and the displacement of time that might otherwise be spent sleeping or engaging in face-to-face social interaction. Furthermore, the algorithmic amplification of emotionally provocative, divisive, and factually inaccurate content has contributed to political polarisation and the erosion of shared factual understanding in many democratic societies.

In conclusion, while social media has clear social value, its current design — optimised for engagement and attention capture rather than user wellbeing — makes its overall societal impact more negative than positive. Addressing this challenge requires not merely individual behaviour change but structural responses: stronger regulatory frameworks, redesigned platform incentive structures, and more robust digital literacy education beginning at an early age.
