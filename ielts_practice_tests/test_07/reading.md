## READING PASSAGE 1

**"Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services"**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 1–13, which are based on Reading Passage 1.*

---

**A**

Earth is home to an estimated 8.7 million eukaryotic species, yet only approximately 1.2 million of these have been formally described and catalogued by science. The overwhelming majority remain unknown to researchers, residing in habitats ranging from tropical rainforests and coral reefs to deep soil layers and mountain peaks. Biodiversity — the variety of life on Earth — operates at three distinct but interconnected levels. Genetic diversity refers to the variation in genetic information within a single species, which enables populations to adapt to changing environmental conditions over time. Species diversity describes the variety and abundance of different species within a given geographic area, and is most commonly measured using indices that account for both the number of species present and their relative proportions. Ecosystem diversity encompasses the range of different habitat types, ecological communities, and biological processes across a landscape or region. Each level of biodiversity plays a critical and non-substitutable role in the resilience, productivity, and functioning of natural systems.

**B**

Ecosystem services — the benefits that human societies derive from natural ecosystems — are commonly classified into four broad categories. Provisioning services encompass the goods that ecosystems produce directly: food, fresh water, timber, fibres, and medicinal plants and compounds. Regulating services include the processes by which ecosystems moderate environmental conditions, such as climate regulation through carbon sequestration, flood prevention through vegetation and wetland buffers, disease control through predator-prey dynamics that regulate host populations, and water purification through filtration by soils and wetland plants. Supporting services such as nutrient cycling, soil formation, and photosynthesis are foundational — they underpin all other ecosystem functions and make the planet habitable. Cultural services — including recreation, aesthetic enjoyment, spiritual significance, cultural heritage, and scientific knowledge — are less easily quantified than other categories but are equally important to human wellbeing and social cohesion.

**C**

Attempts to assign monetary value to ecosystem services have generated both considerable insight and enduring controversy. Economist Robert Costanza and colleagues published a landmark study in 1997 that attempted to estimate the total annual economic value of the world's ecosystem services. Their calculation, which drew on a range of ecological and economic datasets and valuation methodologies, arrived at a figure of approximately $33 trillion per year — a sum that exceeded global GDP at the time. While acknowledging the inherent methodological challenges of such valuations, including the difficulty of pricing goods and services for which no formal markets exist, the study proved highly influential in persuading policymakers of the economic case for conservation investment. Critics argued that valuing nature in monetary terms was philosophically problematic and risked reducing conservation to a purely financial calculation. Nonetheless, the framework has continued to develop: the Natural Capital Coalition has since developed more refined accounting frameworks, the Natural Capital Protocol, which are now used by corporations and governments around the world to systematically assess their dependencies on and impacts upon nature.

**D**

The keystone species concept, introduced by ecologist Robert Paine in 1969, highlights how some species exert a disproportionately large influence on their ecosystems relative to their numerical abundance. The term derives from the architectural keystone — the central stone in an arch that, if removed, causes the entire structure to collapse. The most widely cited example is the grey wolf in Yellowstone National Park in the United States. Following the wolves' reintroduction in 1995, after a seventy-year absence from the park, a cascade of biological and physical changes unfolded. Elk populations, which had previously grazed intensively in riparian areas without predation pressure, began to alter their behaviour and distribution in response to wolf predation risk. This behavioural change allowed riverside vegetation — willows, aspens, and cottonwoods — to recover along stream banks, which in turn stabilised riverbanks, reduced erosion, altered stream channel patterns, and increased habitat diversity for many other species. Sea otters represent another celebrated case: as predators of sea urchins, they regulate urchin populations that would otherwise consume kelp forests to bare rock, thereby maintaining ecosystems that support hundreds of other species.

**E**

The scale of current biodiversity loss is alarming. The Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services (IPBES) published a comprehensive global assessment in 2019 which concluded that approximately one million species currently face extinction, many within decades rather than centuries. Current extinction rates are estimated at between 100 and 1,000 times the natural background extinction rate that characterised Earth's history before the emergence of modern human civilisation. Some ecologists classify the current period as Earth's sixth mass extinction event — a loss of biodiversity on a scale comparable to the five previous mass extinctions identifiable in the fossil record, the most recent of which eliminated the non-avian dinosaurs approximately 66 million years ago. The primary drivers of contemporary biodiversity loss have been identified as: land-use change, particularly the conversion of natural habitats to agricultural land; direct exploitation of animals and plants through hunting, fishing, and harvesting; climate change; pollution of soil, air, and water; and the introduction of invasive species that displace native organisms.

**F**

Conservation responses to the biodiversity crisis operate at multiple geographic and institutional scales. Internationally, the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) established the Aichi Biodiversity Targets, which included a goal to place 17% of terrestrial and inland water areas and 10% of coastal and marine areas under protection by 2020. Assessment of progress towards these targets was largely disappointing. Updated and more ambitious targets have since been adopted under the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, agreed in December 2022, which aim to protect at least 30% of the planet's land and ocean areas by 2030 — the so-called "30x30" target. Rewilding initiatives — which involve reintroducing keystone species, removing barriers such as fences and dams, and reducing intensive human management to allow natural ecological processes to recover — have gained significant traction in Europe and North America. Financial instruments such as REDD+ (Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and forest Degradation) seek to compensate developing nations financially for maintaining their forests rather than converting them. Payments for Ecosystem Services (PES) schemes create direct financial incentives for landowners and communities to protect and restore biodiversity on their land.

## READING PASSAGE 2

**"The History of the Internet: From ARPANET to the Age of AI"**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 14–26, which are based on Reading Passage 2.*

---

**A**

The origins of the internet lie in military and academic research conducted in the United States in the late 1960s. ARPANET — the Advanced Research Projects Agency Network — was established in 1969 by the US Department of Defense to enable reliable communication between geographically dispersed computers in university and government research facilities. A core design principle of the network was decentralisation: rather than routing all communication through a single central node, the network was built so that data could find alternative routes if parts of the network were damaged or destroyed. This design feature was shaped, in part, by the geopolitical reality of the Cold War and the perceived need for communications infrastructure resilient to attack. On its first day of operation, ARPANET connected just four university nodes: the University of California Los Angeles, the Stanford Research Institute, the University of California Santa Barbara, and the University of Utah.

**B**

The technical foundations of the modern internet as it is understood today were established in the early 1980s with the development of TCP/IP — the Transmission Control Protocol and Internet Protocol — by computer scientists Vint Cerf and Robert Kahn. TCP/IP provided a standardised communication language — a suite of protocols — that allowed computers of entirely different types, manufactured by different companies and running different operating systems, to exchange data with one another reliably. Its adoption as the mandatory standard protocol for all ARPANET communications on 1 January 1983 is widely regarded as the formal "birthday" of the internet, marking the transition from a collection of connected networks to a unified, interoperable global system.

**C**

The single invention that most decisively transformed the internet from a tool used primarily by academics, military researchers, and computer scientists into a global public resource available to ordinary citizens was the World Wide Web, developed by British computer scientist Tim Berners-Lee in 1989 while working at the CERN particle physics laboratory in Geneva, Switzerland. The Web introduced the revolutionary concept of hypertext — documents that contained clickable links connecting them to other documents anywhere on the network — along with the foundational technologies of HTML (Hypertext Markup Language) for structuring web pages, URLs (Uniform Resource Locators) for addressing them, and HTTP (Hypertext Transfer Protocol) for transmitting them between computers. Crucially, and in a decision that had enormous historical consequences, Tim Berners-Lee chose not to patent the World Wide Web or any of its foundational technologies, instead making them freely available to anyone who wished to use them. This decision enabled the Web's explosive global growth throughout the 1990s and beyond.

**D**

Commercial exploitation of the internet accelerated dramatically throughout the 1990s. The launch of the Netscape Navigator graphical web browser in 1994 brought the Web to non-technical audiences for the first time, making it accessible through an intuitive point-and-click interface. Companies such as Amazon, founded in 1994, and eBay, founded in 1995, demonstrated that commercial transactions could be conducted online at vast scale, laying the foundations of e-commerce. Venture capital investment flooded into internet startups across the United States and Europe in what became known as the dot-com boom, reaching a speculative peak in early 2000 before a sharp market correction — the dot-com crash — eliminated hundreds of companies and wiped trillions of dollars from global equity markets. Nevertheless, the companies that survived this correction — including Amazon, Google, and PayPal — would go on to reshape entire industries and accumulate extraordinary economic influence.

**E**

The mid-2000s saw the emergence of what technology commentators termed "Web 2.0" — a phrase used to describe a new generation of internet applications and platforms built around user-generated content, participation, and social interaction, in contrast to the earlier model in which websites published static content for passive consumption. Wikipedia demonstrated that collaborative knowledge production at global scale was not only possible but productive. YouTube, launched in 2005, made video distribution accessible to anyone with a camera and an internet connection. Facebook, launched in 2004 initially for university students, grew into a global social platform that reached over one billion registered users by 2012. The defining characteristic of this era was the shift from users as passive consumers of professionally produced content to active creators and curators of their own content and social networks.

**F**

The introduction of Apple's iPhone in 2007 triggered a fundamental and irreversible transformation in how humans accessed and interacted with the internet. Mobile internet access rapidly overtook fixed-line desktop access in many countries, and the smartphone became the primary computing device for the majority of the global population, particularly in developing nations where mobile telecommunications networks expanded far faster than fixed broadband infrastructure could be built. The proliferation of smartphones created an enormous new market for mobile applications — small, dedicated software programmes optimised for touchscreen devices — and gave rise to platform ecosystems of extraordinary reach and commercial value. Platforms such as WeChat in China, which combined messaging, social media, payments, and commerce into a single application, and M-Pesa, a mobile money transfer service launched in Kenya in 2007, demonstrated how mobile internet could serve as truly critical infrastructure in emerging economies, providing financial access to populations previously excluded from formal banking.

**G**

The integration of artificial intelligence into every layer of internet services has defined the most recent and arguably most consequential phase of internet development. Machine learning algorithms now underpin the operation of search engines, social media recommendation feeds, digital advertising systems, translation tools, image recognition services, and virtual assistants. The 2020s have witnessed the public emergence of generative AI — computational systems capable of producing coherent, contextually relevant text, realistic images, functional computer code, and synthetic audio on demand — with profound and still-contested implications for content creation, knowledge work, education, and the fundamental nature of digital information. Whether artificial intelligence will ultimately prove beneficial or harmful to the broader internet ecosystem — and to human societies more generally — is a question that technology researchers, ethicists, policymakers, regulators, and the general public continue to debate with growing urgency and, in some quarters, genuine alarm.

## READING PASSAGE 3

**"Urbanisation in the Developing World: Opportunities and Challenges"**

*You should spend about 20 minutes on Questions 27–40, which are based on Reading Passage 3.*

---

**A**

The twenty-first century is, fundamentally, an urban century. For the first time in recorded human history, more than half of the world's population lives in cities — a demographic milestone that was reached in 2007. By 2050, the United Nations projects that approximately two-thirds of all humanity will be urban dwellers, representing the addition of some 2.5 billion people to the world's cities and towns over the coming decades. Critically, around 90% of this projected urban growth will be concentrated in Asia and sub-Saharan Africa — regions that are still in the early and middle stages of their urbanisation trajectories. This demographic transformation represents the largest and fastest wave of urbanisation in human history, far exceeding in speed and scale the industrialisation-driven urban growth that characterised nineteenth-century Europe and North America.

**B**

Urbanisation is driven by a combination of economic and social push and pull factors that operate simultaneously in rural and urban environments. Cities offer higher wages, more diverse employment opportunities, better educational institutions, and superior healthcare facilities compared to rural areas — particularly in developing nations where rural-urban disparities in public service provision can be extreme and persistent. These "pull" factors attract migrants from rural areas who seek better life chances for themselves and their children. Simultaneously, a set of rural "push" factors — including agricultural land consolidation that displaces smallholder farmers, mechanisation of agriculture that reduces demand for rural labour, declining commodity prices for small-scale farmers, and the increasing frequency and severity of climate-related crop failures and droughts — reduce the economic viability of rural livelihoods and accelerate out-migration to cities.

**C**

Rapid urbanisation in low-income countries frequently and overwhelmingly outpaces the development of formal housing, adequate infrastructure, and public services, leading to the proliferation of informal settlements commonly referred to as slums. The United Nations defines a slum household as one lacking access to at least one of the following: improved water sources, improved sanitation facilities, sufficient living area, durable housing materials, and security of tenure over the occupied dwelling. Currently, approximately one billion people — roughly one in every eight of the global population — live in conditions that meet this definition of a slum. Of the five defining characteristics identified in the UN definition, security of tenure — the legal right of residents to occupy their dwellings without fear of forced eviction — is identified by UN-Habitat as perhaps the single most critical issue facing informal settlement residents, since its absence prevents households from making productive investments in improving their homes and surrounding environments.

**D**

Despite the formidable challenges associated with rapid and unplanned urbanisation, cities in developing countries are nonetheless significant and dynamic drivers of economic growth. The clustering of firms, workers, consumers, and institutions in urban areas generates what economists term agglomeration effects — productivity increases that arise from geographic proximity, economic specialisation, knowledge spillovers between firms and workers, and the thickening of labour markets. Research across a range of countries and contexts suggests that doubling the population size of a city tends to increase average worker productivity by approximately 5 to 15 percent. In rapidly developing economies such as those of China, India, and Vietnam, urban manufacturing and service sectors have functioned as powerful engines of poverty reduction and structural economic transformation, drawing hundreds of millions of people out of subsistence agriculture and into formal wage employment over the course of a few decades.

**E**

Infrastructure deficits represent one of the most formidable and persistent challenges confronting rapidly growing cities in the developing world. Water supply and sanitation systems, road networks, electricity grids, public transportation systems, and waste management facilities all struggle to keep pace with population growth when cities expand primarily through informal, unplanned processes. Lagos, Nigeria — a megacity that is projected by demographers to become one of the world's three most populous cities by the middle of the twenty-first century — exemplifies these pressures in an acute form. Despite generating substantial economic output and serving as the commercial capital of Africa's most populous nation, Lagos's infrastructure remains severely inadequate relative to the demands of its population, characterised by regular and catastrophic flooding, persistent power outages that force businesses to rely on expensive private generators, and one of the most chronically congested road networks on the African continent.

**F**

In some significant respects, however, cities in the developing world have demonstrated remarkable innovation and adaptability in addressing infrastructure constraints rather than simply waiting for the conventional developmental pathways of earlier industrialisers to unfold. Nairobi's technology and innovation district, widely known as the Silicon Savannah, has attracted substantial international investment and incubated globally significant companies in the fintech and mobile services sectors. The M-Pesa mobile money platform, developed initially in Kenya and subsequently extended to operations across several African countries, demonstrates how digital and mobile innovation can provide financial services — including savings, credit, insurance, and payments — to populations previously entirely excluded from the formal banking system. These examples suggest that late-developing cities are not simply replicating the urbanisation trajectories of earlier industrialisers but actively finding new approaches suited to their specific contexts, resource constraints, and technological opportunities.

**G**

The challenges of governance and urban planning are profound in rapidly urbanising cities across the developing world. Political fragmentation — the presence of multiple overlapping tiers and agencies of government each claiming jurisdiction over different aspects of urban management — frequently impedes the coordinated infrastructure investment and coherent strategic planning that effective urbanisation requires. Corruption diverts public funds intended for infrastructure and services towards private benefit. Weak institutional capacity in planning departments means that urban land use evolves informally and chaotically rather than according to coherent strategic frameworks. Yet the record is not uniformly negative. Curitiba, in the southern Brazilian state of Paraná, pioneered integrated bus rapid transit (BRT) systems and innovative solid waste management approaches in the 1970s and 1980s that have been widely studied, emulated, and adapted in cities across Latin America, Asia, and Africa. Medellín, in Colombia, underwent a remarkable transformation from being one of the world's most violent and ungovernable cities in the 1990s to becoming an internationally recognised model of urban regeneration and social innovation, through a sustained programme of targeted infrastructure investment in previously marginalised hillside neighbourhoods and complementary social development programmes.

**H**

The future trajectory of urbanisation in the developing world will depend fundamentally on the choices that national and local governments make about infrastructure investment priorities, land-use planning frameworks, social policy, and institutional design. International organisations such as the World Bank and the United Nations' dedicated urban agency, UN-Habitat, provide technical assistance, policy guidance, and financing to support urban development, but domestic political will and the quality of local institutional capacity remain the decisive factors in whether urbanisation produces broadly shared prosperity or entrenches spatial inequality and social exclusion. The UN's Sustainable Development Goal 11 — "Make cities and human settlements inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable" — sets out an ambitious agenda for the urban twenty-first century, whose full achievement would represent an unprecedented triumph of human planning, institutional capacity, and collective investment over the forces of unmanaged and inequitable urbanisation.
