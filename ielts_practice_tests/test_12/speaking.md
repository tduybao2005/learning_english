# IELTS Speaking — Test 12

**Total time: 11–14 minutes**

---

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*The examiner will ask you general questions about yourself and familiar topics. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

### Topic 1: Music

**Question 1.** Do you enjoy listening to music? Why or why not?

> **Model answer:** Yes, I really enjoy listening to music, especially when I am studying or relaxing after a long day. I find that it helps me focus and lifts my mood considerably. Music has always been a big part of my daily life.

---

**Question 2.** What type of music do you listen to most often?

> **Model answer:** I tend to listen to a mix of classical and contemporary pop music. Classical music helps me concentrate when I need to work, while upbeat pop gives me energy when I exercise or commute. My tastes have become more varied as I have grown older.

---

**Question 3.** Have you ever learned to play a musical instrument?

> **Model answer:** Yes, I learned to play the guitar when I was about twelve years old and took lessons for roughly two years. I can still play a few chords, though I haven't practised regularly since finishing school. I sometimes wish I had continued.

---

**Question 4.** Do you think music education is important for children?

> **Model answer:** Absolutely. I think learning music develops children's creativity, discipline, and emotional intelligence simultaneously. Playing an instrument also teaches patience and the value of consistent practice — qualities that are transferable to many other areas of life.

---

### Topic 2: Travel

**Question 5.** How often do you travel?

> **Model answer:** I travel about two or three times a year, usually within my own country for short trips. I try to go abroad at least once a year when finances allow, though that has not always been possible recently.

---

**Question 6.** What do you enjoy most about visiting new places?

> **Model answer:** I particularly enjoy experiencing different cultures and cuisines. There is something deeply rewarding about stepping outside your comfort zone and gaining a fresh perspective on how other people live. Every trip teaches me something new about myself as well.

---

**Question 7.** Do you prefer travelling alone or with others?

> **Model answer:** I generally prefer travelling with close friends or family, as sharing experiences together makes them far more memorable. That said, I have done some solo travel too, and it has taught me a great deal about self-reliance and independence.

---

**Question 8.** Is there a place you would particularly like to visit in the future?

> **Model answer:** I have always wanted to visit Japan, particularly Kyoto during the cherry blossom season. The combination of natural beauty, traditional architecture, and rich cultural heritage is enormously appealing to me. I hope to go within the next few years.

---

### Topic 3: Learning New Skills

**Question 9.** How do you usually go about learning something new?

> **Model answer:** I tend to start by watching videos or reading about the topic to build a theoretical foundation before attempting to practise. I find that understanding the underlying principles first makes the practical steps much easier to master and retain.

---

**Question 10.** Have you learned any new skills recently?

> **Model answer:** Yes, I have been learning to cook more complex meals over the past few months, experimenting with dishes from different cuisines. It has been both challenging and very satisfying, especially when a recipe turns out well on the first attempt.

---

**Question 11.** Do you think people learn better alone or in a class?

> **Model answer:** It really depends on the type of skill. Practical skills such as cooking, music, or sports often benefit from a teacher's immediate feedback and correction. Knowledge-based subjects, however, can often be studied very effectively alone, especially with online resources.

---

**Question 12.** What skill would you most like to develop in the future?

> **Model answer:** I would love to become fluent in a third language — ideally Spanish or Japanese. I think being multilingual opens doors both professionally and personally, and I find the intellectual challenge of learning a new language genuinely stimulating.

## PART 2 — Individual Long Turn

*The examiner will give you a task card. You have one minute to prepare, and then you should speak for one to two minutes.*

---

### Cue Card

**Describe a piece of music or song that is important to you.**

*You should say:*
- What the music or song is
- When you first heard it
- What feelings or memories it brings to you
- **And explain why it is particularly important to you.**

*You will have one minute to think about what you are going to say. You can make notes if you wish.*

---

### Model Answer — Part 2

The piece of music that holds the most significance for me is *Clair de Lune*, composed by Claude Debussy. It is a solo piano work — the third movement of his Suite bergamasque — and it is widely regarded as one of the most beautiful pieces in the classical repertoire.

I first heard it when I was around fifteen years old. My music teacher played it at the end of a lesson, somewhat unexpectedly, and I remember being immediately captivated. There was something in the gentle, flowing melody that felt quite unlike anything I had encountered before — it seemed to shift between sadness and serenity within a matter of bars.

Whenever I listen to it now, I am instantly transported back to that classroom, with afternoon sunlight coming through the windows. It brings with it a particular sense of calm and quiet reflection that I find very grounding, especially during stressful periods. Over the years, it has become something of an emotional anchor for me.

The piece is important for several reasons. Firstly, it was the music that made me take classical music seriously for the first time. Before hearing it, I had always assumed that classical music was somehow inaccessible — something for specialists rather than ordinary listeners. Secondly, it reminds me of my teacher, who had a considerable influence on my love of learning. In many ways, *Clair de Lune* represents a small but meaningful turning point in how I engage with art and beauty in general.

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion

*The examiner will ask you more abstract questions linked to the topic of Part 2. This part lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

**Question 1.** How important is music to cultural identity?

> **Model answer:** Music is profoundly tied to cultural identity — it often encodes a community's history, values, and emotional life in ways that other art forms struggle to match. Traditional folk music, for instance, can preserve languages and customs that might otherwise disappear entirely over time.

---

**Question 2.** Do you think young people today are losing interest in traditional music?

> **Model answer:** Not necessarily. While mainstream pop dominates what young people consume on streaming platforms, there has been a notable revival of interest in folk and traditional music in many countries. Digital platforms have actually made it far easier to discover and appreciate these genres than it was in previous generations.

---

**Question 3.** How has digital streaming changed the music industry?

> **Model answer:** Streaming has fundamentally altered how music is distributed and consumed, dramatically increasing listeners' access to a global catalogue of music. However, it has also significantly reduced revenues for many artists, since per-stream payments are very low. The shift has particularly affected musicians who are early in their careers and lack a large established audience.

---

**Question 4.** Do you think musicians today are paid fairly for their work?

> **Model answer:** In many cases, I do not think they are, especially those who are not already famous. The streaming model tends to concentrate income among the most popular artists, leaving independent and emerging musicians struggling to earn a sustainable living from their craft alone.

---

**Question 5.** Should governments fund classical music and opera?

> **Model answer:** I believe they should, as these art forms represent an important part of our cultural heritage and are unlikely to survive on commercial revenues alone in the long run. Public funding ensures that high-quality performances remain accessible to all citizens, not just those who can afford premium ticket prices.

---

**Question 6.** How does music affect people emotionally?

> **Model answer:** Music has a remarkable capacity to regulate our emotional states — research has shown that it can reduce anxiety, elevate mood, and even help manage physical pain. This is why it is used therapeutically in a growing range of clinical settings, from hospital wards to mental health facilities.

---

**Question 7.** Do you think there is too much emphasis on commercial success in the music industry?

> **Model answer:** To a significant degree, yes. Commercial pressures do tend to push the industry towards formulaic and easily marketable music, often at the expense of genuine artistic risk-taking. However, the internet has created valuable space for independent artists to build dedicated audiences without the support — or interference — of major record labels.

---

**Question 8.** How can music bring people together across cultural boundaries?

> **Model answer:** Music is often described as a universal language, and there is real substance to that idea — even without understanding the lyrics, people can respond powerfully to rhythm, melody, and harmony. International music festivals and cross-cultural artistic collaborations both demonstrate how music can foster genuine understanding and empathy between very different communities.

---

**Question 9.** Do you think children should be taught music history at school?

> **Model answer:** Yes, I think understanding the history of music gives children a much richer appreciation of the cultural contexts in which it was created. Learning about composers such as Bach or Mozart is not simply about music — it is also about history, philosophy, and the broader evolution of human expression across centuries.

---

**Question 10.** How do you think the role of music might change in the future?

> **Model answer:** I think music will continue to evolve alongside technology. We may see AI-generated compositions become increasingly mainstream, and virtual reality could create entirely new ways of experiencing live performance. That said, I believe that human-created music will always retain a special emotional authenticity that technology, however sophisticated, cannot fully replicate.
