## PART 1 — Interview (4–5 minutes)
*The examiner will ask you questions about familiar topics. Give 2–4 sentence answers.*

### Topic 1: Gardens and Nature

**Q1. Do you have a garden at home, or have you ever had one?**

Model answer: "We don't have a garden at my current home, but when I was growing up, my family had a small vegetable patch at the back of the house. My grandmother used to grow herbs and a few vegetables there. I have really fond memories of helping her water the plants in the evenings. These days I keep a few small pot plants on my balcony, which is the closest I can manage."

**Q2. Do you enjoy spending time outdoors in natural settings?**

Model answer: "Yes, I really do. Whenever I have free time at weekends, I try to go to the park near my house, especially in the mornings when it is relatively quiet. I find that being surrounded by trees and green space helps me to clear my mind and feel less stressed. I think people who live in cities often underestimate how much they need regular contact with nature."

**Q3. What kinds of plants or flowers do you find most attractive?**

Model answer: "I particularly love plants with interesting shapes — succulents and cacti, for example. They require very little maintenance but they look striking. I also like flowering trees such as cherry blossom, which blooms for only a short time each spring but is absolutely beautiful while it lasts. The brief, seasonal nature of cherry blossom actually makes it more special, I think."

**Q4. Do you think it is important for cities to have more green spaces?**

Model answer: "Absolutely. I think green spaces in cities serve multiple purposes. They provide recreational areas for residents, improve air quality, help to manage flooding, and reduce the heat that builds up in urban areas during summer. Beyond the environmental benefits, parks and gardens simply make cities more pleasant and human-scale places to live. I think every major urban planning decision should prioritise green space."

### Topic 2: Health and Medicine

**Q5. How do you generally try to maintain good health?**

Model answer: "I try to follow a fairly balanced routine. I exercise most mornings — usually just a thirty-minute walk or a light workout — and I try to be reasonably careful about what I eat, though I don't follow any strict diet. I also prioritise sleep, which I think many people overlook as a health factor. If I feel unwell, I tend to seek medical advice fairly promptly rather than ignoring symptoms."

**Q6. Have your attitudes towards health changed compared with when you were younger?**

Model answer: "Definitely. When I was a teenager, I barely thought about health at all — I assumed I would always feel fine. Now I am much more aware of the cumulative effects of habits like diet, sleep, and exercise. I think most people develop a greater appreciation for health as they get older, often because they have seen family members or friends deal with serious illness. Experience is a more powerful teacher than advice."

**Q7. Do you trust modern medicine? Why or why not?**

Model answer: "Yes, I do, substantially. The evidence base for modern medicine is remarkable — vaccines, antibiotics, and surgical techniques have saved billions of lives over the past century. That said, I think it is also reasonable to recognise the limits of what medicine can currently do, and to be cautious about over-medicalising ordinary life experiences. I also think that preventive approaches — lifestyle and diet — deserve more emphasis than treatment systems often give them."

**Q8. Is healthcare easily available in your country?**

Model answer: "The situation varies considerably depending on where you live and your economic situation. In major cities, quality medical facilities are generally accessible, though waiting times can be long at public hospitals. In rural areas, access to specialists in particular can be very limited. I think improving equitable access to healthcare, especially preventive care, is one of the most important challenges my country faces."

### Topic 3: Creative Hobbies

**Q9. Do you have any creative hobbies?**

Model answer: "I enjoy photography as a hobby. I have been taking photographs since I was about sixteen — initially just on my phone, but more recently with a proper camera. What I love about photography is that it trains you to look at your surroundings differently, to notice light, composition, and detail that you would otherwise walk past without seeing. It is both a creative and a meditative practice for me."

**Q10. Did you do any creative activities when you were a child?**

Model answer: "Yes, I was quite into drawing and painting as a child. My parents encouraged it, and I remember spending whole weekend afternoons with paints or coloured pencils, usually trying to copy pictures of animals or landscapes from books. I was not particularly talented, but I found the process deeply satisfying. I drifted away from it as schoolwork became more demanding, which I sometimes regret."

**Q11. Do you think creativity is important in everyday life?**

Model answer: "I think it is, even in contexts that we might not traditionally associate with creativity. Problem-solving at work, finding new ways to communicate with people, deciding how to organise your living space — these all involve creative thinking, even if nobody calls them creative. I believe the people who tend to be most adaptable and effective in life are those who approach challenges with curiosity and imagination rather than just following established routines."

**Q12. Is it possible to learn to be more creative?**

Model answer: "I believe so, to a significant extent. Creativity is not purely an innate gift — it can be developed through practice, exposure to diverse ideas, and a willingness to try things and accept that they might not work out. Taking up new hobbies, reading widely, travelling, and spending time with people from different backgrounds all seem to stimulate creative thinking. The key, I think, is maintaining curiosity and not becoming too fixed in your ways."

### PART 2 — BAND 7.5 MODEL RESPONSE (~200 words)

"The creative person I'd like to talk about is Kengo Kuma, a Japanese architect who I first discovered when I was doing some research on sustainable design about two years ago. He's probably best known for designing the National Stadium in Tokyo for the 2020 Olympics, but what really caught my attention was his approach to materials — he has a remarkable ability to integrate natural materials like wood, bamboo, and stone into contemporary buildings in ways that feel both ancient and completely modern at the same time.

What makes him creative, in my view, is that he doesn't just design aesthetically interesting buildings — he genuinely thinks about how a building relates to its natural and cultural context. Many of his designs are specifically rooted in Japanese architectural traditions while solving very modern functional and environmental challenges. That kind of creativity, which bridges tradition and innovation, seems to me rarer and more valuable than pure novelty.

I find him inspiring because he demonstrates that creativity and deep knowledge can coexist — he is clearly a master of his craft who has spent decades learning about materials, construction, and history. His work made me think differently about how we can design spaces that feel genuinely connected to the natural world rather than separate from it."

### FOLLOW-UP QUESTION

**Examiner:** "Do you think creative people are born with their talent, or can creativity be developed?"

**Model answer:** "I think it is genuinely both, in most cases. There may be some innate differences in temperament — some people seem naturally more curious or risk-tolerant, which supports creative behaviour. But the evidence from research on expertise and creative achievement strongly suggests that sustained practice, broad knowledge, and exposure to diverse ideas are what really enable creative output at a high level. Even people we regard as obvious natural talents — musicians, writers, architects — typically have put in years of intensive practice and study before producing their most celebrated work."

### Theme A: Creativity in Education and Work

**Q1. Do you think schools do enough to develop students' creativity?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "In my experience, most school systems focus heavily on convergent thinking — finding the right answer to a defined question — which is the opposite of the open-ended exploration that creativity requires. Art and music programmes are often the first to be cut when budgets are reduced, and the pressure of examinations encourages memorisation rather than original thought. I think this is a real loss. Schools that actively value curiosity, experimentation, and creative risk-taking tend to produce graduates who are better equipped for a rapidly changing world."

**Q2. How important is creativity in professions that are not traditionally considered 'creative'?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "Enormously important, actually. Engineering, medicine, law, business management — all of these fields require creative problem-solving on a daily basis. A doctor who can only follow protocols will struggle when a patient presents with an unusual combination of symptoms. A lawyer needs to construct novel arguments for novel situations. An engineer designing infrastructure for a changing climate cannot simply apply existing solutions. The idea that creativity belongs only to artists and designers seems to me a fundamental misunderstanding of what creative thinking actually involves."

**Q3. Should companies invest more in creative environments for their employees?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "The evidence suggests they should, though the nature of the investment matters more than the amount. Giving employees dedicated time to explore ideas without immediate commercial pressure — as Google famously did with its '20 percent time' policy — can generate valuable innovation. Creating psychological safety, so that people feel comfortable proposing unconventional ideas without fear of ridicule, may be even more important than physical workspace design. The companies that seem most consistently innovative are those where leadership visibly values creative risk-taking rather than just safe, predictable performance."

---

### Theme B: AI and Human Creativity

**Q4. Do you think artificial intelligence can be truly creative?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "This is a genuinely fascinating and contested question. AI systems can currently produce outputs that look creative — paintings, poems, music compositions — and that are sometimes indistinguishable from human-generated work. But whether this constitutes genuine creativity in the sense of original intention and meaning is deeply debated. AI systems recombine existing patterns in novel ways, but they do not have experiences, desires, or a perspective on the world. I tend to think that what AI produces is impressive pattern synthesis rather than creativity in the fullest human sense."

**Q5. In what ways might AI help or hinder human creativity?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "AI could genuinely help by removing technical barriers — a person with a musical idea but no instrumental training can now hear it realised through AI tools. It can help writers overcome blank-page paralysis by generating starting points to react to. On the other hand, if people increasingly consume AI-generated content rather than practising creative skills themselves, there is a real risk that those skills atrophy. The most optimistic scenario is one where AI handles technical execution while humans focus on meaning, intent, and creative vision."

### Theme C: Healthcare and Medical Ethics

**Q6. Do you think people today are more or less healthy than people were fifty years ago?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "The answer differs depending on which aspect of health you measure. Infectious disease rates have fallen dramatically, life expectancy has increased, and acute medical care is vastly better than it was in the 1970s. However, chronic conditions linked to modern lifestyles — obesity, type 2 diabetes, depression, anxiety — appear to be more prevalent. So I would say people live longer but are not necessarily healthier in terms of the quality of those extra years. The challenge now is improving healthspan, not just lifespan."

**Q7. Should doctors always tell patients the full truth about their condition?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "I believe generally yes, with sensitivity and compassion in the delivery. Patients have a right to make informed decisions about their own treatment, their finances, and their personal affairs, all of which depend on accurate information about their health. Withholding a terminal diagnosis, for instance, may seem kind in the moment but deprives a person of the opportunity to say what they need to say and to make choices that matter to them. The how of delivering difficult news is important, but not at the cost of honesty."

**Q8. How should healthcare systems balance the needs of individual patients against limited resources?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "This is one of the hardest problems in healthcare policy. In practice, every healthcare system makes allocation decisions — explicitly or implicitly — about which treatments to fund and which not to. I think transparent, publicly debated frameworks are better than ad hoc decisions made behind closed doors. Cost-effectiveness analysis — measuring the health benefit gained per unit of money spent — provides a useful starting point, though it must be balanced against considerations of equity and the specific vulnerability of different patient groups. There is no perfect answer, but democratic accountability matters."

---

### Theme D: Healthcare Funding Priorities

**Q9. Should governments fund alternative medicine such as acupuncture or herbal remedies?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "I think public funding should be conditional on evidence of effectiveness from rigorous clinical trials, regardless of whether a treatment is described as conventional or alternative. Some practices that were historically considered alternative — certain dietary interventions, mindfulness-based therapies, specific herbal compounds — have accumulated strong enough evidence to merit inclusion in evidence-based clinical guidelines. Others have not, and funding them with public money would be difficult to justify when resources are limited and treatments with proven effectiveness are available."

**Q10. Do you think wealthy people have an unfair advantage in accessing healthcare?**

Model answer (70-90 words): "Undeniably, in most countries. The capacity to pay for private consultations, diagnostic tests, specialist treatments, and quality residential care varies dramatically with income and wealth. Even in countries with universal healthcare systems, wealthier patients tend to be better advocates for themselves, more health-literate, and better able to navigate complex systems to access the care they need. This inequality in health outcomes linked to socioeconomic status is one of the most persistent and morally troubling features of contemporary healthcare systems, and reducing it should be a central policy priority."
