## TASK 1 (Recommended time: 20 minutes | Minimum 150 words)

**The line graph below shows life expectancy at birth in four countries between 1960 and 2020.**

**Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.**

---

### DATA (Line Graph)

| Year | Japan | UK  | Vietnam | Nigeria |
|------|-------|-----|---------|---------|
| 1960 | 68    | 71  | 52      | 39      |
| 1980 | 76    | 74  | 64      | 46      |
| 2000 | 81    | 78  | 74      | 51      |
| 2020 | 85    | 81  | 76      | 55      |

*(All figures in years. Data points: 1960, 1980, 2000, 2020)*

---

### TASK 1 — BAND 7.5 MODEL ANSWER (~175 words)

The line graph illustrates changes in life expectancy at birth across four countries — Japan, the UK, Vietnam, and Nigeria — over a sixty-year period from 1960 to 2020.

Overall, life expectancy increased in all four countries throughout the period, though the rate of improvement and the absolute figures varied considerably. Japan consistently recorded the highest life expectancy by the end of the period, while Nigeria remained the lowest across all years examined.

In 1960, life expectancy ranged from 39 years in Nigeria to 71 years in the UK. By 2020, all four countries had seen substantial improvements: Japan rose dramatically from 68 to 85 years, surpassing the UK's figure of 81 years — a reversal of their positions in 1960, when Japan had trailed behind. Vietnam showed the most impressive proportional increase of the four nations, climbing from 52 years in 1960 to 76 years by 2020 — a rise of 24 years — reflecting substantial investment in healthcare and development. Nigeria also improved, rising from 39 to 55 years, though its 2020 figure remained far below the other three countries, reflecting persistent challenges in healthcare infrastructure and broader economic development.

---

### TASK 1 — EXAMINER NOTES (Band 7.5 Justification)

- **Task Achievement:** All main features addressed — overall trend, specific country comparisons, notable changes (Japan overtaking UK, Vietnam's proportional rise, Nigeria's persistent gap). Overview clearly stated.
- **Cohesion & Coherence:** Logical paragraph structure (intro → overview → details). Effective use of linking phrases ("though", "reflecting", "a reversal of").
- **Lexical Resource:** "proportional increase", "persistent challenges", "healthcare infrastructure", "reversal of their positions" — varied and precise vocabulary.
- **Grammatical Range:** Range of sentence structures; accurate use of comparative forms and past tense narration.

## TASK 2 (Recommended time: 40 minutes | Minimum 250 words)

**"Governments should invest more in preventive healthcare rather than in treatment of existing illness. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"**

---

### TASK 2 — PLANNING GUIDE (Before writing)

**Position:** Strongly agree — preventive healthcare should be prioritised.

**Paragraph plan:**
- Introduction: State position clearly
- Body 1: Prevention is far more cost-effective than treatment
- Body 2: Prevention addresses root causes, improving long-term population health
- Concession: Serious illness still requires treatment investment — cannot be neglected entirely
- Conclusion: Restate position with nuance

**Useful vocabulary:** preventive medicine / health promotion / chronic disease / cost-benefit analysis / intervention / downstream effects / public health policy / burden of disease

---

### TASK 2 — BAND 7.5 MODEL ANSWER (~280 words)

Healthcare systems worldwide face a persistent and fundamental tension between two approaches: treating illness once it has occurred versus preventing illness before it develops. I strongly agree that governments should prioritise investment in preventive healthcare, as the evidence for its cost-effectiveness and long-term benefits is compelling.

The most powerful argument in favour of prevention is economic. Treating established illness — particularly chronic conditions such as type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and certain cancers — is enormously expensive. A single hospitalisation for a heart attack, for instance, may cost tens of thousands of pounds and may need to be followed by years of medication and monitoring. By contrast, the public health interventions that can prevent such conditions — tobacco cessation programmes, nutrition education, subsidised exercise facilities, and screening programmes — cost a fraction of that amount per person reached. Economists consistently find that preventive interventions deliver substantially higher returns per pound spent than acute medical treatment.

Beyond economics, prevention improves population health in a more fundamental way by addressing root causes rather than symptoms. Obesity, smoking, and physical inactivity are underlying causes of multiple costly chronic conditions. Public health campaigns and regulations targeting these behaviours — such as sugar taxes, smoking bans in public spaces, and mandatory nutritional labelling — address illness at its source. The resulting improvements in population health reduce the long-term burden on health services far more effectively than expanding hospital capacity.

Opponents may rightly argue that serious illnesses cannot be left untreated regardless of cost, and that some medical conditions arise despite excellent preventive care. This is true: no government can or should abandon investment in treatment. However, this does not undermine the case for rebalancing spending towards prevention; it simply means that both are necessary, with prevention deserving greater emphasis than it currently receives in most healthcare systems.

In conclusion, while treatment investment remains indispensable, the evidence firmly supports greater government spending on preventive healthcare as the more cost-effective, humane, and long-term approach to improving public health.

---

### TASK 2 — EXAMINER NOTES (Band 7.5 Justification)

- **Task Response:** Clear position maintained throughout. Both sides acknowledged — concession in paragraph 4 shows nuance. Specific examples used (heart attack costs, sugar taxes, smoking bans).
- **Cohesion & Coherence:** PEEL structure (Point → Evidence → Explanation → Link). Clear paragraph progression. Effective use of discourse markers ("Beyond economics", "By contrast", "Opponents may rightly argue").
- **Lexical Resource:** "underlying causes", "burden on health services", "cost-effectiveness", "rebalancing spending" — consistent AWL use. No errors.
- **Grammatical Range:** Mix of complex and compound sentences. Conditional structures ("if... then"), passive voice ("are addressed"), comparative ("far more effectively").

### TASK 2 — COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| "Government should invest..." | "Governments should invest..." (plural noun) |
| "I completely agree with this statement" | Avoid starting with "I" in formal essays — use "This essay..." or "It is argued that..." |
| Very general examples ("for example, in many countries...") | Use specific examples: "In the UK, sugar taxes introduced in 2018 reduced soft drink sugar content by..." |
| Writing only about one side | Always acknowledge the opposing view (concession paragraph) |
| Conclusion that introduces new ideas | Conclusion should summarise and restate — not add new arguments |
