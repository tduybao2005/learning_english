# IELTS Speaking — Test 14

**Time allowed: 11–14 minutes**

---

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview

*The examiner will ask you questions about yourself and familiar topics. This section lasts 4–5 minutes.*

---

### Topic 1: Cities and Living Environment

**Question 1.** Do you live in a city or in the countryside?

**Model Answer:** I live in a fairly large city, which I have called home for most of my life. I have grown quite accustomed to the pace and convenience that urban living offers, from accessible transport to a wide variety of restaurants and cultural venues. That said, I sometimes miss the quietness and open space that rural environments provide.

---

**Question 2.** What do you like most about living in your city?

**Model Answer:** I particularly enjoy the variety of opportunities available here—from cultural events and art exhibitions to diverse food options from around the world. There is always something happening, which makes life feel vibrant and dynamic. The public transport system is also quite reliable, which makes daily commuting considerably more manageable.

---

**Question 3.** What is one thing you would change about your city if you could?

**Model Answer:** If I could change one thing, I would expand the provision of green spaces throughout the city. There are not enough parks and natural areas for residents to relax and escape from the urban noise, and I believe that improving access to nature would significantly benefit the wellbeing of everyone who lives here.

---

**Question 4.** Do you think cities have become better or worse places to live in recent decades?

**Model Answer:** I think the answer depends on the city and the aspect you consider. Many cities have improved their infrastructure, public services, and environmental standards considerably over recent decades. On the other hand, issues such as housing costs and traffic congestion have worsened in many places, making daily life more stressful and less affordable, particularly for younger residents.

---

### Topic 2: Reading and Books

**Question 5.** Do you enjoy reading? What kinds of books do you prefer?

**Model Answer:** Yes, I genuinely enjoy reading, particularly non-fiction books on topics such as history, psychology, and science. I find that reading expands my understanding of the world and introduces me to perspectives I would not otherwise encounter. That said, I also appreciate well-crafted literary fiction when I want something more imaginative and emotionally engaging.

---

**Question 6.** How often do you read, and when do you find time for it?

**Model Answer:** I try to read for at least half an hour each day, usually in the evening before bed. It is a habit I have maintained for several years and one that I find genuinely relaxing. On weekends, I sometimes read for much longer stretches, especially if I am absorbed in a particularly compelling book.

---

**Question 7.** Do you prefer reading physical books or e-books? Why?

**Model Answer:** I have a slight preference for physical books, mainly because I enjoy the tactile experience of turning pages and the absence of screen glare. However, I do make use of e-books when travelling, as they are far more convenient for carrying a large number of titles without adding weight to my luggage.

---

**Question 8.** Has reading become less popular among young people in your country?

**Model Answer:** I think there is some truth to that observation. With the rise of social media platforms and video streaming services, many young people now spend much of their leisure time on screens rather than with books. However, I also know young people who are enthusiastic and avid readers, so I would not want to generalise too broadly across the entire generation.

---

### Topic 3: Friends and Social Life

**Question 9.** How important are friendships in your life?

**Model Answer:** Friendships are extremely important to me. I believe that having a reliable network of close friends provides emotional support, perspective, and a sense of belonging that is essential for mental wellbeing. I make a deliberate effort to maintain my friendships even when life becomes particularly busy or demanding.

---

**Question 10.** How do you usually keep in touch with your friends?

**Model Answer:** I keep in touch primarily through messaging apps and occasional video calls, especially with friends who live in different cities or abroad. For those who live nearby, I try to meet in person at least once a month—for a meal or some shared activity. I believe face-to-face interaction is irreplaceable for maintaining genuinely deep connections.

---

**Question 11.** Do you think it is harder to make friends as an adult than as a child?

**Model Answer:** I do think it is generally more challenging. As children, we are constantly placed in social environments—schools, sports teams, and after-school clubs—that naturally bring us together and create shared experiences. As adults, our social circles tend to be more fixed, and we have fewer opportunities for the spontaneous, repeated interactions that naturally lead to genuine and lasting friendships.

---

**Question 12.** How has technology changed the way people socialise?

**Model Answer:** Technology has fundamentally transformed how people maintain relationships. It has made it far easier to stay in contact across geographical distances and has created platforms through which people can connect with others who share their interests. However, it has also introduced a more superficial mode of interaction that can, in some cases, substitute for deeper in-person relationships rather than genuinely supplementing them.

---

## PART 2 — Individual Long Turn

*The examiner will give you a topic on a card. You will have 1 minute to prepare, then speak for 1–2 minutes. The examiner may ask one or two follow-up questions.*

---

### Cue Card

**Describe a building or architectural structure you find impressive.**

You should say:
- What the building is and where it is located
- What it looks like and what makes it architecturally distinctive
- When you first saw or visited it
- **And explain** why you find it so impressive.

---

### Model Answer — Part 2

The building I find most impressive is the Sagrada Família in Barcelona, Spain. This extraordinary basilica was designed by the Catalan architect Antoni Gaudí and has been under continuous construction since 1882, making it one of the most ambitious architectural projects in human history.

What makes the Sagrada Família architecturally remarkable is its astonishing fusion of Gothic and Art Nouveau styles, expressed through soaring spires, organic sculptural forms, and an interior suffused with coloured light filtered through elaborate stained-glass windows. The façades are covered in intricate stone carvings depicting scenes from the Bible, and every surface seems to pulsate with symbolic detail. It is genuinely unlike any other building I have ever seen—nothing I had read or studied could quite prepare me for the experience of encountering it in person.

I first visited the Sagrada Família during a trip to Barcelona approximately three years ago. I had seen photographs beforehand, but nothing fully conveyed the scale and power of the structure. Standing before it for the first time was genuinely overwhelming.

What I find most impressive is the combination of artistic vision and engineering ingenuity that the building represents. The fact that it has been under continuous construction for over 140 years—sustained across generations of architects, craftspeople, and patrons—strikes me as profoundly moving. It is due to be completed within my lifetime, which makes it feel like a living project rather than a historical monument. To me, the Sagrada Família represents the heights of what human creativity and collective commitment can achieve when sustained across time.

*(~215 words)*

## PART 3 — Two-Way Discussion

*The examiner will ask you questions related to the Part 2 topic. This section lasts 4–5 minutes and involves more abstract discussion.*

---

### Topic: Urban Development, Architecture and Culture, and Sustainable City Planning

**Question 1.** How has rapid urbanisation affected the quality of life in major cities?

**Model Answer:** Rapid urbanisation has had genuinely mixed effects. On one hand, it has driven economic growth and created enormous concentrations of opportunity, innovation, and cultural vitality. On the other hand, it has put immense pressure on housing, transport infrastructure, and public services, leading to overcrowding, pollution, and rising inequality in many of the world's largest cities. The net effect depends enormously on how well the process of urban growth is managed by governments and planners.

---

**Question 2.** Do you think modern architecture reflects the values of contemporary society?

**Model Answer:** To a considerable extent, yes. Modern architecture often prioritises functionality, sustainability, and transparency—values that reflect contemporary concerns around efficiency and environmental responsibility. However, some critics argue that the dominance of glass towers and uniform corporate aesthetics has led to a homogenisation of urban environments that erases local cultural identity and makes cities feel interchangeable.

---

**Question 3.** Why do you think historical buildings are important for a city's identity?

**Model Answer:** Historical buildings serve as physical embodiments of a community's collective memory and cultural heritage. They provide continuity between the past and the present, giving residents a sense of place and belonging that purely modern environments often struggle to provide. Many of a city's most beloved landmarks are historical structures, and they also contribute significantly to economic vitality through tourism and creative industries.

---

**Question 4.** Should governments invest more in public art and architecture? Why or why not?

**Model Answer:** I believe that investment in public art and architecture is genuinely worthwhile and should be a priority. Well-designed public spaces and striking civic buildings can foster community pride, attract visitors, and signal that a city values its residents' quality of life beyond mere economic function. The social and psychological benefits of beautiful urban environments are well documented, and I think they justify the expenditure.

---

**Question 5.** How important is it for cities to preserve green spaces as they grow?

**Model Answer:** Preserving green spaces is absolutely essential. Research consistently shows that access to parks and natural areas is associated with improved mental health, reduced stress, and greater social cohesion among urban residents. As cities expand, the pressure to convert green land into residential or commercial development is enormous, but doing so ultimately undermines the long-term liveability and wellbeing of the urban population.

---

**Question 6.** What role should architects play in addressing social problems such as poverty and inequality?

**Model Answer:** Architects have a meaningful and often underappreciated role to play, though it must be exercised in genuine collaboration with policymakers and local communities. The design of affordable housing, accessible public spaces, and community facilities can significantly affect residents' quality of life and opportunities for social mobility. Architecture that prioritises human needs over aesthetic prestige or commercial return can be a powerful instrument for social inclusion.

---

**Question 7.** How do you think cities will be different fifty years from now?

**Model Answer:** I expect cities in fifty years will be far more integrated with technology than they are today, with smart infrastructure that manages energy, transport, and environmental resources far more efficiently. I also hope they will be substantially greener, with urban agriculture, extensive tree planting, and renewable energy generation becoming the norm rather than the exception. Whether they will be more socially equitable is a harder question that will depend on political choices made over the coming decades.

---

**Question 8.** Do you think people have become more or less connected to their local communities as cities have grown larger?

**Model Answer:** I think the relationship is somewhat paradoxical. Larger cities offer many more opportunities for social connection through shared interests and diverse communities. However, the anonymity and sheer pace of urban life can make it harder to develop the deep, sustained relationships that characterise strong local communities. The rise of digital communication has further complicated the picture, enabling connection across distances while sometimes weakening immediate neighbourhood ties.

---

**Question 9.** Is it possible to build sustainable cities without sacrificing economic growth?

**Model Answer:** I believe it is not only possible but increasingly necessary and indeed economically advantageous. The false dichotomy between sustainability and economic growth is being challenged by evidence from cities such as Copenhagen and Amsterdam, which have combined ambitious environmental policies with robust economic performance. Investing in renewable energy systems, efficient public transport, and green building standards creates jobs and reduces long-term infrastructure costs while simultaneously protecting the environment.

---

**Question 10.** Should cities around the world adopt a universal standard for sustainable urban design?

**Model Answer:** Universal standards could be valuable in establishing a baseline of best practice and in facilitating knowledge sharing between cities. However, they would need to be sufficiently flexible to account for different climates, cultures, and economic circumstances. What works well for a Nordic city with a high tax base and temperate climate will not translate directly to a tropical megacity or a rapidly developing urban centre in the Global South. International cooperation on principles is more realistic and more respectful than imposing rigid uniformity.
