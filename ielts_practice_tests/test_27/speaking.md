# IELTS Practice Test 27 — Speaking
**Chủ đề: Technology & Artificial Intelligence**

---

## PART 1: Câu hỏi ngắn

### Chủ đề 1: Technology & Gadgets

**1. Do you use a lot of technology in your daily life?**
Yes, quite a lot. I use my smartphone constantly for communication and navigation, and I rely on my laptop for work every day. Technology is pretty much woven into every part of my routine at this point.

**2. What is the most useful piece of technology you own?**
Without a doubt, my smartphone. It replaces so many separate devices — a camera, a map, a dictionary, a music player — and it keeps me connected with people no matter where I am. I genuinely can't imagine managing a day without it.

**3. Has technology changed the way you spend your free time?**
Absolutely. I stream films and music instead of buying physical copies, and I spend time on social platforms and online communities. In some ways, technology has given me access to more entertainment than I could ever have found before.

**4. Do you think people today are too dependent on technology?**
To a degree, yes. I notice that many people reach for their phones the moment they feel bored or uncertain, rather than sitting quietly or thinking for themselves. That said, dependence is only a problem when technology starts replacing skills or relationships rather than enhancing them.

---

### Chủ đề 2: Social Media

**5. How often do you use social media?**
I check social media probably two or three times a day, mainly for news updates and to stay in touch with friends from university. I try not to scroll endlessly, but it can be difficult to stop once you start.

**6. What do you think are the main benefits of social media?**
The biggest benefit is the ability to maintain relationships across distances. I have friends living in different countries, and social media means we can still share moments and stay updated on each other's lives. It also allows people to discover communities that share their interests.

**7. Are there any downsides to social media that concern you?**
Yes — the spread of misinformation is something I find genuinely worrying. When false information travels as quickly as verified news, it becomes very hard for people to know what to trust. I also think the pressure to present a perfect life online can affect people's mental health significantly.

**8. Do you think social media will become more or less popular in the future?**
I think it will remain popular, but the specific platforms people use will shift. Younger generations seem to gravitate toward more visual, short-form content, so the format will evolve. What won't change is the fundamental human desire to share experiences and stay connected.

---

### Chủ đề 3: The Future

**9. Are you optimistic or pessimistic about the future of technology?**
Generally optimistic, though cautiously so. Technology has the potential to solve enormous problems — climate change, disease, poverty — but only if it is developed and distributed responsibly. My optimism depends heavily on the choices that governments and companies make in the coming decades.

**10. What technological development do you think will have the biggest impact in the future?**
I believe artificial intelligence will be the defining technology of this century. Its capacity to process information and make decisions at a scale no human could match means it will transform everything from medicine to education to manufacturing.

**11. Do you worry that machines might one day be more intelligent than humans?**
It is something I think about. If AI surpasses human intelligence in most domains, the consequences would be deeply unpredictable. The key question is whether we develop the wisdom to govern that kind of technology — which is a fundamentally human challenge, not a technical one.

**12. Would you like to live in a world where AI does most of the work?**
In theory, yes — a world where repetitive and dangerous work is handled by machines could free people to pursue creativity, relationships, and learning. In practice, I worry about who benefits from that arrangement. If the gains go only to a small group of technology owners, it could create a more unequal world, not a more liberated one.

**Bài mẫu (~200 từ):**

The piece of technology that has had the greatest impact on my life is undoubtedly my smartphone. I received my first one when I started university, around eight years ago, and from that point on it became an indispensable part of how I navigate the world.

Before I had a smartphone, I relied on printed maps and separate devices for music, photography, and communication. My phone consolidated all of those functions into a single device that fits in my pocket. Practically speaking, it transformed the way I manage my time — I can schedule appointments, pay bills, book transport, and access information within seconds, from virtually anywhere.

More significantly, it changed the way I maintain relationships. When I moved away from home for university, video calls with my family became a regular part of my week. Without that technology, the distance would have felt far more isolating.

I am also aware of the downsides. I sometimes find myself checking my phone out of habit rather than necessity, and I have made a conscious effort to limit screen time in the evenings. But on balance, the smartphone has made my life considerably more connected, convenient, and informed, and for those reasons it remains the most life-changing piece of technology I have encountered.

## PART 3: Câu hỏi nâng cao

### AI Ethics, Digital Divide, Automation & Employment

**1. Do you think artificial intelligence should be used to make decisions that affect people's lives, such as in criminal justice or loan approvals?**
This is a question I find genuinely troubling. AI can process data more consistently than a human judge, which might reduce certain forms of subjective bias. However, algorithms also encode the biases present in their training data, and the consequences of an incorrect decision in a legal or financial context can be devastating for individuals. I think AI should assist human decision-makers rather than replace them entirely in these high-stakes domains.

**2. Who do you think is responsible when an AI system makes a harmful mistake?**
Responsibility should be distributed across the entire chain: the developers who built the system, the organisations that deployed it, and the regulators who allowed its use. The challenge is that existing legal frameworks were not designed with autonomous AI systems in mind, so there is often a diffusion of accountability that allows no single party to be held responsible. Closing that gap is one of the most urgent legal challenges of our time.

**3. What is the "digital divide," and why does it matter?**
The digital divide refers to the gap between those who have reliable access to technology and the internet and those who do not. It matters because in an increasingly digital world, access to information, education, healthcare, and economic opportunity are increasingly mediated through technology. People without access are not simply missing a convenience — they are being systematically excluded from participation in modern society.

**4. How can governments help people who lose their jobs due to automation?**
Governments have several tools at their disposal. Investing in retraining and lifelong learning programmes can help displaced workers acquire skills relevant to new industries. Strengthening social safety nets — unemployment benefits, housing support — provides a buffer during transitions. More ambitiously, some economists advocate for universal basic income as a way to decouple basic economic security from employment entirely. The most effective response will likely combine several of these approaches.

**5. Do you think AI could ever be creative in the same way humans are?**
That depends on how you define creativity. AI systems can already generate paintings, music, and written text that is technically impressive and occasionally surprising. But I am not convinced this constitutes genuine creativity — it is sophisticated pattern-matching drawn from human-generated data. True creativity, I would argue, involves not just producing novel outputs but understanding their meaning and being moved by them. Whether AI will ever achieve that kind of consciousness remains one of the most profound open questions in science.

**6. Is it ethical for companies to collect large amounts of personal data to train AI systems?**
It is ethically complex. Data collection enables AI capabilities that genuinely benefit people — personalised medicine, better search results, more accurate translation. But individuals often have little understanding of how their data is being used, and consent is frequently buried in lengthy terms of service that nobody reads. I believe robust data protection legislation, meaningful transparency requirements, and genuine informed consent are necessary conditions for ethical data collection.

**7. How might AI change education in the future?**
AI tutors could provide personalised learning experiences tailored to each student's pace and knowledge gaps — something a classroom teacher with thirty students cannot realistically achieve. AI could also handle administrative tasks, freeing teachers to focus on mentorship and motivation, which are deeply human functions. However, there are real risks: an over-reliance on algorithmic personalisation could narrow students' intellectual horizons, and the social dimension of education — learning to collaborate and navigate disagreement — cannot be replicated by software.

**8. Do you think countries should collaborate on regulating AI, or should each country make its own rules?**
I believe international collaboration is essential, though politically difficult. AI systems operate across borders, and a patchwork of national regulations creates both gaps and opportunities for regulatory arbitrage — companies simply choosing to operate in whichever jurisdiction has the most permissive rules. Coordinated frameworks, similar to what exists for aviation safety or nuclear materials, would be far more effective. However, reaching consensus is challenging given significant differences in political values and economic interests between nations.

**9. What jobs do you think AI will never be able to replace?**
Roles that fundamentally depend on human empathy, ethical judgment, and physical presence seem most resilient. Nurses, therapists, social workers, and teachers fulfil needs that go beyond information processing — they offer genuine human connection and moral guidance. Similarly, jobs requiring improvisation in unpredictable environments — skilled trades, emergency response — remain difficult to fully automate. I think these roles will persist and may actually become more valued as AI handles more routine work.

**10. What responsibilities do technology companies have toward society?**
Technology companies have responsibilities that extend well beyond their shareholders. Given the scale of their influence over information, communication, and increasingly employment, they have an obligation to consider the social consequences of their products before deploying them. That includes transparent reporting on AI performance and risks, meaningful engagement with affected communities, proactive investment in the regions and workers displaced by their innovations, and cooperation with regulatory bodies rather than resistance to oversight.
