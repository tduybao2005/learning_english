# IELTS Academic Speaking — Test 27
**Time allowed: 11–14 minutes (approximately)**

---

## PART 1 — Introduction and Interview
*(4–5 minutes)*

The examiner will ask you questions about yourself and familiar topics.

---

### Topic 1: Decision-Making

**1.** Do you consider yourself a decisive person, or do you tend to take a long time to make decisions?

> *Sample answer:* I would say I fall somewhere in between. For everyday decisions — like what to eat or which route to take — I tend to decide fairly quickly. But for more significant choices, such as selecting a course of study or making a financial commitment, I prefer to take my time and weigh the options carefully. I think I've learned over the years that rushing important decisions often leads to regret.

**2.** Can you describe a time when you made a quick decision that turned out well?

> *Sample answer:* Yes, actually. A few years ago, I impulsively signed up for a public speaking workshop that a friend recommended at very short notice. I almost didn't go because I was nervous, but I'm glad I did — it significantly improved my confidence in presentations, which has been very useful in my academic life.

**3.** Do you think people rely too much on their emotions when making decisions?

> *Sample answer:* To some extent, yes. Emotions can provide useful intuitive signals, but they can also cloud judgement, particularly in high-pressure situations. I think the ideal approach is to use emotional responses as one input among many, rather than letting them dominate the decision-making process entirely.

**4.** Is it better to make decisions alone or to seek advice from others?

> *Sample answer:* I believe it depends on the nature of the decision. For deeply personal matters, individual reflection is important. But for complex decisions — especially those with far-reaching consequences — consulting others often reveals blind spots that you might not recognise on your own. A second perspective can be invaluable.

---

### Topic 2: Food and Diet

**5.** What kinds of food do you typically eat on a daily basis?

> *Sample answer:* My diet is fairly varied. I try to eat plenty of vegetables and lean protein, though I'll admit I have a weakness for noodle dishes. I try to avoid overly processed foods during the week, though I'm less strict about that on weekends.

**6.** Have your eating habits changed since you were a child?

> *Sample answer:* Considerably, yes. As a child I was quite a picky eater and avoided most vegetables. As I've grown older, I've become much more willing to try different foods and have genuinely come to enjoy things I used to refuse. I think maturity and travel both played a role in broadening my palate.

**7.** How important do you think it is to eat locally grown food?

> *Sample answer:* I think it is quite important, both for health and environmental reasons. Locally grown produce tends to be fresher and more nutritious because it hasn't been transported long distances. From an environmental perspective, reducing food miles helps lower carbon emissions. That said, I recognise that locally grown food isn't always affordable or accessible for everyone.

**8.** Do you think people in your country have a healthy diet overall?

> *Sample answer:* Broadly speaking, I think dietary habits have been improving, but there is still significant room for progress. The rise of fast food culture and busy urban lifestyles has led many people to rely on processed, convenient meals rather than cooking fresh food at home. Younger generations seem more aware of nutrition than previous ones, which is encouraging.

### Topic 3: Your Local Area

**9.** Can you describe the area where you currently live?

> *Sample answer:* I live in a mid-sized urban neighbourhood — fairly densely built, with a mix of residential apartments and small businesses. It's well-connected to public transport, which is one of its main advantages. There are a few green spaces nearby, though not as many as I'd like.

**10.** What do you like most about your local area?

> *Sample answer:* Probably the convenience and the sense of community. Most things I need on a daily basis are within walking distance, and the local market is particularly lively on weekends. I also appreciate that it's a relatively safe area where people look out for each other.

**11.** Is there anything you would change about where you live?

> *Sample answer:* If I could change one thing, it would be the amount of green space available. The neighbourhood is quite built-up and there are few parks or gardens. I think access to nature has a significant impact on mental wellbeing, and I would benefit from having more of it nearby.

**12.** How has your local area changed over the past few years?

> *Sample answer:* There has been quite a lot of development — new apartment buildings and commercial spaces have gone up, which has brought more amenities but also more traffic and noise. I have mixed feelings about it. The area feels more vibrant in some ways, but it has also lost some of its quieter, more residential character.

## PART 2 — Long Turn
*(3–4 minutes: 1 minute preparation + 1–2 minutes speaking)*

**Read the task card carefully and make notes. You will then speak for one to two minutes.**

---

### Task Card

**Describe a decision you made that turned out to be wrong.**

*You should say:*
- *what the decision was*
- *when you made it*
- *what happened as a result*
- *and explain what you learned from this experience*

---

### Sample Answer

One decision that turned out to be wrong was choosing to withdraw from a university module midway through the semester during my second year of study. At the time, I was feeling overwhelmed by the combined workload of four subjects and convinced myself that dropping one of them would free up enough time to perform well in the remaining three.

I made the decision fairly impulsively, without seeking advice from my academic tutor or reflecting carefully on the longer-term consequences. The immediate relief I felt was real — my stress levels dropped noticeably — but the repercussions became clear over the following months. The withdrawal appeared on my academic transcript, which required explanation during a subsequent internship application. More significantly, the subject I had dropped covered foundational material that was directly relevant to my final-year research project, and I had to spend considerable time independently catching up on content I had missed.

Looking back, I recognise that I made this decision through what psychologists might call the availability heuristic — I focused intensely on the most immediate and visible problem, which was my current stress, without adequately considering the broader picture. I also underestimated my capacity to manage the workload with better time management strategies.

What I learned from this experience is the value of pausing before making irreversible decisions, particularly when under emotional pressure. I now make a conscious effort to consult trusted advisors, consider the long-term implications of any significant choice, and explore all available alternatives before committing to a course of action. It was a costly lesson, but a genuinely formative one.

*(Approximately 270 words / 2 minutes)*

---

*Follow-up questions the examiner may ask after Part 2:*

- Did you discuss this decision with anyone at the time?
- Do you think you would make a different decision if you were in the same situation today?

## PART 3 — Discussion
*(4–5 minutes)*

The examiner will ask you questions connected to the topics raised in Part 2, focusing on more abstract issues.

---

### Theme: Democracy and Political Participation

**1.** Do you think young people today are sufficiently engaged in the political process?

> *Sample answer:* It varies considerably by country, but in general I would say young people are more politically aware than they are often given credit for — particularly on issues such as climate change and social justice. However, formal engagement, such as voting and joining political parties, does appear to be declining in many established democracies. I think this partly reflects disillusionment with traditional institutions rather than a genuine absence of political interest.

**2.** What factors do you think contribute to declining voter participation in many countries?

> *Sample answer:* Several factors seem to be at play. Political disillusionment is significant — when voters feel that no party genuinely represents their interests, or that their individual vote is unlikely to change outcomes, motivation to participate diminishes. Practical barriers also matter, such as inconvenient voting hours or registration requirements. Additionally, the spread of cynicism through social media, where negative portrayals of politicians are pervasive, may discourage participation by reinforcing the perception that politics is inherently corrupt.

**3.** How has social media changed the way people engage with political issues?

> *Sample answer:* Social media has democratised access to political information in some respects — individuals can now organise, campaign, and hold officials accountable in ways that were previously impossible. However, it has also created echo chambers in which people are primarily exposed to viewpoints that reinforce their own, and it has become a vehicle for misinformation that can distort public understanding of political issues. On balance, I think the effects are deeply mixed.

**4.** Some people argue that direct democracy, where citizens vote directly on all laws, would be more effective than representative democracy. Do you agree?

> *Sample answer:* I find this argument appealing in principle but problematic in practice. Direct democracy assumes that citizens have the time, information, and expertise to evaluate complex policy questions — assumptions that are difficult to sustain for the full breadth of issues governments must address. The Brexit referendum in the United Kingdom is often cited as an example of the dangers of applying a binary vote to a deeply complex issue without adequate public deliberation. I think representative democracy, while imperfect, provides a more realistic framework for governing complex modern states, provided representatives genuinely reflect the will of the people they serve.

**5.** How important is it for citizens to critically evaluate the information they receive from political leaders?

> *Sample answer:* Extremely important. Democratic governance depends on an informed citizenry capable of holding leaders accountable, and that accountability is only possible if citizens can distinguish accurate information from misleading claims. Critical evaluation — checking sources, considering multiple perspectives, and being alert to manipulation — is a civic skill that deserves a much more prominent place in formal education than it currently occupies in most countries.

**6.** Do you think the spread of disinformation online poses a serious threat to democratic governance?

> *Sample answer:* Yes, I think it is one of the most significant threats facing democracy today. Disinformation undermines the quality of collective decision-making by distorting the information environment in which citizens form their views. When voters are operating from fundamentally different and inaccurate factual premises, meaningful democratic deliberation becomes very difficult. The challenge is that addressing disinformation without restricting legitimate free expression is genuinely difficult, and solutions remain contested.

**7.** What role should education play in preparing citizens for democratic participation?

> *Sample answer:* Education should play a central role. Beyond civic knowledge — understanding how government works, what rights citizens have, and how to vote — education needs to cultivate critical thinking skills that enable people to evaluate evidence, identify bias, and engage constructively with those who hold different views. Many education systems currently do this inadequately. I believe media literacy and democratic civics should be treated as core competencies alongside numeracy and literacy.

**8.** Some people argue that economic inequality undermines democracy. To what extent do you agree?

> *Sample answer:* I think there is substantial truth in this argument. When wealth is concentrated in the hands of a small minority, political influence tends to follow — through campaign financing, lobbying, and media ownership. This can skew policy outcomes toward the interests of the wealthy at the expense of the majority, effectively distorting the democratic principle of equal political voice. That said, economic inequality and democratic dysfunction are not inevitable partners; strong institutional safeguards and robust campaign finance regulation can mitigate these effects.

**9.** How can governments encourage greater civic participation among marginalised communities?

> *Sample answer:* Several approaches have shown promise. Making voting more accessible — through extended voting hours, postal voting, automatic voter registration, and polling locations in accessible areas — removes practical barriers. Beyond logistics, governments can invest in civic education targeted at communities that have historically been excluded or discouraged from participation. Ensuring that policies actually address the concerns of marginalised groups is ultimately the most powerful incentive for engagement: when people believe that political participation can lead to meaningful change in their own lives, they are far more likely to participate.

**10.** Do you think international organisations should intervene when a country's democratic institutions are under threat?

> *Sample answer:* This is genuinely difficult. On one hand, democracy and human rights are recognised as universal values, and there is a strong moral case for international support when they are endangered. On the other hand, sovereignty is a foundational principle of international relations, and external intervention — even well-intentioned — can backfire, fuelling nationalist reactions that strengthen the very forces it sought to counter. I think graduated responses — beginning with diplomatic pressure, targeted sanctions, and support for civil society — are generally preferable to more direct forms of intervention, which should be reserved for the most egregious situations.
