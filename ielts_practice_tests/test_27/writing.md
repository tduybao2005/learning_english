# IELTS Practice Test 27 — Writing
**Chủ đề: Technology & Artificial Intelligence**

---

## TASK 1

**Đề bài:**
The line graph below shows internet usage rates (as a percentage of the total population) in five regions — North America, Western Europe, East Asia, South Asia, and Sub-Saharan Africa — between 2000 and 2020.

Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.

**Write at least 150 words.**

---

**Bài mẫu (Band 7–8, ~175 từ):**

The line graph illustrates internet penetration rates across five world regions over a twenty-year period from 2000 to 2020.

Overall, all five regions recorded significant increases in internet usage, though the rates and starting points varied considerably. North America and Western Europe began the period with the highest adoption rates and maintained their lead throughout, both reaching approximately 90 percent by 2020. East Asia experienced the most dramatic growth, rising from around 10 percent in 2000 to roughly 70 percent by the end of the period.

In contrast, South Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa started at comparably low levels — below 5 percent — and, while both regions showed clear upward trends, they remained significantly behind the others by 2020. South Asia reached approximately 45 percent, while Sub-Saharan Africa lagged furthest behind at around 30 percent.

The most notable trend is the widening gap that emerged in the early 2000s before partially narrowing after 2010, suggesting accelerating adoption in less-connected regions, though a substantial digital divide persisted throughout the period.

## TASK 2

**Đề bài:**
*"Artificial intelligence will eventually replace human workers in most industries. The benefits of this outweigh the drawbacks."*

To what extent do you agree or disagree?

**Write at least 250 words.**

---

**Bài mẫu (Band 7–8, ~280 từ):**

The prospect of artificial intelligence displacing the majority of human workers is a topic of intense debate. While I acknowledge that AI will transform many industries profoundly, I largely disagree that its benefits outweigh the drawbacks, primarily because the risks of widening inequality and social disruption are too significant to dismiss.

Proponents of AI-driven automation argue that it brings considerable advantages. By taking over repetitive and hazardous tasks, AI can improve productivity, reduce human error, and lower production costs. In healthcare, for example, AI diagnostic tools already assist doctors in detecting diseases more accurately and rapidly than traditional methods. From a purely economic standpoint, the efficiency gains are undeniable.

However, the social consequences of large-scale displacement are deeply concerning. Unlike previous technological revolutions, AI threatens not only manual occupations but also cognitive roles such as data analysis, legal research, and financial advising. Workers in these fields — often those who have invested years in education and training — face the prospect of sudden redundancy. Without adequate retraining programmes and social safety nets, this displacement could exacerbate poverty and deepen inequality between those who own or operate AI systems and those who are rendered economically redundant by them.

Furthermore, certain dimensions of human work — empathy, ethical judgment, and creative intuition — remain beyond the current capabilities of AI. A doctor's bedside manner or a teacher's ability to inspire cannot be replicated by an algorithm. Reducing these roles to pure efficiency metrics ignores the human value embedded in them.

In conclusion, while AI undoubtedly offers efficiency gains, the potential for social harm means the drawbacks, if unmanaged, could outweigh the benefits. The determining factor will be how proactively governments and institutions act to ensure a fair transition.
