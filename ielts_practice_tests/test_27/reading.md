## PASSAGE 1

**How Smartphones Changed Human Communication**

The smartphone, once considered a luxury item, has become one of the most transformative technologies in human history. Since the launch of the first touchscreen smartphone in 2007, these devices have fundamentally altered the way people interact, share information, and maintain relationships across geographical boundaries.

Before smartphones, communication was largely divided into two categories: synchronous, such as phone calls and face-to-face conversations, and asynchronous, such as letters and emails. Smartphones collapsed this distinction by enabling instant messaging platforms that blur the line between the two. Applications such as WhatsApp, Telegram, and iMessage allow users to send text, voice notes, images, and videos within seconds, creating a form of communication that is both immediate and flexible.

One of the most significant changes brought about by smartphones is the shift toward visual communication. Social media platforms like Instagram and Snapchat have popularised the sharing of images and short videos as a primary means of expression. Research suggests that humans process visual information approximately 60,000 times faster than text, which partly explains why visual content has become dominant in digital communication.

Smartphones have also changed the dynamics of group communication. Before these devices, organising a gathering required multiple phone calls or lengthy email chains. Today, group chats allow dozens of participants to coordinate in real time, making collective decision-making faster and more inclusive. Many workplaces now rely on platforms such as Slack and Microsoft Teams, which integrate messaging, file sharing, and video conferencing into a single interface.

However, critics argue that smartphone communication has eroded the quality of human interaction. Studies conducted at universities in the United States and the United Kingdom have found that the mere presence of a smartphone on a table during a conversation reduces the perceived quality of that conversation, even if the device is never used. This phenomenon, sometimes called "phone displacement," suggests that awareness of potential digital interruption undermines attentiveness.

Furthermore, the constant connectivity enabled by smartphones has created new psychological pressures. The expectation of immediate responses to messages has led to what researchers call "availability anxiety" — the stress associated with feeling obligated to be perpetually reachable. Young adults, in particular, report feeling guilty when they do not respond to messages quickly, even during personal or family time.

Despite these concerns, smartphones have also enabled forms of communication that were previously impossible. People with hearing impairments can communicate through video calls using sign language. Migrants can maintain close relationships with family members in distant countries through affordable video calls. Emergency services can be contacted from virtually any location. In this respect, smartphones have democratised communication, making powerful tools accessible to a wider population than ever before.

## PASSAGE 2

**Artificial Intelligence in Healthcare: Opportunities and Risks**

The integration of artificial intelligence into healthcare systems represents one of the most significant technological shifts of the twenty-first century. While AI offers remarkable opportunities to improve diagnostic accuracy, accelerate drug discovery, and personalise treatment, it also introduces substantial risks related to data privacy, algorithmic bias, and the erosion of the doctor-patient relationship.

**A.** AI-powered diagnostic tools have demonstrated impressive accuracy in detecting certain conditions. A landmark study published in *Nature Medicine* found that a deep learning algorithm could identify diabetic retinopathy in retinal photographs with a sensitivity comparable to that of trained ophthalmologists. Similarly, AI systems trained on thousands of dermatological images have shown the ability to classify skin lesions — including malignant melanomas — with accuracy rivalling that of dermatology specialists.

**B.** The application of AI in drug discovery has the potential to dramatically reduce the time and cost involved in developing new medicines. Traditional drug development is an extraordinarily slow process, often taking over a decade and costing billions of dollars. AI platforms can analyse vast chemical databases to identify promising molecular candidates and predict how they will interact with biological targets, effectively shortlisting compounds that are most likely to succeed in clinical trials.

**C.** Beyond diagnosis and drug discovery, AI is being used to personalise patient care. By analysing a patient's genetic profile, lifestyle data, and medical history, AI systems can help clinicians tailor treatment plans to individual patients rather than relying on generalised protocols. This approach, sometimes called precision medicine, is particularly relevant in oncology, where tumour biology can vary dramatically between patients.

**D.** Despite these advances, significant concerns remain about the reliability and fairness of AI systems in healthcare. A well-documented problem is algorithmic bias, which occurs when AI models trained on non-representative datasets produce results that are less accurate for certain demographic groups. Studies have shown, for instance, that some AI skin cancer detection tools perform less accurately on darker skin tones because the training data was composed predominantly of lighter-skinned patients.

**E.** Data privacy is another critical concern. AI systems in healthcare require access to enormous quantities of sensitive patient information. The storage, transmission, and use of this data create multiple points of vulnerability. High-profile breaches of healthcare data have already occurred in several countries, raising questions about whether existing regulatory frameworks are adequate to protect patients in an era of AI-driven medicine.

**F.** Perhaps the most philosophically complex issue is the potential impact of AI on the doctor-patient relationship. Medicine is not purely a technical discipline; it involves empathy, communication, and ethical judgment. There is a genuine concern that over-reliance on AI recommendations may cause clinicians to defer too readily to algorithmic outputs, reducing their own critical engagement. At the same time, patients may feel less comfortable discussing sensitive issues with a system perceived as impersonal.

## PASSAGE 3

**The Automation Paradox: Will Robots Take Our Jobs?**

Few questions in contemporary economics provoke as much debate as the impact of automation on employment. The fear that machines will render human workers obsolete is not new — it surfaced during the Industrial Revolution and again with the rise of computers in the twentieth century. Each time, dire predictions of mass unemployment failed to materialise. Instead, technology destroyed some categories of work while creating entirely new ones. Yet many economists argue that the current wave of automation, driven by artificial intelligence and robotics, is categorically different from anything that has come before.

The basis for this concern lies in the nature of AI capabilities. Earlier forms of automation were primarily physical: machines replaced human muscle in factories and farms. Computer automation later took over routine cognitive tasks such as basic accounting and data entry. But modern AI systems can now perform tasks that were once considered the exclusive domain of human intelligence — recognising faces, interpreting language, generating creative content, and making complex decisions. This expanded scope means that a far wider range of occupations is now potentially vulnerable.

A widely cited 2013 study by economists at the University of Oxford estimated that approximately 47 percent of US jobs were at high risk of automation within the following two decades. The study identified characteristics that made jobs susceptible: high levels of routine task content, limited requirement for social or creative skills, and physical tasks that could be performed in structured, predictable environments. Jobs at greatest risk included telemarketing, data entry, and loan processing.

However, the relationship between automation and employment is not simply one of substitution. Economists point to what is called the "productivity effect": when automation increases efficiency, it reduces costs, which can lower prices for consumers, stimulate demand, and ultimately create new jobs to meet that demand. The automobile industry provides a historical example. The introduction of assembly line robots reduced labour costs significantly, but the resulting growth of the industry created millions of jobs in manufacturing, sales, and maintenance.

The impact of automation is also highly unequal across different segments of the workforce. Workers in low-skilled, routine occupations face the greatest risk of displacement, while high-skilled professionals — whose work demands creativity, emotional intelligence, and complex reasoning — are considered relatively secure. This dynamic risks deepening existing economic inequalities, as those least equipped to retrain for new roles may find themselves permanently excluded from the labour market.

Governments and policymakers are increasingly aware of the need to manage this transition. Proposals range from large-scale investment in workforce retraining programmes and stronger social safety nets to more radical ideas such as a universal basic income — a guaranteed payment to all citizens regardless of employment status. Advocates of UBI argue that it would provide a financial buffer during periods of technological disruption, while critics contend that it is unaffordable and may reduce incentives to work.

What seems clear is that the future of work will not be determined by technology alone. Social choices — about education, regulation, and redistribution — will play an equally important role in shaping how the benefits and costs of automation are distributed across society.
