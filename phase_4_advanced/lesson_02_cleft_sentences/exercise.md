## SECTION B: Rewrite Using What-Cleft (Pseudo-Cleft)

**Instructions:** Rewrite each sentence using a What-cleft structure (What + S + V + is/are + focus). The underlined word(s) represent the focus element.

**Example:**
- Original: We need **more funding for medical research**.
- Answer: What we need is more funding for medical research.

---

1. The government must prioritise **affordable housing** in the next fiscal budget.

2. The report fails to address **the root causes of urban poverty**.

3. Many developing nations lack **reliable access to clean drinking water**.

4. Society often overlooks **the psychological toll of long-term unemployment**.

5. The committee proposed **the immediate suspension of the controversial programme**.

6. The professor emphasised **the importance of citing primary sources** in academic writing.

7. Climate scientists are calling for **a binding international agreement on carbon emissions**.

8. The article argues most convincingly **that economic growth alone cannot eliminate inequality**.

**Instructions:** Rewrite each What-cleft sentence as a Reversed What-cleft (focus element + is/are + what clause).

**Example:**
- Original: What she values most is **honesty**.
- Answer: Honesty is what she values most.

### Section D — Model Answers

**Context 1:** It-cleft — *It is lack of education, not poverty, that drives crime.*
Explanation: It-cleft is appropriate here because the speaker is correcting a specific misconception about which factor is responsible — emphasising a noun phrase that contrasts with another element ("not poverty").

**Context 2:** What-cleft — *What would most effectively reduce traffic congestion is investment in public transport infrastructure.*
Explanation: What-cleft suits an academic recommendation because it foregrounds the action/solution rather than correcting a specific named alternative. It sounds more deliberate and analytical.

**Context 3:** It-cleft — *It was last Monday that the announcement was made, not yesterday.*
Explanation: The focus is on a specific time expression. It-cleft is the natural structure for emphasising adverbials of time, especially when making a factual correction.

**Context 4:** It-cleft — *It is this phenomenon in a Southeast Asian context that no previous study has examined.*
Or: *It is in a Southeast Asian context that no previous study has examined this phenomenon.*
Explanation: It-cleft highlights the specific gap in the literature — a named location/context — making it ideal for emphasising a prepositional phrase.

**Context 5:** It-cleft — *It was a shortage of qualified personnel that caused the delay.*
Explanation: The cause is being identified and singled out. It-cleft is the standard choice for highlighting a cause noun phrase.

**Context 6:** What-cleft — *What students gain from a university education is the ability to think independently and critically.*
Explanation: The focus is on the outcome or benefit (the "thing gained") — a noun phrase that completes a process. What-cleft naturally frames this as a conclusion.

### Section E — Error Corrections

1. **Error:** Redundant pronoun "it" after "which".
   **Corrected:** It was the economic recession that caused millions of workers to lose their jobs. *(or: which caused — but no double pronoun)*

2. **Error:** Verb agreement — "What...recommends" is singular, so the verb should be "is", not "are".
   **Corrected:** What the report recommends is a complete overhaul of the healthcare system.

3. **Correct as written.** "Who" is the correct pronoun for "the younger generation" (people). No error. ✓

4. **Error:** "but are" should be "but" — the second clause shares the same subject (What concerns environmentalists) and verb (is), so "are" is redundant and grammatically wrong.
   **Corrected:** What concerns most environmentalists is not the rate of deforestation but the lack of political will to address it.

5. **Error:** Redundant subject pronoun "it" after "Britain".
   **Corrected:** It was during the Industrial Revolution that Britain became the world's leading economic power.

6. **Error:** Redundant pronoun "it" after "debating".
   **Corrected:** What the committee has been debating is whether to extend the deadline for public submissions.
