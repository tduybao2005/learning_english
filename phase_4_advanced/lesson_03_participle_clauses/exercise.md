## PHẦN H: SPEAKING / DISCUSSION PROMPT

*Trả lời miệng hoặc viết câu trả lời, dùng cấu trúc/từ vựng của bài.*

1. How do participle clauses contribute to the concision and elegance of academic writing?
2. Why is it essential to ensure that a participle clause's implied subject matches the subject of the main clause?
3. In what ways can dangling participles undermine the credibility of an academic or professional text?
4. Do you think learners of English as a foreign language are taught enough about participle clauses in formal education?

**Model answer (Band 7–8):** *Participle clauses represent one of the most effective tools for achieving the concision and syntactic sophistication that advanced academic writing demands. Having mastered their use, writers can compress complex relationships — of time, cause, and result — into compact phrases that enhance the text's overall flow. Recognised by examiners as indicators of grammatical range, these structures consistently distinguish Band 7 and Band 8 writing from more elementary responses. Acknowledging their potential while remaining vigilant against dangling participles is, therefore, an essential discipline for any writer aiming for the highest levels of formal precision.*

### Section B — Dangling Participle Corrections

1. **Problem:** "a clear correlation" cannot examine data.
   **Corrected:** Examining the long-term data, **the researchers** established a clear correlation between pollution levels and respiratory disease.

2. **Problem:** "a press conference" cannot approve a budget.
   **Corrected:** Having approved the new budget, **the minister** held a press conference to announce the decision.

3. **Problem:** "the experiment" cannot be trained.
   **Corrected:** Trained in classical methods, **the scientist** designed the experiment with exceptional rigour.

4. **Problem:** "the application" cannot receive a response.
   **Corrected:** Not having received a response, **the applicant** assumed their application had been rejected.

5. **This is acceptable** in formal English. "Considering" is used as an impersonal/dangling participle that functions like a prepositional phrase — widely accepted in academic writing. However, a stricter version: Considering the scale of the investment required, **investors** should not expect immediate returns.

6. **Problem:** "the extent of the destruction" cannot walk.
   **Corrected:** Walking through the flood-damaged neighbourhood, **we** became aware of the full extent of the destruction.

7. **Problem:** "the scientific community" did not publish the findings — the researchers did.
   **Corrected:** Having been published in a leading journal, the findings **were** enthusiastically embraced by the scientific community.

8. **Problem:** "the demand" does not operate the factory.
   **Corrected:** Operating at full capacity, **the factory** still could not meet the demand for the product.

### Section E — Error Corrections

1. **Error:** Dangling participle — Darwin wrote the book; "On the Origin of Species" didn't write itself.
   **Corrected:** **Writing** in 1859, **Darwin** challenged prevailing notions of divine creation **in** *On the Origin of Species*.

2. **Error:** "caused" should be "causing" (result participle after comma).
   **Corrected:** The researchers published their findings, **causing** significant controversy within the scientific community.

3. **Error:** Dangling participle — "the results" did not submit the application.
   **Corrected:** Having submitted the application, **the team** eagerly awaited the results.

4. **Error:** "but it had" — adding a full clause with a conjunction after a participle creates a structural error. Remove "but it":
   **Corrected:** The policy, designed with the intention of reducing inequality, **had the opposite effect** in practice.

5. **Error:** Dangling participle — "its impact" did not fail to understand.
   **Corrected:** Not having understood the full implications of the legislation, **policymakers** severely underestimated its impact on small businesses.

6. **This sentence is correct.** "Treated with the experimental drug for six months" is a past participle clause correctly modifying "the patients" (the subject of the main clause). The patients were treated (passive), and they were showing improvement. No dangling participle. ✓

### Section G — Model Rewritten Paragraph

> **Aware of the potential limitations of their methodology**, the researchers nonetheless proceeded with the study, believing the benefits outweighed the risks. **Having collected data from over two thousand participants across six countries**, they identified several unexpected trends. These trends, **initially considered to be statistical anomalies**, proved to be consistent across all demographic groups. **Having failed to account for cultural variables in the original study design**, the team required careful reinterpretation of their findings before publication. The paper, **eventually submitted to three peer-reviewed journals**, was accepted by all three.

**Changes made:**
1. "Although they were aware" → **"Aware of..."** (adjective used as reduced adverb clause)
2. "After they had collected data" → **"Having collected data"** (perfect participle)
3. "which were initially considered" → **"initially considered"** (reduced passive relative clause)
4. "Because the team had failed" → **"Having failed"** (perfect participle for reason)
5. "which was eventually submitted" → **"eventually submitted"** (reduced passive relative clause)
