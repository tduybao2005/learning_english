### TECHNOLOGY (10 questions)

**Q1.** How do you think the internet has changed the way people access information?

Framework:
- Signal: *"That's quite a profound shift actually..."*
- Position: Internet has democratised access to information in ways previously unimaginable
- Reason 1: Anyone with connectivity can access the same information as experts (e.g., medical research, legal documents)
- Concession: But volume and accessibility have also created a misinformation crisis
- Conclude: Net positive, but requires new critical literacy skills

*Starter:* "That's quite a profound shift, actually. I think the most significant change is that..."

---

**Q2.** Do you think technology is making people more or less creative?

Framework:
- Signal: *"That's a question I find genuinely interesting..."*
- Position: Mixed — depends on how technology is used
- Reason 1: Tools like design software, music production apps lower barriers to creative production
- Reason 2: BUT passive consumption (streaming, scrolling) can crowd out active creativity
- Conclude: Technology has creative potential that is often undermined by consumption habits

*Starter:* "I think the honest answer is that technology's effect on creativity is genuinely double-edged..."

---

**Q3.** What impact has social media had on political discourse?

Framework:
- Signal: *"This is quite a contested area, but my view is..."*
- Position: Broadly negative — has polarised debate and amplified extremism
- Reason 1: Algorithmic amplification rewards outrage over nuance
- Concession: Has also enabled social movements and given voice to marginalised groups
- Conclude: Potential is real, but current design incentivises division

*Starter:* "My overall assessment is that social media has had a profoundly ambivalent effect on political discourse..."

---

**Q4.** Should governments regulate what people can post online?

Framework:
- Signal: *"That's one of the central regulatory debates of our era..."*
- Position: Yes, targeted regulation — but not censorship
- Reason 1: Some content (child safety, coordinated disinformation) causes demonstrable harm
- Concession: Risk of government overreach in defining "harmful" content
- Conclude: Transparent, limited, harm-specific regulation — not broad content control

*Starter:* "I think the case for some degree of regulation is quite compelling, though the design of that regulation is critical..."

---

**Q5.** How might artificial intelligence change the role of teachers?

Framework:
- Signal: *"That's something educators are grappling with right now..."*
- Position: AI will change the role but not eliminate it
- Reason 1: AI can handle content delivery and assessment — freeing teachers for mentorship and motivation
- Reason 2: The irreplaceable human elements of education — empathy, inspiration — cannot be automated
- Conclude: Teachers will become more important as facilitators, less as information deliverers

*Starter:* "I think the honest answer is that AI will transform teaching rather than replace teachers..."

---

**Q6.** Do you think technology has made society more or less equal?

Framework:
- Signal: *"This is genuinely complex — the evidence points in different directions..."*
- Position: Technology has increased some equalities while widening others
- Reason 1: Internet access has democratised education and information for billions
- Reason 2: BUT automation has eliminated mid-skill jobs; wealth from technology has concentrated dramatically
- Conclude: Depends on whether access and benefits are distributed equitably — currently they are not

*Starter:* "I think technology has simultaneously created new equalities and deepened existing inequalities..."

---

**Q7.** Why might some people resist adopting new technology?

Framework:
- Signal: *"That's worth thinking about carefully, because the reasons are quite varied..."*
- Position: Multiple legitimate reasons — not simply ignorance or fear
- Reason 1: Justified scepticism about privacy and surveillance
- Reason 2: Economic barriers — not everyone can afford to upgrade; also loss of skills/identity for older workers
- Conclude: Resistance is sometimes rational and deserves respect, not dismissal

*Starter:* "I think it's too easy to characterise technology resistance as simply fear of change..."

---

**Q8.** Is online privacy becoming harder to maintain? Why?

Framework:
- Signal: *"I'm fairly convinced that it is, and the reasons are structural..."*
- Position: Yes, significantly harder — and by design
- Reason 1: Business models of major platforms are built on data collection and surveillance
- Reason 2: Vast majority of people lack either the knowledge or means to protect themselves
- Conclude: Privacy now requires active effort against systems designed to erode it

*Starter:* "I think online privacy has become almost illusory for the average person, and I'd argue this is largely by design..."

---

**Q9.** What are the potential risks of humans becoming too dependent on technology?

Framework:
- Signal: *"This is something I think about quite a bit, actually..."*
- Position: Serious risks — cognitive, social, and strategic
- Reason 1: Cognitive deskilling — GPS has reduced spatial reasoning; calculators have eroded mental arithmetic
- Reason 2: Systemic fragility — civilisations critically dependent on technology become extremely vulnerable to disruption
- Conclude: Dependence itself is not the risk — the risk is dependence without resilience or understanding

*Starter:* "I think the most concerning risks are not the ones that usually make headlines..."

---

**Q10.** How do you think the internet will develop over the next twenty years?

Framework:
- Signal: *"It's difficult to predict with confidence, but I'd speculate that..."*
- Position: More immersive, more integrated into physical reality, more AI-mediated
- Reason 1: AR/VR will blur boundaries between digital and physical environments
- Reason 2: AI will increasingly mediate our access to information — filtering, curating, personalising
- Conclude: More powerful and more intimate — which makes governance questions more urgent

*Starter:* "Predicting the internet's future is genuinely hazardous, but I imagine we'll see..."

## SECTION 2: MODEL ANSWERS for 5 Selected Questions (100–120 words each)

---

### Q1 (Technology): How has the internet changed the way people access information?

*"That's quite a profound transformation, actually. I think the most fundamental change is the democratisation of information — anyone with internet connectivity can now access the same academic research, news, and expert analysis that was previously available only to those with access to well-resourced libraries or expensive professional networks. This is genuinely revolutionary. However, I'd also say that this democratisation has come with a significant downside: the sheer volume of available information, combined with the collapse of trusted editorial gatekeepers, has created a misinformation ecosystem that is genuinely difficult to navigate. So the internet has given us unprecedented access to both extraordinary knowledge and extraordinary nonsense — distinguishing between the two now requires critical literacy skills that education systems haven't fully caught up with."*
*(~110 words)*

---

### Q11 (Education): Does the education system prepare young people for modern life?

*"My honest assessment is that most educational systems do some things very well and fall short in other important areas. They are generally effective at transmitting established knowledge — mathematics, science, literature — and at credentialling students for further education and employment. However, I think there are significant gaps. Critical thinking, financial literacy, emotional intelligence, and the ability to evaluate information in a saturated media environment are skills that most curricula neglect. What strikes me particularly is that the pace of change in the economy and in technology is outrunning the pace at which educational institutions can adapt. That said, I recognise that some systems are genuinely innovating — Finland and Singapore, for instance, have invested seriously in pedagogy reform."*
*(~115 words)*

---

### Q21 (Environment): Who bears more responsibility — individuals or governments?

*"I'd argue that governments bear the greater structural responsibility, while acknowledging that individuals are far from irrelevant. My reasoning is this: individual behaviour changes, however laudable, are structurally insufficient to address the scale of the problem. If I choose to cycle to work and eat less meat, that has a negligible effect on global emissions when entire industries are operating largely without carbon constraints. Governments, by contrast, have the power to set regulatory frameworks, price carbon emissions, and fundamentally reshape the incentive structures within which individuals and corporations operate. That said, governments ultimately respond to democratic pressure — which means informed, engaged citizens have an indirect but real influence over the policy choices that matter most."*
*(~113 words)*

---

### Q31 (Society): How is globalisation affecting local cultures?

*"I think globalisation's cultural effects are genuinely double-edged, and I'd resist characterising them as simply positive or negative. On one hand, the global spread of particular cultural products — Western media, fast food, English as a lingua franca — has undoubtedly eroded certain aspects of local distinctiveness. Walk into a shopping centre in almost any major city in the world and the brands and aesthetics are often remarkably similar. However, I find it particularly interesting that globalisation also creates conditions for cultural assertion and revival. When a minority culture feels threatened, communities often mobilise to preserve and celebrate it, and the internet has given small languages and traditions the ability to reach global audiences they never could before. So globalisation simultaneously homogenises and diversifies."*
*(~120 words)*

---

### Q41 (Health): Why are mental health issues becoming more widespread?

*"I think several distinct forces are converging here, and I'd be cautious about attributing the trend to any single cause. Social media is probably the most discussed factor, and the evidence that certain patterns of use — particularly passive consumption and social comparison — are associated with anxiety and depression is genuinely suggestive, particularly for adolescents. But I'd also point to broader structural changes: the weakening of traditional community networks — extended families, religious communities, stable long-term employment — has left many people navigating uncertainty with fewer support structures than previous generations had. There's also a legitimate argument that increased awareness and reduced stigma mean that conditions which existed previously are now being identified and counted. But I don't think that fully accounts for the trends we're observing."*
*(~120 words)*

### Bảng tự đánh giá Band Speaking Part 3:

| Tiêu chí | Band 5 | Band 6 | Band 7 | Band 8 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Fluency & Coherence** | Dừng lâu, nhiều filler (um, uh), không mạch lạc | Đôi khi dừng dài; ý tưởng nối được nhưng thiếu tự nhiên | Phần lớn trôi chảy; tự nhiên; dừng ngắn và có chủ đích | Hoàn toàn tự nhiên; không dừng không cần thiết; lưu loát như người bản ngữ |
| **Lexical Resource** | Từ vựng cơ bản, lặp nhiều từ giống nhau | Đủ dùng; một vài từ không phổ thông | Đa dạng; chính xác; biết dùng collocation | Tinh tế; từ vựng học thuật tự nhiên; idiomatic |
| **Grammatical Range** | Chủ yếu câu đơn; nhiều lỗi | Một số cấu trúc phức; vài lỗi | Dùng được nhiều cấu trúc; ít lỗi quan trọng | Cấu trúc phức và chính xác; lỗi rất hiếm |
| **Content Depth** | Câu trả lời quá ngắn hoặc lạc đề | Ý tưởng cơ bản; ít phát triển | Ý tưởng rõ ràng + được phát triển; có ví dụ | Phân tích sâu; liên kết khái niệm rộng hơn; nuanced |
| **Hedging & Nuance** | Không có hedging; ý kiến quá chắc chắn | Hedging đôi khi ("I think") | Hedging phong phú hơn; thừa nhận ý kiến đối lập | Hedging tinh tế; phức tạp; tự nhiên |
| **Length** | Dưới 30 giây | 30–45 giây | 50–70 giây | 70–90 giây, tự nhiên, không bị cố gắng |

### Mục tiêu theo IELTS Band:

| IELTS Target | Tiêu chí cần đạt |
|---|---|
| Band 6.0 | Tất cả tiêu chí ở Band 5–6; length 30–45 giây; vài từ không phổ thông |
| Band 6.5 | Phần lớn ở Band 6; length 45–60 giây; hedging xuất hiện; ít lỗi nghiêm trọng |
| Band 7.0 | Phần lớn ở Band 6–7; length 60–75 giây; từ vựng phong phú; có concession |
| Band 7.5+ | Phần lớn ở Band 7–8; length 70–90 giây; phân tích sâu; hedging tự nhiên |
