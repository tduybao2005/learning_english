### PASSAGE 1: "The Promise and Perils of Vertical Farming"

**Paragraph A**

Vertical farming — the practice of growing crops in vertically stacked layers, typically in controlled indoor environments — has emerged as one of the most debated innovations in contemporary agriculture. Proponents argue that it represents a solution to some of the most pressing challenges facing global food production: land scarcity, water consumption, and the vulnerability of conventional agriculture to climate change. Critics, however, question whether the energy demands of indoor farming can ever make it economically or environmentally viable at scale.

**Paragraph B**

Unlike conventional agriculture, which depends on sunlight, rainfall, and soil, vertical farms utilise LED lighting, hydroponic or aeroponic growing systems, and precise climate control to cultivate crops year-round regardless of external weather conditions. These controlled conditions allow for significantly reduced water usage — some vertical farming operations claim to use up to 95% less water than field farming — and eliminate the need for pesticides, as crops are isolated from outdoor pests and diseases. Space efficiency is another major advantage: a single hectare of vertical farm floor space can theoretically yield the equivalent of tens of hectares of conventional farmland, depending on the crop.

**Paragraph C**

The most frequently cited limitation of vertical farming is energy consumption. The artificial lighting and climate control systems required to operate indoor farms are enormously energy-intensive. Studies have shown that the carbon footprint of vertically farmed produce can exceed that of field-grown equivalents when energy is sourced from fossil fuels. Several companies in the sector have sought to address this by powering facilities with renewable energy, though this adds significantly to capital costs. As a result, vertical farming currently remains largely uneconomical for staple crops such as wheat, rice, and maize, and is instead most viable for high-value, fast-growing produce such as leafy greens, herbs, and strawberries.

**Paragraph D**

Despite economic constraints, vertical farming has found a foothold in certain urban contexts, particularly in countries with limited agricultural land such as Singapore and Japan, or in regions with extreme climates such as the Middle East. In Singapore, where 90% of food is imported, vertical farms represent a national food security strategy, supported by government subsidies and long-term policy commitments. In Japan, the aftermath of the 2011 Fukushima nuclear disaster — which contaminated significant areas of agricultural land — accelerated investment in controlled-environment agriculture as a means of producing food untainted by radioactive contamination.

**Paragraph E**

Technological innovation continues to reduce the barriers to vertical farming. The cost of LED lighting has fallen dramatically over the past decade, improving the energy economics of indoor cultivation. Research into photosynthetically efficient LED wavelengths — the precise spectrum of light that plants absorb most effectively — has led to systems that maximise plant growth while minimising electricity use. Automation and robotics are increasingly used to manage planting, harvesting, and quality control, reducing labour costs. Some researchers are also investigating the integration of vertical farms with urban buildings, using waste heat from offices or data centres to reduce heating costs.

**Paragraph F**

The long-term viability of vertical farming may ultimately depend on the trajectory of energy prices and the broader transition to renewable power. If solar and wind energy continue to decline in cost, the economic calculus for vertical farming shifts considerably. Some advocates predict that within two decades, vertical farms powered by cheap renewable energy could produce a meaningful proportion of urban food supplies, reducing dependence on long supply chains and the fuel required to transport produce from rural areas. Whether this vision is realistic or utopian depends heavily on policy choices, technological progress, and investment decisions that are yet to be made.

### PASSAGE 2: "Cognitive Biases and Decision-Making"

**Paragraph A**

Human beings have long prided themselves on their capacity for rational thought. Yet decades of research in psychology and behavioural economics have revealed that human decision-making is routinely distorted by systematic errors in thinking — patterns known as cognitive biases. These biases are not random mistakes but predictable tendencies that arise from the mental shortcuts, or heuristics, that the human brain uses to process information efficiently. While heuristics are often adaptive and effective, they can lead to poor judgements when applied inappropriately.

**Paragraph B**

Perhaps the most influential framework for understanding cognitive biases is that proposed by psychologist Daniel Kahneman, who distinguishes between two modes of thinking. System 1 thinking is fast, automatic, and intuitive — it operates below the level of conscious awareness and is responsible for rapid decisions and emotional responses. System 2 thinking, by contrast, is slow, deliberate, and logical — it requires conscious effort and is used for complex problem-solving. Kahneman and his collaborator Amos Tversky demonstrated that many cognitive biases arise from the inappropriate reliance on System 1 thinking in contexts that require System 2 analysis.

**Paragraph C**

Confirmation bias is among the most pervasive cognitive biases documented in research. It refers to the tendency to seek out, interpret, and remember information in ways that confirm one's pre-existing beliefs, while disregarding evidence that contradicts them. Confirmation bias has been documented across a wide range of domains, including political belief, medical diagnosis, and financial investment. Studies have shown that people presented with mixed evidence on a controversial topic will typically rate the evidence supporting their prior view as more credible and persuasive than evidence that challenges it.

**Paragraph D**

A related phenomenon is the anchoring effect, whereby an individual's judgements are disproportionately influenced by the first piece of information encountered — the "anchor" — even when that information is irrelevant or arbitrary. Classic experiments have shown that people's estimates of quantities can be dramatically influenced by an initial number they were asked to consider, even when told explicitly that the number was randomly generated. In commercial settings, the anchoring effect is routinely exploited: the practice of displaying a high "original price" alongside a discounted price makes the discounted price appear significantly more attractive than it would if presented alone.

**Paragraph E**

The availability heuristic leads people to judge the likelihood of an event based on how readily examples of it come to mind. Because dramatic or emotionally resonant events — plane crashes, shark attacks, terrorist incidents — receive disproportionate media coverage and are consequently more "available" in memory, people tend to overestimate the frequency of such events relative to statistically more common but less memorable risks, such as car accidents or cardiovascular disease. This distortion has significant implications for public policy, as governments may allocate resources according to perceived rather than actual risk levels.

**Paragraph F**

The sunk cost fallacy represents another category of systematic error, one with particularly significant financial and professional consequences. This bias leads individuals to continue investing in a course of action — whether a business project, a relationship, or an educational programme — simply because they have already committed significant resources, even when objective analysis clearly suggests that continuing is unwise. The irrational persistence motivated by past investment, rather than future prospects, can lead to compounding losses and missed opportunities. Training people to recognise the sunk cost fallacy and make forward-looking decisions has become an important element of financial and management education.

**Paragraph G**

Awareness of cognitive biases does not automatically eliminate their influence, but research suggests it can reduce their impact in certain circumstances. Structured decision-making frameworks that require explicit consideration of alternative hypotheses can help counteract confirmation bias. Seeking the perspective of individuals with different viewpoints — a practice known as red-team thinking — can expose hidden blind spots. The practice of "pre-mortem" analysis, in which decision-makers imagine that a project has already failed and work backwards to identify what went wrong, has been shown to reduce overconfidence in planning. These interventions are now widely applied in fields ranging from intelligence analysis to medical diagnosis.

### PASSAGE 3: "Rethinking Economic Growth"

**Paragraph A**

For most of the twentieth century, Gross Domestic Product — the total monetary value of goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given year — served as the principal measure of national prosperity and progress. Governments, international institutions, and media commentators treated rising GDP as virtually synonymous with improving quality of life. Yet critics have long argued that GDP is a fundamentally flawed measure of wellbeing, one that counts pollution cleanup, car accidents, and arms manufacturing as positive contributions to national wealth, while assigning no monetary value to volunteer work, household labour, environmental health, or social cohesion.

**Paragraph B**

The Easterlin Paradox, first identified by economist Richard Easterlin in 1974, presented early evidence that higher incomes do not indefinitely translate to greater happiness. Easterlin observed that although wealthier individuals within a given country tend to report higher life satisfaction than their less affluent peers, countries that become wealthier over time do not necessarily experience corresponding increases in average national happiness. This counterintuitive finding suggested that once basic material needs are met, the relationship between income and wellbeing weakens significantly — a phenomenon that subsequent researchers have continued to debate and refine.

**Paragraph C**

One early and influential alternative to GDP was developed in the small Himalayan kingdom of Bhutan. In the 1970s, the Fourth King of Bhutan proclaimed that "Gross National Happiness is more important than Gross National Product", inaugurating a national policy framework built around four pillars: sustainable development, cultural preservation, environmental conservation, and good governance. Bhutan's GNH index has attracted considerable international attention and has been cited by policymakers in countries ranging from New Zealand to Canada as an inspiration for broadening the metrics by which government success is measured.

**Paragraph D**

The United Nations Human Development Index (HDI), introduced in 1990, represented a more globally influential effort to move beyond GDP as a measure of national progress. The HDI combines GDP per capita with measures of life expectancy and educational attainment, offering a richer picture of development than economic output alone. However, the HDI has also been criticised for failing to capture dimensions of wellbeing such as inequality, environmental sustainability, and subjective life satisfaction. More recent composite indices, such as the OECD's Better Life Index and the Genuine Progress Indicator, have sought to address these limitations by incorporating a wider range of social and environmental factors.

**Paragraph E**

More radical is the "degrowth" movement, which argues that the pursuit of perpetual economic growth is not merely an inadequate measure of wellbeing but an active driver of environmental destruction and social inequality. Degrowth theorists contend that wealthy nations should deliberately reduce their economic throughput — the flow of materials and energy through the economy — in order to operate within ecological limits. This would involve shortening working hours, consuming less, and reorienting economic life around care, community, and sustainability rather than production and consumption. Critics of degrowth argue that it is politically unrealistic and could jeopardise employment levels and living standards, particularly in less affluent economies.

**Paragraph F**

Several national governments have begun incorporating wellbeing metrics into their formal budget processes, a trend sometimes called the "wellbeing economy" approach. New Zealand's Wellbeing Budget, introduced in 2019, explicitly prioritised government spending on mental health services, child wellbeing, and environmental sustainability. Scotland, Finland, and Iceland have developed similar frameworks and collaborate through the Wellbeing Economy Governments (WEGo) network. Proponents argue that these approaches produce more equitable and sustainable policy outcomes over the long term. Sceptics, however, question whether wellbeing can be measured with sufficient objectivity to guide public spending decisions, and raise concerns about the appropriate role of government in defining and managing citizens' happiness.

**Paragraph G**

The emergence of these alternative economic frameworks reflects a growing recognition among economists, policymakers, and the public alike that the exclusive focus on GDP growth is insufficient for addressing the complex challenges of the twenty-first century — among them, climate change, rising inequality, mental health crises, and the erosion of social trust. Whether these alternatives represent genuine transformations in economic thinking or merely rhetorical adjustments to existing systems remains contested. What is clear, however, is that the conversation about what economies are ultimately for, and what governments should optimise for, has fundamentally and perhaps irreversibly shifted.
