### Passage 1 — Vertical Farming

**Q1–5: TRUE / FALSE / NOT GIVEN**

**1. FALSE**
Paragraph B states that vertical farms use "up to 95% *less* water than field farming." The statement claims they require *more* water — the direct opposite.
*Trap:* Read carefully — "less" is easy to miss when scanning quickly.

**2. TRUE**
Paragraph B: "eliminate the need for pesticides, as crops are isolated from outdoor pests and diseases."
The question paraphrases "eliminate the need for pesticides" — the meaning is identical.

**3. TRUE**
Paragraph C: "the carbon footprint of vertically farmed produce can *exceed* that of field-grown equivalents when energy is sourced from fossil fuels."
This directly confirms the statement.

**4. FALSE**
Paragraph C explicitly states that wheat, rice, and maize are "uneconomical" for vertical farming; the passage says the most viable crops are "leafy greens, herbs, and strawberries." The statement claims the opposite.

**5. NOT GIVEN**
Paragraph D mentions that Singapore's farms receive "government subsidies" and that Japan "accelerated investment" after Fukushima, but the passage makes *no claim* about which government was "first" to subsidise vertical farming at scale.

---

**Q6–9: Matching Headings**

**6. Paragraph B → ii** *(How the core technology of vertical farming operates)*
Paragraph B explains the technical systems used in vertical farming: LED lighting, hydroponics, climate control, and space efficiency. The *main idea* is explaining how the technology works.

**7. Paragraph C → vi** *(The fundamental energy limitations of controlled-environment agriculture)*
Paragraph C focuses on the core problem of vertical farming: enormous energy consumption and the resulting carbon footprint. This is the *fundamental limitation*.

**8. Paragraph D → iv** *(Specific national contexts where vertical farming has taken hold)*
Paragraph D discusses Singapore, Japan, and the Middle East — specific countries and why vertical farming has succeeded there.

**9. Paragraph F → v** *(The potential impact of declining energy costs on future viability)*
Paragraph F centres on how the trajectory of renewable energy costs will determine vertical farming's long-term economic viability.

*Why incorrect headings don't fit:*
- i (Economic pressures and viable crop types): describes Paragraph C, not F or B
- iii (Policy solutions to energy problem): no paragraph focuses purely on this
- vii (Comparisons with greenhouse farming): this topic is not discussed anywhere

### Passage 2 — Cognitive Biases and Decision-Making

**Q14–18: TRUE / FALSE / NOT GIVEN**

**14. FALSE**
Paragraph B: "Kahneman and his *collaborator* Amos Tversky demonstrated..." — he clearly had a collaborator.

**15. TRUE**
Paragraph C directly confirms: "the tendency to seek out, interpret, and remember information in ways that confirm one's pre-existing beliefs."

**16. TRUE**
Paragraph D: "people's estimates...can be dramatically influenced by an initial number they were asked to consider, *even when told explicitly that the number was randomly generated*."

**17. FALSE**
Paragraph E states the availability heuristic causes people to *overestimate* events that receive dramatic media coverage (plane crashes, shark attacks), while *underestimating* car accidents. Car accidents are given as an example of a *underestimated* risk, not an overestimated one.
*This is a common trap — the statement reverses the direction of the bias.*

**18. TRUE**
Paragraph G: "The practice of 'pre-mortem' analysis...has been shown to reduce overconfidence."

**Q23–26: Multiple Choice**

**23. B** — *To describe practical approaches to reducing the impact of cognitive biases*
Paragraph G discusses structured decision-making frameworks, red-team thinking, and pre-mortem analysis — all *practical approaches* to reducing bias.

**24. B** — *Media coverage can distort perceptions of which events are most frequent*

This is never suggested anywhere in the passage. The other three options are all explicitly mentioned in Paragraph G.

**Q27–31: Matching Information to Paragraphs**

**27. D** — Paragraph D describes the Human Development Index (HDI), which "combines GDP per capita with measures of life expectancy and educational attainment."

**28. B** — Paragraph B presents the Easterlin Paradox: countries becoming wealthier do not experience corresponding increases in happiness.

**29. E** — Paragraph E describes the degrowth movement and its argument that wealthy nations should "deliberately reduce their economic throughput."

**30. F** — Paragraph F discusses New Zealand, Scotland, Finland, and Iceland incorporating wellbeing metrics into their budget processes.

**31. G** — Paragraph G: "the conversation about what economies are ultimately for...has fundamentally and perhaps irreversibly shifted."

**Q32–36: TRUE / FALSE / NOT GIVEN**

**32. FALSE**
Paragraph A explicitly states GDP assigns "no monetary value to volunteer work, household labour, environmental health, or social cohesion." The statement claims these *are* included — the opposite of what the passage says.

**33. NOT GIVEN**
Paragraph B says wealthier individuals "tend to report higher life satisfaction" — the word "tend" is important. The statement uses "always," which is an absolute that the passage never makes.

**34. NOT GIVEN**
Paragraph C says New Zealand *cited* Bhutan's GNH as "an inspiration" — not that it officially adopted GNH as national policy. Inspiration ≠ adoption.

**35. TRUE**
Paragraph D: the Genuine Progress Indicator, among other indices, "sought to *address these limitations*" of the HDI.

**36. FALSE**
Paragraph E: "Critics of degrowth *argue that it is politically unrealistic* and could jeopardise employment levels and living standards." The passage clearly shows not all economists agree — some explicitly oppose degrowth.

### Task 1 Model Answer — Band 7.5 (~175 words)

The table illustrates how household internet access changed across five countries — South Korea, Vietnam, Nigeria, Brazil, and Germany — over a twenty-year period from 2000 to 2020.

Overall, internet connectivity increased substantially in all five countries, though significant differences existed in both starting points and the rate of growth. Developed economies — South Korea and Germany — began the period with comparatively high rates and achieved near-universal connectivity by 2020.

South Korea demonstrated the most rapid early adoption, climbing from 44% in 2000 to 83% by 2010, and eventually reaching 96% in 2020. Germany followed a similar trajectory, rising from 30% to 92% over the same period. Among developing nations, Vietnam showed the most impressive proportional increase, rising from just 1% to 70%, marginally outpacing Brazil, which grew from 3% to 74%. Nigeria, despite starting from a negligible base of 0.1%, nonetheless reached 61% by 2020, reflecting substantial infrastructure investment over the two decades.

### Task 2 Model Answer — Band 7.5 (~275 words)

The idea that young people should be required to complete a year of community service before entering university or employment has both compelling merits and significant practical drawbacks. While I appreciate the social value of such a scheme, I ultimately believe it should be incentivised rather than compulsory.

There are strong arguments in favour of mandatory service. Firstly, it would expose young people to social realities beyond their immediate personal experience, cultivating empathy and civic responsibility at a critical formative stage of life. A school leaver who has worked in a healthcare facility, for instance, is likely to develop a deeper appreciation for public welfare systems and the vulnerabilities of others. Secondly, structured youth service programmes can provide genuine benefit to communities, supplementing underfunded public services. Countries such as Germany, where voluntary civil service — the Freiwilliges Soziales Jahr — has been widely practised, suggest that well-designed schemes can generate real social value for both participants and recipients.

Nevertheless, I would argue against making service compulsory. Requiring a full year before education or career entry could be profoundly inequitable: young people from disadvantaged backgrounds may simply be unable to sustain twelve months of unpaid or low-paid service without compromising their own or their family's financial stability. Moreover, the effectiveness of community contribution is arguably undermined when coerced; meaningful volunteerism requires genuine motivation rather than legal obligation.

In conclusion, while the spirit behind mandatory community service is admirable, its compulsory application would risk entrenching existing inequalities. A more equitable approach would be to incentivise voluntary participation through scholarship credits, university admission recognition, or employer schemes — rewarding commitment without penalising those who cannot afford it.

### Phân tích lỗi phổ biến trong đề này:

**Reading thường sai:**
- Q1: Đọc nhầm "less water" thành "more water" (sai hướng)

**Writing thường sai:**
- Task 1: Mô tả từng số liệu riêng lẻ thay vì nhóm và so sánh
- Task 1: Thiếu câu overview (tổng quan)
- Task 2: Không có luận điểm cá nhân rõ ràng (just "discuss" mà không "give your opinion")
- Task 2: Đoạn body quá ngắn, thiếu ví dụ cụ thể

**Speaking Part 3 thường sai:**
- Câu trả lời dưới 30 giây
- Không có hedging ("I think definitely..." thay vì "I'd be inclined to say that...")
- Không có concession (không thừa nhận ý kiến đối lập)
- Dùng "good/bad/nice" thay vì từ vựng chính xác hơn
