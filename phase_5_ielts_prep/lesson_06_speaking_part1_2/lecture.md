# BÀI 6: IELTS SPEAKING PHẦN 1 & 2

---

## TỔNG QUAN VỀ IELTS SPEAKING

Bài thi IELTS Speaking kéo dài **11–14 phút**, diễn ra trực tiếp với một giám khảo người bản ngữ. Bài thi được chia thành **3 phần** và được đánh giá theo **4 tiêu chí**, mỗi tiêu chí chiếm **25%** tổng điểm:

| Tiêu chí | Mô tả |
|---|---|
| **Fluency & Coherence** | Nói trôi chảy, liên tục; ý tưởng liên kết mạch lạc |
| **Lexical Resource** | Vốn từ phong phú, đa dạng, dùng đúng ngữ cảnh |
| **Grammatical Range & Accuracy** | Cấu trúc ngữ pháp đa dạng, chính xác |
| **Pronunciation** | Phát âm rõ ràng, tự nhiên, không ảnh hưởng đến hiểu |

### Cấu trúc 3 phần:

| Phần | Thời gian | Nội dung |
|---|---|---|
| **Part 1** | 4–5 phút | Giới thiệu bản thân + câu hỏi về các chủ đề quen thuộc |
| **Part 2** | 3–4 phút | Monologue dài về một chủ đề cho sẵn (cue card) |
| **Part 3** | 4–5 phút | Thảo luận chuyên sâu về các vấn đề trừu tượng |

**Công thức vàng:** Direct answer + Reason + Example or Personal detail + Follow-up thought

#### WORK / STUDY (Questions 1–5)

**Q1. Do you work or study?**

"I'm currently a student at a university in Ho Chi Minh City, where I'm majoring in International Business. It's quite an intensive programme — we have a heavy workload most semesters — but I genuinely enjoy it because the subject matter is so relevant to the real world. I can already see how the theories I'm studying apply to Vietnamese businesses going global."

**Q2. What subject do you study? / What do you do for work?**

"I study International Business, which covers everything from marketing strategy to cross-cultural management and global finance. What I find particularly interesting is the international dimension — understanding how companies adapt their products and communication style for different markets. It's opened my eyes to how interconnected the modern economy really is."

**Q3. Why did you choose that subject/career?**

"Honestly, it was a combination of personal interest and practical thinking. I've always been fascinated by how businesses operate beyond their home country, and I figured a degree in International Business would give me skills that are in high demand. Vietnam is integrating more deeply into the global economy every year, so I felt this field had real long-term prospects."

**Q4. What aspect of your studies/work do you find most challenging?**

"Probably the group projects, to be honest. Working with people who have different communication styles and work habits requires a lot of patience and coordination. That said, I think it's also one of the most valuable aspects — learning to collaborate effectively is essential for any career, and it's a skill I'm actively trying to develop."

**Q5. Would you like to change your career in the future?**

"It's hard to say with certainty. Right now, I'm focused on building a solid foundation in business, but I could see myself moving into entrepreneurship down the line. I have a few ideas for start-ups that I think could address real gaps in the Vietnamese market. Whether that happens depends on how the next few years unfold."

#### HOMETOWN (Questions 6–10)

**Q6. Where are you from originally?**

"I grew up in Da Nang, which is a coastal city in central Vietnam. It's often described as one of the most liveable cities in the country, and I'd have to agree — it has beautiful beaches, a relaxed atmosphere, and a very welcoming community. Moving to Ho Chi Minh City for university was quite a contrast!"

**Q7. What do you like most about your hometown?**

"Without a doubt, the food. Da Nang has this incredible local cuisine that you just don't find anywhere else — dishes like Mi Quang and Banh Mi Hoi An that are completely unique to the region. Whenever I go back to visit, I spend an embarrassing amount of time eating. It's like a food pilgrimage every time."

**Q8. Has your hometown changed much in recent years?**

"Dramatically, yes. When I was a child, Da Nang was a much quieter place. Over the past decade, it's transformed into a major tourist destination — there are new hotels, international restaurants, and infrastructure improvements everywhere you look. On the positive side, the economy has thrived, but I do sometimes miss the quieter, more intimate atmosphere it used to have."

**Q9. Would you like to return to your hometown in the future?**

"I think so, yes, eventually. At the moment I want to gain professional experience in a bigger city, and perhaps even work abroad for a few years. But in the long run, Da Nang feels like home — the pace of life there suits me, and I'd love to raise a family in a place with clean air and access to the sea."

**Q10. Is your hometown a good place to live?**

"I genuinely think it is, for most people. The cost of living is much lower than Hanoi or Ho Chi Minh City, the people are incredibly friendly, and the natural environment is stunning. For families and older people especially, I'd say it's close to ideal. The only drawback might be the limited career opportunities in specialised fields, which is why many young people like me end up leaving for bigger cities."

#### TECHNOLOGY (Questions 11–15)

**Q11. How often do you use the internet?**

"Constantly, I'd say — it's just completely embedded in my daily routine at this point. I use it for research, streaming music and films, keeping up with the news, and communicating with friends and classmates. I can't honestly imagine going a full day without it, which probably says something about how dependent we've all become on digital connectivity."

**Q12. What do you mainly use your smartphone for?**

"A mix of practical and leisure activities. On the practical side, I use it for messaging, scheduling, and accessing my university's learning platform. For leisure, I probably spend more time than I should on YouTube and podcasts. I've recently been using it to listen to English podcasts during my commute, which I've found is a really efficient way to improve my listening skills."

**Q13. Do you think technology has made people's lives better?**

"On balance, I think it has, though the picture is more complicated than a simple yes or no. Technology has given us instant access to information, made communication effortless across distances, and created opportunities that simply didn't exist a generation ago. At the same time, I think we're only beginning to understand the downsides — things like social media anxiety, declining attention spans, and digital inequality."

**Q14. Would you find it difficult to give up technology for a week?**

"Professionally, it would be incredibly disruptive — so much of my coursework is submitted online, and group projects are coordinated through messaging apps. Personally, though, I think I'd actually enjoy aspects of it. I sometimes feel overwhelmed by the constant stream of notifications, and I suspect a week offline might be surprisingly refreshing."

**Q15. What kind of technology do you think young people use most?**

"Smartphones, without question. They've essentially become an extension of our social lives — most young people I know check their phones dozens of times a day. Beyond that, I'd say streaming platforms and gaming are hugely popular. The shift away from traditional television has been remarkable, even in the few years I've been at university."

#### FREE TIME & HOBBIES (Questions 16–20)

**Q16. What do you like to do in your free time?**

"I have a few different interests depending on my mood and energy levels. When I want something active, I go for a run or play badminton with friends — it clears my head after a long day of studying. When I want to wind down, I tend to read, or I'll watch a documentary. I find documentaries particularly satisfying because I'm learning something while relaxing."

**Q17. Have your hobbies changed since you were a child?**

"Yes, quite significantly. As a child I was absolutely obsessed with football — I played every chance I got. These days I still enjoy watching it, but I rarely play. University life has introduced me to new interests: I've developed a real passion for reading non-fiction, and I've started learning the guitar, which I never would have predicted when I was younger."

**Q18. Do you prefer spending your free time indoors or outdoors?**

"It honestly depends on the season. During the cooler months in Ho Chi Minh City, I much prefer being outside — going to parks or exploring different neighbourhoods. But during the intense heat of summer, I tend to retreat indoors. I don't think I have a strong preference either way — what matters more is the activity and who I'm spending time with."

**Q19. Is it important to have hobbies?**

"I think it's genuinely important, yes. Hobbies provide a counterbalance to the pressures of work and study — they give you something that's purely yours, without the stress of performance or evaluation. I've noticed that when I'm regularly doing things I enjoy, my concentration during study sessions improves noticeably. There's definitely a psychological benefit to having interests outside of academic or professional obligations."

**Q20. What hobbies are popular with young people in Vietnam?**

"Gaming is enormous — Vietnam has a very active esports community, and many young people aspire to compete professionally. Beyond that, coffee culture has become a real social activity; going out to a café is as much about the experience as the coffee itself. Badminton is popular as an accessible outdoor sport, and I've noticed a recent surge in interest in yoga and fitness, particularly among young women."

#### FOOD (Questions 21–25)

**Q21. What is your favourite type of food?**

"It's hard to pick just one, but if I had to choose, I'd say Vietnamese home cooking. Not the elaborate restaurant dishes, but the everyday food that families prepare — simple things like canh chua, braised pork with eggs, or a really good bowl of bun bo Hue. There's a comfort and familiarity to it that no other cuisine quite replicates for me."

**Q22. Do you enjoy cooking?**

"I enjoy it more than I expected to when I first started living alone at university. I've learned a handful of dishes that I can prepare reasonably well, and there's something genuinely satisfying about eating food you've cooked yourself. That said, I'm not a passionate cook — I do it out of practicality and occasional creativity rather than real culinary enthusiasm."

**Q23. Has the way people eat in Vietnam changed in recent years?**

"Dramatically. There's been a significant shift towards international food — Japanese, Korean, and Western chains have proliferated in major cities, particularly among younger generations. At the same time, there's a parallel trend towards health-consciousness: plant-based eating is growing, and organic markets have become much more common. Traditional street food culture still thrives, but it now coexists with a much more globalised food landscape."

**Q24. Do you think it's important to try food from other cultures?**

"Absolutely. Food is such an accessible entry point into understanding a different culture — the ingredients people use, the way meals are structured, the social rituals around eating tell you so much about a society's values and history. Beyond the cultural dimension, trying different cuisines simply expands your palate and makes life more interesting. I'd encourage anyone to be adventurous with food."

**Q25. Do you prefer eating at home or in restaurants?**

"It depends on the occasion. For everyday meals, I prefer eating at home — it's cheaper, healthier, and more comfortable. But restaurants serve a different social function: they're spaces for conversation, celebration, and discovering new flavours. Some of my most memorable experiences have been at restaurants, particularly when I've tried something I'd never encountered before. Both have their place, I think."

#### SOCIETY & ENVIRONMENT (Questions 26–30)

**Q26. Do you think people are more or less concerned about the environment than they used to be?**

"I think awareness has increased enormously, driven largely by social media and high-profile events like extreme weather. However, I'm not sure this awareness always translates into meaningful behaviour change. People understand that climate change is serious, but the gap between knowing and acting remains wide. I'd say concern has increased, but consistent action hasn't kept pace with that concern."

**Q27. What environmental problems concern you most?**

"Climate change is the overarching issue, but what concerns me most on a day-to-day level is plastic pollution. Vietnam's coastlines and waterways are heavily affected, and the scale of the problem is visible everywhere. It's distressing because the solutions exist — better waste management, reduced single-use plastic — but implementation is slow. I try to minimise my own plastic use, though I recognise individual action alone won't solve a systemic problem."

**Q28. What can individuals do to protect the environment?**

"The most impactful changes are usually the least glamorous: reducing meat consumption, cutting unnecessary air travel, switching to public transport, and consuming less generally. Beyond personal choices, I think civic engagement matters enormously — supporting policies that address environmental issues, even when they're inconvenient, is perhaps the most effective thing an individual can do. Consumer pressure on companies also has a demonstrable effect."

**Q29. Do you think young people care more about social issues than older generations?**

"There's some truth to that stereotype, but I'd be cautious about overgeneralising. Younger people have grown up with social media, which amplifies social issues and makes it easier to engage with causes. But I know many older people who are deeply committed to environmental and social justice causes — often more so than young people, because they've seen problems develop over decades and understand the long-term stakes better."

**Q30. Is there a social problem in your country that you think deserves more attention?**

"Mental health, without question. In Vietnam, there's still a significant stigma attached to mental health struggles — people are often expected to cope privately or are told their problems are a sign of weakness. The resources available are also very limited compared to what's needed. As academic and economic pressures increase, particularly for young people, I think the failure to adequately address mental health is becoming a serious social issue."

### USEFUL LANGUAGE FOR PART 1

**Natural fillers (use sparingly):**
- "Well, that's a good question..."
- "To be honest with you..."
- "Let me think about that for a moment..."
- "That's quite an interesting one..."

**Extending your answer:**
- "What's more, I think..."
- "And on top of that..."
- "Not only that, but..."
- "Building on that thought..."
- "What I find particularly interesting is..."

**Personal framing:**
- "From my perspective..."
- "In my experience..."
- "Speaking personally..."
- "As far as I'm concerned..."

**Hedging (for uncertainty):**
- "I'd say..."
- "As far as I know..."
- "If I'm honest..."
- "I'm not entirely sure, but I think..."

## PHẦN 2: LONG TURN / CUE CARD

### Tổng quan:
- Giám khảo đưa cho bạn một **cue card** với một chủ đề và **4 bullet points** gợi ý
- Bạn có **1 phút để chuẩn bị** (BẮT BUỘC phải ghi chú!)
- Nói **1–2 phút** liên tục
- Giám khảo có thể hỏi **1–2 câu follow-up** ngắn

### Chiến lược chuẩn bị trong 1 phút:
1. Đọc kỹ **4 bullet points** — tất cả phải được đề cập
2. Quyết định: Who? What? When? Where? Why memorable?
3. Chọn **thì động từ** phù hợp (quá khứ cho sự kiện đã qua, hiện tại cho thói quen)
4. Ghi **4–5 từ khóa** (KHÔNG ghi câu đầy đủ — tốn thời gian)
5. Lên kế hoạch **câu mở đầu** và **câu kết thúc**

### Template cấu trúc Part 2:

| Đoạn | Nội dung | Từ khóa gợi ý |
|---|---|---|
| **Opening** | Giới thiệu chủ đề | "I'd like to talk about..." / "The [topic] I'd like to describe is..." |
| **Para 1 — What/Who** | Mô tả đối tượng chính | Who are they? What is it? What does it look like? |
| **Para 2 — Context** | Bối cảnh / hoàn cảnh | When? Where? Under what circumstances? |
| **Para 3 — Detail/Story** | Chi tiết / câu chuyện thú vị | The specific details that made it memorable |
| **Para 4 — Feelings** | Cảm xúc / phản ánh | How did it affect you? Why does it matter? |
| **Closing** | Kết luận | "And that's why [topic] has stayed with me." |

**CUE CARD 1: "Describe a teacher who had a significant influence on you."**
*You should say: who this person was / what subject they taught / what they did that influenced you / and explain why they had such an impact on you.*

"I'd like to talk about my secondary school English teacher, Ms. Linh, who I believe had a more profound influence on my life than she probably realises.

Ms. Linh taught English at my school in Da Nang, and from the very first lesson it was clear she approached her subject differently from most teachers. Rather than drilling grammar rules, she encouraged us to read English novels and discuss them, to write journals, and to watch films in English without subtitles. At the time, many of us found this challenging and somewhat unconventional, but looking back, it was genuinely transformative.

What influenced me most was her absolute conviction that language learning should be about connecting with ideas, not just mastering a system of rules. She treated us as intellectuals capable of engaging with complex texts, and that respect changed the way I saw myself as a learner. I remember the first time I read a short story in English and actually felt something — that was largely due to the reading habits she had cultivated in us.

The impact she had on my life has been enormous. The confidence she gave me in using English has opened professional doors and allowed me to engage with a global community of ideas. Whenever I make progress in a language exam or have a meaningful conversation with someone from another culture, I think of her. She showed me that language is not a subject to study — it's a world to inhabit. And that's why she remains the most influential teacher I've ever had."

**CUE CARD 2: "Describe a time when you learned something new that surprised you."**
*You should say: what you learned / how you learned it / why it surprised you / and explain what effect this learning had on you.*

"The experience I'd like to describe happened about two years ago when I stumbled upon the field of behavioural economics, which completely changed the way I think about human decision-making.

I had been studying standard economics, which largely assumes people make rational, calculated decisions. Then a friend recommended a book called 'Thinking, Fast and Slow' by Daniel Kahneman. I picked it up without high expectations, thinking it would be another dense academic text.

What surprised me deeply was discovering how irrational humans actually are in their decision-making — systematically and predictably so. Kahneman describes how our minds operate on two systems: a fast, intuitive system prone to biases, and a slower, more deliberate reasoning system. The examples he gives are startling: how the way a question is framed changes our answer entirely, how we overweight recent information, how we're loss-averse in ways that lead us to make objectively worse choices.

What made it so surprising was the realisation that I recognised every bias in myself. I had assumed I was a reasonably rational thinker, but the evidence in the book made it clear that no one is exempt from these cognitive shortcuts.

The effect has been lasting. I now try to pause before making significant decisions — to ask myself which system I'm operating in. It's also made me much more interested in psychology and human behaviour as a field of study. That single book genuinely altered how I see the world, which is a remarkable thing for a few hundred pages to accomplish."

**CUE CARD 3: "Describe a place you would like to visit in the future."**
*You should say: where this place is / why you want to visit it / what you would do there / and explain what you think the experience would be like.*

"The place I'd most like to visit in the future is Japan, specifically Kyoto during the cherry blossom season in late March and early April.

I've been fascinated by Japan for years, drawn initially by its popular culture — anime and film — but increasingly by its history, aesthetics, and philosophy. Kyoto in particular represents something I find profoundly appealing: the coexistence of deep historical tradition with meticulous modern organisation. It's a city where ancient temples sit alongside high-speed railway stations, and that layering of time seems remarkable.

If I were able to go, I'd want to spend at least two weeks there. I'd visit the famous temples, of course — Fushimi Inari with its thousands of torii gates, and the Arashiyama bamboo forest — but I'd also want to wander aimlessly through the Gion district, trying local food at small family restaurants rather than tourist-oriented establishments. I'd particularly want to experience a traditional tea ceremony, which I think embodies something about Japanese culture — the elevation of the ordinary into something meditative and meaningful — that I find deeply admirable.

I imagine the experience would be one of pleasurable disorientation. As a Vietnamese person, I'm used to a certain kind of organised chaos; Japan, from everything I've read, seems to operate on entirely different principles of social harmony and aesthetic restraint. I think immersing myself in that would be both challenging and genuinely eye-opening. It's a trip I'm absolutely determined to make within the next few years."

**CUE CARD 4: "Describe an important decision you made."**
*You should say: what the decision was / when and why you made it / what the consequences were / and explain how you feel about that decision now.*

"I'd like to talk about the decision I made two years ago to pursue a self-directed intensive English study programme alongside my university studies. At the time, it felt like a significant commitment, and it's turned out to be one of the most consequential choices I've made.

The context was this: I was in my second year of university and had been studying English formally for years, but I recognised that my conversational ability and my vocabulary for academic and professional contexts were both quite limited. I couldn't comfortably read academic papers, and speaking felt stilted and unnatural. I decided to radically increase my English exposure — setting aside two hours every day for reading English content, listening to podcasts, and practising speaking through language exchange apps.

The decision was difficult to stick to initially. University already demands a great deal, and two additional hours each day meant sacrificing social time and sleep. There were weeks when I questioned whether it was worth the effort.

The consequences, however, have been transformative. My reading speed in English has increased dramatically; I now read for pleasure in English rather than treating it purely as a chore. My academic writing has improved noticeably, and my confidence in speaking — as evidenced by the fact that I'm now preparing for IELTS — has grown enormously.

Looking back, I feel deeply glad I made that decision. The discipline it required has also reinforced a broader lesson: that consistent, deliberate effort over a long period produces results that feel almost miraculous from the starting point."

**CUE CARD 5: "Describe a skill you would like to improve."**
*You should say: what the skill is / why you want to improve it / what you would do to develop it / and explain how improving it would benefit you.*

"The skill I'd most like to improve is public speaking — the ability to speak confidently and persuasively in front of an audience, whether in a professional meeting, an academic presentation, or a more formal setting.

At the moment, I consider myself a reasonable communicator in small group settings, but when speaking to a larger audience, I find that my confidence decreases noticeably. I become more conscious of how I sound, I speak faster, and I lose the natural expressiveness that characterises my normal conversation. This is something I'm genuinely keen to address.

My interest in improving stems from a clear professional aspiration. I want to work in international business, a field where the ability to present ideas compellingly — to clients, partners, or teams — is enormously valuable. Leaders who can speak well command more authority, inspire more trust, and are simply more effective at making things happen.

My plan for developing this skill involves several steps. I've already joined a university debate club, which provides regular low-stakes opportunities to speak in front of a small audience. I'm also watching recordings of TED talks and specifically analysing the speakers' techniques — their pacing, use of pausing, eye contact, and storytelling structure. I plan to record myself speaking and critically review the recordings.

I believe improving this skill would open significant opportunities. But beyond the career benefits, there's something personally meaningful about developing the courage to speak with clarity and conviction. It connects deeply to the kind of person I want to become."

**CUE CARD 6 (Brief outline): "Describe a film or book that made a strong impression on you."**
- Opening: Name of film/book and when encountered it
- Para 1: Brief synopsis — what the story is about
- Para 2: Specific scene/moment/passage that affected you most
- Para 3: The idea or emotion it provoked — what it made you think or feel
- Para 4: How it changed your perspective or stayed with you
- Closing: Why it remains significant

**CUE CARD 7 (Brief outline): "Describe a time when you helped someone."**
- Opening: Set the scene — who, when, context
- Para 1: What the person's situation or difficulty was
- Para 2: What you actually did to help — specific actions
- Para 3: The outcome — how things changed for that person
- Para 4: How the experience made you feel; what you learned from it
- Closing: Why being able to help someone matters to you

---

**CUE CARD 8 (Brief outline): "Describe a celebration or festival that is important in your culture."**
- Opening: Name the festival (e.g., Tết Nguyên Đán) and introduce its significance
- Para 1: When it takes place and general traditions
- Para 2: How your own family celebrates — specific customs and foods
- Para 3: What the festival means to you personally — community, memory, identity
- Para 4: How the festival may be changing in modern Vietnam
- Closing: Why you think this cultural tradition is worth preserving

### TIPS QUAN TRỌNG CHO PART 2

1. **Dùng 'signpost' language để dẫn dắt người nghe:**
   - "To start with..." / "Moving on to..." / "As for..." / "Finally, and perhaps most importantly..."

2. **Nói ở tốc độ tự nhiên** — đừng đọc nhanh. Nghỉ giữa các ý.

3. **Nếu nói xong sớm, thêm vào:**
   - "The reason this experience stands out for me is..."
   - "What I find most interesting in retrospect is..."
   - "And I think what makes this particularly memorable is..."

4. **KHÔNG BAO GIỜ nói:** "I don't know what to say" hoặc "I'm sorry, I forgot."

5. **Sử dụng thời gian chuẩn bị thực sự để ghi từ khóa**, không ngồi chờ.

6. **Về tense consistency:** Nếu kể chuyện quá khứ, dùng past tense xuyên suốt. Nếu mô tả thói quen/sự thật, dùng present tense.
